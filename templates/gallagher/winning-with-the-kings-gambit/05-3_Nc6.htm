5) 3 ... Nc6

Game 32. Glaskov - Soloviev
Moscow 1971

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 Nc6 (102)

This variation has never really caught on, probably because it does nothing to address Black's immediate problems. In fact, it is rather similar to the idea behind the Fischer Defence - a waiting move. Black wants to see White's next move before he makes his mind up.

4.Nc3

I should just mention that the position is more often reached via the Vienna Game (1.e4 e5 2.Nc3 Nc6 3.f4 ef 4.Nf3). The King's Gambit move order offers White an additional possibility, namely 4.d4. In fact, this move has been frowned upon by theoreticians, but I decided to look at it in some detail, as I found it hard to believe that such a natural move leads to a good game for Black. My conclusion is that it leads to rough equality (or unclarity) and I present my analysis in order to open the debate on 4.d4:

4...d5 (4...g5 5.d5) 5.ed (5.Nc3 is bad because of 5...de 6.Nxe4 Qe7! 7.Qe2 Bf5 8.Nc3 Qxe2+ 9.Bxe2 O-O-O; and 5.e5 can be met by 5...g5 6.h4 g4 7.Ng1 Bh6 and the early closing of the centre is not in White's favour) 5...Qxd5 6.Bxf4 (6.Nc3 Bb4 7.Bxf4 Bg4 transposes, but maybe Black can try 7...Qa5) 6...Bg4 7.Nc3 (This is better than taking on c7. Glaskov gives 7.Bxc7 Bxf3 8.Qxf3 Qxf3 9.gf Nxd4 as good for Black. This is not true as 10.Nc3! gives White a dangerous attack. Unfortunately, however, Black can play instead 7...Rc8! and then 8...Bxf3 which does offer him good chances of an advantage) 7...Bb4 (better than 7...Qe6+ 8.Qe2! and White retains an edge, e.g. 8...Bxf3 9.Qxe6+ fe 10.gf O-O-O (10...Nxd4 11.O-O-O) 11.O-O-O Rxd4 (11...Nxd4 12.Bh3 is strong) 12.Rxd4 Nxd4 13.Bh3 and White will win back his pawn with a good game; or 8...Qxe2+ 9.Bxe2 Bxf3 10.Bxf3 Nxd4 11.Bxb7 Rb8 12.O-O-O with advantage) 8.Be2 (8.a3 also deserves attention. Although slightly time consuming, it does force Black to exchange on c3, thereby considerably bolstering the defences of d4) 8...O-O-O 9.O-O Qd7 (Other squares are not so good, e.g. 9...Qa5 10.Ng5 Bxe2 11.Nxe2 or 9...Qh5 10.h3 in both cases with advantage to White) 10.d5!? (10.Ne5 Bxe2 11.Qxe2!? is a pawn sacrifice Black shouldn't accept. Instead, after 11...Nxe5 12.Bxe5 f6 13.Bg3 Bxc3 (13...Qxd4+ 14.Bf2! is still very dangerous for Black) 14.bc Ne7, the position is roughly level) 10...Bxc3 (Moving the knight is obviously disastrous (11 Ne5) and 10...Bxf3 11.Bxf3 Bc5+ 12.Kh1 Nd4 13.Be3 or 13.Bg4 f5 14.Be3 are clearly better for White) 11.dc Qxc6 (103) (It would be wrong for Black to exchange queens, e.g. 11...Qxd1 12.Rxd1 Bf6 (Black has little choice as after 12...Rxd1 13.Rxd1, 14.Rd7 is in the air) 13.cb+ Kxb7 14.Rxd8 Bxd8 15.Bc4 with a clear advantage to White).

12.Ne5. Suddenly, the board is ablaze. If Black now manages to avoid a few nasty traps he can reach a roughly level game:

(a) 12...Bxe5?? 13.Bxg4+.

(b) 12...Rxd1?? 13.Bxg4+ Kb8 14.Nxc6+.

(c) 12...Bxe2. This gives rise to some fascinating complications. White now has:

(c1) 13.Qxe2 Bxe5 14.Bxe5 (14.Qg4+ Qd7) 14...f6. It seems unlikely that White's slight initiative will fully compensate for the pawn.

(c2) 13.Qxd8+ Kxd8 14.Nxc6+ bc 15.bc! (15.Bxc7+? is met by 15...Kc8!) 15...Bxf1 16.Rxf1 and a draw is the likely outcome.

(c3) 13.Nxc6! and now:

(c31) 13...Bxd1. White now has a most elegant way to achieve an advantage: 14.Nxa7+ (14.Nxd8 Bd4+ 15.Kh1 Bh5! is good for Black) 14...Kb8 15.Nb5! (The black pieces remain en prise and White will collect in the most favourable fashion) 15...Be2 (15...Bd4+ 16.Nxd4 Rxd4 17.Be5! wins) 16.Bxc7+ Kc8! 17.Nxc3 (17.bc Bxb5) 17...Bxf1 18.Bxd8 Bxg2 19.Kxg2 (19.Bb6!?) 19...Kxd8 20.Rd1+ Kc8 21.Nd5 and White is very active.

(c32) 13...Rxd1 14.Nxa7+ Kb8 15.Rfxd1 Bxd1 (If 15...Kxa7 then 16.Rd8 is strong) 16.Nb5!? (16.Rxd1 Kxa7 17.bc Nf6 18.Bxc7 leads to a sight edge) 16...Bxc2 17.bc and White will restore material equality whilst retaining a sizeable initiative.

(d) 12...Qc5+ (Along with variation 'e', Black's best choice) 13.Kh1 Bxe2 (13...Rxd1 loses to 14.Bxg4+ Kb8 15.Raxd1 Nf6 16.bc; and 13...Be6 is met by 14.Nd3) 14.Qxe2 Bxe5 (14...Bxb2 15.Nd3! and there is no time for 15...Rxd3: 16.cd Bxa1 17.Qe8 mate!) 15.Bxe5 Nf6 (15...f6 16.Qg4+ is good for White) 16.Bxf6 gf 17.Rxf6 with approximate equality.

(e) 12...Qb6+ 13.Kh1 Bxe2 (13...Be6!? 14.Nd3! Bf6 15.a4 gives White a dangerous initiative. Black's queen is rather awkwardly placed; 13...Rxd1? is the same as in 'd') 14.Qxe2 Bxe5 15.Qg4+ Qe6 16.Qxe6+ fe 17.Bxe5 Nf6 18.Rxf6! gf 19.Bxf6 Rhf8 20.Bxd8 Rxd8 (20...Kxd8 21.Kg1 is a little better for White) 21.Rf1. A difficult rook ending has arisen in which White's chances shouldn't be inferior. A straight race seems inadvisable for Black, e.g. 21...Rd2 22.h4 (22.g4 is also good) 22...Rxc2 23.Rf7 Rxb2 24.Rxh7 c5 25.h5 c4 26.h6 c3 27.Rh8+ Kd7 28.h7 and wins: if 28...c2 then 29.Rd8+ Kc7 30.Rc8+; or 28...Rb1+ 29.Kh2 c2 30.Rd8+ Kxd8 31.h8Q+ Kc7 32.Qh7+ and Qxc2.

4...g5

Now that ... d5 has been ruled out, Black's most obvious plan is to hang on to his f-pawn.

4...Nf6 has also been played, but by replying 5.Bc4 the game has transposed into a variation of the Bishop's Gambit known to be favourable for White, e.g. 5...Bb4 6.Nd5! O-O 7.O-O Nxe4 (or 7...Nxd5 8.ed Ne7 9.Ng5 h6 10.Ne4 Ng6 11.c3 Ba5 12.d4 with advantage to White) 8.d4. White will soon win one of his pawns back and Black's development looks particularly ineffective. Here are a couple of examples from practice:

(a) 8...Be7 9.Bxf4 d6 10.Qd3 Nf6 11.Ng5 g6 12.Nxe7+ Nxe7 13.Nxf7! Rxf7 14.Bxf7+ Kxf7 15.Bg5 Neg8 16.Rxf6+ Nxf6 17.Rf1 with a winning position for White in Spielmann - Grünfeld, Innsbruck 1922.

(b) 8...Nf6 9.Ng5!? (9.Nxb4 and 10.Bxf4 also looks good) 9...h6 (Against 9...Nxd5, Bogoljubov recommended 10.Nxf7 with a crushing attack) 10.Nxf7! Rxf7 11.Nxf6+ gf 12.Qh5 with a winning attack Khlusevich - Verkhovtsev, USSR 1973.

5.h4

With this move White signals his intention of playing an Allgaier (or a Hamppe-Allgaier as it is known with the queen's knights already out). 5.d4 can be seen in game 34.

5...g4

6.Ng5 (104)

6...h6

Black naturally forces the sacrifice on f7. Other continuations are dubious:

(a) 6...f6 7.Qxg4 fg 8.Qh5+ Ke7 9.Qxg5+ Nf6 10.Nd5+ Kf7 11.Bc4 (Chigorin).

(b) 6...d5 7.d4 h6 8.ed with a good game for White.

7.Nxf7 Kxf7

8.d4!

This is the strongest, opening more lines and holding the check at c4 in reserve. The first time I came across Allgaier-type positions, I could not take them seriously. White gives up a piece for a pawn and then calmly continues as if nothing had happened. But after a little (in fact a lot of) study, I began to understand that Black's defensive task is by no means easy.

Black's main problem is that his king will never find a safe haven and if White manages to complete his development, picking up a pawn or two along the way, he will have very good compensation.

The Hamppe-Allgaier is probably more favourable for White than the normal version (the inclusion of Nc3 means that White is one step nearer to evacuating his king to the queen-side, whilst the knight on c6 can sometimes be attacked by d5 or ed5). Of course, Black does have defensive resources, including suddenly switching to an attack against the white king if circumstances permit.

8...d5 (105)

For 8...f3 see game 33.

8...d6 has also been played, but it does little to hinder White's smooth development, e.g. 9.Bxf4 Nf6 (or 9...Bg7 10.Be3 Bf6 11.g3 Nge7 12.Bc4+ Kg7 13.Qd2 Bd7 14.O-O-O with excellent attacking chances) 10.Bc4+ Kg7 11.a3!? (White waits to see where the black pieces are deployed before deciding on the continuation of the attack) 11...Be7 12.O-O Nxe4?! 13.Nxe4 d5 14.Nf2 dc 15.d5 Bxh4 16.dc Qxd1 17.Raxd1 Rd8 18.Rxd8 Bxd8 19.cb Bxb7 20.Nxg4 with a clear advantage to White in Triguda-Tarasov, Corr. 1982/83.

9.Bxf4!

White doesn't waste any time before opening the f-file.

9...Bb4

There are a number of other defensive tries:

(a) 9...Kg7 10.Nxd5 Bd6 11.e5 Qe8 12.Qd2 with advantage to White.

(b) 9...Bg7 10.Be3 Bf6 11.g3 de 12.Bc4+ Kg7 13.O-O Bxd4 14.Rf7+ Kg6 15.h5+ Kxh5 16.Rg7! with a mating attack. This occurred in a blindfold simultaneous exhibition of Pillsbury's in 1900.

(c) 9...Nf6 10.Bd3 (10.Be2!? or 10.Nxd5 Nxd5 11.Bc4 deserve attention) 10...de (or 10...Bb4 11.O-O Bxc3 12.bc Kg7 13.ed Qxd5 14.Qd2 Bd7 15.Rab1! Rae8 16.Rb5 Qxa2 17.Bxh6+! with a winning attack) 11.Bc4+ Ke8 12.d5 Na5 (12...Ne7 13.Nb5) 13.Be2 Bd6 14.Qd2 h5 15.O-O and White has a strong attack. In Romashkevich - Shabelsky, Corr. 1895, Black lost his extra piece after 15...Qe7 16.Bxd6 cd 17.Qd4!

10.Be2 Bxc3+

11.bc Nf6

12.O-O Kg7

13.c4! (106)

Now Black is unable to maintain a foothold in the centre and the opening of the position will give added power to the white bishops.

13...de

13...Nxe4 loses in spectacular fashion: 14.cd Nc3 (14...Qxh4 15.dc g3 is too slow: 16.Be5+ Kg6 17.Bh5+ wins; 14...Qxd5 15.c4 followed by 16 d5 gives White a strong attack) 15.dc!! Nxd1 16.Be5+ Kg6 (16...Kg8 loses to 17.Bc4+ Kh7 18.Rf7+ Kg6 19.Rg7+ Kf5 (19...Kh5 20.Bf7+ Kxh4 21.Kh2) 20.Rxd1 Rf8 21.Rf1+ Ke4 22.Re1+ Kf5 23.d5 and White wins) 17.Bd3+ Kh5 18.cb! Bd7 19.baQ Qxa8 20.Raxd1 Rf8 21.Rxf8 Qxf8 22.Rf1 Qb4. So far all this analysis was carried out by a character named Trumberg in 1893. Here he went astray with 23.g3 but Glaskov has pointed out the way to victory: 23.Bg3! and Black is powerless against the threat of Re1-e5.

Game 33. Gallagher - Hresc
Geneva 1991

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 Nc6

4.Nc3 g5

5.h4 g4

6.Ng5 h6

7.Nxf7 Kxf7

8.d4 f3 (108)

The main purpose of this move is to ensure that the f-file stays closed for as long as possible. The move also has disruptive qualities which could especially be seen after 9.gf Qe7.

9.Bc4+

White can also consider various other moves, such as 9.Be3 or 9.Bf4, but the text is the most logical. Black is forced to make the difficult decision between ... Kg7 or ... Ke8, but first...

9...d5

... lines are opened for his undeveloped pieces.

10.Bxd5+

10.ed is inadvisable, offering Black the pleasant choice of 10...Na5 or 10...fg!?

10...Kg7

By placing his king on g7, Hresc opts for the most common way. The main problem with 10...Ke8 is that, with the king stuck in the centre, White will have long-term compensation for the piece, even if his attack may be initially less strong. It is quite probable that, theoretically speaking, White doesn't have enough for the piece, but over the board (or even through the post) the position is very hard to defend, e.g. 11.gf Be7 (Chigorin considered that after 11...Nf6 12.f4 Bb4 13.Bxc6+ bc 14.Qd3 (with the threat of e5) White has a strong attack) 12.Be3 Bxh4+ 13.Kd2 Bg5 14.f4 Bf6 15.Qg1! and Black has a difficult defence in front of him (don't forget he can't castle!).

11.gf Bb4

11...Be7 looks like the critical test, e.g. 12.O-O Bxh4 13.f4 (13.fg Nf6) and the position is very hard to assess.

Against 11...Nf6. Tarrasch gave the following variation: 12.Bf4 Ne7 13.Be5 Ng6 14.f4! with a dangerous attack.

12.Be3 (109)

I spent a long time here wondering about the best square for the bishop. I eventually settled for the solid Be3. My reasoning was along the following lines: I've got two pawns for the piece, a big centre and my opponent's king is not too happy. All that remains to be done is to defend the d-pawn so that I can retreat my light-squared bishop, castle long and mate will follow shortly. It is, of course, necessary to think positively when you play the King's Gambit!

12...Nf6

13.Bc4 Qe7

13...Re8 could well be better. My opponent didn't like it because it weakened his h6-pawn.

14.Qe2

14.Qd2 also came into consideration but I preferred to keep my f-pawn defended and I didn't wish to have my knight on c3 still pinned after I castled.

14...gf

Black needs the g4-square for his minor pieces.

15.Qxf3 Bg4?

But this is the wrong one. 15...Ng4 offered better chances. Of course 15...Qxe4 loses to Rg1+.

16.Rg1 h5

17.e5!

Now Black realises that if he moves the knight the exchange sacrifice on g4 will be murderous (probably White should castle first). There is little choice but to give back the piece.

17...Nxe5

18.de Qxe5 (110)

19.O-O-O

This was a most enjoyable move to play for two reasons. Firstly, it was pleasant to have my king out of the centre; and secondly, because I found it very artistic to use my queen as a shield in order to provide the move with its legality.

19...Bc5?

Black overlooks the threat, but his position was pretty hopeless anyway, e.g. 19...Rad8 (19...Bxc3 20.bc Qxc3 21.Bh6+) 20.Nd5! followed by Bd4 will prove decisive.

20.Rd7+! Nxd7

Obviously this is not the best, but 20...Be7 21.Bd4! or 20...Kf8 21.Bxc5+ Ke8 22.Re7+ (there are also other ways e.g. 22.Qf2!?) 22...Qxe7 23.Bxe7 Bxf3 24.Bxf6 win for White.

21.Qf7 mate

Game 34. Motwani - Kula
Berlin 1991

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 Nc6

4.Nc3 g5

5.d4 (111)

5...g4

Black has no other good way of meeting the threat of d5, e.g.

(a) 5...Bg7 6.d5 Ne5 7.d6! with the advantage.

(b) 5...d6 6.d5 (6.h4 g4 7.Ng5 h6 8.Nxf7 Kxf7 9.Bxf4 with a Hampe-Allgaier position favourable for White) 6...Ne5 7.Bb5+ Bd7 8.Bxd7+ Kxd7 (8...Nxd7 is clearly weaker, e.g. 9.Qd4 f6 10.h4 g4 11.Ng5! Nc5 12.Ne6 Nxe6 13.de c6 14.Bxf4 Qb6 15.Qd3 O-O-O 16.O-O-O h5 17.Qg3 Qc7 18.Rd3 Qe7 19.Rhd1 Qxe6 20.Bxd6 Bxd6 21.Rxd6 with a clear advantage, Arnason-Adams, Manila (ol) 1992) 9.h4 Nxf3+ 10.Qxf3 gh 11.Bxf4 and White is better.

6.Bc4 gf

We are now in the so-called Pierce Gambit.

7.O-O (112)

It has been known for a long time that 7.Qxf3 Nxd4 is good for Black, but maybe White can try 7.Bxf4!? here. One important point being that 7...fg 8.Bxf7+ Kxf7 9.Qh5+ Kg7 10.Rg1 wins for White. 7...Bg7 8.O-O transposes to the note to Black's seventh move, whilst avoiding the 7...d5 variation.

7...Qg5?!

Of course, Black has a large number of alternatives. I would just like to remind the reader that it will be of more benefit to study the variations in order to get a feel for the attack, rather than to remember them in rote fashion. If the latter approach is adopted, you will find yourself at sixes and sevens when faced with a novelty or, heaven forbid, when you forget the theory.

(a) 7...fg (This is suicidal) 8.Bxf7+ Kxf7 9.Qh5+ Kg7 10.Qg4+ Kf7 11.Rxf4+ Nf6 12.Nd5 (Analysis by Lange, 1856).

(b) 7...Bg7 8.Bxf4 Bxd4+ (Against 8...Qf6 9.Be3 is strong as 9...Nxd4 10.Nd5 Qg6 11.Rxf3! Nxf3+ 12.Qxf3 Qc6 13.Bb5 Qd6 14.Rf1 wins; 8...Nxd4 9.Be3 (9.Bxf7+ deserves attention) 9...c5 10.Nb5 Nxb5 11.Bxb5 Qb6 12.Qxf3 f6 13.Qh5+ Kd8 14.Bxc5 and White has a very strong attack) 9.Kh1 Bxc3 (As usual in this variation capturing on g2 only serves to open further lines for White) 10.Bxf7+ (The second piece sacrifice is a typical way of strengthening the attack) 10...Kxf7 11.Qd5+ Ke8 (11...Kg7 is worse: 12.Rxf3 Bf6 13.e5 Be7 14.Rg3+ Kf8 15.Rxg8+ 1-0 is Keres - Wilkins, Corr. 1933) 12.Qh5+ Ke7 and now Glaskov's and Estrin's suggestion of 13.e5 looks very strong.

(c) 7...d6 8.Qxf3 Be6 9.Bb5! Bd7 10.Qxf4 Qf6 11.Qxf6 Nxf6 12.Rxf6 Bg7 13.Bg5 O-O 14.Raf1 with advantage to White (Tseitlin).

(d) 7...Nxd4!? 8.Bxf4! (Not 8.Qxd4?? Qg5 and White can resign) 8...Bc5! (An improvement on the old 8...Qf6 9.Nd5 Qg7 10.Nxc7+ Kd8 11.g3 with a winning position for White). After 8...Bc5, Polaksek-Karolyi, Prague 1988, continued: 9.Bxf7+ Kxf7 10.Be3 Ke8! 11.Bxd4 Bxd4 12.Qxd4 Qf6 13.Qd3 Ne7 14.Rxf3 Qe5 and Black eventually beat off the attack and converted his material advantage. A better try for White would have been 9.Kh1!

(e) 7...d5 (This is the most common move) 8.ed Bg4 (113).

Now White has two possibilities:

(e1) 9.Qe1+ Be7! (9...Nce7 10.Bb5+ Bd7 11.Qe5) 10.Bxf4 Nxd4 11.Be5 Ne2+ 12.Nxe2 fe 13.Bxe2 Bxe2 14.Qxe2 f6 and now White's best is to force a draw with 15.Rxf6! Nxf6 (15...Qxd5? 16.Qh5+ Kd8 17.Rd1 wins) 16.Bxf6 Rf8 17.Qh5+ Rf7 (17...Kd7 18.Qf5+ is also a draw) 18.Re1 Kf8 19.Qh6+ Ke8 20.Qh5.

(e2) 9.Qd2. White wishes to capture with the queen on f4, hoping that this will create decisive threats on the f-file. Black has a number of defensive tries, e.g.

(e21) 9...Bg7 10.Qxf4! Bxd4+ 11.Kh1 fg+ 12.Kxg2 Qh4 13.dc (Estrin also gives 13.Qxf7+ Kd8 14.Qf8+ Kd7 15.dc+ bc 16.Qf4 Bh3+ 17.Kh1 Qxf4 18.Rxf4 Bxc3 19.bc Ne7 20.Ba3! with advantage to White) 13...O-O-O 14.cb+ Kxb7 15.Qg3 Qxg3+ 16.hg Be6 17.Bxe6 fe 18.Rf7 with advantage to White.

(e22) 9...Na5 10.Bb5+ c6 (Weaker is 10...Bd7 11.Qxf4 Bxb5 12.Nxb5 Bd6 13.Qxf3 Qd7 14.Nxd6+ cd 15.b4 and White is better) 11.Qxf4 Nf6 (After 11...cb probably best is 12.Qxg4 with a very strong attack) 12.Re1+ (Lepeshkin has shown that 12.Ne4 Bg7! is good for Black, e.g. 13.Nd6+ Kf8 14.gf Bh5 with every chance of a successful defence or 13.Nxf6+ Qxf6 14.Qxg4 Qxd4+) 12...Kd7 13.Ne4 (114)

Here Lepeshkin gives 13...f2+ as clearly better for Black. This assessment doesn't seem correct, e.g. 14.Qxf2! (14.Nxf2 is less good, on account of 14...Bd6!) 14...Nxe4 (After 14...Be7, the simple 15.dc+ bc 16.Bd3 leaves White with excellent play for the piece, whilst 15.Ng5 could also be worth investigation) 15.Rxe4 (15.Qxf7+ Qe7 16.dc+ bc 17.Qd5+ Kc7 18.Bf4+ Kb6 is a false trail, although 18.Qxe4 may not be completely hopeless) 15...f5 16.Re6! cb 17.h3!

Now Black will lose one of his extra pieces (17...Bh5 or 17...Bd1 lose to 18.Qxf5) and on top of this he will be unable to prevent the infiltration of the white queen to f5. In the game Gallagher - Kamber, Olten 1992, Black ran away with his king, but after 17...Kc8 18.hg b6 19.Qxf5 Kb7 20.Bg5, White had an overwhelming position.

17...Bxh3 fares no better: 18.gh Rg8+ (or 18...Qc7 19.Qxf5! Qg3+ 20.Kh1) 19.Kh1 Rg6 20.Qxf5 Rxe6 21.Qxe6+ (21.de+ is also better for White) 21...Kc7 22.Bf4+ Bd6 23.Rf1! Nc4 24.b3 Bxf4 25.bc Qh4 26.d6+ and White wins. Relatively best for Black is to bring his knight back into play. After 17...Nc4 18.hg Nd6 19.gf White's position remains clearly preferable.

Instead of 13...f2+ Black can play 13...Nxe4. There could then follow 14.Qxg4+ Kc7 15.Qxe4 cb 16.Bf4+ Kb6 (16...Bd6 loses to 17.Qe7+!) and White obviously has some compensation for the piece but it is difficult to say how much. 17.a4 looks a tempting continuation.

(e23) 9...Nce7 10.Qxf4 Qd7 (10...Nh6 is well met by 11.Ne4! Neg8 (11...Bg7? 12.Qxh6) 12.Bb5+ Bd7 13.Qe5+ Qe7 14.Bxd7+ Kxd7 15.Nc5+ Kc8 16.Qxh8 fg 17.Rd1 b6 18.Bxh6 Nxh6 19.Re1 and White had the advantage in Zuckerman - Markov, Corr. 1985/86) 11.d6! Ng6 (Lepeshkin considers 11...O-O-O to be better for Black, but after 12.dc! Re8 (12...Qxd4+ loses to 13.Be3 Qxf4 14.cdQ+!) 13.Bxf7 and White's position is clearly preferable) 12.Qe4+ Kd8 13.h3 Be6 14.Bxe6 Qxe6 15.Qxb7 Rc8 16.dc+ Rxc7 17.Qa8+ Rc8 18.Qxf3 and White has more than enough for the piece.

Let us return to Motwani - Kula after 7...Qg5.

8.Rxf3 Nxd4

9.Bxf7+!

Of course not 9.Qxd4 Bc5. Now Black is unable to take the bishop, e.g. 9...Kxf7 10.Rxf4+ Nf6 11.Nd5 Qe5 12.Rxf6+ Kg8 13.Qg4+ and wins.

10.Rf2

10.Rxf4 is also possible but Motwani's move threatens to take the knight.

10...Qg7

11.Bxf4 Ne7

12.Qh5

White's attack is already decisive.

12...d6

13.Bg5 Nxc2

14.Raf1

15.Bg8!

If Black takes the bishop, mate follows very shortly.
