8) Rare Third Moves

Game 39. Spassky - Seirawan
Montpelller 1985

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 Ne7 (128)

The so-called Bonsch-Osmolovsky variation. Black plans to bring his knight around to g6 in order to give protection to his extra pawn. But 3...Ne7 doesn't really put any pressure on White's position and g6 turns out to be not such a solid base.

4.d4 d5

Black has to stake a claim in the centre. After 4...Ng6 5.h4! gives White a good game, e.g. 5...h5 6.Nc3 Be7 7.Nd5 Nxh4 (7...Bxh4+ 8.Nxh4 Nxh4 9.Bxf4 d6 10.Bg3 Ng6 11.Rxh5 is good for White) 8.Bxf4 with a clear advantage.

5.Nc3

Blocking the centre with 5.e5 unnecessarily concedes control of the white squares, e.g. 5...Ng6 6.Bd3 Be7 7.Bxg6 fg 8.Bxf4 g5 9.Bg3 g4 and Black already had the upper hand in Naftalan - Martiroshan, Corr. 1985/87.

5...de

6.Nxe4 Ng6

7.h4!

Spassky's improvement on his game against Novopashin, 23 years earlier, where he played 7.Bc4.

7...Qe7 (129)

7...Be7 8.h5 Nh4 9.Bxf4 Bg4 10.h6! with advantage to White in Kuznetsov - Bonsch-Osmolovsky, Moscow 1964.

8.Kf2!

A wonderful move, after which Black's pieces seem to be on silly squares. Of course the knight is taboo:

8...Qxe4 9.Bb5+ and 10.Re1.

8...Bg4

9.h5 Nh4

10.Bxf4

So White has regained the pawn and his well-centralised position gives him a clear advantage.

10...Nc6

11.Bb5!

This ensures that Black's king will find no sanctuary.

11...O-O-O

12.Bxc6 bc

13.Qd3

Unpinning and also threatening Qa6+.

13...Nxf3

13...Bxf3 is out of the question as Black must retain this bishop to try to plug the holes around his king.

14.gf Bf5

15.Qa6+ Kb8

16.Nc5 Bc8

17.Qxc6 Rxd4

If Black doesn't take the pawn he is totally lost.

18.Rae1!

The queen has no square as c7 must remain defended.

18...Rxf4

19.Qb5+ Ka8

20.Qc6+ Kb8

21.Rxe7 Bxe7

22.Rd1 Rf6

22...Bxc5 23.Qxc5 Bb7 24.Rd3! is very strong.

23.Nd7+ Bxd7

24.Qxd7 Rd8

24...Bc5+ would put up a little more resistance, but the ending is technically lost.

25.Qb5+ Kc8

26.Rxd8+ Bxd8

27.Qa4

If Black's queenside pawns were united he might have some slight drawing chances.

27...g5

28.Qxa7 Rf4

28...g4 fails to 29.Qa8+ and 30.Qa4+.

29.Qa6+ Kb8

30.Qd3 Be7

31.Qxh7 g4

32.Kg3 1-0

Game 40. Schlechter - Teichmann
Vienna 1903

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 f5 (130)

This only serves to weaken Black's kingside and even in 1903 it wasn't thought of very highly.

4.e5!

4.ef d5 would justify Black's third move.

4...g5

Other moves also lead to a bad game:

(a) 4...d5 5.h4!? followed by d4 and Bxf4.

(b) 4...d6 5.Qe2 Be7 6.d4 Nc6 7.Bxf4 de 8.de Nd4 9.Qc4 Ne6 (9...Nxf3+ 10.gf Bh4+ 11.Ke2 is good for White) 10.Bd3 with the better game for White.

5.d4 g4

5...d5 6.c4 Be6 7.Nc3 Bb4 8.h4 was good for White in Glaskov - Yaroshevsky, Moscow 1971.

6.Bxf4!

This traditional piece sacrifice is given added venom by the fact that Black has played ... f5.

6...gf

7.Qxf3 Qh4+

This is the only sensible way to stop Qh5+.

8.g3 Qg4

9.Qe3

Black has gained a moment's breathing space, but is unable to do anything with it.

9...Nc6

10.Be2 Qg6

11.Nc3 Bb4

12.d5!

Forcing one of Black's few developed pieces to retreat.

12...Nd8

13.O-O-O h6

14.g4!

White opens lines on the kingside, which provide added fuel for the attack.

14...Ne7

15.d6 Ne6

If the knight had moved, White could have won easily by 16.gf or 16.Nd5.

16.de Bc5

17.Qg3 Bxe7

Material equality has been restored, but Black hasn't solved any of his problems.

18.gf Qxf5

19.Be3 Bg5

20.Nd5 Bxe3+

21.Qxe3 Kd8

21...Qg5+ is a little better. After 22.Qxg5 hg 23.Bg4! Rb8 24.Bxe6 de 25.Nxc7+ White is a pawn up with a good position.

22.Rhg1 Qf8

23.Rdf1 Qc5

24.Qf3 Rf8 (131)

Black is playing without his queenside and so it is not surprising that White now has a decisive blow.

25.Rg8! 1-0

An elegant finish.

25...Rxg8 26.Qf6+ Ke8 27.Qf7+.
