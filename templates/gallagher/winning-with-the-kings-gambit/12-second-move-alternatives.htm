12) Second Move Alternatives

Game 57. Bronstein - Yusupov
USSR 1981

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 Nf6 (192)

This counter-attacking move has never attracted a great following, as the best that Black can hope for is transposition to the Schallop Defence.

2...Qh4+ is the subject of game 58.

2...d6 is extremely passive and is only likely to be played by devotees of the Philidor Defence. White has no particular difficulty in achieving a pleasant position with a space advantage. Here are a couple of examples after 3.Nf3 :

a) 3...Nd7 4.d4 Ngf6 5.fe Nxe4 6.Bd3 d5 7.Nc3!? Nxc3 8.bc Be7 9.O-O O-O 10.Qe1 Re8 11.Qg3 Nf8 12.Ng5 f6 13.ef Bxf6 14.Bd2 c5 15.Rxf6! Qxf6 16.Rf1 Qe7 17.Rxf8+ Kxf8 18.Qf3+ Kg8 19.Bxh7+ Kh8 20.Qh5 Bg4 21.Qh4 Qf6 22.Bg6+ Kg8 23.Bf7+ Kf8 24.Qxg4 Re4 25.Nh7+ Kxf7 26.Nxf6 Rxg4 27.Nxg4 with a winning endgame for White in Vritsky - Lepman, Simferopol 1986.

b) 3...Nc6 4.d4 ed 5.Nxd4 Nf6 6.Nc3 Bd7 7.Be3 Be7 8.Qd2 O-O 9.O-O-O a6 10.Be2 Qc8 11.h3 Nxd4 12.Bxd4 Bc6 13.Bf3 Rd8 14.g4 with a clearly better position for White, Shikova -Honfi, Plovdiv 1980.

3.Nf3

3.fe is also playable. The game Fischer - Wade, Vinkovci 1968, continued: 3...Nxe4 4.Nf3 Ng5 5.d4 Nxf3+ 6.Qxf3 Qh4+ 7.Qf2 Qxf2+ 8.Kxf2 Nc6 9.c3 d6 10.ed Bxd6 11.Nd2 Be6 12.Ne4 Be7 13.Ng5 Bxg5 14.Bxg5 with a better endgame for White.

3...Nxe4

Black can play 3...d5 and after 4.fe Nxe4 (4...de 5.ef ef 6.Qxf3 Nc6 7.Bb5 Qxf6 8.Qxf6 gf 9.d4! gives White a good game) 5.d3 Nc5 we transpose back to the game.

3...ef is the Schallop Defence.

4.fe

After 4.Qe2, Black should avoid 4...Nc5 5.d4 Ne6 6.f5! winning. Better is 4...d5.

4...d5

5.d3 Nc5

6.d4 Ne6 (193)

6...Ne4 is dubious as Black would soon be forced to exchange it off, at the same time assisting White's development, e.g. Rohde - Martz, Lone Pine 1977, continued: 7.Bd3 (7.c4) 7...Be7 8.O-O O-O (8...c5 9.c4 is good for White) 9.Nbd2! Nxd2 10.Bxd2 c5?! 11.dc Bxc5+ 12.Kh1 Bg4 13.Qe1 Nc6 14.Bxh7+! winning.

7.c4!

It's important to gain some space on the queenside and to place d5 under some pressure.

7...Bb4+

7...c6 8.Nc3 Be7 9.Be3 O-O 10.Qd2 b6 11.Bd3 Na6 12.cd cd 13.Rd1 led to a good game for White in Bronstein - Holmov, Vilnius 1975.

8.Bd2 Bxd2+

9.Qxd2 c6

10.Nc3 O-O

11.Rc1 Nc7?

Black has spent five moves wandering around with this knight only to end up on c7. 11...b6 followed by 12...Ba6 would have been a better try.

12.cd cd

12...Nxd5 seems more logical, but after 13.Nxd5 Qxd5 14.Bc4 Qe4+ 15.Qe2 Qxe2+ 16.Kxe2 White has a very active position.

13.Bd3

Black's kingside is beginning to look decidedly shaky.

13...Bg4

14.Ng5! f5

This is forced as 14...h6 loses to 15.Nh7! Re8 16.O-O intending 17.Nf6+.

15.h3

15.O-O would have been even stronger, for example: 15...h6 16.Nxd5! Nxd5 17.Ne6 Qe7 18.Nxf8 Kxf8 19.h3 Bh5 20.Rxf5+, winning.

15...Bh5

16.O-O Bg6

16...h6 17.Nxd5! is similar to the previous note.

17.Nb5!

White's knight forces his way into the fine outpost on d6, as 17...Nxb5 18.Ne6! wins, e.g. 18...Qb6 19.Nxf8 Qxd4+ 20.Kh1 Kxf8 21.Qg5! Qxd3 22.Qd8+ Kf7 23.e6+ Kxe6 24.Rfe1+.

17...Nba6

18.Nd6 h6

19.Nf3 Ne6

20.Kh1! Rb8

21.Ng1!

White threatens to win the f-pawn and gives himself the possibility of rerouting the knight to a more effective square (c3 or f4).

21...Qg5

22.Qf2 Nb4

More solid would have been 22...Nac7 and 23...Ne8.

23.Bb5! f4

23...Nxa2 24.Ra1.

24.Nf3!

Times have changed, so the knight returns.

24...Qe7

25.Qd2 Na6

26.Bd3 Bh5 (194)

An exchange of bishops would have enabled White to penetrate on the king-side white squares.

27.Bc2!

With the idea of 28.Qd3.

28.Qd3 g6

30.Ba4

Over the next ten moves or so, White slowly improves his position, not wishing to undertake anything drastic until the time control has been negotiated.

With a strong passed pawn, control of the c-file and a beautiful outpost on e5, White has a decisive advantage.

Game 58. N. McDonald - Bachmayr
Zug 1991

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 Qh4+

About an hour before the game McDonald decided to play the first King's Gambit of his life. I hurriedly gave him a few tips in some of the critical lines, but when he enquired about 2...Qh4+ I replied "Oh don't worry Neil, nobody ever plays that!".

3.g3 Qe7 (195)

4.fe

This leads to an endgame with only a small advantage. If White feels like punishing Black for his cheeky check, then 4.Nc3! is the move. Black now has a choice of taking on f4 or playing the solid ... d6:

a) 4...ef 5.d4 fg 6.Bf4!. White has a very dangerous initiative and the black queen is looking rather silly. Relatively best for Black is 6...d5 (6...Nf6 7.e5 d6 8.Qe2!) and now 7.hg! looks best (Glaskov recommends 7.Nxd5, but I believe that Black has sufficient defensive resources after the exchange of queens). White is only a pawn down with a big lead in development (Note that the rook on h1 is already developed). I'm looking forward to a practical testing.

b) 4...d6 5.Nf3 (5.d4 is interesting and 5.Nd5 Qd8 6.d4 c6 7.Nc3 deserves attention. If you count the useful moves White is about one and a half up) 5...Bg4 6.h3 Bxf3 7.Qxf3 Nf6 8.d3. After this calm move, White's superiority becomes apparent: the bishop pair and a kingside space advantage can be added to the awkward placing of the black queen.

4...d6!

5.ed Qxe4+

6.Qe2 Qxe2+

7.Nxe2 Bxd6

8.Nbc3 c6

Black is worried about 9.Nb5 and 8...Bd7 can be met by 9.Nd4!, but the text does hinder Black's development. A better equalising try would have been 8...a6 and after 9.Bg2, simply 9...Nc6.

9.d3

This is the most natural but White has an interesting alternative: 9.b3 Nf6 10.Bb2 Bg4 11.Bg2 Nbd7 12.h3 Bf5 13.O-O-O O-O-O 14.Rhf1 Bg6 15.Nf4 with an edge for White, Murey - Eng, Beersheeva 1985.

9...Nf6

10.Bg5 Nbd7

11.Bg2 h6

12.Bf4

White is happy to exchange these bishops as 8...c6 also weakened the dark squares.

12...Bxf4

13.Nxf4 O-O (196)

It was more accurate to have played 13...Nc5. After 14.b4 Ne6 15.Nxe6 Bxe6 16.b5 Nd5 17.Nxd5 Bxd5 18.Bxd5 cd 19.Kd2 White has a slightly better endgame, but with careful defence Black should be assured of a draw.

14.b4!

White finds a very good plan ... Ne5 is prevented and the b-pawn can be advanced further in order to soften up the long diagonal.

14...Re8+

15.Kd2 Ne5

16.b5 Bg4

17.bc bc

Black has a temporary initiative, but his weak queenside is not going to go away.

18.h3 Bf3

19.Rhf1 Bxg2

20.Nxg2 Rad8

21.Rae1 c5

22.Rf5!

The only move but a good one.

22...Nc4+

23.Kd1 Rxe1+

24.Kxe1 Re8+

25.Kf2 Ne5

26.Ne3 Re6

27.Rf4!

Because of the threat of Ra4, Black has little choice but to allow an exchange of rooks. After this, the white knights will be able to occupy menacing squares, harassing the queenside pawns, whilst the more active position of his king will add to his advantage.

27...Ra6

28.Ra4 Rxa4

29.Nxa4 Ned7

30.Nc4 Nd5

31.a3 Nc7

This knight manoeuvre was Black's best way to try to hold the queenside.

32.Nd6 Kf8

33.Ke3 a6

34.Nb7 Nb5

The alternative 34...Ne6 35.Ke4 Ke7 36.Kd5 is unattractive for Black. The attempt to complicate with 36...f5 fails to 37.Naxc5 Ndxc5 38.Nxc5 f4 39.Nxe6! f3 40.Nd4 f2 41.Nf5+ and the pawn is stopped just in time.

35.Naxc5 Nxc5

36.Nxc5 Nxa3

37.c4 Nc2+

38.Kd2 Nb4

39.Kc3 a5

40.d4 Nc6

41.d5 Ne5

42.Kd4 f6

43.d6 h5

44.Kd5 Ke8

45.Ke6 1-0

McDonald certainly hadn't expected his first King's Gambit to provide one of his best positional games!
