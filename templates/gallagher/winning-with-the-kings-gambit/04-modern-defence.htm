4) Modern Defence

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 d5 (73)

Black makes no attempt to keep the gambit pawn and concentrates on opening lines for his pieces and joining in the battle for the initiative.

As a King's Gambit player, you should make sure that you are thoroughly acquainted with the lines arising from the Modern Defence, not least because it has been recommended by several publications as a simple way to equalise against the King's Gambit. But of course this is not the case, even when the position becomes simplified White often maintains a slight, but persistent advantage. This can usually be attributed to the extra central pawn in his possession, or to the activity of his major pieces on the open files of the kingside.

After 4.ed Nf6. I have selected two of the most interesting lines for White (at least in my opinion): 5.Bc4 and 5.Bb5+. The latter is normally regarded as the main line, but recently Black has been finding life more difficult against 5.Bc4. Let's have a look at some games.

Game 24. Spassky - Bronstein
Leningrad 1960

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 d5

4.ed Bd6 (74)

Normally in this variation, Black captures the pawn on d5 and White the one on f4. With 4...Bd6, however, Black intends a different sort of game, but one in which he will have to be careful to avoid being steam-rollered by the white centre.

Apart from 4...Nf6 (the subject of subsequent games), there are a number of other rarely played alternatives:

(a) 4...Qxd5 (This can't be good) 5.Nc3 Qe6+ (Other queen moves are no better, e.g. 5...Qh5 6.Be2 ; or 5...Qa5 6.Bc4 Nf6 7.Qe2+ Be7 8.d4 ; 5...Qd8!?) 6.Be2 Bd6 7.O-O Ne7 8.d4 O-O 9.Ng5 with a clear advantage to White.

(b) 4...Be7 5.Bc4 (or 5.Bb5+ c6 6.dc bc 7.Bc4 Bh4+ 8.Kf1 with a good game for White) 5...Bh4+ 6.Kf1. This position could also be reached via the Cunningham. It seems to me that Black has wasted too much time. The game Blaess -Schoppmeyer, Bundesliga 1986/87, continued: 6...Bf6 7.d4 Ne7 8.Nc3 O-O 9.Bxf4 b5 10.Nxb5 Ba6 11.d6! cd 12.a4 and Black has no compensation for the pawn.

(c) 4...c6 5.d4 Bd6 (After 5...cd 6.Bxf4 White's favourable pawn structure will guarantee a slight advantage). Now 6.Nc3 leads to the Nimzowitsch Counter-Gambit (which we consider later), but 6.dc Nxc6 7.Bd3 is also interesting.

5.Nc3 Spassky has a liking for an early Nc3 in many lines of the King's Gambit, but here it only serves to limit White's options by blocking the c-pawn.

After the natural 5.d4, Black normally chooses from 5...Nf6 or 5...Ne7 :

(a) 5...Nf6 6.c4 (Against 6.Qe2+ Black should play 6...Be7!. It would then be dangerous for White to try and win a pawn as Black can create awkward threats on the e-file, e.g. 7.c4 O-O 8.Bxf4 Re8 9.Nc3 Bd6 10.Ne5 c5!) 6...O-O (6...Bg4 7.Bd3 O-O 8.O-O and White's central pawns are strong). In this position, 7.Ne5 is normally recommended, but with the rest of his pieces still at home, this knight foray looks premature, e.g. 7...Re8 8.Bxf4 Nbd7 9.Be2 (9.Qe2 Nxe5 10.de Ng4) 9...Nxe5 10.de Bxe5 11.Bxe5 (or 11.O-O Bxb2) 11...Rxe5 12.O-O Qe7 13.Nc3 Qc5+ 14.Kh1 Bd7 and Black controls the dark squares and the e-file.

Instead of 7.Ne5, 7.Be2 looks better. After 7...Re8 8.O-O c5 9.Nc3 (9.b4!?) 9...Bg4 10.Kh1 White has the better chances.

(b) 5...Ne7 6.c4 Ng6 7.Be2!? (7.Bd3 O-O 8.O-O c5 9.b4 b6 10.bc bc 11.Nc3 Bg4 Hebden - Johansen, London 1982, and now 12.Bxg6!? fg 13.Ne4 is a recommendation of Bangiev's) 7...O-O 8.O-O b6 9.Nc3 c6 10.dc Nxc6 11.Nb5 Be7 12.d5 Bc5+ 13.Kh1 Nce7 14.Nfd4 a6 15.Nc3 Bd6 16.Ne4 Be5 17.Nf3 Bb8 18.b4 with a clear advantage to White in Gallagher - Boulard, Paris 1990.

5.Bc4 (76)

5.Bb5+ will be seen in games 29-31.

Although 5.Bc4 is less forcing than 5.Bb5+, it certainly doesn't contain less venom. White calmly develops his bishop, anticipating the opening of the a2-g8 diagonal after 5...Nxd5 (games 27-28). In this game, however, we shall be examining various alternatives for Black apart from 5...Bd6, which is game 26.

5...Nbd7

Black borrows a plan from the Caro-Kann Panov Attack, but he will soon realise that White has a c-pawn (instead of just doubled isolated d-pawns). Another dubious move is 5...Qe7+ which is answered by 6.Be2! leaving the black queen very badly placed. The game Gallagher-Orlov, Royan 1988, continued: 6...Nxd5 7.O-O Qf6 8.c4 Ne7 9.d4 c5? (I must admit that around about here I was finding it hard to believe my opponent's 2400 rating) 10.Nc3 cd 11.Nxd4 Ng6 12.Nd5 Qd8 13.Re1 Bc5 14.Bf3+ Be6 15.b4 Bb6 16.c5 O-O 17.Nxf4 and Black lost on time (fortunately).

6.d4 Nb6

(Black's position was already difficult, but after the coming exchanges his queenside will be too weak) 17.de Ne4 18.Qxd8 Raxd8 19.Be3 a5? 20.e6 fe 21.Bxe6+ 1-0.

For the more adventurous, 9 Ne5 is also worth looking at.

Game 26. Gallagher - Metzger
Lenk 1989

[...] 1.e4 e5

4.ed Nf6

5.Bc4 Bd6

This move is slightly illogical as White's d5-pawn should be stronger than Black's f4-pawn.

6.Qe2+

Black's main chances lie in some sort of kingside attack, therefore White offers to exchange queens.

6.Nc3 is an interesting alternative, e.g. 6...O-O 7.O-O and now Black has several ways to try and complete his development:

(a) 7...c6 8.d4 cd (8...Bg4 should be met by 9.Qd3 with ideas of Ne5; 8...b5 is dubious, e.g. 9.Bd3 cd 10.Nxb5 Nc6 11.Nxd6 Qxd6 12.Ne5! Nxe5 13.Bxf4! with advantage to White) 9.Nxd5! Be6 10.Nxf6+ Qxf6 and, according to ECO, 11.Be2 gives White the edge.

(b) 7...Nbd7 8.d4 Nb6 9.Bb3 Bg4 10.Qd3 a5 11.a3 a4 12.Ba2 Bxf3 13.Qxf3 with a good game for White, Spielmann - Fahrni, Baden 1914.

(c) 7...Bg4 8.d4 Nbd7 9.Qd3 Bh5 (9...Re8 10.Bb3 Bh5 11.Ng5 Bg6 12.Qf3 is better for White; 9...Nb6 10.Bb3 transposes to 'b') 10.Ng5 Bg6 11.Qh3 h6 12.Ne6! fe 13.de Kh8 14.ed Nxd7 (Heuer - Uusi, Tallinn 1964) 15.Bd3! with a good game for White.

6...Qe7

Black has been known to give up the right to castle in this position with 6...Kf8. After 7.d4 Bg4 8.Nc3 a6 I found the wrong plan in the game Gallagher-Cladouras, Luxembourg 1989, which continued 9.O-O? Nbd7 10.a4 h6 11.Bd2 g5 12.Rae1 Kg7 13.Qd3 Re8 14.Rxe8 Qxe8 15.Re1 and a draw was agreed.

Much better would have been 9 Bd2 followed by castling long, which would enable White to attack on the kingside.

6...Be7 was played against me in the game Gallagher - Chudinovski, Lenk 1991. Play continued 7.d4!? (7.Nc3 could well be best, but I was hoping to steer the game into familiar lines with an extra tempo) 7...Nxd5 8.Bxd5 Qxd5 9.Bxf4 c6 (9...Qd8? 10.Qe5!) 10.O-O (10.Nc3 Qe6!) 10...Be6 11.Nc3 Qa5? (It was better to play 11...Qd8 in order to defend his bishop on e7) 12.Ne4! O-O 13.Neg5 Bxg5 14.Nxg5 Re8 15.Qd3 (15.Qh5 was also strong as 15...h6 loses to 16.Be5 hg 17.Qxg5 f6 18.Rxf6!) 15...Bf5? (The last chance was 15...g6) (79).

16.Qb3 Re7 17.Bd6 Be6 (17...Rd7 loses to 18.Bb4) 18.Nxe6 Rxe6 19.Qxe6! 1-0.

Game 27. Gallagher - Balashov
Lenk 1991

(One might be forgiven for thinking that Lenk is a modern day theme tournament for this variation, but I assure you it is just a normal international open!)

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 d5

4.ed Nf6

5.Bc4 Nxd5 (80)

Black's most popular choice.

6.O-O...

It is quite possible that White can achieve an edge with the naive-looking 6.Bxd5 Qxd5 7.Nc3. Black has a number of squares for his queen:

(a) 7...Qh5 8.d4 Bd6 9.Qe2+ Kd8 10.Ne5 (10.O-O!?) and White had the advantage in Leonhardt - Szekely, Abbazia 1912.

(b) 7...Qd8 8.d4 Bd6 9.Qe2+ Qe7 10.Qxe7+ Kxe7 11.Nd5+ and White has a promising ending.

(c) 7...Qf5 8.Qe2+ Be7 9.d4 c6 (9...Be6 10.d5! Bxd5 11.Nd4 Qd7 12.Bxf4 O-O 13.O-O-O is dangerous for Black) 10.O-O (also good is 10.d5!? cd 11.Nd4) 10...Be6 11.Ne5 g5 12.g3 Rg8 (or 12...Nd7 13.gf g4 14.Ne4) 13.gf gf+ 14.Kh1 Nd7 (14...Bb4 15.Rxf4! Qh3 16.Rf3 Qh5 17.Nxf7) 15.Bxf4 O-O-O 16.Bg3 Qh3 17.Nxc6 Rxg3 18.Nxe7+ Kb8 19.Qf2 with a clear advantage for White (Glaskov).

6...Be7

The main alternative, 6...Be6, will be seen In game 28.

7.d4...

Again White should consider taking on d5: 7.Bxd5 Qxd5 8.d4 O-O 9.Bxf4 and we have transposed to the text. However, Black does have the extra possibility of 8...g5!?

7...O-O

Black can also play 7...Be6. Plans with Bb3 and c4 are not so effective when White has already played d4 as Black will be able to answer c4 with Ne3. 8.Qe2 looks best and after 8...O-O 9.Nc3 Black has several moves:

(a) 9...Nxc3 10.bc Bxc4 11.Qxc4 Bd6 12.Qb5 b6 13.Ng5! with a good game for White.

(b) 9...c6 10.Nxd5 Bxd5 11.Bd3! (This is Glaskov's improvement on the old 11.Bxd5 cd 12.Bxf4 with rough equality. I should just mention that 10...cd is not so good as Black remains with the bad bishop) 11...Bxf3 12.Rxf3 Qxd4+ 13.Kh1 Bd6 14.Bd2! (Whites bishops are lining up for a direct assault on the black king. If now 14...Qxb2 15.Bc3!) 14...Nd7 15.Bc3 Qc5 16.Rh3 g6 17.Bxg6 fg 18.Qe6+ Rf7 19.Rxh7! and White wins.

(c) 9...Nc6!. White seems to have nothing better than 10.Bxd5 Bxd5 11.Nxd5 Qxd5 12.Bxf4 Bd6! 13.Bxd6 (13.c4? Nxd4) 13...Qxd6 14.c3 with only the slightest of edges.

8.Bxd5 Qxd5

9.Bxf4 (82)

9...c5?

It is natural for Black to want to open the centre for his two bishops, but White's lead in development will prove too much to cope with.

In the game Bangiev - Aleksandrov, Minsk 1987, Black played 9...c6, but after 10.Nc3 Qh5 11.Qd2 h6 12.Rae1 Be6 13.Ne5 Re8 14.Ne4 Nd7 15.Ng3 Qh4 16.Ng6! fg 17.Rxe6 White had clearly the better chances. Maybe 9...Qd8 is Black's best, but White has the freer game.

10.Nc3 Qc4

This suspicious-looking move is virtually forced as any other queen move (apart from the horrible ... Qf5) would allow 11.d5 with a marked positional superiority.

11.Qe1!...

The main idea behind this 'creeping' queen move is to defend the knight on c3, thereby creating the threat of 12.b3. Instead, in the game Wall - Morris, British Ch 1989, White played 11.Ne5 and after 11...Qxd4+ 12.Qxd4 cd 13.Nb5 Na6 White's slight initiative never really troubled Black.

11...Bf6

Forced, as 11...cd 12.Qxe7 dc 13.Bd6 Nd7 14.Ne5! is decisive.

12.Bd6 Bxd4+

12...Rd8 13.dc is clearly better for White.

13.Kh1...

This game against Balashov was in fact the third time I'd reached this position. One of the previous occasions was against the very same Morris who drew with such consummate ease against Wall (see above). However, on coming face to face with the truth about his position, Morris decided not to put up any resistance at all and played 13...Qe6. After 14.Bxf8 Qxe1 15.Raxe1 Kxf8 16.Nxd4 cd 17.Nb5 Nc6 18.Nc7 he resigned (Gallagher -Morris, Hastings 1990/91).

13...Rd8 (83)

14.Ne4...

The first time I had this position (Gallagher - Campora, Biel 1990) I thought over an hour here. I was fascinated by the variations arising from 14.Qh4!? Rxd6. White can now play 15.Ne4 or 15.Rae1 :

(a) 15.Ne4. Black has to stay defending d8 so he has a choice of 15...Rd5, 15...Rd7 or 15...Qd5 :

(a1) 15...Rd5. This ugly move meets with a swift refutation: 16.Qe7!

(a2) 15...Rd7 is more resilient but also loses: 16.Rae1!. Black is now faced with the threat of 17.Neg5 and has 16...h6 or 16...f6.

(a21) 16...h6 17.Nxd4 and now each of the three recaptures lose: 17...cd 18.Nf6+ gf 19.Re8+ Kh7 20.Qe4+ Kg7 21.Qg4+ and mate; 17...Qxd4 18.Nf6+ gf 19.Re8+ Kh7 20.Qg3 mating; 17...Rxd4 is a little tougher but 18.Nf6+ is again good: 18...gf 19.Re8+ Kh7 20.Qxf6 Qxf1+ 21.Qxf1 Be6 22.h3 Kg7 and now 23.Qe1! is very strong.

(a22) 16...f6. If you've studied the above variations, the first move should be obvious: 17.Nxf6+! gf 18.Re8+ Kf7 19.Qh5+ (19.Ne5+ wins the queen, but White will have virtually no pieces left. 19.Rfe1 Qf1+!! is not so clear) 19...Kg7 20.Qg4+ Kh6 21.Nd2! Qf7 22.Rg8! and Black has to play 22...Qxg8 to avoid mate (22...Qg6 23.Qh4+! Qh5 24.Rxf6+ Bxf6 25.Qxf6+ Qg6 26.Qf4+ Kh5 27.g4+ Kh4 28.Nf3+ Kh3 29.Qg3 mate). After 23.Qxg8 Nc6 24.Rf4 Rg7 25.Rh4+ Kg6 26.Qe8+ White has a winning attack.

(a3) 15...Qd5!. This is a far superior defence to either of the rook moves and, in fact, I can't find any way for White to obtain the advantage.

(a31) 16.Nxd4!? (16.Qe7 Re6) 16...cd (not 16...Qxd4? 17.c3! Rh6 (17...Qe5 18.Nxd6 Qxd6 19.Rad1) 18.Qxh6 Qxe4 19.Qd6 Qh4 20.Rf4 Qg5 21.h4 winning) 17.Qe7 Rd8 18.Rxf7! Qxf7 19.Qxd8+ Qf8 20.Qd5+ Qf7 21.Qd8+ with a draw by perpetual. If Black tries to win with 20...Kh8, White gets a very strong attack, e.g. 21.Ng5 h6 22.Nf7+ Kh7 23.Qe4+ Kg8 24.Rf1 Qc5 25.Nxh6+.

(a32) 16.Nxd6 Qxd6 and White has several tries:

(a321) 17.Ng5 Qg6! and now both 18.Rxf7 (18.Nxf7 Bf6) 18...Nc6 19.c3 (19.Raf1 Be6) 19...Bf6 20.Qc4 Ne5 and 18.Rae1 Bd7 (18...f6 19.Qxh7+!) 19.Rxf7 h6 20.Ref1 Nc6 21.Rxd7 hg lead nowhere.

(a322) 17.Rae1 Bd7 18.Qd8+ Qf8 19.Qc7 Bc6 and Black has no worries.

(a323) 17.Rad1 Qf6! 18.Ng5 Bf5 19.c3 (19.g4 Qc6+) 19...h6 20.Nf3 Qxh4! (20...Be5? 21.Rd8+ Kh7 22.Rd5 Nc6 23.Qxf6 gf 24.Nxe5 fe 25.Rxf5 winning) 21.Nxh4 Bg4 22.cd Bxd1 23.Rxd1 cd 24.Rxd4 Nc6 25.Rd7 Rd8! with a winning ending.

So, 15.Ne4 only seems to offer equality (Line a31).

Returning to the position after 14...Rxd6 (84):

(b) 15.Rae1! and now Black has:

(b1) 15...h6? 16.Nd2! Qb4 (or 16...Qa6 17.Re8+ Kh7 18.Rxc8) 17.a3 Qxb2 18.Nc4 Qxc3 19.Re8+ Kh7 20.Nxd6 and White has a crushing attack.

(b2) 15...f6 16.Re8+ Kf7 17.Ne5+ Bxe5 18.Qxc4+ Kxe8 19.Qg8+ Kd7 20.Ne4 with advantage to White.

(b3) 15...Be6 16.Qe7 (a strange way to win a rook) 16...Nc6 17.Qxd6 Rd8 18.Qc7 and although Black has some compensation for the exchange, White's chances are to be preferred.

Let's return to the game after 14.Ne4.

14...f5?!

This is an understandable reaction as Black wasn't too keen on having his kingside shattered after 14...Nc6 15.c3 Bf6 but nevertheless this is his best course.
