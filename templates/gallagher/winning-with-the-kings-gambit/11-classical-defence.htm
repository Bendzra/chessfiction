11) Classical Defence

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 Bc5 (169)

Black is not interested in the complications arising from the King's Gambit Accepted and declines in classical form, immediately bringing his king's bishop to its best square.

There are now two main schemes of development at White's disposal. The first involves a quick c3 and d4, so as to build a strong pawn centre as quickly as possible. This can be rather doubled-edged as White may well fall behind in development and have his centre subjected to strong pressure. The second, quieter, method involves bringing out the minor pieces quickly, and maybe playing Na4, to remove Black's strong bishop.

In my opinion, both systems offer White reasonable chances of obtaining the advantage.

4...Bg4

With this pin, Black joins in the struggle for d4. However, as we shall see, White is able to gain the advantage. There are a number of alteratives, of which 4...f5 and 4...Nf6 can be seen in games 52 and 53 respectively. The others are examined below.

b) 4...Bb6. This prophylactic move is not without some danger for White, especially if he naively continues with 5.d4. After 5...ed 6.cd either 6...Bg4 or 6...Nf6 gives Black good play. White has to play more slowly. 5.Bd3 merits attention, but my preference is for 5.Na3. b1) 6...Ng4 7.d4 de 8.h3 Nf6 9.Nxe5 Nxe4 10.Qh5! g6 (After 10...Qf6, White has the strong reply 11.Bb5+. 4.c3 f5!? (173)

This is obviously the sharpest choice at Black's disposal and from now on the game will be balanced on the edge of a precipice.

5.fe

5.ef Qe7! is difficult for White; 5.d4 is also dubious. Keres gives 5...ed 6.Bc4 fe 7.Ng5 d5! (5.fe prevents this defence) 8.Nxe4 dxc4 9.Qh5+ Kf8 10.Qxc5+ Qe7 with a better ending for Black.

5...de

5...fe allows 6.Qa4+ Nc6 7.Qxe4 de 8.Bb5 with a good game.

6.d4 ed

7.Bc4! (174) White hurries to occupy this crucial diagonal. There is certainly no time for 7.cd, but Glaskov has suggested 7.e5!? as an alternative.

7...fe!

This is the only way for Black to justify his previous play. If it is not sound he must accept his fate. Other moves have been tried though:

a) 7...d3 8.Bg5! Nf6 9.e5 h6 10.Bh4 g5 11.ef gh 12.Ne5 and White had a very good game in Spielmann - Rubinstein, Trieberg 1921.

b) 7...Nc6 8.b4! Bb6 9.Qb3 Nh6 (Black has to defend f7. If 9...Nf6 10.b5 Na5 11.Bf7+ Kf8 12.Ba3+ ; or 9...Nge7 10.Bf7+ Kf8 11.O-O!) 10.Bg5 (10.O-O fe 11.Bg5! also gives good attacking chances) 10...Qd6 11.Nbd2. Black is going to come under heavy fire from White's raging bishops which keep his king locked in the centre, e.g. 11...dc 12.Qxc3 Bd4 13.Nxd4 Qxd4 14.Qxd4 Nxd4 15.O-O-O and the exchange of queens has hardly diminished White's attack.

c) 7...Nf6. This has been Black's most popular choice here, but White can obtain a clear advantage without any great difficulty: 8.e5 Ne4 9.cd Bb4+ (9...Bb6 10.Nc3 Nc6 11.Be3 is good for White) 10.Bd2 (10.Ke2 has also seen the light of day for some strange reason) 10...Nxd2 11.Nbxd2 and White is clearly better.

8.Ng5

I played this automatically as I knew that White was supposed to take the rook and after a few nervous moments beat off the attack. As we shall see, the game didn't go exactly according to plan. Afterwards, I felt quite downhearted (and not only because it cost a big prize). It somehow didn't feel right to be grabbing pieces and then fending off a massive attack. That's not why you play the King's Gambit! A new idea was necessary; so my attention turned to 8.Nxd4! (175).

White removes Black's potentially dangerous d-pawn and, in return for the slight material deficit, has great attacking chances. Black is especially weak on the a2-g8 diagonal as well as having difficulties on the f-file. The immediate threat is Qh5+ and Black has two ways to avoiding this:

a) 8...Bxd4 9.cd Nc6 10.Be3 (10.d5 Ne5 11.Qh5+ can also be considered but 10.Be3 is more flexible) 10...Nf6 11.O-O Black has problems completing his development, e.g.

a1) 11...Bg4 is well met by 12.Qb3. Now 12...Na5 is bad because of 13.Qb5+ c6 14.Qe5+ ; 12...Nxd4 13.Qxb7 Nc2? loses to 14.Rxf6! e.g. 14...gf 15.Qxe4+ or 14...Qd1+ 15.Rf1 or 14...Nxe3 15.Qc6+ Bd7 16.Qxe4+. Instead of 13...Nc2, Black can try 13...Rb8, but then 14.Qxa7 and Black still can't play 14...Nc2 (15.Qa4+) whilst his king remains stuck in the centre.

a2) 11...Na5 12.Rxf6! (12.Nc3!?) 12...Nxc4 13.Qh5+ (13.Rf2?! is not met by 13...Nxe3? 14.Qh5+ winning but by 13...Be6!) 13...g6 14.Rxg6 hg 15.Qxg6+!. White undoubtedly has a very dangerous attack, for example:

a21) 15...Kf8 16.Bh6+ Rxh6 17.Qxh6+ Ke7 18.Qg7+ and after 18...Ke8 or 18...Kd6 19.Nc3! brings the remaining white pieces into the attack.

a22) 15...Kd7 16.Qf7+ Qe7 17.Qxc4. White has very good play for the exchange with his queenside pieces about to enter the game. 17...Qh4 is bad because of 18.Qd5+ Ke8 19.Qe5+ followed by 20.Nc3.

a3) Maybe Black should try 11...Qe7, but after 12.Nc3 Bg4 13.Qa4 we can see that Black's king will not be very safe on the queen-side and 13...Bd7 14.Nb5! looks strong.

b) 8...Nf6 9.Bg5 (This threatens to take on f6, followed by Qh5+ and is more precise than 9.O-O, which after 9...Nc6 10.Be3 Ne5! seems good for Black) 9...Bxd4 10.cd Nc6. The drawback of White's ninth move is that he can't defend his d-pawn (Obviously we're not going to retreat the bishop) and 11.d5 Ne5 is not very attractive.

b1) 11.Bxf6 comes into consideration and is quite likely to end in a draw after 11...Qxf6 12.Qh5+ g6 13.Qd5! Nb4 (13...Nxd4 14.Rf1!) 14.Qb5+ (14.Qxe4+ Qe7 is about equal) 14...Nc6 15.Qd5.

b2) 11.Nc3!. From the above variations, it has become apparent that White has to invest further material to fuel the attack. It is very dangerous for Black to take the pawn.

b21) 11...Qxd4 12.Qxd4! Nxd4 13.O-O-O Ne6 (other moves are no better, e.g. 13...Nf5 allows 14.Nxe4 and 13...c5 14.Bxf6 gf 15.Nxe4 is very good for White) 14.Bxf6 gf 15.Nxe4 and White is close to winning.

b22) 11...Nxd4 is well met by 12.O-O with nasty threats of 13.Bxf6 and 13.Nxe4.

It is also difficult to find a good move for Black if he doesn't take on d4. 11...Na5 and 11...Bg4, for example, both lose at once to 12.Bxf6.

I think that we can conclude that 8.Nxd4 offers White good chances for the advantage, whilst eagerly awaiting some practical tests.

So White has won his rook, but Black's pieces are very active and his central pawns menacing. Nevertheless, theory considers the position as very good for White, but I think this game will change that assessment.

It is worth noting that if White plays 10.cd, Black can achieve a good game by 10...Bb4+ 11.Nc3 Rf8 12.Ne5 Ng4!

10...Nc6 Other moves seem to lead to a good game for White:

a) 10...d3 11.Bg5 Bf2+ 12.Kxf2 Qc5+ 13.Be3 Qxc4 14.h3 Be6 15.Nd2 Qd5 16.g4 Nc6 17.c4 Qd7 18.g5 Bg4 19.Qf1! and White is winning, Stoltz - Spielmann, Stockholm 1932.

b) 10...Bg4 11.Qb3 Nbd7 12.Qxb7 Rb8 13.Qc6 d3 14.b4! (Korchnoi).

11.Bg5

This looks very natural, preventing 11...Bg4, but after Black's next move White is in some trouble. Probably the best move is 11.Nf7. This was pointed out to me by my wife! As I sat huddled over the chessboard, desperately trying to repair the variation, she strolled by, baby on one arm, and remarked "Why don't you take that knight out of the corner?" And indeed, the move does have its points: firstly, and most importantly, it covers the e5-square; secondly, it prevents Black from castling; and thirdly, the knight may be able to hop out one day, leaving White a whole rook up.

On the minus side however, time is being spent which could have gone towards development, and the knight is also much more vulnerable to immediate capture on f7. Let's have a look at some concrete variations.

a) 11...d3 12.b4! (177)

This crucial move takes away the c5-square from Black. The importance of this can be seen from the following variation: 12.Bg5?! Bf2+! 13.Kxf2 Qc5+ 14.Be3 (14.Kg3) 14...Qxc4 with a dangerous attack for Black. After 12.b4! Black has:

a1) 12...Nxb4. This fails to 13.cb Bxb4+ 14.Bd2 e3 15.Qa4+ Bd7 16.Bxb4!. White is now so many pieces up that he will hardly notice returning a few to defuse the attack.

a2) 12...Bg4 13.Qb3 Bb6 14.a4!. After studying this position for some time, I came to the conclusion that White can't really develop very effectively and is better off creating a few threats of his own on the queenside. White has taken over the initiative which ensures that Black doesn't have enough play for the rook. 14...a6 15.a5 Ba7 16.b5 confirms this assessment.

b) 11...Bg4! 12.Qb3 Bb6!. Black calmly takes a time out in order to protect b7. In doing so, he has also created the annoying threat of ... Na5. If Black can win the knight on f7, he will clearly have very good play for a mere exchange. Moves such as 13.O-O and 13.Bg5 can be discarded because of ... Na5, e.g. 13.O-O d3+ 14.Kh1 Na5 15.Qa4+ Bd7 16.Bb5 c6 with advantage to Black. 13.Ng5 can also be met by 13...Na5!. This leaves White with:

b1) 13.Qb5!?. Black should now play 13...d3! (178) (13...a6 14.Qg5 Na5 15.Ne5!).

How is White to continue? One of the main worries is that Black is now threatening to play e3, as Bxd3 loses the knight on f7. 14.Be3 is an interesting attempt to blockade the pawns, but after 14...Bxe3 15.Qxb7 Rb8 16.Qxc6+ Bd7 17.Qxc7 Rxb2 Black's attack is worth at least a draw, e.g. 18.Bb3 Re2+ 19.Kd1 (19.Kf1) 19...Bg4! with a murderous attack; 14.Ng5 allows Black to castle with a good game; 14.Qg5 e3 15.Bxd3 (15.Qxg7 d2+ 16.Nxd2 ed++ 17.Kxd2 Be3+ 18.Kc2 Bf5+ 19.Kb3 Na5+ and Black is winning) 15...Kxf7 and the attack will continue; another idea is 14.Bf4 e3 15.Ne5 but 15...d2+ 16.Nxd2 ed+ 17.Kxd2 O-O-O+ is strong. So 13.Qb5 doesn't seem to solve White's problems.

b2) 13.Qa3! appears to be White's strongest, with the idea of 13...Bc5 14.Qb3 and a draw by repetition (Not 14.b4? Nxb4). Black can play for a win with 14...Qd7 but this would be risky because White's position has clearly been improved by the two little queen moves.

5.d4

White has a major alternative here; 5.fe de and now:

Nd7 16.Qxb7 O-O 17.Qxc6 and Black didn't have enough for the pawn in S.Polgar - Flear, Brussels 1987.

b) 6.Nxe5 O-O (6...Qe7 7.d4 Bd6 8.Nf3 Nxe4 9.Be2 O-O 10.O-O c5 and now Glaskov's suggestion of 11.Bd3 was tried out in the game Thinat - Garie, Corr. 1990. After 11...cd 12.Re1 f5 13.cd Nd7 14.Nc3 Ndf6 15.Bg5 Qf7 16.Bxf6 Nxc3 17.bc Qxf6 18.Qb3+ Kh8 19.Ne5 b6, White could have gained a clear advantage with 20.Qd5! e.g. 20...Rb8 21.Bc4 Bb7 22.Nf7+ Kg8 23.Qxd6 winning) 7.d4 Bd6 8.Nf3 Nxe4 9.Bd3 Re8 10.O-O h6 11.Nbd2 Nf6 12.Nc4 (Tartakower - Schlechter, St. Petersburg 1909) and now 12...Nc6 would have given Black an equal game.

5...ed

6.cd Bb4+

Also possible is 6...Bb6, maintaining pressure on d4 and offering some tactical possibilities against the white centre. Play can continue 7.Nc3 O-O 8.e5! (White has to play this advance now, as otherwise every one of Black's natural developing moves, ... Nc6, ... Bg4 and ... Re8, will bring further pressure against the centre, forcing him forward under less favourable circumstances) 8...de 9.fe Nd5 10.Bg5 Nxc3 (10...f6 11.Bc4! c6 12.ef gf 13.Bh6 Re8+ 14.Kf2 was clearly better for White in Suttles - Addison, USA Ch 1965) 11.bc (182).

11...Qe8 (This move has been awarded an exclamation mark in a number of previous books, but in fact it probably loses by force. Better is 11...Qd5 but White still has a good game, e.g. 12.Bd3 h6 13.c4! and White's centre is very strong or 12...Bg4 13.Qc2!) 12.Bd3 f6 (183).

Previous commentators have only given 13.Bf4 Bg4 with a good game for Black, but after 13.O-O! White's attack is decisive, e.g. 13...fg 14.Nxg5. White has strong threats on the a2-g8 diagonal, along the f-file and against h7. Black has several defensive tries:

a) 14...Be6 15.Bxh7+ Kh8 16.Qh5!! and Black can resign.

b) 14...h6 15.Qb3+ Kh8 16.Rxf8+ Qxf8 17.Nf7+ Kg8 18.Nxh6++ Kh8 19.Rf1 Qe8 20.Bg6! Be6 21.Qxe6! winning.

c) 14...g6 15.Qb3+ Kg7 (15...Kh8 16.Rxf8+ Qxf8 17.Rf1 Bf5 (17...Qe8 18.Rf7) 18.Bxf5 gf 19.Qe6 is crushing) 16.Rxf8 Qxf8 17.Rf1 winning.

d) 14...Rxf1+ 15.Qxf1 h6 (or 15...g6 16.Bc4+ and 17.Qf6+ wins; 15...Be6 of course allows 16.Nxe6 and 17.Bc4 ; 15...Qe7 can be met by 16.Bc4+ (16.Bxh7+!? Kh8 17.Bg6 also looks strong) 16...Kh8 17.Qf7 Qxf7 (17...Qd8 18.Qh5 wins) 18.Nxf7+ Kg8 19.Nd6+ and White is winning) 16.Bc4+ Kh8 17.Nf7+ Kh7 18.Qd3+ g6 19.Qe3! Qf8 (If 19...g5 20.Rf1 with the idea of Rf6 wins) 20.Rf1 Qg7 21.Nxh6! and White wins.

Black doesn't fare much better if he declines to take on g5, e.g. 13...fe 14.Nxe5! Qxe5 15.Rxf8+ Kxf8 16.Qf1+! Kg8 17.Re1! and White wins. Maybe Black can try 13...h6 but his kingside is in tatters, e.g. 14.Bf4 fe 15.Nxe5 Rxf4 loses to 16.Rxf4 Qxe5 17.Re4! with the idea of Re8+.
