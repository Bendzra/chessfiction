3) Kieseritzky Gambit

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 g5

4.h4 g4

5.Ne5

Before dealing with the Kieseritzky, I would just like to comment a little on the moves leading up to the Gambit, as these will not be examined elsewhere in this book.

3...g5 is, of course, one of the most important replies to the King's Gambit. Black isn't going to play half-heartedly; the soundness of the gambit is going to be tested. The two most common fourth moves for White are 4.Bc4 and 4.h4. In this book, we shall be only examining the latter. Although 4.Bc4 is undoubtedly of great interest, both historically and analytically (especially the famous Muzio or Polerio Gambit), I feel that the best White can hope for is an equal game. So, in a book titled Winning With the King's Gambit, we sometimes have to be a little ruthless.

4.h4 has the advantage of forcing 4...g4, thereby weakening Black's kingside pawns. Now, White has to consider 5.Ng5. I have to admit that I have a certain weakness for the Allgaier Gambit, and it is with a heavy heart that I inform you that my attempts to rehabilitate the line have not been rewarded. However, the Hamppe-Allgaier Gambit can be seen in chapter five of this book.

5.Ne5 is the Kieseritzky, a gambit which has been known for over four hundred years. Strangely enough, this is more than one can say for Mr Kieseritzky.

Black now has many ways to combat the gambit, which will be studied in the following games.

Game 16. Bronstein - Dubinin
Leningrad 1947

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 g5

4.h4 g4

5.Ne5 (50)

5...h5

The so-called "Long Whip" variation. The fact that it is not seen very often these days does not need much explanation. Black falls behind in development and soon becomes exposed to a strong attack.

There are many other possibilities, of which 5...d5, 5...d6, 5...Bg7 and 5...Nf6 will be seen in subsequent games. The other less common alternatives are dealt with below:

(a) 5...Qe7 6.d4 and now:

(a1) 6...f5 is best met by 7.Bc4 Nh6 (7...Nf6 8.Nc3 d6 9.Bf7+ Kd8 10.Bxf4 Nbd7 11.Bb3 Ke8 12.Nf7 Rg8 13.Ng5 Rg7 14.Ne6 Winning is Cozio's analysis from 1766) 8.Bxf4 Qb4+ 9.Nc3 d6 10.a3 Qb6 11.Nd5 Qxb2 12.Nxc7+ Kd8 13.Nb5 with a crushing attack for White in Szewczak - Donato, Golden Knights 1980.

(a2) 6...d6 7.Nxg4 f5 (after 7...Qxe4+ 8.Qe2 d5 (8...Bf5 9.Bxf4 Qxe2+ 10.Bxe2 Bxc2 11.Nc3 Bf5 12.Nd5 is also good for White) 9.Nf2 Qxe2+ 10.Bxe2 Bd6 11.Nd3 with advantage to White in Kieseritzky - Dumonch, Paris 1849) 8.Nf2 Nf6 9.Bxf4 Nxe4 (9...fe 10.d5!) 10.Qh5+ Kd8 11.Be2 Nf6 12.Qf3 Nc6 13.c3 and White is clearly on top.

(b) 5...Nc6 6.d4! Nxe5 7.de d6 8.Bxf4 Qe7 (8...Bg7 9.Nc3 de 10.Qxd8+ Kxd8 11.O-O-O+ Bd7 12.Be3 with a clear advantage to White) 9.Bb5+ c6 10.ed Qxe4+ 11.Qe2 with a better game for White (Bhend).

(c) 5...Be7 6.Bc4! Bxh4+ 7.Kf1 d5 8.Bxd5 Nh6 9.d4 Bg5 10.Nc3 c6 11.Bb3 f6 12.Nd3 Qxd4 13.Bxf4 Bxf4 14.Nxf4 Qxd1+ 15.Rxd1 with a clear advantage for White (Bilguer).

6.Bc4 Rh7

Black has fared no better with 6...Nh6 : 7.d4 Qf6 (7...d6 8.Nd3 f3 9.gf gf (9...Be7 10.Be3 Bxh4+ 11.Kd2 is good for White) 10.Qxf3 Bg4 11.Qf2 Qd7 12.Nc3 c6 13.Bg5 with an excellent position for White, Peev - Atansov, Bulgaria 1954) 8.O-O! Qxh4 9.Rxf4 Bd6 10.Nf3! Qg3 11.e5 Be7 12.Nh2 Qh4 13.Nc3 Bd8 14.Ne4 Nc6 15.Nf3 with a winning position for White in Hebden - J. Benjamin, London 1987.

7.d4

The immediate sacrifice on f7 is unnecessary.

7...Bh6

After 7...d6 8.Nxf7 Rxf7 9.Bxf7+ Kxf7 10.Bxf4 White has a monstrous attack; alternatively, 7...f3 8.gf d6 9.Nd3! (Now that Black has managed to keep the f-file closed the sacrifice on f7 is not so clear) 9...Be7 10.Be3 Bxh4+ 11.Kd2. Positions of this type arise quite frequently in the King's Gambit and are nearly always favourable for White. This one is no exception. The game Kolisch - Anderssen, Paris 1860, continued: 11...Bg5 12.f4 Bh6 13.Nc3 Bg7 14.f5 Nc6 15.Qg1 Bd7 16.Re1 and White stood clearly better.

9.Nxf7! Rxf7

10.Bxf7+ Kxf7

11.Bxf4!

This neat tactical point ensures that Black will be defenceless against the coming onslaught.

12.O-O Qxh4

This is just a waste of time.

Berlin 1946

[...] 1.e4 e5

5.Ne5 d5 (52)

The Bretano variation.

6.d4!

This secures the knight on e5 and attacks the f-pawn. As usual in the King's Gambit, when this drops, Black's kingside resembles swiss cheese.

6...Nf6

7.Bxf4 Nxe4

Very similar positions were encountered in game 4.

8.Nd2!

This is the key move, introduced into practice by Caro. Once Black's only active piece is exchanged, he will have grave difficulties in beating off the white attack.

8...Nxd2

Other moves don't help:

(a) 8...Qf6 9.g3 Bh6 10.Nxe4 de 11.Nxg4 Bxg4 12.Qxg4 Bxf4 13.Qxf4 Qxf4 14.gf with a good ending for White.

(b) 8...Bg7 9.Nxe4 de 10.Bc4 O-O 11.c3 Nd7 12.Nxf7! with a very strong attack in Lutikov - Shakh-Zade, Tashkent 1950.

9.Qxd2 Bg7

Alternatives are:

(a) 9...Be6 10.O-O-O Nd7 11.Re1 Bg7 (11...Be7 12.Qe2 Rg8 13.Nxf7! Bxf7 14.Bxc7! was rather attractive, Cleemskerk - Rhijn, Corr. 1896. Relatively best is 11...Bd6 transposing to 'b') 12.Nxg4 O-O 13.Bg5 Nf6 14.Nxf6+ Bxf6 15.Bd3 with a winning position for White in Caro - Schiffers, 1897.

(b) 9...Bd6 10.O-O-O Be6 11.Bd3 (53) and now:

(b1) 11...f6 (This wins a piece but subjects Black to a fearsome attack) 12.Rde1! fe (If 12...Bxe5, 13.Bxe5! Kd7 14.Bxf6! Qxf6 15.Rhf1 Qg7 16.Rxe6! Kxe6 17.Bf5+ Ke7 18.Qb4+ wins for White) 13.Bxe5 Kd7 14.Qh6!. This is more efficient than taking the rook, and Black now has no defence, e.g. 14...Bxe5 15.Rxe5 Re8 16.Rxe6 Rxe6 17.Bf5 ; or 14...Bf8 15.Bg7! Qe7 16.Bxf8 Rxf8 17.Rxe6! ; or 14...Re8 15.Bxd6 Kxd6 (15...cd 16.Rxe6) 16.Bf5 Qd7 17.Re3! with the simple threat of 18.Rhe1. 17...Nc6 (to defend with ... Nd8) allows 18.Qf4+.

(b2) 11...Nd7!. With this, Black should avoid getting mated: 12.Rde1 Nxe5 13.Bxe5 Bxe5 14.Rxe5 Qd7 15.Qg5! Qe7 16.Bf5 and, as Keres points out, White has a very good ending in prospect.

10.Bh6

White doesn't waste any time in removing the defender of the black squares.

10...Bxh6?

Stronger is 10...O-O and now 11.Bd3!? f6?! 12.Bxg7! Kxg7 13.O-O-O! gives White a strong attack. Also possible are 11.O-O-O and 11.Be2.

11.Qxh6 Be6

12.Bd3 Nd7

13.Nxf7! Bxf7

13...Kxf7 loses to 14.O-O+ Ke7 (If 14...Nf6 15.Rf4 is simplest) 15.Rae1 Qg8 16.Bf5 Nf8 17.Bxe6 Nxe6 18.Rf6 and wins.

Game 18. De La Villa - Fernandez
Barcelona 1990

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 g5

4.h4 g4

5.Ne5 d6 (54)

This line doesn't have a very good reputation but, along with 5...Nf6, it seems to offer Black the best chances of reaching equality.

6.Nxg4 Nf6

Black has several alternatives:

(a) 6...f5? 7.Nf2 Nf6 8.d4 fe 9.Bxf4 d5 10.g4 is better for White.

(b) 6...h5 7.Nf2 Nf6 8.d4 Bh6 9.Be2. White has a clear advantage because of the weakened black king-side. Keres gave the following line: 9...Nc6 10.Nc3 Ng4 11.Nxg4 Bxg4 (11...hg 12.Nd5 Bg5 13.g3!) 12.Bxg4 (12.Qd3 Bxe2 13.Nxe2 Qf6 14.Bd2 with a good game for White - Glaskov) 12...hg 13.Nd5 f3 14.g3.

(c) 6...Be7 7.d3! (This new idea seems to give White clearly the better game. Previous authors have only considered 7.d4, after which Black can achieve equality, e.g. 7...Bxh4+ 8.Nf2 Qg5 9.Qf3 Nc6! 10.Qxf4! Bxf2+ 11.Kxf2 Qxf4+ 12.Bxf4 Nxd4 13.Nc3! Be6! 14.Nb5 Nxb5 15.Bxb5+ Bd7. White has enough for the pawn, but no more. A correspondence game, Ressegnier - Letz, 1912, continued 16.Be2 Nf6 17.e5 Ne4+ 18.Ke3 d5 19.c4 c6 20.Rad1 Be6 21.Bf3 f5! 22.ef Nxf6 23.Be5! and a draw was soon agreed) 7...Bxh4+ 8.Nf2 Qg5 9.Qd2! (Now the queen is better off here, as on f3 there would be some risk of getting trapped) 9...Bg3 (The main advantage of 7.d3! is that Black is unable to counter-attack against the centre. Instead, he has to try to hang on to his f-pawn) 10.Nc3 Nf6 (if 10...Nc6 11.Nd5 is strong) 11.Ne2! (It turns out that the f-pawn cannot be held and 11...Ng4 fails to 12.Nxg3) 11...Bxf2+ 12.Kxf2 Ng4+ 13.Kg1 Ne3 14.Nxf4 Nxf1 15.Kxf1 and Black is positionally busted.

7.Nxf6+

After 7.Nf2 Rg8 8.d4 Bh6 9.Nc3 Nc6! White can only achieve a roughly equal game with 10.Nd3 Bg4 11.Be2 Bxe2 12.Nxe2 Qe7 13.Bxf4 Bxf4 14.Ndxf4 Qxe4 15.Qd2 O-O-O 16.O-O-O Nd5.

7...Qxf6

8.Nc3 Be6! (55)

This is clearly stronger than the old 8...c6. As Korchnoi points out, White can then get a strong attacking position by 9.Be2 Rg8 10.Bf3 Bh6 11.d4 Na6 12.e5! de 13.Ne4 Qe7 14.O-O.

9.Qe2!?

With this move, White threatens 10.Qb5+ and prevents 9...Nc6 (10.Nd5). A few months earlier the same two players had met in Salamanca where De La Villa chose instead 9.Qf3 but after 9...Rg8 10.Qf2 Nc6! 11.Bb5 O-O-O! 12.Bxc6 bc 13.d3 Bh6, Black had a good position (14.Qxa7 f3 15.gf Bxc1 16.Rxc1 Qxf3 17.Rf1 Qh3 with a clear advantage to Black).

9...Nd7

10.b3

The only sensible way for White to develop.

10...Rg8

11.Bb2 Bg4!

In this way, Black makes it hard work for White to castle. After 11...O-O-O 12.O-O-O! Bg4 (of course if Black doesn't accept the offer, he is left with a terrible position) 13.Qf2 Bxd1 14.Qxa7 Bg4 15.Ba6! Nc5 16.Bb5 Nd7 (16...c6 is a better defence) 17.Nd5 Qe6 18.Bc6! wins.

12.Qf2 d5!

Black must play actively to compensate for his bad structure.

13.Be2! Bc5

14.Qf1

This looks more uncomfortable than it actually is.

14...Bxe2

15.Qxe2 O-O-O

16.O-O-O de

17.Nxe4 Qg6

18.Qc4!

18.Nxc5 Nxc5 19.Qf2 offers Black a chance to solve his problems tactically with 19...Qc6!

18...Nb6

After this, White's advantage is obvious. 18...Be7 19.Ng5 also gives White the better game. 18...Bb6 is recommended by the two players, but 19.Ng5 still seems to offer White the better chances.

19.Qxc5 Qxe4

20.Rhe1 Qd5

20...Qxg2 is well met by 21.Qf5+ and 22.Qxf4.

21.Qf2 Rg4

22.Qf3!?

White opts for the favourable ending.

22...Qxf3

23.gf Rg3

24.Bf6 Rf8

The rook ending after 24...Rdg8 25.Re7 Nd5 26.Rxf7 Nxf6 27.Rxf6 Rxf3 28.Rf7 is a very difficult one for Black.

25.Bg5?!

25.c4 would have been an improvement, trying to keep the knight locked out of the game: 25...Nd7 26.Bb2 Rxf3 doesn't help because of 27.Rf1.

25...Nd5

26.c4?

And this throws away the remaining advantage. After 26.Re5! c6 27.Rf5, Black would still have had a difficult task ahead of him.

26...Nb4

27.Kb1 Nd3

Game 19. R. Byrne - Keres
USSR - USA 1955

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 g5

4.h4 g4

5.Ne5 Bg7 (56)

This, the Paulsen variation, was formerly considered one of Black's best defences to the King's Gambit, but recent analysis has cast doubt upon its viability.

6.d4

Schlechter's move, 6.Nxg4, also deserves consideration: 6...d5 7.d4 (not 7.ed?? Qe7+ and 7.Nf2 de 8.Nxe4 Qe7 9.Qe2 Nc6 10.c3 Nh6 11.Ng5 Ne5 12.d4 Bg4 with advantage to Black) 7...de 8.Bxf4 Qxd4 9.Qxd4 Bxd4 10.c3 and although White has a pawn less, the ending is by no means worse for him, e.g.

(a) 10...Bg7 11.Be2 (only 11.Nf2? was considered in an analysis by Panov) and 11...f5 can be met by 12.Ne3 with a good game for White. Black probably has to play 11...Bxg4 12.Bxg4 Nf6, but after 13.Bh5 (13.Bc8 Nd7 is unclear) White has good play for the pawn.

(b) 10...Bxg4 11.cd Nc6 12.Bb5! O-O-O 13.Bxc6 bc 14.O-O f6 15.Nc3! (Keres) 15...Rxd4 16.Rae1. White has a considerable positional advantage, and soon he will be only one doubled (and isolated) pawn down.

6...Nf6 (57)

6...d6 is the alternative: 7.Nxg4 Bxg4 8.Qxg4 Bxd4 and now 9.Nc3! gives White a good game. If Black takes on c3, then the two bishops will far outweigh the ropey pawn structure and after 9...Nf6 10.Qf5!? Qe7 11.Bd3 Rg8 12.Bxf4 Rxg2 13.O-O-O, White had clearly the better game in Gallagher - Sanchi, Paris 1989.

7.Bc4

7.Nc3 is the strongest and most natural move in this position. (I should just mention that I have altered the move order of Byrne - Keres, so that we could use it to examine the variations after 5...Bg7. The actual move order was 1.e4 e5 2.f4 ef 3.Nf3 g5 4.h4 g4 5.Ne5 Nf6 6.Bc4 d5 7.ed Bg7 8.d4).

After 7.Nc3 d6 (7...O-O 8.h5 is awkward and 7...d5 8.Bxf4 Nxe4 9.Nxe4 de 10.Bc4 is very good for White) 8.Nd3 O-O (Euwe gave the following variation as good for White: 8...Nh5 9.Nxf4 Ng3 10.Rh2 O-O 11.Be2! Nxe2 12.Ncxe2 f5 13.c3 fe 14.Qb3+ Kh8 15.Be3) 9.Nxf4! (This is much better than 9.Bxf4 which runs into trouble against 9...Nc6 10.Nf2 Nh5 11.Bg5 f6 12.Be3 Ng3 13.Rh2 f5) 9...Nxe4 10.Nxe4 Re8 11.Kf2! Rxe4 12.c3 Qf6 (After any other move 13.Bd3 gives White the advantage) 13.g3 Bh6 14.Bd3 Bxf4 15.Bxf4 Rxf4+ 16.gf Qxf4+, Blacks attack looks menacing but Rubinstein demonstrated the correct way for White to continue: 17.Ke2! (17.Kg2 b5!) 17...g3 18.Qd2! Bg4+ 19.Ke1 g2 20.Qxg2 Nc6 21.Be2 Re8 22.Rf1! and wins.

7...d5

8.ed Nh5

8...O-O has been recommended, but after 9.O-O White appears to have the better game, e.g.

(a) 9...c5 10.dxc5 Nh5 11.Nxg4 Qxh4 12.Be2! with advantage to White.

(b) 9...Nxd5 10.Bxd5 Qxd5 11.Nc3 (For some reason, only 11.Bxf4 c5! had been considered here) 11...Qd8 12.Bxf4 Qxh4 13.Nd5 with a very good game for White.

(c) 9...Nh5 10.Nxg4! Qxh4 11.Nh2 Nd7!? (11...Qf6 12.Qxh5 Qxd4+ 13.Kh1 Qxc4 14.Bxf4 Bxb2 15.Bh6 with advantage to White) 12.c3 Ndf6 13.Qe1!? (White could also consider keeping the queens on and play something like 13.Nd2 or 13.Qf3. Now a very messy ending is reached) 13...Qxe1 14.Rxe1 Bf5 15.Re5 Rfe8 16.Nf3 Bg6 17.Nd2 Rad8 18.Rxe8+ Nxe8 19.Ne5 Nef6 20.Nb3! with a slight advantage to White in Gallagher - Macles, Dijon 1987.

9.O-O

9.Nc3 is rather dubious here: 9...O-O 10.Ne2 c5! 11.Nxf4 (11.Bxf4 loses a piece to 11...b5, whilst 11.c3 cd 12.cd Nd7 13.Nxd7 Bxd7 was better for Black in Steinitz - Zukertort, Vienna 1882) 11...Ng3 12.Ne6 and, as Glaskov points out, Black can gain a clear advantage by 12...fe 13.de Bxe6! 14.Bxe6+ Kh8 15.Qxg4 Qxd4 16.Qxd4 (16.Qxg3 Bxe5 17.Qe3 Qd6) 16...cd 17.Nf7+ Rxf7 18.Bxf7 Nxh1.

9...Qxh4

10.Qe1!

It is time to exchange queens.

10...Qxe1

11.Rxe1 O-O

12.Nc3

12.c3 is not sufficiently active.

12...Nd7

12...c5? 13.Nb5 Nd7 14.Nc7 Rb8 15.d6! cd 16.Nxf7!

13.Nb5 (58)

13...c6

14.Nc7

More critical is 14.dc Nxe5 15.de (15.cb Bxb7 16.de Rac8 17.Nd6 Rc5! could prove dangerous for White) 15...bc 16.Nc7 Rb8 17.e6 Rb4!. The position is positively alive with tactical possibilities, e.g.

(a) 18.e7? Bd4+ 19.Kh2 (19.Kh1 Rxc4 20.efQ+ Kxf8 21.Re8+ Kg7 22.Rxc8 Ng3+ 23.Kh2 Nf1+ 24.Kh1 Rc5! (Black may have a rook less, but it feels like he's a few pieces up) 25.g3 Rh5+ 26.Kg2 f3+ 27.Kxf1 Rh1 mate) 19...g3+ 20.Kh1 Rxc4 21.efQ+ Kxf8 22.Re8+ Kg7 23.Rxc8 f3 24.c3 (24.gf Rxc2) 24...f2 25.Bg5 Bf6 26.Ne8+ Kg6 27.Nxf6 Kxg5 and wins.

(b) 18.ef+ Kh8 19.Be6 (19.Ne6 Rxc4 20.Nxf8 Bxf8 21.Re8 Kg7 with advantage to Black) 19...Bxe6 20.Nxe6 Rxf7 and White is in trouble.

White does best to maintain the tension and safeguard his bishop with 18.Bb3! leaving a very unclear position on the board.

14...cd!

The exchange sacrifice is necessary as 14...Rb8 15.d6! is strong.

15.Nxa8?

This is very optimistic. Safer was 15.Bxd5! Rb8 16.c3 Nxe5 17.de Rd8 (17...Kh8 18.Bd2 Bf5 19.c4 is good for White) 18.e6! fe 19.Bxe6+ Bxe6 20.Nxe6 Re8 21.Bxf4 with about equal chances (Glaskov).

15...dc

16.Bd2 Nxe5

17.de Bf5

18.Nc7 Bxc2?!

Keres criticises this move and gives instead 18...Rd8 (taking d5 away from the knight) 19.Bc3 Bxc2 and with three pawns for the exchange, plus active pieces, Black has a clear advantage.

19.Rac1 Bd3

20.Nd5 b5

21.Bxf4 Rd8

Keres didn't like White's counterplay after 21...Nxf4 22.Nxf4 Bf5 23.Nh5.

22.Ne7+ Kf8

23.Bg5 Re8

24.Nc6 Ng3

And here Keres thought 24...a5 stronger, in order to deprive the knight of the b4-square.

25.Rcd1 Re6

26.Nxa7

It was better for White to have played 26.Nb4 and after 26...Ne2+ 27.Kf1!, although Black does retain winning chances after 27...Ng3+ 28.Kg1 Bg6 29.Nc6 Nf5 (Keres).

26...Bxe5

Black has a wonderfully centralised position and White's king is feeling the draught.

27.Nxb5 Ne2+

28.Rxe2!

White bails out, hoping for some drawing chances in the ending.

29.Rd8+ Re8

Game 20. Gallagher - Hresc
Royan 1989

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 g5

4.h4 g4

5.Ne5 Nf6 (60)

The Berlin Defence is the most popular answer to the Kieseritzky.

6.Bc4

The main alternative 6.d4 will be seen in games 22 and 23. White's other possibility, 6.Nxg4, leads to very sharp play where Black seems to be at least able to hold his own: 6...Nxe4 7.d3 Ng3 8.Bxf4 Nxh1 9.Qe2+ (9.Bg5 was refuted in the game Hebden - Stean, Marbella 1982: 9...Be7 10.Qe2 h5! 11.Qe5 f6! 12.Bxf6 d6 13.Qe4 Bxg4 14.Bxh8 Kd7 15.Bd4 Bxh4+ and White resigned) 9...Qe7 10.Nf6+ Kd8 11.Bxc7+ Kxc7 12.Nd5+ Kd8 13.Nxe7 Bxe7 14.Qg4 d6 15.Qf4 Rg8 and Black's pieces should prove stronger than the queen (Hebden - P. Littlewood, Hastings 1982/83).

6...d5

Philidor recommended 6...Qe7 but this is not good:

7.d4 d6 8.Bxf7+ Kd8 9.Bxf4 de 10.de+ Bd7 11.Bb3 Qb4+ 12.Nd2 Nxe4 13.c3 Qxc3 14.Bg5+ with a clear advantage to White (ECO).

7.ed Bd6

7...Bg7 transposes to Byrne - Keres (game 19).

8.d4

8.O-O is the incredible Rice Gambit, which was once so popular that whole tournaments were devoted to it. Basically, White sacrifices a piece and castles into a raging attack, but according to theory, he miraculously holds the balance. Nevertheless, I still advise you to steer well clear of it.

8...Nh5 (61)

8...O-O! is game 21.
