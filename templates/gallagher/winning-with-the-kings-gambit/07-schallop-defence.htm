7) Schallop Defence

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 Nf6

Black intends to hold on to his f-pawn, defending it with his knight from h5. In this way he avoids weakening his kingside pawns, but h5 is hardly the sort of outpost that knights dream about. Apart from having very little mobility, its exposed position offers White some tactical possibilities.

Game 37. Glaskov - Shapoval
Corr. 1985/86

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 Nf6

4.e5

Obviously this is the critical continuation. 4.Nc3 is met by 4...d5. If now 5.ed, play has transposed into a variation of the Modern Defence, where White has little hope of gaining the advantage, whilst 5.e5 is less favourable than the text.

4...Nh5

Averbakh's move 4...Ne4 is seen from time to time, a recent example being Illescas - Motwani. Thessaloniki 1988: 5.d3 Ng5 6.Bxf4 Ne6 7.Bg3 d6 8.d4 de 9.d5 (9.Bxe5) 9...Nf4 10.Nxe5 Nxd5 11.Bc4 Be6 12.Qe2 Be7 13.Nc3 with an unclear position.

White does better to follow the recommendation of Keres and play 5.d4. After 5...d5 6.Bxf4 c5 7.Nbd2 Nc6 8.Bd3 he has the better game.

5.Be2!?

White prepares to castle quickly which will enable him to move his knight from f3, thereby causing embarrassment to the knight on h5. 5.d4 is game 38.

5...g5

There are a number of alternatives:

(a) 5...d5 6.O-O g5 7.Nxg5!

(b) 5...g6 6.d4 Bg7 7.O-O d6 8.Nc3 O-O and, as Chigorin pointed out, 9.ed! cd (9...Qxd6 10.Ne5) 10.Ne1.

(c) 5...d6 6.O-O (In the game Camarra - Sayed, Lucerne 1982, White decided to throw the kitchen sink at his opponent and won in spectacular fashion: 6.d4?! de 7.O-O ed (7...e4 8.Ne5 f3 9.gf ef 10.Bc4 Be6 11.Bxe6 fe 12.Be3 gives White good compensation) 8.Bc4 Be6 9.Bxe6 fe 10.Qe2 Nc6? (Here Black could have cast serious doubts upon the correctness of White's idea with 10...Qd5!) 11.Qxe6+ Qe7 12.Qh3 Qc5 (12...Qf7 13.Re1+ Be7 14.Ng5 Qg6 15.Ne6, with the threat of Qxh5, is good for White) 13.b4! Nxb4 (13...Qd5 14.c4!) 14.Re1+ Be7 15.Re5 Qxc2 16.Qxh5+ g6 17.Rxe7+ Kxe7 18.Qe5+ Kd7 19.Qxd4+ Ke7 20.Qxb4+ c5 21.Qxb7+ Ke6 22.Ng5+ Ke5 23.Bb2+ Kf5 24.Qd5+ Kg4 25.h3+ 1-0) 6...de 7.Nxe5 and Black's two main choices don't seem to give him equality.

(c1) 7...Qd4+ 8.Kh1 Nf6 9.Nd3 and White wins back the f-pawn with a good game as 9...g5 10.b3! is strong.

(c2) 7...Bc5+ 8.Kh1 Nf6 (The attempt to play for a snap mate fails, e.g. 8...Ng3+ 9.hg fg 10.Bb5+ (10.Nf3) 10...c6 11.Qh5 g6 12.Nxc6 Nxc6 13.Qe5+ Qe7 14.Qxh8+ Kd7 15.Qxh7) 9.c3 Bd6 (9...Nbd7 10.Nxd7 Bxd7 11.d4 Bd6 12.Bxf4 Bxf4 13.Rxf4 O-O 14.Bd3 is clearly in White's favour. In the game Verdikhanov -Ilyin, Corr. 1982, Black tried 9...g5 but after 10.d4 Bd6 11.Nd2! Nbd7 12.Ndf3 h6 13.Bc4 Qe7 14.Qb3 White stood clearly better) 10.d4 Bxe5 11.de Qxd1 12.Bxd1 (12.Rxd1 Ne4 is unclear) 12...Nd5 13.Bf3! Ne3 14.Bxe3 fe 15.Na3! and White's chances are to be preferred. The e-pawn is not long for this world and the bishop exerts strong pressure on the long diagonal.

6.c4

Again we have the 'Queen's Gambit' variation of the King's Gambit. White can also consider 6.Be2 when after 6...g5 7.O-O Rg8 play has transposed to the seventh move note of Glaskov - Shapoval. After 6.Be2, the game S. Bucker - S. Nikolic, Biel 1984, took an independent course: 6...Bg4 7.O-O g6 8.Re1 Be7 9.c3 c5 10.Qb3 b6 with a complicated struggle ahead. Instead of the strange 8.Re1, 8.Ne1 seems more to the point, and after the exchange of bishops White can play Nd3 and Nxf4.

6...g5 (126)

Black has a large number of alternatives:

(a) 6...Bb4+ 7.Nc3 Nc6 8.Be2 O-O 9.O-O. ECO considers White to be slightly better, e.g. 9...Bxc3 10.bc Bg4 11.Ne1 Bxe2 12.Qxe2 g6 13.Bxf4 with advantage to White in Muchnik - Dzhalalov, Moscow 1952.

(b) 6...Nc6 7.cd Qxd5 8.Nc3 Bb4 9.Be2 (9.Kf2!?) 9...Qa5 (9...g6 10.O-O Bxc3 11.bc Bg4 12.h3 Bf5 13.Nh4 was slightly better for White in Gragev - Chernakov, Corr. 1972) 10.Bd2 Bg4 11.a3 Bxc3 12.bc O-O 13.Ng5! (White starts a lethal attack) 13...Bxe2 14.Qxe2 g6 15.g4! fg 16.hg Nxg3 17.Nxh7! and White won, Glaskov - Malyuzhinets, Moscow 1950.

(c) 6...c6. White can now exert strong pressure with Nc3 and Qb3.

(d) 6...Be7 7.Be2 Bh4+ 8.Kf1 Bg4 (8...Be7 9.cd Qxd5 10.Nc3 Qd8 11.d5 and the threat of Nd4 gives White the advantage, e.g. 11...Bg4 12.Nd4 Bxe2 13.Qxe2 g6 14.Qe4) 9.cd O-O 10.Nc3 and White's powerful centre is more important than his misplaced king.

7.g4!

This incredible looking move guarantees White a clear advantage.

7...Ng7
