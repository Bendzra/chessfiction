1) Fischer Defence

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 d6 (2)

Over the last few years this variation has developed into the most popular way of accepting the gambit. Personally, I feel quite happy about this as Black seems to be struggling in most of the lines. I only hope that this chapter won't frighten off any potential opponents!

After his famous defeat against Spassky at Mar del Plata 1960, the great Bobby decided to refute the King's Gambit. In the summer of 1961, the American Chess Quarterly published his analysis. "A high-class waiting move", was how he described 3...d6.

The main point of the variation is demonstrated after the moves 4.d4 g5 5.h4 g4. White does not have the possibility of 6.Ne5, as in the Kieseritzky Gambit. Therefore he has to go 'all in' with 6.Ng5 (not to be recommended, I'm afraid) or return to base with the rather sad-looking 6.Ng1 (3), when quite a comical position has arisen on the board - after six moves neither side has managed to get a piece off the back rank!

At the moment, Black is a pawn up but f4 is seriously weak and, if White manages to restore material equality without any side-effects, Black's punctured kingside will not allow him an easy game. Let's have a look at some games to see how Black attempts to solve his problems.

Game 1. Gallagher - Conquest
British Championship, Blackpool 1988

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 d6

4.d4

White does have other possibilities, but 4.d4 is clearly the most critical. However, the plan of Bc4 followed by d3 is also interesting and can be seen later in games 9 and 10.

4...g5

Other moves have been tried from time to time without much success, for example: 4...Nf6 5.Nc3 Nh5 6.Be2 (maybe 6.Qd3, Bd2 and 0-0-0) 6...Bg4 7.O-O c6 (7...g6 8.Nd5) 8.Ne1! Bxe2 9.Qxe2 g6 10.Nd3 leaves White with a clear advantage.

5.h4

5.Bc4 is illogical: if White wants to play this he should play it on the fourth move, as then Black is forced play ... h6. The Dutch player Bosboom has experimented with 5.Nc3, his idea being 5...g4 6.Bxf4 and on with the game. His game with Ermenkov, Amsterdam 1985, continued instead: 5...Bg7 6.h4 g4 (6...h6 should be considered) 7.Ng1 Nc6 8.Bb5 Bd7 9.Bxc6 bc 10.Bxf4 Ne7 11.h5 with advantage to White. Further tests awaited!

5...g4

6.Ng1

As already mentioned, 6.Ng5 runs into trouble. Not because of 6...h6 though, as after 7.Nxf7 White has a decent version of the Allgaier Gambit (if there is such a thing), but 6...f6! and after 7.Nh3 gh 8.Qh5+ Kd7 9.Bxf4 Qe8! 10.Qf3 Kd8 White doesn't have enough for the piece (Fischer).

6...f3!? (4)

Black decides to give back his pawn in order to disrupt the white kingside. The other possibilities: 6...Nf6, 6...Bh6 and 6...Qf6, will be considered in games 4 to 8.

7.gf

7.Bg5 can be seen in games 2 and 3, whilst 7.Be3 occurred in the game Popovic - Spassky, New York 1986. However, after 7...Be7 8.Bf2 Nf6 9.gf Rg8 10.Bg2 Nc6 11.Nc3 gf 12.Bxf3 Ng4, Black had the initiative.

8.Be3

This sensible developing move minimises the disruptive value of the check on h4. During the game I was sorely tempted to play 8.h5 but couldn't quite summon up the courage to do so. Only Mark Hebden has, in his game with R. Alvares, Costa del Sol 1987, which continued: 8...Bh4+ 9.Ke2 (Not such a pretty place for the king, but we still have our h-pawn) 9...gf+ 10.Nxf3 Bg4 11.Kd3 Nc6 12.c3 Ne5+?! (I have a vague memory of looking at 12...f5 with Mark and after 13.Bh3 fe+ 14.Kxe4 Nf6+ 15.Kd3 coming to the conclusion that Black's king was in more trouble than White's. Maybe we were drunk!) 13.de de+ 14.Ke3! Qxd1 15.Bb5+ c6 16.Rxd1 cb 17.Rd5 and White had the better endgame.

Game 6. Gallagher - Hübner
Biel 1991

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 d6

4.d4 g5

7.Nc3 Be6 (20)

Black finds a novel way of protecting his d5-square (in order to play ... Qf6 if necessary). The text has one important advantage over 7...c6 : the d6-square is not weakened, which means that the dangerous attacking plan of e5 and Ne4 will lose a lot of its potency.

White can, of course, gain time by attacking the bishop with d5 but this is extremely anti-positional and should only be played if there is a very strong follow up.

8.Qd3

I decided to play a plan with Qd3, Bd2 and 0-0-0 as Black can no longer harass the queen with ... b6 and ... Ba6. However, if I get this position again I think I would prefer 8.Nge2, immediately fighting for some space on the kingside. Play could continue: 8...Qf6 9.g3 (9.Qd2!? f3 10.Nf4 Bxf4 (or 10...fg 11.Bxg2 Bxf4 12.Qxf4 Qxd4 13.e5! with good play for White) 11.Qxf4 Qxd4 12.gf with play for the pawn) 9...fg (9...f3 10.Nf4 is pleasant for White) 10.Nxg3 Bxc1 11.Rxc1 and, in spite of his material deficit, White has the better chances (The position is very similar to the note to Black's ninth move in Gallagher - Jackson).

8...a6!
