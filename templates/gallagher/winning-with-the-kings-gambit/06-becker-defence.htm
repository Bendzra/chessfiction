6) Becker Defence

Game 35. Gallagher - Spyrsl
Fribourg 1987

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 h6 (116)

The Becker Defence signals Black's intention to hold on to the gambit pawn, but without the weaknesses inherent in the Kieseritzky. By playing ... h6 first, Black won't be forced into playing ... g4 if White decides to undermine the pawn chain with h4. But, as we shall see, White has other ways to develop his initiative.

4.d4

4.b3, in order to dissuade Black from playing ... g5, is interesting, and can be seen in game 36; whilst 4.Bc4 g5 leads to the Classical which is not part of our repertoire (5.Ne5 Rh7! doesn't disturb Black).

4...g5

5.Nc3

5.h4 Bg7 6.g3 is another, but less promising, method.

5...d6

5...Bg7 caught me napping a little in the game Gallagher - Nunn, Islington 1990. I just continued in the normal fashion: 6.g3 fg 7.h4 g4 8.Ng1 but was then very surprised by 8...d5!. Previously this idea hadn't been considered at all, theory normally stating that whether Black plays 5...d6 or 5...Bg7 doesn't make any difference as play simply transposes. After 8...d5!, 9.Nxd5 is bad because of 9...c6, so play continued: 9.ed Ne7 10.Nge2 c6! 11.Bg2 cd 12.Bf4 Nc6 13.Qd2 Qa5 with advantage to Black.

However, all is not doom and gloom. White has a very interesting possibility on his seventh move: 7.hg (117), instead of 7.h4.

Now 7...d6 looks normal for Black. 8.Bc4 (White can even consider the speculative 8.Nxg5!?, e.g. 8...hg 9.Rxh8 Bxh8 10.Qh5 Bxd4 11.Bxg5! Qd7 (11...Bf6 12.Qh7 Bxg5 13.Qxg8+ Kd7 14.Qxf7+ Qe7 15.Bh3+) 12.Bc4 followed by 13.O-O-O and Rf1 with a dangerous attack) 8...Nf6 (Black has to watch his step. For example, 8...Nc6? 9.Nxg5 hg 10.Rxh8 Bxh8 11.Qh5; or 8...Ne7 9.Nxg5! hg 10.Rxh8+ Bxh8 11.Qh5 Ng6 12.Bxg5 Qd7 (12...Bf6 13.Bxf7+ Kxf7 14.Qh7+) 13.O-O-O with a crushing attack) 9.Qd3 Nc6 10.Be3 Bg4 11.Rf1. This is a little inconvenient, but White maintains a firm grip on the centre. Black now has to decide which way to castle.

(a) 11...Qd7 12.O-O-O O-O-O 13.Bb5! causes Black serious problems.

(b) 11...O-O 12.O-O-O Qd7 (12...Qe7 13.Rde1 (13.Nxg5!?) 13...Rae8 14.Nxg5 hg 15.Bxg5 Be6 16.Bb3 (or 16.Nd5!?) with excellent compensation for the piece) 13.Rf2 and White plans to double rooks on the f-file. Black has difficulties in countering this plan, e.g. 13...Rae8 14.a3! and now 14...Bxf3 15.Rxf3 Qg4 16.Rdf1 Nxe4 17.Rxf7 is good for White.

6.g3!

White has to take action against the pawn chain before Black has time to consolidate.

6...fg

6...g4 doesn't fit in with the Becker system (3...h6 was played to hold the kingside): 7.Ng1 f3 8.h3 is good for White, whilst 7.Bxf4 is also highly tempting, e.g. 7...gf 8.Qxf3 Nc6 9.O-O-O with a strong attack.

More critical is 6...Bg7 7.gf g4 8.Ng1 Qh4+ 9.Ke2 g3 10.Nf3 Bg4, but after 11.Be3 White has the better game, e.g. 11...gh 12.Kd2 Qg3 13.Be2 Nc6 14.Nxh2 or 11...Nc6 12.Kd2! Qh5 13.Be2 g2 14.Rg1 Qh3 15.d5 Ne7 16.Nd4 (Bhend).

7.h4

This is the normal way of continuing the attack but, in view of the analysis above, 7.hg can also be considered.

7...g4

7...Bg4 is not good because of 8.hg Nc6 (8...Bg7 9.gh Bxh6 10.Bg2!) 9.Nd5! Bxf3 10.Qxf3 Nxd4 11.Qc3 Bg7 12.Nxc7+ Kf8 13.Nxa8 Qxa8 14.gh with a clear advantage for White (Glaskov).

If 7...gh, 8.Nxh4 and White will soon pick up the g-pawn and remain with excellent compensation for the other one sacrificed.

8.Ng1 Bg7?!

8...Be7 9.Bg2 (9.h5!?) 9...Bxh4 10.Bf4 Qf6 11.Qd2 and Korchnoi considers that Black's three extra pawns are no match for White's strong centre and lead in development.

8...g2 9.Bxg2 Be7 10.h5 Bh4+ 11.Ke2 is also pleasant for White, e.g. 11...Bg5 12.Bxg5 Qxg5 13.Qd2 Qxd2+ (13...Nf6 14.Rf1 Nxh5 15.Rxh5 Qxh5 16.Nd5 is good for White) 14.Kxd2 Ne7 15.Nge2 and White has the better ending in spite of Black's extra pawn.

9.Be3

Black has no counterplay and is suffering terribly from a lack of space.

9...Nc6

10.Nge2 Qf6?

Black must have overlooked that after 11.Nd5 Qf3 the bishop on e3 would be defended by the knight. 10...Bf6 looks best, but after 11.Bg2 (11.h5!?) 11...Bxh4 12.Qd2 with 0-0-0 to follow is similar to 8...Be7 in the note to Black's eight move.

11.Nd5 Qd8

12.Nxg3 Nf6

13.Nf4

White avoids exchanges and has the option of playing Nh5.

13...Qe7

14.Qd3 Bd7

15.O-O-O h5

16.Bg2

White is in no rush and calmly continues developing.

16...O-O-O

17.Qa3!? a6

18.Rhe1 Rde8

19.Kb1 Bh6

20.Qc3! Kb8 (118)

21.e5!

The preparation is over - now it is time for the attack; White's bishop on g2 comes forcefully into the game.

21...Nh7

Unpleasant though it may be, Black should have taken on e5. After 21...de 22.de Qxe5 (22...Nxe5 23.Bc5 Qd8 24.Qb4! wins for White) 23.Ba7+ Kxa7 24.Rxe5 Nxe5 25.Qxc7 White has a clear advantage but Black can soldier on.

22.Bxc6

22.Nd5 Qf8 23.Nxc7 is also good.

22...Bxf4?

The final error. To avoid immediate capitulation Black had to play 22...bc (22...Bxc6 23.Nf5 wins quickly).

23.Bxf4 Bxc6

24.ed Qxh4

25.d5 Bxd5

26.dc+ Kc8

27.Nf5 1-0

Game 36. Bangiev - Karolyi
Kecskemet 1987

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 h6

4.b3 (119)

Over the past few years, this has become an increasingly popular way of dealing with the Becker Defence. White prevents g5, or at least makes it unattractive, and thereby leaves 3...h6 looking like a waste of time.

White's plan is to develop his queenside pieces as quickly as possible, castle long, and then play g3 to open lines on the kingside. He should then receive the sort of compensation one normally associates with the Benko Gambit, with the added bonus of having the black king to aim at. Nevertheless, a quick mate is unlikely and it is the long lasting pressure which should make Black suffer.

4...d6

After this, White has no difficulty in carrying out his plan.

4...Nf6 is interesting, when White has normally played 5.Qe2 (5.e5 could well be stronger when we arrive in a strange sort of Schallop Defence. Admittedly, 3...h6 is marginally more useful than 4.b3, but then again I'm sure that God would never choose the Schallop against the King's Gambit) 5...d5!. By the opening of the centre, White's development is made to look rather clumsy. Hebden - Pein, London 1987 continued: 6.ed+ Be7 7.Bb2 (7.c4 is too slow) 7...O-O 8.Nc3 Re8 9.O-O-O Nxd5 10.Qe5 Nxc3 11.dc Bd6 12.Qh5 Nd7?! (This is where Black starts going downhill. The knight is fated never to arrive at its destination, leaving behind it an undeveloped queenside. Better was 12...Nc6, after which White is hard pressed to justify his pawn minus, e.g. 13.Bc4 Qf6 14.Rhe1 Bd7; or 13.c4 Qe7 14.Bd3 Ba3 15.Rhe1 Bxb2+ 16.Kxb2 Qf6+) 13.Bc4 Qf6 14.Rhe1 Re3 15.Rxe3 fe 16.Rf1 g6 17.Qa5 Qf5 18.Qa4 c6 19.Nd4 e2 20.Nxf5 Nb6 21.Bxe2 1-0.

5.Bb2 Nf6

6.Nc3 Be7

7.Qe2 O-O

8.O-O-O Nc6 (120)

In the game Hebden - Romanishin, Moscow 1986, Black chose instead 8...c6. Play continued 9.g3! fg 10.Rg1 Re8 (Of course, taking another pawn is incredibly risky) 11.Rxg3 Bf8 12.d3 (12.Qe3 looks more precise) 12...Nbd7 13.Qd2 Ne5 14.Be2 g6 and now 15.Rdg1 would have given good play for the pawn.

9.g3! Bg4

Black aims to simplify the position but this won't relieve the pressure. 9...fg 10.Qg2 also gives White a strong attack.

10.Qf2 Ne5

11.Be2 fg

12.Qxg3 Nh5

Black's activity is of a temporary nature.

13.Qf2 Bxf3

14.Bxf3 Bh4

15.Qf1 Nf4

16.Rg1

The open g-file and long diagonal are the most important aspects of the position. White also has, in reserve, the option of playing d4 at a good moment.

16...c6

17.Bg4!

The bishop sets off for the active f5-square and Black's minor pieces begin to look as if they are in a tangle.

17...Nfg6

18.Bf5 Bg5

19.Kb1 Ne7

20.d4!

Black's knights are driven back and, after the exchange of dark-squared bishops, the open files on the kingside look even more menacing.

20...N5g6

21.Bc1! Bxc1

22.Kxc1 d5

Black should probably try to complicate the issue with something like 22...Qa5 23.Kb2 c5, although White should still be better. After 22...d5 the situation is close to being beyond repair.

23.e5 Nxf5

24.Qxf5 Ne7

24...Qc8 25.e6!

25.Qg4 g6

Now the kingside pawns have been fatally weakened.

26.Rdf1 Qc8

27.Qh4 Nf5

28.Qf6 Kh7

29.Ne2!

It just remains for the knight to be transferred to the kingside.

29...Qe6 (121)

What can Black do? Waiting passively is out of the question and 29...Qd8 allows 30.Rxf5.

30.Rxg6! Qxf6

31.Rxf6 Ng7

32.Rxf7

32.R1f3 looks even stronger.

32...Kg8

33.Rxf8+ Rxf8

34.Rxf8+ Kxf8
