2) Cunningham Defence

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 Be7 (35)

In practice, 3...Be7 is Usually seen as one of the most solid ways of meeting the King's Gambit. Black Calmly starts to develop his kingside whilst also giving himself the option of ...Bh4+. It is especially popular amongst the well-schooled ex-Soviet players.

To check or not to check? That is a much posed question in this variation. The general opinion seems to be that if the white king has f1 at his disposal, Black's loss of time is a bigger problem than White's lost right to castle. On the other hand, if the monarch has to bravely advance to e2, Black usually can't resist the check.

White has two main choices on his fourth turn: 4.Bc4 and 4.Nc3. Even if you intend to play the 4.Nc3 variation, you should still familiarise yourself with the lines arising from 4.Bc4, as there are many transpositional possibilities.

Game 11. Gallagher - P.Wells
Islington 1990

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 ef

3.Nf3 Be7

4.Bc4

One of my very first King's Gambits was against Maya Chiburdanidze in a London weekend tournament in 1985. 3...Be7 was the end of my theoretical knowledge but I had a recollection of the king coming to e2, so I played 4.d4, assuming it was the normal move. The game continued 4...Bh4+ 5.Ke2 d5 (This is why 4.Nc3 is stronger; it covers the d5-square) 6.e5 Bg4 7.Bxf4 Ne7 8.h3 Ng6!? 9.hg!? Nxf4+ 10.Ke3! Bg3

What a baptism. I remember now spending some time looking at 11.Ng1!?. with the idea of winning the bishop with Kf3 and Kxg3 but I had the feeling Black might be able to get a strong attack, so I played 11.Nc3 and after 11...f6 12.Ne2 Nxe2 13.Qxe2 fe I had to go pawn grabbing with 14.Qb5+. There followed: 14...Nc6 15.Qxb7 Nxd4 16.Bd3! Rb8 17.Qa6 e4 18.Qg6+!! (This certainly startled my opponent) 18...hg 19.Rxh8+ Kd7 20.Rxd8+ Rxd8 21.Kxd4 ed 22.Kxd5! dc 23.Rc1 with a small advantage for White, although the game was eventually drawn in a blitz finish. After this game my appetite for King's Gambits became insatiable.

4...Nf6

4...Bh4+ 5.Kf1 (Cunningham used to play 5.g3 fg 6.O-O gh+ 7.Kh1. In previous centuries, many quick wins were scored with these visual sacrifices, but modern defensive technique has rendered 5.g3 harmless. These days, players don't take everything that's offered as a matter of honour, but return the material - or at least some of it - at an appropriate moment to nullify the attack. So, instead of 6...gh+, 6...d5 7.Bxd5 Nf6 8.Bxf7+ (8.Nxh4 Nxd5 9.ed Qxh4 10.Qe2+ Kd8 is good for Black) 8...Kxf7 9.e5 (9.Nxh4 Qd4+) 9...Bh3 10.ef Bxf1 11.Qxf1 gh+ 12.Kh1 Bxf6 with a clear advantage for Black in Krejcik - Schlechter, Vienna 1918) 5...d5 (This is the only way for Black to fight for the initiative) 6.Bxd5 (6.ed is interesting and, in fact, transposes to the note to Black's fourth move in Spassky - Bronstein, game 24) 6...Nf6 and now White has:

(a) 7.Nxh4!? Nxd5 8.ed Qxh4 9.Qe1+ (9.d4) with a favourable pawn structure in the ending.

(b) 7.Nc3 O-O 8.d4!? (8.d3 is more solid) 8...Nxd5 9.Nxd5 f5 10.Nxh4 fe 11.Qh5 Be6 12.Nxf4 Qxd4! with good attacking chances for Black.

(c) 7.Bb3 Bg4 8.d3 O-O (37).

9.Qd2!? (9.Bxf4 allows 9...Nxe4 and 9.Nc3 Nh5! 10.Nd5 Qd6 11.Qd2 Bxf3 12.gf c6 13.Nc3 Kh8 14.Qg2 led to a slight advantage for Black in J. Wells - Corkett, Corr. 1989, although White should be able to improve on 10.Nd5) 9...Bxf3 10.gf Nh5 11.Qg2! Nc6 12.Qg4 Qg5 13.Rg1 Qxg4 14.fg! with a good game for White.

5.e5

The only testing move:

(a) 5.d3 is passive: 5...d5 6.ed Nxd5 7.Bxd5 Qxd5 8.Bxf4 is about equal.

(b) 5.Nc3 has a justifiably poor reputation: 5...Nxe4 6.Ne5 (6.Bxf7+ Kxf7 7.Ne5+ Kg8! 8.Nxe4 d6! 9.Nf3 d5 is bad) 6...Ng5 7.d4 d6 8.Nd3 f3! 9.gf O-O (9...Nh3!?) 10.Be3 Re8 11.Kd2 Bf6 12.Qf1 Nc6 13.d5 Rxe3 14.Kxe3 Bh3 and White was in bad shape, Zweigberk-Oechslein, Corr. 1962.

5...Ng4

5...Nh5 is rather dubious: 6.Nc3 d6 7.ed Qxd6 8.d4 Nc6 9.O-O O-O 10.Be2 Bg4 11.Ne4 followed by 12.Nf2 with good attacking chances for White (Estrin and Glaskov).

6.d4!?

For 6.O-O see game 12.

6.Nc3 is the main alternative, with play often transposing to the text. It is difficult to say which is the more accurate move order: 6...d6 (6...d5?! 7.Bxd5 Bh4+ 8.Kf1 Nc6 9.Bxc6+ bc 10.d3 O-O 11.Bxf4 f6 12.e6 f5 13.Nxh4 Qxh4 14.Qe1 is good for White (Keres); or 6...Bh4+?! 7.Kf1 Nf2? 8.Qe1 winning) and now:

(a) 7.d4 de 8.de Qxd1+ 9.Nxd1 Be6! 10.Bxe6 fe 11.h3 Nh6 12.Bxf4 and although White has slightly the better of it, Black should be able to defend.

(b) 7.ed Qxd6 (7...Bxd6 8.Qe2+ is similar to the main game) 8.Qe2 (8.d4 is the note to Black's seventh move) 8...O-O 9.d4 Nc6 10.Nd5 (10.Nb5 is interesting when Black should play 10...Qh6! with an unclear game) 10...Ne3 11.Bxe3 fe 12.Qxe3 Re8 13.O-O Gallagher - Vladimirov, Hastings 1990/91.

6...d5

6...Bh4+ occurred in Gallagher - Hebden, Hastings 1989/90. After 7.Kf1 Ne3+ (not 7...Nf2 8.Qe1) 8.Bxe3 fe 9.Qd3 O-O 10.Nc3 d6 11.Qxe3 Nc6 White could have obtained dangerous attacking chances by 12.Qe4! Be7 13.h4.

7.ed!

Previous publications have usually condemned 6 d4, but for some reason they have only considered 7.Bd3 here. That this is a mistake can be seen from the continuation of the game Lutikov - Estrin, Leningrad 1951: 7...Bh4+! 8.Ke2 Nf2 9.Qe1 Nxd3 10.Qxh4 Nxc1+ 11.Rxc1 Qxh4 12.Nxh4 Nc6 13.c3 O-O 14.Kf2 f6 with advantage to Black.

7...Bxd6

As this seems to lead into a bad endgame, 7...Qxd6 is more critical. The game Gallagher - Chiburdanidze, Biel 1990, worked out badly for me after 8.Nc3 Be6 9.d5?! Bd7 10.Qe2 O-O 11.Ne4 Qb6 12.Bxf4 Bf5 13.Ng3 Bg6 14.h3 Bd6 15.Bxd6 Qxd6 16.O-O-O Qf4+ 17.Kb1 Ne3 18.Rd4 Qxg3 19.Qxe3 Qxg2 20.Rh2 Qg3 21.Rg4 Qd6 and White was a pawn down with no attack.

However, 9.d5 doesn't really fit in with White's scheme of development. Instead, 9.Bd3! would enable White to fight for the advantage; 9...Ne3 can be answered by 10.Qe2.

The game Hebden - Fassert, Guernsey 1988, followed a course more to White's liking: 7...Qxd6 8.O-O O-O 9.Nc3 c6? (This is a serious mistake after which White's attack soon becomes irresistible) 10.h3 Ne3 (10...Nf6 11.Ne5) 11.Bxe3 fe 12.Ne5 Bh4 (This is rather optimistic, but 12...Be6 13.Ne4 is pretty terrible) 13.Rxf7 Be6 14.Ne4 Bf2+ 15.Kh2 Qxe5+ 16.de Bxf7 17.e6 1-0.

18.gf Na6 (38)

There are several factors which, when put together, add up to a sizeable advantage for White:

(a) Extra central pawn on d4, typical of many King's Gambit endings.

(b) Good bishop against poorly placed black knight.

(c) An active king.

(d) The opportunity to use the semi-open g-file to cause Black some problems on the kingside.

When you bear in mind that, on top of all these pure chess reasons, Black was already desperately short of time (not uncommon in the King's Gambit), one begins to appreciate the full extent of his difficulties.
