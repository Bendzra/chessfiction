9) Falkbeer Counter-Gambit

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 d5

3.ed e4 (132)

Instead of accepting the pawn on offer, Black decides to sacrifice one himself. In return, he hopes to be able to develop swiftly and easily, whilst also demonstrating how out of place the advance f2-f4 now is. At one stage this counter-gambit was scoring so well that it prompted Rudolf Spiel-mann to write his tragic article "From the deathbed of the King's Gambit". As we shall see, this was certainly a trifle premature.

On his fourth move White has tried several continuations, but in this book we shall only be examining 4.d3!. Modern practice has shown that to free his position White has to exchange Black's strong e-pawn as quickly as possible.

Game 41. Murey - Nikitin
USSR 1970

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 d5

3.ed...

I should just mention in passing that declining the Falkbeer with 3.Nf3 has a number of supporters. However, this idea sprang to prominence when Black was scoring well, and now that White seems able to prove an advantage in practically every variation, there is no need to decline the gambit.

3...e4

3...c6 is the Nimzowitsch Counter-Gambit (see chapter ten).

4.d3! ed?!

This is feeble and simply contributes towards White's development. There are two serious alternatives, one of which, 4...Nf6, will be the subject of subsequent games.

4...Qxd5 is also insufficient for equality, but White must play carefully. For example, 5.Qe2 Nf6 6.Nd2! (6.Nc3 Bb4 is not so clear) and now:

a) 6...Bf5 7.de Bxe4 (7...Nxe4 8.g4) 8.g4 gives White a clear advantage e.g. 8...Be7 9.Nxe4 Qxe4 (According to Keres 9...Nxe4 10.Bg2 Qa5+ 11.Kf1 Nd6 12.Bd2 is very good for White) 10.Bg2! Qxe2+ 11.Nxe2 c6 12.g5 and White has a considerable advantage due to his bishop pair and active development.

b) 6...Bg4 7.Ngf3 Bxf3 (If 7...Nc6, analysis by Glaskov shows how White can retain the advantage: 8.de Qh5 9.Qb5 O-O-O 10.Qxh5 Nxh5 11.Nc4 Nb4 (11...Bxf3 12.gf Nd4 13.Bd3 Nxf3+ 14.Kf2 is good for White) 12.Bd3! (Previously only 12.Na3? had been considered, which allows Black a strong attack in return for the pawn) 12...Nxd3+ 13.cd. Black cannot win back his pawn, as the following variation shows: 13...Bxf3 14.gf Rxd3 15.Ne5! Rd4 16.Be3 Rb4 17.Nxf7 Rg8 18.O-O-O with a clear advantage for White) 8.gf e3 9.Ne4 Be7 10.Bxe3 Nh5 (Keres considers that after 10...O-O White can gain the advantage with 11.Bg2 and 12.O-O) 11.Nc3 Qf5 12.O-O-O Nxf4 13.Qd2 (133)

Black has fallen seriously behind in development. 13...Bg5 (13...Ne6 14.f4 ; or 13...Nd5 14.Bh3! are both good for White) 14.h4 Bh6 15.Bh3! Nxh3 16.Bxh6 gh 17.Qe3+ and Black is unable to defend, e.g. 17...Kd8 18.Qd4+ ; or 17...Qe6 18.Qd4 O-O 19.Rxh3! wins quickly; 17...Kf8 18.Rxh3! (18.Qxh6+ is also good) 18...Qxh3 19.Qc5+. If Black goes to the g-file he gets mated and if he goes to the e-file he loses his rook.

5.Bxd3

5.Qxd3 is equally good, after which Black has very little play for the pawn, e.g 5...Nf6 6.Nc3 (6.c4) 6...Be7 (or 6...Bc5 7.Bd2 O-O 8.O-O-O Nbd7 9.g3! with a good game for White in Stoltz - Marshall, Folkestone 1933) 7.Be3 followed by 8.O-O-O.

5...Qxd5

Black grabs his chance to restore material equality, realising that if he doesn't do so now he probably never will. But there is a price to be paid for bringing out the queen so early. When you look at the alternative, though, it is hard to criticise Black's choice: 5...Nf6 6.Nc3 Be7 (6...Nxd5 7.Bb5+ is strong) 7.Nf3 O-O 8.O-O Nbd7 9.Bc4 Nb6 10.Bb3. The only way for Black to get his pieces out is by 10...Bb4 but after 11.Ne5 Bxc3 12.bc Nbxd5 13.Ba3 Re8 14.Qd4 White stood clearly better in Keres - Lilienthal, Moscow 1941.

6.Nc3 Qe6+

Of course, 6...Qxg2 7.Be4 Qg4 8.Qxg4 Bxg4 9.Bxb7 wins for White.

7.Nge2 Nh6 (134)

Black also got into great difficulties after 7...Nf6 8.O-O Qb6+ 9.Kh1 Be7 10.Qe1, Keres - Vidmar, Corr. 1936.

8.f5!...

This simple pawn sacrifice opens up further lines and diagonals as well as gaining even more tempi for the attack.

8...Nxf5

9.O-O Ne3

10.Bxe3 Qxe3+

11.Kh1 Bd6

12.Nf4!...

Combinations flow naturally when you are far ahead in development and the opponent's king is still in the middle.

12...O-O

13.Qh5 g6

After 13...h6 14.Rae1 gives White a massive position.

14.Nxg6!...

The attack crashes through whilst the black pieces stand and watch.

14...hg

15.Bxg6 fg

16.Qxg6+ Kh8

17.Nd5 Rxf1+

18.Rxf1 Qe2

19.Qh6+ Kg8

20.Nf6+ (135)

Mate follows shortly: 20...Kf7 21.Qh7+ Ke6 (21...Kf8 22.Qg8+ Ke7 23.Qe8) 22.Qg8+ and mate follows.

Belgrade 1964

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 d5

3.ed e4

4.d3 Nf6 (136)

5.de!

Experience has shown this capture to be the strongest in this position.

5...Nxe4

6.Be3!?...

White takes immediate control of the sensitive a7-g1 diagonal, not even worrying about ... Qh4+.

6.Nf3 can be seen in games 43 and 44.

6...Qh4+

Black has to take up the challenge or he will end up in an inferior position, e.g. 6...Bd6 7.Nf3 O-O 8.Bd3 Re8 9.O-O Nf6 10.Ne5!? Nbd7 11.Nc4 Nf8 12.Kh1 with the better game for White (Glaskov).

7.g3 Nxg3

8.Nf3!...

Of course, when Mikhail Tal had this position he was unable to resist the exchange sacrifice, but even he couldn't generate enough compensation: 8.hg?! Qxh1 9.Qe2 Bb4+ 10.c3 Bd6 11.Bg2 Qh6 12.Bd4+ Kd8 13.Nf3 Bg4 and Black's play along the e-file and extra exchange clearly outweigh any random attacking chances White might have (Tal - Trifunovic, Havana 1963).

8...Qe7

Of course, now that White has an extra tempo, it would be quite foolhardy to allow him to sacrifice the exchange by playing 8...Qh5.

9.hg Qxe3+

10.Qe2 Qxe2+

10...Bc5 11.Nc3 doesn't change the assessment.

11.Bxe2 (137)

So Black has managed to exchange queens and obtain the bishop pair. One would think he is over the worst - but this is by no means the case for a number of reasons:

a) White has a useful lead in development which means that he should get first use of the open e-file. Note that the rook on h1 is already well developed.

b) His dynamic pawn structure gives him a firm grip on the centre.

c) White's knights have some fine squares in the centre to occupy, which means that they are in no way inferior to the bishops.

11...Bg4

11...Bf5 12.Nbd2 c6 13.Nd4 Bd7 14.Bf3! gave White the better game in Vilner - Shukmann, Moscow 1977.

11...Bc5! would have kept Black's disadvantage to a minimum.

12.Nc3 Bb4

13.Ng5! Bxe2

14.Kxe2 Bxc3

15.bc h6

16.Kd3!...(138)

Now Black has the difficult decision of whether to leave his king in the centre or castle.

16...Rf8

This move was strongly criticised after the game, when it was suggested that by 16...O-O 17.Ne4 Rd8 Black could achieve an equal game. This does not seem to be the case, however: 18.c4 c6 19.Rh5! (It is always enjoyable to develop a rook in this fashion) 19...Na6 20.d6 b6 21.Rd1 Nc5+ 22.Rxc5! bc 23.Kc3 with a very good endgame for White in Listengarten - Kozlov, Baku 1977.

17.Nf3...

With the black king in the centre, this is much stronger than Ne4. From f3, the knight has the choice of several good squares and in fact the major part of White's advantage can be attributed to his superior minor piece.

17...Na6

18.Rae1+ Kd7

19.c4 f6

Black was, of course, worried about the e5-square, but now he has seriously weakened e6. He is relying on his knight to cover this square from c5.

20.Kd4...

With the threat of 21.c5.

20...b6

21.f5! Nc5

22.Nh4...

Black's position is full of holes.

22...Rfe8 (139)

23.Re6!...

The culmination of White's strategy. Taking the rook would leave Black in a near hopeless position: 23...Nxe6 24.fe+ Ke7 25.Nf5+ Kf8 26.c5!

23...g5!

The best chance.

24.Ng6?...

This is careless, throwing away most of the advantage. Instead, 24.Ng2! leaves Black in a sorry state, e.g. 24...Nxe6 25.fe+ Ke7 26.Ne3 Rh8 27.g4 and White's knight is the strongest piece on the board.

24...Nxe6

25.fe+ Rxe6!

26.de+ Kxe6

27.Rxh6 Kf7 (140)

And now the point of Black's little trick is revealed: the knight is trapped and ... Kg7 is going to win back the piece.

28.Ne5+ fe+

29.Kxe5 c5

White still has slightly the better of it, but the weakness of his queenside eases Black's defensive task.

30.Kf5 Ke7

31.Kxg5 Rf8

32.Rh2...

This is passive, but 32.Rh7+ Kd6 33.Rxa7 doesn't really offer any chances, e.g. 33...Rf3 34.g4 Rc3 35.Kf5 Rxc4 36.g5 Rxc2 37.g6 c4 and Black has a dangerous passed pawn of his own.

32...Kd6

33.g4 Rg8+

34.Kf4 Rf8+

35.Kg3 Ke5!

36.Re2+ Kd4

37.g5 Kxc4

38.Kg4 Kc3

Black has enough counterplay on the queenside.

39.g6 c4

40.Kg5 b5

41.g7

And at this point the players agreed to a draw, as after 41...Rg8 42.Kg6 a5 43.Kf7 Rxg7+ 44.Kxg7 b4 45.Kf6 a4 the outcome is apparent.

Game 43. Bronstein - Tal
Riga 1968

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 d5

3.ed e4

4.d3 Nf6

5.de Nxe4

6.Nf3 (141)

White's most popular and probably strongest move in this position. 6...Qh4+ is stopped and White judges that the play on the a7-g1 diagonal is not too dangerous.

6...Bc5

Other moves don't really meet the demands of the position:

a) 6...c6 7.Nbd2! Nxd2 8.Bxd2 Qxd5 9.Bd3 with a strong initiative for White.

b) 6...Bf5 7.Be3 c6 8.Bc4 b5 9.Bb3 c5 10.d6! c4 11.Qd5 Nd7 12.Qxf5 Nxd6 13.Qd5 and White stands clearly better, Alekhine -Tarrasch, St Petersburg 1914.

7.Qe2 (142)

7.Bd3 was recommended long ago by Tartakower and although it seems good for White, nobody appears to have heard him. After 7...Nf2 8.Qe2+ Qe7 9.Rf1 Nxd3+ 10.cd Qxe2+ 11.Kxe2 White has the better game.

7...Bf5

Black's position is much too loose to try to exploit the weak dark squares:

a) 7...Bf2+ 8.Kd1 Qxd5+ 9.Nfd2 f5 10.Nc3 Qd4 11.Nxe4 fe 12.c3 Qe3. White has a pawn if he wants it, but 13.Qh5+ promises much more.

b) 7...Qxd5 8.Nfd2! is similar to 'a', and also very good for White.

c) 7...O-O should be answered by 8.Qxe4 Re8 9.Ne5 f6 10.Bd3 g6 11.Qc4! and White's position is clearly preferable.

d) 7...f5 has also been seen, but after 8.Be3 Qxd5 9.Bxc5 Qxc5 10.Nc3 White had obtained the better position (Spielmann - Wolf, Dusseldorf 1908).

e) 7...Qe7 is also well met by 8.Be3!. White achieved a clear advantage in the game Arnason - D' Amore, Groningen 1980/81, after 8...Bxe3 9.Qxe3 Nd7 10.Nbd2 Ndf6 11.Nxe4 Nxe4 12.O-O-O O-O 13.Bd3.

8.Nc3...

In the game that so depressed Spielmann, he played 8.g4? against Tarrasch in Ostrau 1923. After 8...O-O 9.gf Re8 Black had an enormous attack.

8...Qe7 (143)

8...O-O is unsound: 9.Nxe4 Re8 10.Ne5 Bxe4 11.Qxe4 f6 12.d6! Qxd6 13.Be3! (to block the e-file) 13...Bxe3 14.Qc4+ (Blackburne - Marco, Berlin 1897).

9.Be3! Nxc3

9...Bxe3 is the subject of the next game.

The attempt to avoid simplification with 9...Bb4 isn't very good for Black, e.g. 10.Bd4 (10.Bd2 also gives White some advantage) 10...O-O 11.O-O-O Re8 (11...Bxc3 12.Bxc3 Qc5 meets an elegant refutation: 13.Bxg7! Kxg7 14.Nh4) 12.Nxe4 (12.Qb5 also looks good) 12...Bxe4 13.Be5 Bg6 14.Qc4 and White is a pawn up (Sembukhov - Brichkov, USSR Corr. 1985/86).

10.Bxc5 Nxe2

11.Bxe7 Nxf4

12.Ba3! Nd7

After 12...Nxd5 13.O-O-O c6 14.Ng5!, the threat of Re1+ is very hard to meet.

13.O-O-O Be4

Unfortunately for Black, 13...O-O-O comes up against a strange refutation: 14.Rd4! Ng6 15.g4!

14.Ng5 Bxd5 (144)

15.g3!?

15.Re1+ looks simple and strong (15...Ne6 16.c4), but Bronstein was dreaming of the brilliancy prize.

15...Bxh1

16.gf c5

To stave off immediate disaster, Black has to close the a3-f8 diagonal.

17.Bc4 Bc6

18.Nxf7 b5

19.Nd6+...

Of course, White should not be side-tracked by 19.Nxh8.

19...Ke7

20.Nxb5 Rhf8?!

Black's best choice was probably 20...Bxb5 21.Bxb5 Rhd8. White doesn't have to cash in with Bxd7 and Bxc5+ which gives Black decent drawing chances; instead he can keep up the pressure, perhaps with 22.b3!?. The text allows White to improve the position of his knight, with devastating effect.

Of course, with two pawns for the exchange and a strong attack, White still has an extremely good position.

USSR Corr. 1985/87

[...] 1.e4 e5

2.f4 d5

3.ed e4

4.d3 Nf6

6.Nf3 Bc5

7.Qe2 Bf5

8.Nc3 Qe7

9.Be3 Bxe3

10.Qxe3 Nxc3

11.Qxe7+ Kxe7

12.bc (146)

This is a critical position for the assessment of the Falkbeer. Although Black is sure to get his pawn back, he will waste time in doing so and when you add this to the weak position of his king, White's advantage begins to look serious.

12...Be4

Or 12...Bxc2 13.Kd2 Bg6 (or 13...Bf5 14.Re1+ Kf6 15.Nd4 Bd7 16.h3 with king-side expansion to follow) 14.Re1+ Kd6 15.Nd4 (15.Ng5!? looks interesting). Black's position is most unsatisfactory. The only two pieces he has succeeded in getting off the back rank are both in serious trouble: 15...Kxd5 fails to 16.f5 Bh5 17.g4, 18.Bg2+ and 19 Bxb7; 15...Nd7 allows 16.Nb5+, so Black has to try something like 15...h5, but then 16.f5 Bh7 17.Rb1! forces ... b6 which makes it extremely difficult for Black to develop.

13.Ng5!...

13.c4? Bxf3 14.gf Nd7 only leads to equality.

13...Bxc2!?

Black is willing to waste a tempo in order to play lines similar to those in the twelfth move note but with White's knight on the inferior g5-square.

The alternative is 13...Bxd5, after which 14.O-O-O! gives White the better game, e.g.

a) 14...Bxa2 15.c4 b5 16.cb a6 17.Bd3!? (Glaskov recommends 17.Kb2) 17...ab?! 18.Rhe1+ Be6 (There is no square for the king) 19.f5 Kf6 20.fe Kxg5 21.ef Rf8 22.Re8 and White soon won in Foune - Mathieu, Corr. 1985.

b) 14...Be6 15.Nxe6 (15.Bd3 also looks tempting, but the text does guarantee a clear advantage) 15...fe 16.Bc4 Rf8 (16...Nd7 17.Bxe6) 17.Rhe1 Rf6 18.f5! with a good game for White.

c) 14...c6 15.c4 Be6 16.Bd3 with threats of Rhe1 and f5.

d) 14...Rd8 15.c4 Be6 16.Rxd8 Kxd8 17.Nxe6+ fe 18.Bd3 h6 19.Re1! Kd7 20.Re3 Nc6 21.Rg3 and White wins (Korchnoi and Zak).

14.Kd2 Bg6

15.Re1+...(147)

15...Kf8

Other moves seem to be even worse:

a) 15...Kd6 16.f5!

b) 15...Kf6 16.g4! Rd8 17.c4 c6 18.Nh3 and Black is in serious danger of being mated.

c) 15...Kd7 16.Bd3 f6 17.Ne6 with a clear advantage.

16.Bb5!?...

White finds an interesting way to create attacking chances. 16.g4 also looks good.

16...c6

17.f5!...

This is the point behind White's previous move. The black bishop is forced to h5, as after 17...Bxf5 18.Rhf1 g6 19.g4 White should win.

17...Bh5

18.dc Nxc6

19.Bxc6 bc

20.c4

White creates a safe square for his king. His advantage springs from Black's total lack of coordination.

20...g6

Black's bishop will now return to the game, but his kingside remains in a tangle. If instead 20...h6, White gains a clear advantage by 21.Ne4 f6 22.Kc3 Bf7 23.Nc5.

21.f6 h6

22.Ne4 Bg4

23.Kc3 Kg8

24.Nc5 g5

25.Re7...(148)

25...Kh7!?

25...Bh5 26.h4 is very bad so Black preferred to connect his rooks and hope to survive the coming onslaught.

White forces Black to take the f-pawn with his king, in order to improve his attacking chances.
