Black Plays 7...Nc6!?

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Nc6

A Rauzeresque reply

7...Nc6!? is a move more in keeping with the Classical Sicilian than the Najdorf, but that doesn't mean that it's a bad one. For many years the move was rather neglected, but it had begun to be taken more seriously by the late nineties and was even Tony Kosten's choice against 6.Bg5 in his Easy Guide to the Najdorf. Even today, White players are still often a little unprepared for 7...Nc6!? after which the position can quickly become fairly unbalanced.

8.Nxc6

One of two critical responses; the other being the 8.e5 h6 9.Bh4 of Nunn-Gormally. With 8.Qf3 ruled out, White shouldn't though prefer 8.Qd2?! as 8...h6! once again forces him to cede the bishop-pair.

8...bxc6 9.e5 h6 10.Bh4 g5! 11.fxg5 Nd5

Breaking the pin

The point of Black's play and he will now hope to show that White has become rather overextended. However, Black remains for the moment a pawn down and must not forget that it isn't just the white king which can come under attack.

TIP: 10...g5 may appear to have left the black king unable to safely castle, but it is usually fairly happy in the centre, well protected behind Black's strong central pawn majority.

12.Ne4

Increasing the tension, but 12.Nxd5 is an important alternative:

(a) 12...cxd5 13.Qh5 Qb6 begins counterplay on the dark squares, but White has scored very well in response with the aggressive 14.Bd3! ,

Supporting g6

intending to advance the g-pawn and to gain a strong initiative after 14...Bg7 15.g6 Qxb2?! (Black should try 15...O-O 16.Be7 dxe5!? ) 16.O-O! .

(b) 12...exd5 is the safer option, although 13.exd6 ( 13.Qe2 hxg5 14.Bg3 is fine for Black after Kosten's 14...Rh6! ) 13...hxg5 14.Bg3 Bxd6 15.Qf3! Qe7+ 16.Be2 retained a small edge for White in M.Hebden-D.Gormally, Port Erin 2001.

12...Qb6!

Taking aim at the b2 and e2 squares, while also threatening to check on b4.

13.Bd3 hxg5

Where to move the bishop?

Avoiding the risky 13...Qxb2?! 14.O-O Qxe5 15.Bg3 , when Black can easily find himself too far behind in development, and leaving White with a choice of bishop moves:

(a) 14.Bf2 Qxb2! 15.O-O Qxe5 16.Bg3 is now possible because of 16...Qd4+ 17.Kh1 f5! as Walter Browne has employed, although Kosten's 16...Nf4!? may be even better.

(b) 14.Bg3!? has been considered critical and will be examined in Tischbierek-Shirov.

(c) 14.Bxg5 sets a devious trap:

WARNING: Black must avoid the tempting 14...Rxh2? 15.Rxh2 Qg1+ due to 16.Bf1 Qxh2 17.Qxd5! exd5 18.Nf6+ Ke7 19.Ng4+ Ke6 20.Nxh2 and Black was a piece down in N.Castaneda-W.Browne, Philadelphia 1997.

Black has thus been advised to prefer 14...Qxb2 when both 15.O-O Qxe5 and 15.Nf6+ Nxf6 16.Bxf6 Qc3+ 17.Ke2 Rg8 are fine for him. 15.c4!? is more critical, intending to take the piece with 15...Qxg2!? 16.Rf1 dxe5 17.cxd5 , although that isn't so clear after 17...Bb4+! 18.Bd2 Bxd2+ 19.Nxd2 cxd5 .

Theoretical?

There are some fairly forcing lines after 7...Nc6!? and both sides should know some theory as the resulting positions are often very unclear. However, in comparison to the other two variations considered in this chapter, 7...Nc6 is pretty untheoretical!

Statistics

7...Nc6 is still a fairly recent development, but has made 50% from just over 450 games and Black's score remains the same after the common continuation of 8.Nxc6 bxc6 9.e5 h6 10.Bh4 g5 11.fxg5 Nd5 . However, Black should be more worried, statistically at least, by an immediate 8.e5 with which White has scored 61% from 85 outings.

Game 50
Tischbierek,R - Shirov,A [B96]
Bundesliga 2000

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Nc6 8.Nxc6 bxc6 9.e5 h6 10.Bh4 g5 11.fxg5 Nd5 12.Ne4 Qb6 13.Bd3 hxg5 14.Bg3 dxe5?!

Tischbierek quickly obliterates a 2700 in this game and so we must take his choice of 14.Bg3 pretty seriously, although Shirov's play is already sub-optimal:

(a) 14...Qxb2? is very risky and certainly no improvement; 15.O-O Qd4+ 16.Kh1 dxe5 17.c3! Qa7 18.Bxe5 Rh6 19.Qf3 f5 would have given White a winning attack in L.Perez Rodriguez-B.Lalic, El Sauzal (rapid) 2003 after 20.Rae1! g4 21.Qg3 fxe4 22.Bxe4 Bg7 23.Bxd5! cxd5 24.Qxg4 (Lalic).;

(b) 14...Nf4! is the correct response when Black isn't troubled by 15.Nf6+ Ke7 and 15.Bxf4 gxf4 16.Nxd6+ Bxd6 17.exd6 Qxb2 is also acceptable for him because of 18.O-O Qd4+ 19.Kh1 Qxd6 , regaining the pawn in M.Gongora-A.Abreu, Cuban Ch., Las Tunas 2001.

15.Bxe5 Rh4 16.Qd2! f5

A thematic move and one in keeping with Shirov's style, but perhaps he should have preferred 16...Qb4!? 17.Qxb4 Bxb4+ 18.c3 Be7 19.Nd6+ Bxd6 20.Bxd6 Bb7 when White is better, but Black's pieces are fairly well placed and not so easy to contain.

17.Nf6+ Nxf6 18.Bxf6 Bb4?

He had to try 18...Kf7! 19.Be5 Bh6 when Black would still be in the game, but even members of the elite have off-days, albeit rare ones.

19.c3 Bd6 20.Bxg5?

Black immediately returns the favour after this and so this may well have been a 9am start Sunday Bundesliga clash! Instead Tischbierek should have preferred 20.Qxg5 Rg4 21.Qh5+ Kf8 22.Be2 with a crushing advantage.

20...Rg4? 21.Be2 Rxg5 22.Qxg5 Qxb2 23.Bh5+ Kd7 24.Qg7+
