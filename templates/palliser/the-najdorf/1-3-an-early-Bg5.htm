An Early Bg5

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.Bg5

Fighting for control of d5

This move, logically fighting for control of d5, has never been especially popular. Indeed for a long time White would gain control of d5, but would then lack a follow-up, while Black played around the d5-knight and gained dynamic counterplay on both flanks in Sveshnikov style. However, this system has received the attention of Kramnik and his games with it have led to a fair amount of interest in it, especially due to the white idea of castling long and attacking on the kingside.

White can also insert 8.O-O O-O before going 9.Bg5 , although practice has shown that Black is fairly comfortable after 9...Be6 10.Bxf6 Bxf6 11.Nd5 and then 11...Nd7 12.Qd3 Rc8 13.c3 Bg5 as played by Fischer or after an immediate 11...Bg5 , transposing to Sareen-Palliser.

8...Be6

Covering d5 which Black might prefer to do with 8...Nbd7 . However, after the accurate sequence 9.a4! b6 10.Bc4! White has an edge with V.Kramnik-B.Gelfand, Cap d'Agde (rapid) 2003 continuing 10...Bb7 11.Qe2 O-O 12.O-O Qc7 13.Rfd1

White is en route to claiming d5

13...h6 14.Bxf6 Nxf6 15.Nd5 Nxd5 16.Bxd5 Bxd5 17.Rxd5 and White was fully in control. Black lacked counterplay and had to painfully spectate as Kramnik slowly brought his knight to d5.

9.Bxf6 Bxf6 10.Qd3 Nc6 11.O-O-O

A fashionable concept

This is the aggressive idea which has caught the attention of many grandmasters of late. Castling long is actually quite logical as Black usually aims to meet Nd5 by playing for ...f5 and kingside counterplay. However, with the white king not on the kingside, it is White who is able to seize the initiative on that flank and Black must be careful not to be left lacking counterplay as we'll see in Bruzon-Quezada. Before the strengths of going long were fully realized, an immediate 11.Nd5 was the main move, but this was dealt a blow in J.Arnason-G.Kasparov, World Junior Ch., Dortmund 1980. Kasparov gave a typically dynamic and model handling of the black pieces with 11...Bg5 12.O-O Ne7!

Challenging White's control of d5

13.Nxe7 Qxe7 14.Rfd1 Rd8 when Black had sufficient counterplay, especially after 15.Na5?! O-O 16.Nc4 d5! , thematically opening the position for the two bishops as 17.Nxe5 Bc8 would have regained the pawn with interest.

Theoretical?

White's plan after 8.Bg5 is pretty easy to understand, although it isn't especially theoretical. It is though fairly popular and so Black would be advised to know a line against 11.O-O-O to ensure himself of some essential counterplay, while also understanding the common plans arising when White prefers to castle short.

Statistics

White has scored just 49% from almost 300 games with 8.Bg5 , but after Kramnik's 8...Be6 9.Bxf6 Bxf6 10.Qd3 Nc6 11.O-O-O , that rises to an impressive 72% albeit from only 18 games. Thus this line is still in its infancy and Black is yet to settle on the best reply to 10.Qd3 .

An Early Bg5: Illustrative Games

Game 4
Sareen, V. - Palliser, R.
Isle of Man Open 2005

[ECO "B92"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nc3 d6 3.Nf3 Nf6 4.d4 cxd4 5.Nxd4 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.Bg5 Be6 9.Bxf6 Bxf6 10.Nd5

Sareen avoids the fashionable continuation and now there is nothing wrong with simply 10...Nd7 instead of my provocative choice.

10...Bg5!? 11.O-O

11.h4!? would have attempted to punish my last when 11...Be7 12.g3 ( 12.Qd3 O-O 13.O-O-O!? sacrifices the h-pawn as Kramnik has done after 10.Qd3 , although here White's king hasn't made it to b1 which slightly helps Black) 12...Nd7 13.a4 Nf6 14.Nxf6+ Bxf6 15.c4 O-O was roughly level in V.Wolf-D.King, Bundesliga 1985. White has a bind on d5, but his kingside has been weakened and the b3-knight isn't especially well-placed. Wolf thus opted for 16.Nd2 , but after 16...Qa5! Black instructively improved his dark-squared bishop with ...Bd8-b6.

11...O-O 12.c4?!

Seventy years ago Black's position would have been condemned, but I was quite happy here as Black has a clear plan, unlike White.

NOTE: Modern chess players are more interested than their predecessors in the dynamics of a position, rather than its static features.

12...Nd7 13.Qd3 Rc8 14.Rfd1 g6!

Preparing to recapture on f5 with a pawn and already Black is slightly for choice as White lacks any real play. He can't even aim to tie Black down to the defence of d6 with 15.Nc3 due to the weakness of c4.

15.g3 Bh6 16.Bf1 Kh8 17.Nc1?!

Both sides have manoeuvred and aimed to improve their pieces, but bringing the knight to c3 is simply too slow. Instead 17.Bg2 was preferable, although Black would still have been doing well after the intended 17...f5 18.exf5 Bxf5! ( 18...gxf5 19.Nc3! gains some counterplay) 19.Qe2 b5! with a strong initiative.

17...f5! 18.exf5 Bxf5 19.Qe2 e4!

It becomes clear that White has been outmanoeuvred and that he now faces a powerful attack.

20.Ne3 Ne5 21.Nxf5 gxf5 22.Nb3 Qf6 23.Rd5 f4!

Black has no interest in the c4-pawn and instead homes in on f2.

24.Rad1 Nd3 25.Qxe4? Nxf2!

Crushing and, had he had a little more time, Sareen might well have stopped here rather than first lose his queen.

26.Kxf2 fxg3+ 27.Kxg3 Qf2+ 28.Kg4 Rf4+ 29.Qxf4 Qxf4+ 30.Kh3 Qf3+ 31.Kh4 Rg8 0-1

Game 5
Bruzon, L. - Quezada, Y.
Capablanca Memorial, Havana 2004

[ECO "B92"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.Bg5 Be6 9.Bxf6 Bxf6 10.Qd3 Nc6

The main move, but quite possibly Black should prefer 10...O-O 11.O-O-O Qb6!? , refusing to defend the d-pawn and hoping to trade it for f2. In A.Fernandez-Y.Quezada, Santa Clara (rapid) 2005 White prevented that with 12.Rhf1 , but after 12...Nc6! 13.Qxd6 Bxb3! 14.axb3 Rad8 15.Qa3 Be7 16.Qa4 Nd4 the strong d4-knight and misplaced white queen supplied more than enough compensation.

11.O-O-O Be7

Black has also preferred to force exchanges with 11...Nd4 12.Nxd4 exd4 13.Nd5 Bxd5 14.exd5 O-O and in time it may be shown that this is sufficient to equalise. However, of late White has often been gaining the edge after 15.Qf3 when he relies on the opposite-coloured bishops and his kingside pawns to gain an attack.

12.Nd5 Bg5+ 13.Kb1 O-O 14.h4!

A strong sacrifice of Kramnik's which Black must accept and then aim to defend, as 14...Bh6 15.g4 is even worse for him.

14...Bxh4 15.g3 Bf6 16.Qf3 Bg5!

Inspired defence as Quezada brings his bishop to h6 rather than suffer with a weakened kingside after 16...h6 17.Nxf6+ Qxf6 18.Qxf6 gxf6 19.Rxd6 .

17.Qh5 Bh6!?

Previously Black had managed to draw in V.Kramnik-V.Anand, Wijk aan Zee 2004 after 17...h6 18.f4 Bf6 19.Nd2 Nd4 20.Bc4 , although at this stage White still retained dangerous kingside chances and Anand had to proceed very accurately.

18.Bg4! a5! 19.Ne3

Preparing to exchange bishops and then remove the h6-bishop with 21.Ng4, but Quezada continues to counter actively.

19...a4 20.Nc5 Qe7 21.Nd3 a3 22.b3 Nd4 23.Bf5 Rac8?

Too slow and now Bruzon implements a bind and ends Black's counterplay. Quezada thus should have preferred 23...Qc7! 24.c4 (but not 24.Nb4?? Qc3 ) 24...Qb6! with sufficient counterplay when the game should lead to a draw after 25.Ng4! Bxf5 26.exf5 Nxb3! 27.Nxh6+ gxh6 28.Qxh6 Nd4+ 29.Kc1 Rfc8 .

WARNING: In a sharp opposite-side castling situation, each tempo is usually very important and just one mistake can be fatal.

24.Nb4! Qc7 25.c4 Bxf5 26.Nxf5 Nxf5 27.Qxf5 f6 28.Nd5

White is still a pawn down, but he has a huge bind on the position as well as by far the superior minor piece. Bruzon now steadily improves his pieces and advances on the kingside.

28...Qf7 29.Rd3 Rc6 30.Nc3 Ra8 31.Rhd1 g6 32.Qh3 Bf8 33.g4 Qe6?! 34.Nd5 Bg7 35.g5! Re8 36.Rh1 Qxh3 37.Rdxh3 fxg5 38.Rxh7 Rcc8 39.R7h3 Re6 40.Rg1 Rf8 41.f3 Bf6 42.Kc2 Ree8 43.Rh6 Kf7 44.b4! Rh8 45.Rgh1 Rxh6 46.Rxh6 Kg7??

Allowing an instant finish, although White was winning in any case due to the weakness of a3.

47.Rh7+! Kxh7 48.Nxf6+ Kg7 49.Nxe8+ 1-0
