The Subtle 9 Kh1

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.Kh1

The modern handling of 6.Be2

How does 9.Kh1 help White?

9.Kh1 has been White's main continuation in the 6.Be2 Najdorf for the past decade and is effectively a high-class waiting move. Its aims are primarily prophylactic and aimed against the development of the black light-squared bishop. 9...Be6 is natural, but 10.f4 exf4 11.Bxf4 Nc6 gives White a better move than transposing into our last section with 12.a4 . Instead 12.Qe1! , intending Qg3 and Rad1, gives White the edge as Black lacks effective counterplay while White builds up on the kingside. The alternative is 10...Qc7 , but if anything this is even worse due to 11.f5 Bc4 12.g4! . Black would like to, and really needs to be able to, classically meet this with 12...d5 , but he comes under a fair amount of pressure after 13.g5! Nxe4 14.Nxe4 dxe4 15.f6 .

White gains good kingside chances

Black might thus prefer the alternative development of his light-squared bishop with 9...b5 . However, White is ready to meet that with 10.a4! Bb7 11.Nd5 when he has good chances of gaining the advantage, especially after 11...Nxe4? 12.Bf3 f5 13.Bxe4 fxe4 14.Na5! Qxa5 15.Nxe7+ and 16.Qxd6 .

Can Black be equally subtle in reply?

Time has shown that 9.Kh1 is far from a refutation of the Najdorf and Black has worked out a couple of good schemes with which to counter it. Foremost amongst these is the Gelfand-pioneered 9...b6!?

Insisting on developing the bishop

with which Black refuses to give up on the idea of giving his light-squared bishop a good role, such as with 10.f4 Bb7 . White has no direct response to 9...b6 , unlike after 9...b5 , while Black also has far from ruled out later gaining queenside play by pushing the pawn on from b6. However, 9...b6 isn't the only move Black can consider:

(a) In Galkin-Dworakowska we'll see if 9...b5 10.a4! is really so problematic for Black.

(b) 9...Nc6 has established itself as a solid alternative to 9...b6 as Dervishi-Kobalija reveals.

(c) 9...Qc7!? was fairly popular in the mid-nineties, intending 10.f4 b5 . Critical instead is the aggressive 10.g4! Be6 11.g5 Nd7 when the jury is still out on 12.f4 and 12.Bg4!? , but many Black players have departed for a simpler life after 9...Nc6 and 9...b6 .

Returning to 9...b6 :

10.a4

Ruling out a further advance of the b-pawn, although initially White thought that 10.Bg5 might be his answer. However, with 10...Nbd7! Black keeps d5 covered when Gelfand has demonstrated that 11.Nd5 Nxd5 12.Qxd5 Rb8 13.Bxe7 Qxe7 14.Rad1 Nf6 15.Qxd6 Qxd6 16.Rxd6 Nxe4 17.Rd5 doesn't give White any edge after 17...f6! , when the black knight will come to f7 and the bishop to e6, thereby preventing White from making any inroads down the d-file.

White's most popular move is actually 10.Be3 , but after 10...Bb7 11.f3 Black has enough development to be able to play 11...b5! . Following 12.a4 b4 13.Nd5 Nxd5 14.exd5 Nd7 15.c3 bxc3 16.bxc3 Bg5!

Black isn't worse on either flank

Black ensures himself of good counterplay; he should aim to tie White to the defence of his c-pawn and to prevent a c5 break, before turning his attention to exploiting his kingside majority.

10...Bb7 11.f3

Now Black can either harmoniously develop his knight to d7 or bring to c6 from where it may head to b4 to keep an eye on d5. We'll examine that choice further in Kulaots-Carlsen.

Theoretical?

Whether Black opts for a 9...b6 or a 9...Nc6 approach, he should ensure he knows a few more moves beyond them. General understanding, as throughout this chapter, remains important after both, but this variation is quite popular and does contain some forcing lines such as after 9...b6 10.Bg5 .

Statistics

White initially enjoyed some success with 9.Kh1 and that is still reflected in the statistics which reveal that he has scored 54% from 850 games. 9...b6 has now established itself as Black's main defence and with it Black has managed a reasonable 48% from just over 200 games.

The Subtle 9 Kh1: Illustrative Games

Game 8
Galkin, A. - Dworakowska, J.
Isle of Man Open 2005

[ECO "B92"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.Kh1 b5 10.a4! b4

10...Bb7 11.Nd5 b4 transposes to our next note, but Black should beware pawn grabbing with 11...bxa4? 12.Rxa4 Bc6 13.Ra3 Nxe4 . In typically powerful fashion Kasparov has continued 14.Na5! Nf6 15.Nxc6 Nxc6 16.Bc4 Nd4 17.Rh3! which threatened 18.Qd3 and exploited his light-squared control to launch a strong attack against Anand at Las Palmas 1996.

11.Nd5 Nxd5?

11...Bb7 has to be played when 12.Nxe7+ Qxe7 13.f3 d5 exchanges the bishop-pair for the ideal break. However, with some accurate play White ought to emerge with the edge, as he did after 14.exd5 Bxd5 15.Bg5 Rd8 16.Qe1! Nc6 17.Qf2 in D.Jakovenko-E.Najer, Russian Ch., Krasnoyarsk 2003.

WARNING: Black must be very careful about tactics down the long diagonal should he consider grabbing e4.

Here 11...Nxe4 loses to 12.Bf3 f5 13.Bxe4 fxe4 14.Nxe7+ Qxe7 15.Qd5+ and 16.Qxa8 .

12.Qxd5 Ra7 13.Be3 Be6 14.Qd2 Rb7

Black has regained control over d5, but she stands somewhat worse. 14...Rd7 15.Qxb4 d5 16.Bc5 Nc6 17.Qa3 doesn't offer enough compensation, but in the game Galkin quickly seizes a dangerous initiative.

15.f4 Qc7 16.f5 Bd7

Not a nice move to have to make, but 16...Bc4 drops an exchange and 16...Bxb3 17.cxb3 opens the c-file for the white rooks and enables the white bishop to reach a powerful outpost.

17.f6! Bxf6 18.Rxf6!

NOTE: An exchange is but a small price to pay to wreck the opponent's now undefended kingside.

18...gxf6 19.Bh6 Re8 20.Bxa6!

Finishing in style as a further sacrifice enables the white queen to decisively enter the attack.

20...Nxa6 21.Qf2 Bf5 22.Qxf5 Qd7 23.Qf3 1-0

Game 9
Dervishi, E. - Kobalija, M.
European Ch., Silivri 2003

[ECO "B92"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.Kh1 Nc6 10.f4 b5!

Striking out and gaining counterplay, rather than allow White a pleasant edge with 10...exf4 11.Bxf4 Be6 12.Qe1! .

11.a4

Logically challenging on the queenside, whereas after 11.Bf3 , Black can exchange knights and 11...Na5! 12.Nxa5 Qxa5 13.Nd5 Nxd5 14.exd5 exf4 15.Bxf4 Bf5 was fine for him in V.Kramnik-V.Topalov, Wijk aan Zee 2004.

Waiting with Nunn's 11.Be3!? currently appears critical when Black is yet to find a fully satisfactory answer, although 11...Bb7 ( 11...exf4 12.Rxf4 Be6 13.a4 b4 14.Nd5 gave White an edge in N.Short-J.Xu, Taiyuan 2004) (and 11...Be6? 12.f5 Bc4 13.Bxc4! bxc4 14.Nd2 was rather awkward for Black in J.Nunn-D.Gormally, Paignton 2000 as 14...d5 15.exd5 Nxd5 16.Nxd5 Qxd5 17.Qg4 netted a clear pawn) 12.a4! b4 13.Nd5 Nxd5 14.exd5 Na5 15.Nxa5 Qxa5 16.fxe5 was J.Van Oosterom-H.Elwert, correspondence 2000 when Black eventually equalised with some accurate defence after 16...Rad8 (but not 16...dxe5? 17.d6! ) 17.e6 fxe6 18.dxe6 Qe5 .

11...b4!?

The c6-knight will not be heading to b4 in this line, while it could find itself a little misplaced if White manages to make Nd5 work when Black has to exchange on d5. Thus Kobalia rejects the reasonable 11...Bb7 in favour of exploiting the knight's position on c6 to open the position.

12.Nd5 Nxe4 13.Bf3 f5 14.Nxe7+ Nxe7 15.fxe5 dxe5 16.Qxd8 Rxd8

The last few moves have been fairly forced, but Black will always be quite comfortable after the exchange on e4 due to his active pieces.

17.Bxe4

Dervishi was quite possibly put off 17.Nc5 by the promising exchange sacrifice 17...Nxc5! 18.Bxa8 f4 .

17...fxe4 18.Bg5 Ra7 19.Rae1 h6 20.Bd2

20.Bxe7 Rxe7 21.Rxe4 Bf5 22.Re2 Rd5 is also in Black's favour as his strong bishop outweighs the weakness of e5.

20...a5 21.Rxe4 Nc6 22.Be3 Rf7 23.Re1?

Permitting the black pieces to spring forwards, whereas after 23.Kg1! Rxf1+ 24.Kxf1 Rd1+ 25.Kf2 Be6 26.Bb6 White should be able to hold the draw without too much difficulty.

23...Be6 24.Nd2 Bf5 25.Rc4 Nd4 26.Rc1 Nxc2!

TIP: In an ending a rook and pawn usually become at least as effective as a bishop and knight.

Kobalia converts smoothly after this, although he would also have been much better after Ftacnik's 26...Rfd7 27.Bxd4 Rxd4 28.Rxd4 exd4 29.Nb3 Rd5 .

27.R1xc2 Bxc2 28.Rxc2 Rd3 29.Bg1 b3 30.Rc8+ Kh7 31.Nc4? Rf1! 32.Nxe5 Rd2 33.h4 Rxb2 34.Rb8 Rf4 35.g3 Rf1 0-1

Game 10
Kulaots, K. - Carlsen, M.
Gausdal 2005

[ECO "B92"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.Kh1 b6 10.f3 Bb7 11.Be3

11.a4 is also played when 11...Nbd7 12.Be3 transposes, but Topalov's preferred 11...Nc6!? is an important alternative. 12.Bg5 (the knight helps Black to usefully blockade the queenside in the case of 12.Nd5 Nxd5 13.exd5 Nb4 14.c4 a5! ) 12...Nb4 13.Nb1! refuses to let the knight settle on b4. After 13...h6 14.Bxf6 Bxf6 15.Na3! White is slightly better and so Black should prefer 13...Nh5!? and 14.Bxe7 ( 14.Be3 Bg5! 15.Bf2 Nf4 supplies good activity) 14...Qxe7 15.Na3 Rad8 16.c3 Nc6 17.Nc4 Qc7 18.Ne3 enables him to cover d5 with 18...Ne7 or first 18...Nf4 which is roughly equal.

11...Nc6 is thus a perfectly acceptable and solid alternative to 11...Nbd7 which often leads to a rather lively position.

11...Nbd7 12.a4 Rc8 13.Rf2!?

Bringing the rook in front of the queen in a bid to limit Black's counterplay. 13.Qd2 Rc7 14.Rfd1 was preferred in P.Charbonneau-Z.Efimenko, Montreal 2004 when after 14...Qa8 15.Nc1 Rd8 , White tried the enterprising 16.a5!? b5 17.Bb6 . This appears strong and even temporarily nets a pawn, but with 17...Nxb6 18.axb6 Rcc8 19.Nxb5 the white pieces neglect an important square and 19...d5! 20.Nc7 Qb8 gave Black an active and promising game.

13...Rc7!

An important concept which sees the black queen go to a8. Already Carlsen is hoping to blow the position open with an exchange sacrifice on c3 after which ...d5 will enable his pieces to quickly spring to life.

14.Bf1 Qa8 15.Rd2 Rfc8 16.Qe2

White can also re-route his knight, but after 16.Nc1 Rxc3! 17.bxc3 d5 Black has good compensation along the lines of the game.

NOTE: An exchange sacrifice on c3 is a recurring theme in the Najdorf. Sometimes Black immediately wins the e4-pawn with it, whilst it usually leaves the black pieces much better coordinated than their ragged counterparts.

16...h6 17.Rad1

Carlsen has prevented any annoying Bg5 ideas, but now Kulaots threatens 18.Qf2 when Black would be under pressure and so he must respond immediately.

17...Rxc3! 18.bxc3 d5! 19.exd5 Nxd5 20.Rd3 N7f6!?

Playing for the initiative and to open the long diagonal, rather than regain a pawn and maintain positional compensation.

21.Qe1 e4 22.fxe4 Nxe4 23.Bd4 Nf4 24.Rf3 Ng6!

It's impressive as to how Carlsen maintains the tension in a very mature fashion. Kulaots has defended well thus far, but how long can he keep it up? Instead he was probably mainly analysing forcing lines like 24...Bg5!? when Kosten gives 25.h4 Nc5 26.hxg5 Bxf3 27.Rd2! hxg5 28.Bxc5 bxc5 29.gxf3 Qxf3+ 30.Kg1 , but this isn't too special for Black. White has managed to safeguard his king and it's not just White who has a vulnerable queenside.

25.Kg1 Re8 26.Bc4? Nd6! 27.Be2 Nh4 28.Rg3 Ndf5?!

Missing a good opportunity with 28...Bg5! 29.Bxb6 Bxg2 30.Rxd6 Bf3! (Kosten) to very dangerously rip open the white kingside.

29.Qf2! Bg5 30.Bf1 Re4?

Permitting White to strongly return the exchange on g5. Kulaots doesn't, however, and by now time trouble must have taken over this complex and very interesting tussle. Instead 30...Nxg3 31.hxg3 Ng6 32.Bxb6 Ne5 would have continued to maintain Black's excellent compensation.

31.Bd3? Rf4 32.Qe2 Nxg3 33.hxg3 Nxg2? 34.gxf4 Nxf4 35.Qe5! f6 36.Qf5 g6 37.Bc4+ Kf8 38.Qd7 Nh3+ 39.Qxh3 1-0
