White Plays f4

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.a4 Be6 10.f4

Gaining space

Why is this the most accurate way to play f4?

Advancing the f-pawn is a natural plan and if permitted White intends to play f5 with tempo, after which he will aim to construct a central bind with moves like Bg5. However, before playing f4 it makes sense to restrain Black's queenside counterplay with 9.a4 as an immediate 9.f4 can be well met by 9...b5 . Black then intends 10.Bf3 Bb7 11.a3 Nbd7 12.Be3 Rc8 when he enjoys a very harmonious position. However, 10.a4 also fails to trouble Black due to 10...Bb7! .

Gaining good counterplay against e4

TIP: Black should generally be happy to exchange his b-pawn for the white e-pawn as an extra central pawn tends to be much more important than a flank pawn in the middlegame.

White can also follow up 9.Be3 Be6 with 10.f4 , but after 10...exf4 11.Bxf4 the bishop has had to move twice. Black is unsurprisingly thus pretty comfortable and should play by analogy with the ...exf4 lines considered below. 11.Rxf4!? is the alternative and, although it's never been too popular, shouldn't be underestimated. 11...Nbd7 , intending 12.Nd5 Bxd5 13.exd5 Rc8 14.Rb4 Qc7 15.c3 Rfe8! , leads to rough equality.

Finally, Dolmatov used to employ 8.Be3 Be6 9.f4 exf4 10.Bxf4 Nc6 11.Qd2 when White can still go long. However, he had to give it up as White has no advantage after 11...d5! 12.exd5 Nxd5 13.Nxd5 Qxd5 and 14.O-O-O!? Qe4! 15.Bf3 Qc4! , preparing a queenside attack, was shown to favour Black in R.Byrne-D.King, London 1991.

Why has 10 f4 declined in popularity?

Leading grandmasters of the sixties and seventies used to take on the Najdorf like this, but nowadays this variation is considered pretty comfortable for Black. Let's see why:

10.f4 exf4!

The simplest method for Black and one which White has been unable to find any advantage against. Back in the era of Fischer, Black would never have made this exchange, preferring 10...Qc7 with the idea of 11.f5 Bc4 . However, by the late seventies he was coming under some pressure in theresulting positions. This line has never regained its popularity but it may well not be so bad as we'll see in Jansa-Szekely, although after 15 Bg5 Black may well, just like after 10...exf4 , struggle to generate any winning chances.

11.Bxf4 Nc6

Black has reasonable central control

NOTE: Black is happy to accept a isolated d-pawn as it is well defended and because it supplies control over the very useful e5-square from where a bishop or knight will be well placed. It is important too not to forget that e4 is also isolated and can become weak as the game goes on, as can the white king with only a two pawn shield.

12.Kh1

By far White's main move which usefully sidesteps any checks on b6 and enables White to bring his b3-knight back into play with Nd4.

12...Rc8

Bringing the rook to a useful square and keeping the ...d5-break in reserve. If Black is after some rapid liquidation then he can prefer an immediate 12...d5 , but White keeps a small edge after 13.e5 due to 13...Ne4 14.Bd3 f5 15.exf6 Bxf6 16.Nxe4! dxe4 17.Bxe4 Bc4 18.Bd6! (Gallagher) and 13...Nd7 14.Nxd5 Ndxe5 15.c4! Bg5 16.Qd2 ,

Black is solid, but slightly worse

although clearly Black is pretty solid in the latter variation.

13.Qd2

13.Qe1 Ne8! 14.Rd1 Bh4 15.Qd2 Bf6 transposes, while the play after White's most popular move, 13.Nd4 , is also very similar as Dolmatov-Pigusov reveals.

13...Ne8!

Activating the dark-squared bishop

A key idea; first employed at the 1980 Malta Olympiad by both Nona Gaprindashvili and Lajos Portisch. The knight temporarily covers d6 and thus allows the dark-squared bishop to emerge from the passive e7-square to a position of importance on e5.

14.Rad1 Bf6 15.Qe3

Wisely not grabbing with 15.Bxd6?! when 15...Nxd6 16.Qxd6 Qxd6 17.Rxd6 Be5 18.Rd2 Nb4 (Kosten) leaves Black with queenside pressure allied to a centralised and strong bishop-pair.

15...Be5

TIP: White may not have played the most challenging system, but that doesn't mean that Black can afford to be sloppy. Immediately bringing the bishop to e5 is far better than 15...Qe7?! when 16.Nd5! Bxd5 17.exd5 gives White the edge as Black hasn't yet managed to unravel the e8-knight.

16.Bxe5 Nxe5 17.Nd4 Nf6

Black is fully equal

Black has a very solid position and should be fairly happy with it. An exchange on e6 would now enable him to cover d5, but he also is fine after 18.Nf5 Bxf5 19.Rxf5 Re8! , beginning to play against the e4-pawn.

Theoretical?

This old line is nowadays a rare guest in international chess, but at lower levels it hasn't fully died out. If Black is happy to exchange on f4 then a good understanding of Portisch's fine manoeuvre should suffice. However, if he still prefers to play Ã  la Fischer then both sides should ensure that they have some knowledge of the theory, as well as the typical positional motifs, as one error can quickly lead to a grim position.

Statistics

White has scored an average 51% from 240 games with 9.a4 Be6 10.f4 , but after 10...exf4 11.Bxf4 Nc6 12.Kh1 Rc8 that falls to just 44% from just under 100 games. Against 10...Qc7 , the critical 11.Kh1 Nbd7 12.f5 Bc4 13.a5 b5 14.axb6 Nxb6 15.Bg5 has still only received 19 tests, albeit with White scoring 63% from them.

Game 6
Jansa, V. - Szekely, P.
Keres Memorial, Tallinn 1984

[ECO "B92"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.a4 Be6 10.f4 Qc7 11.Kh1

A useful waiting move which enables the white dark-squared bishop to reach g5 in one go. Fischer's opponents usually placed their bishop on e3, but after 11.f5 Bc4 12.a5 Nbd7 13.Be3 b5! 14.axb6 Nxb6 Black has sufficient counterplay to offset the weakness of d5. 15.Bxb6?! ( 15.Kh1! was Geller's improvement, although after 15...Rfc8 16.Bxb6 Qxb6 17.Bxc4 Rxc4 18.Qe2 Rac8! 19.Ra2 Bd8! 20.Rfa1 Qb7 Karpov twice failed to prove any advantage as White in the early seventies) 15...Qxb6+ 16.Kh1 was used by both Tal and Unzicker against Fischer in 1962, but is well met by 16...Bb5! , intending 17...Bc6 when the bishop is very well-placed. After 17.Bxb5 axb5 18.Nd5 Nxd5 19.Qxd5 Ra4! Black stood better in both games; Tal (Curacao Candidates) was fortunate to draw whereas Unzicker (at the later Varna Olympiad) lasted just another seven moves: 20.c3 Qa6 21.h3 Rc8 22.Rfe1 h6 23.Kh2 Bg5 24.g3? Qa7! 25.Kg2 Ra2 26.Kf1 Rxc3! 0-1.

TIP: Always try to keep your king well protected by a pawn shield. An early advance of the f-pawn by White can have far reaching implications for his king later on as this drastic example demonstrates.

11...Nbd7 12.f5 Bc4 13.a5 b5 14.axb6 Nxb6 15.Bg5!

Fighting for control of d5 and this is more challenging than transposing into our last note with 15.Be3 .

15...Rfc8!

WARNING: Black must play as actively as possible on the queenside in this variation to offset the weakness of d5.

15...Rfd8? is too slow owing to 16.Bxf6 Bxf6 17.Bxc4 Qxc4 18.Na5! when the a-pawn is blockaded and cannot supply counterplay. After 18...Qc7 19.Qd3 Rd7 20.Rfd1 h6 21.Ra2! Bg5 22.Qe2 Qc5 23.Nd5! Rb8 24.b3 White's advantage continued to grow in L.Kavalek-P.Ostermeyer, Bundesliga 1977.

16.Bxc4 Nxc4 17.Bxf6 Bxf6 18.Nd5 Qd8 19.Ra2 a5!

Jansa has played very accurately thus far, but this advance of the a-pawn secures Black of just enough counterplay to hold the balance.

20.Nd2 Nb6 21.b3 a4 22.Nxb6 Qxb6 23.Nc4 Qc6 24.Qd3 axb3 25.Rxa8 Rxa8 26.cxb3

This ought to be an ideal ending for White with his theoretically superior minor piece and yet he isn't able to make any real progress. Quite simply b3 and e4 are as vulnerable as d5 and d6, and the active black major pieces keep White's tied down.

26...h6 27.Rd1 Be7 28.h3 Ra2 29.Qb1 Re2! 30.Re1 Rxe1+ 31.Qxe1 f6

Szekely has played well to equalise and now gains his draw. Black threatens to break with 32...d5 and after 31...f6 32.Na5 Qc2 33.Qb4 , to prevent that, 33...Qc1+ forces perpetual check.

Novosibirsk 1993

[ECO "B92"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.Kh1 Nc6 10.a4 Be6 11.f4 exf4 12.Bxf4 Rc8 13.Nd4

Immediately returning the knight to the heart of the battle, although Pigusov isn't too impressed as he exchanges knights before implementing Portisch's plan.

13...Nxd4 14.Qxd4 Ne8! 15.Rad1 Bf6

This is fine from an objective perspective, but it does allow White to force a fair amount of simplification. If Black is thus determined to win, he might prefer Kosten's suggestion of 14...Rc6 or here 15...Qa5 . White still has 16.e5 ( 16.Qd2 Bf6! is fine for Black as 17.Nd5 Qxd2 18.Rxd2 Bxd5 19.exd5 Bxb2 - King - picks up a pawn) 16...dxe5 17.Bxe5 , but the queens have stayed on and after 17...Nf6 18.Bd3?! Rfd8 19.Qf4 Ng4! Black had managed to seize the initiative in J.Khadilkar-N.Gaprindashvili, Thessaloniki Olympiad 1984.

16.e5!?

Dolmatov is a respected theoretician, but realising that he hasn't anything after 16.Qe3 Qa5 and ...Be5, hastens to reach a drawn ending.

16...dxe5 17.Qxd8 Bxd8 18.Bxe5 Bf6 19.Bxf6 Nxf6

The dust has settled to reveal that Black is still well coordinated. Pigusov now does his best to target the white queenside, but is hampered by the limited material and Dolmatov's technique.

20.Rf4 Rfd8 21.Rfd4 Rxd4 22.Rxd4 Kf8 23.Bf3 a5! 24.Rd6 Bf5 25.Kg1! Bxc2 26.Rd2 Bf5 27.Bxb7 Rb8 28.Bc6 Rb4 29.h3 Be6 30.Kf1 Ke7 31.Ke2 g5! 32.Ke3 h5 33.Bb5 h4 34.Be2
