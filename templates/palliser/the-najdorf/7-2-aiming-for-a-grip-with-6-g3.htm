Aiming for a Grip with 6 g3

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.g3

A positional choice

Whereas 6.f4 was tricky because of its direct intent, 6.g3 is tricky as it is all too easy for Black to quickly find himself positionally much worse. This variation is at least as positional in nature as 6.Be2 and, although fairly rare, must not be underestimated, while it has also received some attention and an injection of new ideas of late. Black must be alert during the opening moves to various nuances and especially to White's aiming to gain a grip on the position. However, by combining some typical Najdorf motifs and being prepared to delay developing his kingside, Black can gain a perfectly acceptable position.

6...e5

This shouldn't come as a surprise, especially here where it makes it rather hard for White to ever open the long diagonal for his light-squared bishop. One reason why 6.g3 may not be more popular is that here White must also be happy to play against a Scheveningen ( 6...e6 ) and Dragon ( 6...g6 ) having already committed himself to a fianchetto.

7.Nde2

Making use of the bishop's going to g2 and planning to increase White's light-square control with h3, g4 and Ng3. An important alternative is 7.Nb3 ,

d5 remains a key square

a favourite of Sergei Movsesian's. Black usually hurries to fianchetto his queen's bishop as he does in Socko-Karjakin, although he can also play the position along the lines of the Karpov Approach from Chapter One. However, after 7...Be7 8.Bg2 O-O 9.O-O Be6 10.a4 , White's light-squared bishop is better placed on g2, controlling d5, than on e2 and White is slightly for preference after 10...Nbd7 11.a5 Qc7 12.Nd5! Nxd5 13.exd5 Bf5 14.c3 .

7...Nbd7

Delaying moving the b-pawn; not because of 7...b5 8.a4 Bb7! , but because 8.Bg5 Nbd7 9.Nd5 is a little annoying.

8.Bg2

An important alternative is 8.a4 when Black should proceed in similar vein with 8...b6 9.Bg2 Bb7 10.h3 Rc8 .

NOTE: Black delays moving the f8-bishop because he would like to meet g4 and Ng3 with ...g6 when his dark-squared bishop would prefer to be on g7, rather than e7.

11.g4 Nc5 12.Ng3 sees White implement his standard plan, but after 12...g6! 13.O-O Ne6

Hampering White's intentions

Black has a good position. The e6-knight covers the important f4 and g5 squares, while Black doesn't have to immediately exchange on d5 after 14.Nd5 and should prefer 14...Bg7 . With g4 and Ng3 White wants to steadily advance on the kingside, but that has been prevented and White is left without a good plan.

8...b5 9.h3

Preparing kingside expansion

White can also use the move-order 9.O-O Bb7 10.h3 as was advocated by Nigel Davies in his Taming the Sicilian. Thus this system for White with 7.Nde2 may well already be becoming popular at club level and it is certainly quite easy to play. After 10...Be7 Black can easily find himself lacking play or that any queenside advance actually rebounds on him as it did on Kasparov in Spangenberg-Kasparov. Black should thus again not rush to develop his king's bishop; an important concept which Davies rather neglects. Instead of 9.O-O Bb7 10.h3 Be7 , Black can, by analogy with the main line, prefer to improve his d7-knight or aim to exploit White's move-order with 10...Rc8!? . The latter looks especially promising because 11.g4?! is well met by 11...b4! 12.Nd5 Nxd5 13.exd5 Qc7 (Kosten) when 14.c3 bxc3 15.bxc3 badly compromises the white queenside, but after 15.Nxc3 , the absence of the knight from g3 leaves the white kingside a little loose.

9...Bb7 10.g4 Nb6!

Threatening 11...d5

Once again Black disrupts White from his steady kingside build-up and hopes that White will later regret weakening his kingside. Instead 10...Be7?! 11.Ng3 b4 12.Nd5 Nxd5 13.exd5 O-O 14.O-O is another route into Spangenberg-Kasparov, but a good alternative is again 10...Nc5 11.Ng3 g6 , intending ...Ne6 and ...Bg7.

11.Nd5

Preventing ...d5, but Ghaem Maghami has preferred the aggressive 11.g5!? Nfd7 12.Ng3 . Black should then challenge White's kingside space with Arizmendi and Moreno's suggestion of 12...h6!? , intending 13.gxh6 g6! , and 13.h4 hxg5 14.hxg5 Rxh1+ 15.Bxh1 g6 is also comfortable for Black who will tie White to g5 with ...Be7, while the black king will be quite safe in the centre or on g7.

11...Nfxd5 12.exd5

The b6-knight is well-placed monitoring the a4 and c4 squares, while White's kingside play isn't left looking too dangerous as Black will exchange pawns after any f4-break. Black now enjoys two promising continuations:

(a) 12...h5!? 13.g5 seals up the kingside and effectively ends any attacking hopes White may have had. After Yuferov's 13...g6!

Black is for choice

and 14...Bg7 , the black dark-square bishop is well-placed and Black will attack on the queenside with good prospects in store.

(b) 12...Be7 13.O-O O-O 14.b3 Rc8 15.Be3 Bg5! prepared a favourable exchange of bishops and left Black very solid and fully equal in S.Movsesian-M.Palac, Croatian Team Ch. 2003.

Theoretical?

Whether White opts for 7.Nde2 or for 7.Nb3 , he is playing with a clear plan in mind. This variation isn't very theoretical but understanding the key principles, such as delaying ...Be7 after 7.Nde2 , is important.

Statistics

White has scored 51% from just over 1000 games with 6.g3 e5 7.Nde2 . 7...Nbd7 8.Bg2 b5 9.h3 Bb7 10.g4 Nb6 is still only really being discovered but has started well with a whopping 79% from 7 outings for Black! 7.Nb3 is similar for White with 52% from 367 games, but Black should expect to see more of Movsesian's 7...Nbd7 8.Bg2 as Black has only managed 39% from 19 games after 8...b5 9.O-O Be7 .

Game 56
Socko,B - Karjakin,S [B91]
Calvia Olympiad 2004

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.g3 e5 7.Nb3 Be7

As we have seen, 7...Nbd7 is slightly more flexible when the older move is 8.a4 . Black should again first develop his queenside with 8...b6 9.Bg2 Bb7 10.O-O (or 10.Nd2 Rc8! preparing to destroy White's centre after 11.Nf1?! Rxc3! , whereas 11.O-O Be7 12.Re1 transposes) 10...Rc8 . Now 11.Nd5 Nxd5 12.exd5 g6 vindicates Black's decision to leave his bishop on f8 and so White usually prefers 11.Re1 Be7 12.Nd2 when Black must be careful. White is now threatening to gain a grip on the position and especially d5 with 13.Nf1 and 14.Bg5 , preparing Bxf6 and Ne3. 12...Rc5! 13.Nf1 Qa8 is the way to respond when a thematic exchange sacrifice is again threatened. 14.Ne3!? is an interesting idea, striving for the initiative ( 14.Qd3 wisely covers c3 and e4, but Black is fine after either either Kosten's 14...Rc7!? , preparing 15...Nc5 , and 14...O-O 15.Ne3 Rfc8! when 16.Nf5 Bf8 weakens White's control over d5 and 17.Nxd6? Rxc3 18.bxc3 Nc5 19.Qd1 Rd8 wins material) but it doesn't quite convince after the cool 14...Nxe4! 15.Nf5 Nxc3 16.Bxb7 Qxb7 17.bxc3 g6! of P.Popovic-D.King, Palma de Mallorca 1989.

TIP: Returning an extra pawn to eliminate the opponent's initiative is often the best way to make use of it! Here 18.Nxd6+ Bxd6 19.Qxd6 Qc6 ends White's aggressive intentions and leaves him worse due to his inferior structure.

It's worth just seeing a few more moves as King continued to play excellent practical chess with 18.Nxe7 Kxe7 19.Ba3 Rhc8! and after 20.Bxc5 Rxc5 21.Qd2 Qc6 Black's superior coordination and structure left him clearly better.

8.Bg2 b5 9.O-O Nbd7 10.Bd2

White wants to play on the queenside, but he can also do so with 10.a4 b4 11.Na2!? a5 12.c3 as Adams has employed against Topalov. However, after 12...Ba6!? 13.Re1 bxc3 14.Nxc3 O-O 15.Bd2 Bc4! 16.Nb5 Qb6 Black was at least holding his own on the queenside in M.Dutreeuw-P.H.Nielsen, European Team Ch., Gothenburg 2005, especially after 17.Bf1 d5! when he shortly seized a powerful initiative.;

Prior to playing 10.Bd2 , Movsesian tried 10.Nd5 against Kasparov in his 2001 Prague simul against the Czech national side. Unlike in our next illustrative game, Kasparov gained a good position and succeeded in thwarting White's aims with 10...Nxd5 11.exd5 a5! 12.a4 b4 13.Bd2 Bg5! , forcing White to weaken his position with 14.f4 Bf6 so that he could play c3 and recapture with his bishop.

10...O-O 11.a4

White's queenside play after this isn't too troublesome for Karjakin to handle, but 11.Re1 Nb6 12.a4 Bg4! , trying to provoke the weakening 13.f3 , was also very comfortable for Black in S.Movsesian-B.Gelfand, Budapest 2003.

11...b4 12.Nd5 Nxd5 13.exd5 a5 14.c3 bxc3 15.Bxc3

Thus Socko has achieved his main aim, but it turns out that Black can actually defend a5 with some ease.

15...Qb6! 16.Ra3 f5 17.Re1?!

17.Qd2 Bd8 18.Rc1 was a more logical way of getting White's major pieces into play, but after 18...Bb7 Black is still slightly for choice and intends to target f2 with ...Qa7 and ...Bb6.

17...Bd8 18.Nd2 Ba6!

Preventing 19.Nc4 and gaining a useful tempo, while White is already in quite serious trouble. Socko is unable to find anything better than moving his knight back and forth, but 19.Bf1 was hardly an improvement when the exchange of bishops further weakens d5 and the white kingside.

19.Nb3 Bb7 20.Nd2 Rc8 21.Nb3 Qa7 22.Qd2 Ra8

Keeping matters simple, but 22...f4!? 23.Bxa5 Bg5 must also have been rather tempting, preparing to destroy the white king's pawn shield.

23.Nc1 Nf6 24.b4 Ne4!

White has been completely outplayed and now, with the f-file opening up, his days are already numbered.

25.Bxe4 fxe4 26.bxa5 Bg5! 27.Qa2

The advance of the e-pawn now decides, just as it also would have after 27.Qe2 Ba6 28.Qb2 e3 .

27...e3 28.f3 e2+ 29.Kg2 Rxf3! 30.Rxe2

Karjakin finishes in style as 30.Kxf3 Qe3+ 31.Kg2 Qe4+ forces mate.

30...Bxc1 31.Kxf3 Bxa3

Game 57
Spangenberg,H - Kasparov,G [B91]
Buenos Aires Simul 1997

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.g3 e5 7.Nde2 Be7

This remains surprisingly common even though Black has been struggling after it for a number of years.

8.Bg2 b5 9.O-O Bb7 10.h3 Nbd7 11.g4

Preparing a kingside attack and Kasparov hastens to gain counterplay, but Spangenberg is quick to pinpoint the downside to Black's next.

11...b4 12.Nd5! Nxd5 13.exd5 O-O 14.Ng3 Re8

This has been considered the 'main line', but unsurprisingly many grandmasters no longer wish to enter this as Black and so delay developing their king's bishop. Kasparov prepares to meet 15.Nf5 with 15...Bf8 , but 14...g6 is an alternative. However, after 15.Bh6 Re8 16.Qd2 a5 17.f4! , Black came under some pressure in S.Kindermann-I.Stohl, Trnava 1987.

15.a3! a5

Black is also left with a weak queenside pawn after 15...bxa3 16.Rxa3 Nb6 when 17.Rb3 Bc8 18.f4! left White with prospects on both flanks and an edge in S.Kindermann-C.Lutz, German Ch., Gladenbach 1997.

16.Be3 Ba6?! 17.Re1 Qb8 18.axb4 axb4 19.b3!

Fixing the weakness and now Spangenberg is able to build up on the queenside. Black is already desperately low on any counterplay and, although this was a clock simultaneous against the Argentinean national team, Kasparov has rarely been so comprehensively outplayed.

19...Rc8 20.Qd2 Nc5 21.Ra2 Bb7 22.Rea1 Rxa2 23.Rxa2 Ba8 24.Ne4 Nd7

Black will just about be able to avoid losing his b-pawn after this, but he would also have remained under heavy pressure after 24...Nxe4 25.Bxe4 Rc3 26.Qe2! g6 27.Qa6 .

25.Ra4 Qc7 26.Ra7! Qd8 27.Ng3 Bf8?

A blunder, albeit in a rather grim position, while this was Kasparov's only poor performance in both rounds of the simul. Kasparov decided that it wasn't worth playing on after this as he has nothing to counter the gradual advance of the white queenside pawns. He had instead to cling on to b4 with 27...Rb8 , intending 28.Ra4 Qc8 . White should now bring his knight to f5 and slowly increase the pressure as the b4-weakness is hardly about to run away, whereas the materialistic and weakening 28.f4 Bf8 29.fxe5 Nxe5 30.Ra4 Qc8 31.Be4 allows some counterplay with 31...h5! .
