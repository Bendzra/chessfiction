White's Rare Options

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6

White has several minor options

6.f4 and 6.g3 are both important systems and are also quite common, but we now come to White's rather unusual options. These are all fairly rare, but they have their merits and Black should understand White's aims with each.

NOTE: Black should especially beware moving his e-pawn in response to some of these unusual lines. Often doing so is fine, but Black may consider that it plays slightly into White's hands and that he prefers an alternative system of development.

The 6.a4 of Van der Wiel-Hoelzl rules out ...b5 and also aims, with 6...e5 7.Nf3 , to reach an improved version of 6.Be3 e5 7.Nf3 . 6.a4 may traditionally be the most common of these rare variations, but 6.Rg1!? has received quite a bit of attention over the past seven or so years. White aims to play by analogy with the Keres Attack ( 5...e6 6.g4 ) by quickly advancing his g-pawn as we'll see in Fontaine-Relange where the related 6.h3 is also examined. That leaves:

(a) 6.Bd3 enables the d4-knight to retreat to e2, but gives Black a reasonable choice:

(a1) 6...g6 reaches a Dragon-type position in which the bishop is a little unusually placed on d3, although 7.f4 should transpose into Games 51 and 52.

(a2) 6...Nc6!? also exploits a downside to White's last and that 7.Be3 is well met by 7...Ng4! . Instead both 7.Nb3 b5 and 7.Nxc6 bxc6 8.O-O g6 are quite pleasant for Black. In the latter case, he no longer has to worry about the d5-square and 9.f4 Bg7 10.Qe1 O-O 11.Qh4 Nd7!

White is starting to struggle

leaves Black for choice. White's attack isn't going anywhere with 12...e6 next up, whereas Black has good chances on the queenside and can advance with ...a5-a4.

(a3) 6...e5 7.Nde2 has been considered a little suspect for Black due to 7...Nbd7 8.O-O b5?! 9.Bg5 Be7 10.b4! O-O 11.a4! with a useful queenside initiative for White. However, 7...Be7 8.O-O O-O

Black has wisely delayed ...b5

is fine for Black. 9.Be3 b5! is now a good choice due to 10.b4?! Bb7 11.a4 d5! , whereas 9.f4!? Nbd7 10.Kh1 b5!? 11.Ng3 g6 12.f5 b4 13.Nd5 Nxd5 14.exd5 Nf6 15.Bh6 Bb7! was a thematic exchange sacrifice to safeguard the black monarch and to pick up a central pawn in J.Polgar-R.Ponomariov, Moscow (rapid) 2002.

(b) 6.Qf3!? races the queen to an aggressive square and 6...e6 7.Be3 has received some attention from Michael Adams and in the United States. Black should probably respond flexibly with 7...Qc7 when 8.O-O-O Nbd7 9.Qg3 b5

Commencing counterplay

10.a3 Rb8! left White wishing he hadn't castled quite so quickly in E.Tate-Xu Jun, Las Vegas 2001.

Theoretical?

Not at all. White generally employs these lines to avoid theory and to try to quickly lure Black on to unfamiliar territory.

Statistics

6.a4 has astonishingly been played in more than 2600 games! It has also scored 51%, although White's score falls to 48% from 160 games after 6...Nc6!? 7.Be2 e5 . 6.Rg1 's burst of fashion now appears to be waning, but Black should not treat this aggressive move lightly and White has made 56% from 234 games with it. Black's best defences have, however, now been worked out and he has scored well with 6...b5 (53%) and with 6...e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.g4 d5! (57%).

White's Rare Options: Illustrative Games

Game 58
Van der Wiel,J - Hoelzl,F [B90]
Austrian Team Championship 1997

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.a4 Nc6!?

Black still wishes to play ...e5, but delays it because for a long time 6...e5 7.Nf3 was considered to favour White. However, that was largely because Black continued 7...Qc7 when White was able to play 8.Bg5 in one move. Black should instead prefer 7...Be7 when the industrious Spaniards, Arizmendi and Moreno, have shown that the black position is quite playable:

8.Bg5 ( 8.Bc4 Be6 neutralises the diagonal and 9.Qe2 O-O 10.O-O Qc7 11.Ba2 Nc6! preparing ...Nb4, exploiting a downside to 6.a4 to cover d5, was about equal in S.Marjanovic-M.Chandler, Nis 1983) 8...Be6 9.Bxf6 Bxf6 10.Nd5 seizes the d5-square, but Black can regain control of it with 10...Nd7 11.Bc4 Nb6 12.Bb3 Nxd5 13.Bxd5 Qa5+! when White will be left with a pawn on d5 and the remaining black bishop is in no way inferior to the white knight.;

Along with 6...Nc6!? , 6...g6 is a fairly popular choice, reaching a Dragon in which White won't be rushing to castle long. After 7.Be2 Bg7 8.O-O Nc6 9.Be3 O-O 10.f4 a sort of Classical Variation has arisen, but Black must be aware of an important difference due to the inclusion of a4 and ...a6.

WARNING: 10...Qb6? is the normal response in the Dragon proper, but here 11.a5! is a strong response. 11...Nxa5 12.e5! is rather strong and 11...Qxb2 12.Na4 Qb4 13.c3 Qxa5 14.Nxc6 bxc6 15.Bb6 traps the black queen. Black should thus prefer 10...Rb8 or 10...Bg4!? when play normally continues 11.Nb3 Qc8 as 11.Nxc6 Bxe2 12.Nxe7+ Qxe7 13.Qxe2 Rae8 regains the pawn on e4 with equality.

7.Be2

White's main move, but he can also transpose to a 6.Bc4 sideline with 7.Bc4 or prefer 7.f4 . Now 7...g6 is tempting, because the Levenfish Attack ( 6.f4 in the Dragon) does not have a good theoretical reputation, but 7...Bg4 8.Nf3 g6 9.h3 Bxf3 10.Qxf3 Bg7 11.Bd3 O-O 12.O-O Nd7! was also fine for Black, and a good version of Game 52, in Ye Jiangchuan-B.Gelfand, Yrevan Olympiad 1996.

7...e5

With White's light-squared bishop committed to e2, Black plays his favourite central advance.

8.Nxc6

Van der Wiel continues with a move which retains some independence for 6.a4 , whereas 8.Nb3 Be7 9.O-O O-O 10.f4 exf4 11.Bxf4 Be6 12.Kh1 Rc8 transposes into Game 6.

8...bxc6 9.f4 a5!

A strong idea of Rashkovsky's; Black doesn't mind an exchange of queens due to his control of the d5 square and prevents White from gaining an effective queenside clamp.

10.fxe5?!

Critical, but this fails to convince, although 10.O-O Be7 11.Kh1 O-O 12.Qe1 Nd7! (Gallagher) is fine for Black.

10...dxe5 11.Qxd8+ Kxd8 12.Bg5

White has also tried 12.Bc4 , but after 12...Bb4!? 13.Bg5 Ke7 14.O-O-O Bxc3 15.bxc3 Be6 16.Bxe6 Kxe6 17.Bxf6 gxf6 Black clearly had no problems in Jo.Hodgson-R.Palliser, Nottingham 2000 where it transpired that the white pawns were slightly the more vulnerable.

12...Be7

Hoelzl doesn't want to damage his structure and he is fine after this, but 12...Be6! appears even better. It would be interesting to know what leading 6.a4 specialist Van der Wiel has in mind after this as 13.Nd1?! Rb8 14.Ne3 Bb4+! 15.Kf2 Bc5 16.b3 Kc7 17.Bxf6 gxf6 18.Bd3 f5! saw the position open up to the huge delight of the black bishop-pair in D.King-N.Rashkovsky, Cappelle la Grande 1990.

13.O-O-O+ Ke8 14.Bc4

Taking control of the diagonal, but White remains unable to make any inroads down the d-file as Hoelzl sensibly makes some exchanges.

14...Ng4! 15.Bxe7 Kxe7 16.Rd2 Ra7! 17.Nd1! Rd7 18.Rxd7+ Bxd7 19.h3 Nf6 20.Nf2

Van der Wiel has improved his knight and is now within striking distance of gaining an edge, but Black is able to cover e5 in time.

20...Ne8 21.Nd3 f6 22.Nc5 Nd6

With e4 a little weak, Black is happy to play a knight against bishop ending. White thus doesn't want to exchange on d7, but lacks a good alternative.

Game 59
Fontaine,R - Relange,E [B90]
Clichy 1998

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Rg1!?

Black can still reply to this with 6...e5 , but he should not do so to 6.h3 as 7.Nde2 , followed by g4 and Ng3 gives White a souped up 6.g3 variation. Instead 6...e6 7.g4 is a Keres Attack in which White has played the rather slow h3. Black should exploit that with 7...d5! , energetically opening the centre. 8.e5? Nfd7 leaves White without a good way to defend e5 as 9.Bf4 Qc7 10.Qe2 Ba3! (Kosten) is rather awkward and so he usually prefers 8.exd5 Nxd5 9.Nde2 . Black should now aim to catch up in development, while neutralising any pressure down the long diagonal and, for example, 9...Bb4! 10.Bd2 Nxc3 11.Bxc3 Qxd1+ 12.Rxd1 Bxc3+ 13.Nxc3 Bd7 14.Bg2 Bc6 gave him full equality in R.Fontaine-L.Fressinet, French Ch., Val d'Isere 2002.

6...e5

One of the reasons for the interest in 6.Rg1 at the turn of the millenium was that this was considered to allow a promising pawn sacrifice. However, it is actually far from promising, while Black has another good option here in 6...b5 when 7.g4 Bb7 8.Bg2 e6 9.Qe2?! Nfd7! 10.Be3 b4 11.Nb1 e5 12.Nb3 a5 gave him an excellent version of an English Attack in C.Philippe-J.Lautier, Paris 2000.

7.Nb3 Be6 8.g4 d5!

TIP: The old adage that 'a flank attack is best met by a central break' is often true in the Najdorf and Black should always be on the look-out to make ...d5 work.

9.g5?!

Sacrificing a pawn, but the black king isn't especially troubled by it. However, after 9.exd5 Nxd5 10.Nxd5 Qxd5 White may well later still regret weakening his kingside and 11.Be3 Qxd1+ 12.Rxd1 Nd7 13.Bg2 O-O-O 14.Bd5 Nc5 was fairly even in L.Dominguez-A.Naiditsch, Wijk aan Zee 2004.

9...Nxe4 10.Nxe4 dxe4 11.Qxd8+ Kxd8 12.Be3

White hopes to be able to regain his pawn and that g5 will cramp the black kingside. However, that pawn can be used to begin counterplay, such as with 12.Bg2 Kc7!? 13.Bxe4 h6! when 14.gxh6 g6 leaves Black with the superior structure.

12...Nd7 13.O-O-O Kc7 14.Bg2 h6!

Black isn't able to maintain the extra pawn, but has reached a reasonable position and now seizes the kingside initiative.

15.gxh6 g6 16.Bxe4 Bxh6 17.Bxh6 Rxh6 18.Rg3! Rc8! 19.Bd5

Fontaine may well have relied on this to gain some activity and to get back into the game, but Relange now comes up with an enterprising temporary sacrifice to maintain the initiative.

19...Bf5!? 20.Bxf7 Kb8 21.c3 Nf6

Black's kingside pressure ensures that he will regain the pawn and White should find a way to bring his knight back into play. Instead Fontaine targets g6, but underestimates the attacking strength of the black bishop.

22.Rdg1? Nh5! 23.Re3 Nf4 24.Rd1 Rxh2

White has covered d3, but now f2 is dropping off and the white bishop has to scurry back to prevent the threats along the second.

25.Bd5 Rxf2 26.Be4 Ng2! 27.Rf3 Rxf3 28.Bxf3 Nf4

With two mobile passed pawns and a large check on d3 looming, the ending is winning without too much difficulty.
