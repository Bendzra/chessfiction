White Attacks with 6 f4

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.f4

An aggressive choice

This was John Nunn's recommendation against the Najdorf in his three Beating the Sicilian books. White seizes space, threatens 7.e5 and prepares a kingside attack, while the system isn't especially difficult to play. Unsurprisingly it thus became popular during the eighties and, although now rare amongst 2600s, it is still seen quite often in all levels of swiss tournament.

Black should be as ready for 6.f4 as he is for the likes of 6.Be3 and 6.Be2 , but he can gain good counterplay, due to White's slight weaknesses on e4 and down the a7-g1 diagonal. Initially Black tried a number of sharp responses to White's aggression, but these days he tends to prefer one of three systems which we will explore. Two of these are fairly solid and easy to learn, revolving around some important positional principles.

6...e5

Fighting for central control and preparing to meet 7.Nf5 with 7...d5! , while Black's main alternative (transposing to a Scheveningen with 6...e6 is also a reasonable choice, but is once again outside the scope of this book) is to control e5 with 6...Qc7 . He will then follow up by fianchettoing on the kingside; a plan which may appear to permit White a ready-made kingside attack with Qe1-h4 and f5. However, the black position is actually pretty resilient and Black can certainly hope to prove that any attack leaves White overextended. We consider this approach in Almasi-Lalic and in Al Modiahki-Anand.

7.Nf3 Nbd7

Securing the e5-square

TIP: Black should always aim to maintain control of e5 in this variation. That helps to keep control of the centre and ...exf4 is often a possibility; an exchange which enables the e4-pawn to be pressurized and sometimes a knight can make use of e5.

Unable to blast through in the centre, White must decide how to develop his light-squared bishop. He usually restrains Black's queenside counterplay and then places it on d3, bolstering e4, but an immediate 8.Bd3 remains surprisingly common and should be met by 8...b5 as we'll see in Gormally-Palliser. There we will also see a more aggressive development with 8.Bc4 , but once again Black is then able to gain counterplay by targeting the bishop.

8.a4! Be7 9.Bd3

With ...b5 restrained, the alternative is 9.Bc4 against which Black should renew the threat and attack e4 with 9...Qa5! .

Early counterplay

This may appear risky in view of 10.Bd2 , but Black is ready with 10...exf4 , intending 11.Nd5 Qc5 and 11.Qe2!? O-O 12.Nd5 Qd8 13.Bxf4 Nxd5 14.Bxd5 Nf6 is also fine for him. However, neither did 10.Qe2 cause Black any problems after 10...O-O 11.O-O exf4! 12.Bxf4 Ne5 in P.Leko-G.Kasparov, Sarajevo 1999.

NOTE: As usual in the 6...e5 variation, Black would here have been happy to see an exchange on e5, freeing his e7-bishop, while any pressure down the f-file can be soaked up without any major difficulties.

Leko thus tried 13.Nd5 , but after 13...Nxd5 14.Bxd5 Be6 15.Bxe6 Nxf3+ 16.Qxf3 fxe6 17.Qb3 Qc5+ , the position was pretty level.

9...O-O 10.O-O Nc5

Unravelling by making use of the tactic 11.fxe5 dxe5 12.Nxe5?? Qd4+ . In view of that, and to avoid any checks, White usually now tucks his king away into the corner. Black does, however, have an uncompromising alternative in 10...exf4 , intending 11.Bxf4 Qb6+ , but White often prefers to enter the equally complex 11.Kh1 Nh5 ; a favourite of Gelfand's and the subject of Polgar-Gelfand.

11.Kh1 exf4 12.Bxf4 Bg4!

The bishop heads for g6

This clear-cut plan has now almost fully superseded the once popular, but rather risky, 11...d5 . Black's plan is to simply thwart White's kingside ambitions by bringing his bishop all the way round to g6. It then usefully blocks the g-file, covers the f5-square and pressurizes e4. White's most challenging ideas against this plan are 13.Be3!? and 13.Qd2 as will become apparent in Sokolov-Gallagher, but it is also useful to know what happens if White simply continues with his kingside build-up.

13.Qe1 Rc8

A useful move as Black waits for the queen to continue to g3, but instead 14.Nd4 Qb6! was a little irritating for White in J.Polgar-V.Anand, Buenos Aires 1994. White was, however, hoping for 13...Bh5 when 14.Nh4! prepares to exchange on g6 and Black can't respond with 14...Nfxe4? as 15.Nf5 wins a piece down the e-file.

14.Qg3 Bh5

15.Bh6 will now be foiled by 15...Bg6 , but Black has a good position in any case:

(a) 15.Nd4 Bg6 16.Nf5 Bxf5 17.exf5

The attack is dying out

is an exchange Black is happy to make as White can't recapture with the rook and so must permit the f-file to be closed. Now 17...Nh5!? 18.Qg4 Nxf4 19.Qxf4 Bf6 is promising, although 17...Re8 left White with nothing better than to repeat with 18.Bh6 Bf8 19.Bg5 Be7 in P.Gayson-R.Palliser, British Rapidplay Ch., Leeds 2001.

(b) 15.Rae1 Bg6 16.Bc1?! , avoiding ...Nh5 and ...Qb6 ideas, was Gayson's unfortunate 'improvement' as 16...d5

More than a liberating break

was rather strong in P.Gayson-R.Palliser, Leeds 2004, with Black netting a pawn after 17.e5 Nfe4 18.Bxe4 Nxe4 19.Nxe4 dxe4 20.Rd1 Qa5 .

(c) White might thus regroup by bringing his bishop to d4, although after 15.Be3 Bg6 16.Bd4 , Arizmendi and Moreno point out that Black can once again exploit his central control to gain a comfortable position with 16...d5! 17.Bxf6 Bxf6 18.e5 Nxd3 19.cxd3 Be7 .

Theoretical?

6.f4 couldn't be described as an especially theoretical choice, but there is still some theory. Black should be aware of the general aims for both sides and also some key nuances, such as when to complete the manoeuvre to g6 after 12...Bg4 . He appears to have a good choice between three different approaches, although pawn grabbing with 10...exf4 is certainly the most theoretical option.

Statistics

With 6.f4 e5 7.Nf3 Nbd7 Black has scored a reasonable 50% from just over 1300 games. That rises to 52% from 50-odd games with 10...exf4 11.Bxf4 Qb6+ , while 11.Kh1!? Nh5 scores 55% from a similar number of games. Thus the sharp course is currently in good shape, whereas Black mustn't relax and assume that the hard work is done after 10...Nc5 and 12...Bg4 , as his 48% from 137 games reveals. 6...Qc7 7.Nf3 Nbd7 is also a good choice, although a number of poor defences and drastic defeats reduce Black's score to just 46% after both 8.a4 g6 9.Bd3 Bg7 10.O-O O-O and 8.Bd3 g6 9.O-O Bg7 10.Qe1 b5 11.e5!? .

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.f4 Qc7 7.Bd3

Threatening e5 once again and bringing the knight over to the kingside in preparation for an attack there. White can also first restrain ...b5 with 7.a4 , but after 7...g6 8.Bd3 Bg7 he must play 9.Nf3 ; the subject of our next illustrative game.

TIP: White is slightly vulnerable down the a7-g1 diagonal; very vulnerable though after 9.O-O? Qb6! , winning a piece. White may think he has that covered with 10.Kh1 Qxd4 11.Bb5+ , but after 11...Nfd7 the trapper becomes the trapped.;

After 7.Nf3 , White's attack is fairly easy to play, but he should also consider the aggressive 7.Qf3!? when Black should abandon his intended fianchetto and prefer 7...e5! ( 7...g6 8.Be3 Bg7?! 9.h3! left Black very quickly in trouble on the kingside in V.Ivanchuk-G.Kasparov, Amsterdam 1994 after 9...e5 10.fxe5 dxe5 11.Bh6! ) 8.Nb3 Be7 9.Bd3 O-O 10.O-O and now, not 10...Nbd7? 11.g4 when White gets into d5, but 10...b5! (Gallagher) with a reasonable position.

7...g6 8.O-O Bg7 9.Nf3 Nbd7 10.Qe1!?

The most ambitious try, although White could still restrain ...b5 and transpose into Al Modiahki-Anand with 10.a4 .

10...b5!

WARNING: When White has saved a tempo on a4, his attack is too fast should Black castle into it: 10...O-O? 11.Qh4 b5 12.f5 is very strong. One idea being simply an exchange on g6, Ng5, Be3, Rf3 and Raf1 which is far from easy to prevent.

11.e5!

With Lalic delaying castling, White needs a plan and so Almasi aims to blast through in the centre, using a pawn sacrifice from the second and third editions of 'Beating the Sicilian'. White could also pass with 11.a3 , but after 11...Bb7 12.Kh1 O-O 13.Qh4 e6! , Black's extra queenside space gives him a slightly improved version of the main line after 7.a4 .

11...dxe5 12.fxe5 Ng4 13.e6 fxe6 14.Qh4

White also gains reasonable compensation after 14.Qe4!? Qa7+! 15.Kh1 , but just as in our main game the black position isn't actually that loose. After 15...Ndf6 16.Qe2 O-O 17.Bd2 Kh8 Black was hoping for the weakening 18.h3? , driving his knight towards a good square on f5, in J.Hall-J.Lautier, Malmo 1999. Hall instead carried out White's main plan of weakening the black queenside and after 18.a4! b4 19.Ne4 Nxe4 20.Qxe4 Lautier had to continue to play accurately, but did with 20...e5! 21.Qe1 Bf5 22.h3 Bxd3 23.cxd3 e4! 24.dxe4 Ne5 25.Nxe5 when a draw was agreed before the b-pawns and lots of pieces were traded off.

14...Nde5! 15.Nxe5 Nxe5 16.Be4 Bb7 17.Bh6

White's problem here is that Black has some quite active pieces which it is natural to exchange, but doing so makes a draw rather likely. 17.Bxb7 Qxb7 18.Bh6 Bxh6 19.Qxh6 Qb6+ 20.Kh1 Ng4 (Lalic) is also fine for Black after 21.Qg7 (or 21.Qh4 Nf6 ) 21...O-O-O! . 14.Qe4!? thus appears a better way to keep the tension and to force Black to find some accurate moves.

17...Bxh6 18.Qxh6 Bxe4 19.Nxe4 Ng4! 20.Qf4

The queens must come off as 20.Qh4? h5! would leave Black the side pressing.

20...Qxf4 21.Rxf4 Ne3 22.c3 e5

After 22...e5 23.Rf3 Nc4 24.b3 Nd6 , White can win back his pawn but the resulting rook ending is drawn, such as after 25.Nc5!? e4! 26.Re3 O-O (Almasi).

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.f4 Qc7 7.Nf3 Nbd7

Wisely preventing 8.e5 and now Al Modiahki decides that it's worth a tempo to restrain Black's intended queenside expansion.

8.a4 g6 9.Bd3 Bg7 10.O-O O-O

Anand is happy to let White attack when a double-edged battle ensues. White's direct attacking chances should never be underestimated, but neither should the resourceful black defences be by White. However, Black can also try to delay castling with 10...b6 11.Qe1 Bb7 , intending 12.Kh1 Nc5 , although White can reply with another unclear sacrifice in the shape of 12.e5!? .

11.Kh1

Tucking the king away is usual in this variation and avoids any checks on c5 later on, when the black queen may usefully be able to come to e5 to defend or to counterattack with ...Qb4.

11...b6 12.Qe2

White is happy to just to complete his development, but this does risk being left without a good plan. Usually White goes for broke on the kingside with 12.Qe1! Bb7 13.Qh4 when 13...e6! is a key resource to help break up the attack. After 14.f5 Rae8 , keeping the other rook on f8 for defensive purposes, Black is very solid, but his position also contains a fair amount of dynamism along Scheveningen and Hedgehog lines. Nf4 23.Rf1 h6 24.Nf3 Nxe4! made great use of the potentially powerful b7-prelate) 15...Qc5 activates the queen and she may even go to b4. In C.Wagener-L.Dominguez, Bled Olympiad 2002 Black defended well with 16.Ng5 exf5!? 17.exf5 Re5! when 18.Rf4?! Qe3 19.Raf1 Nh5! 20.Bxg7 Kxg7 21.R4f2 Ndf6 left Black fairly active, well coordinated and still solid enough on the kingside.

12...Bb7

Preventing 13.e5 as Black would then exchange on f3 and Al Modiahki soon commences queenside operations. However, the black position remains quite flexible and Anand correctly responds in the centre.

13.Bd2 Nc5 14.b4!? Ncd7! 15.a5 b5 16.Rac1! Rae8! 17.Nd1

Forcing through c4 as the game quickly livens up.

17...e5 18.c4 bxc4 19.Rxc4?

Allowing Black to liquidate the centre with a neat resource after which Anand is rather well coordinated. White should thus have taken his chances in the very unclear position after 19.Bxc4 Qb8 20.Bxa6 Bxa6 21.Qxa6 Nxe4 22.Rb1! (Anand) when his centre has collapsed, but Black must act quickly in view of White's dangerous queenside duo.

19...Qb8 20.fxe5 d5!

NOTE: Queens are rarely happy standing on the same file as an opposition rook when that file can suddenly open up.Here Anand has managed to exploit the queen's position, and that 21 exf6 Nxf6 regains his material with interest, to favourably open the position.

21.exd5 Nxe5 22.Nxe5 Rxe5 23.Qf2 Rxd5 24.Bc2 Qd8!?

Slowly increasing the pressure and gradually aiming to pressurise the white kingside. Al Modiahki's position is pretty solid, but he can't unravel in a hurry.

25.Bc1 Re8 26.Nc3 Rh5 27.Kg1! Kh8!

Freeing up the f6-knight and Anand later instructively commented that "the main idea was to pass the move back to him. I couldn't see any useful moves with the other pieces, so..." Good practical chess from the Indian superstar and White soon loses his way in a complex position as his severe lack of time became a major factor.

28.Qb6 Qb8 29.Rc7 Ng4 30.Bf4 Qd8 31.Rc4 Qh4?!

A scary move to face when down to just a few seconds, but this was just bluff from Anand!

32.Bg3?

32...Qg5! 33.Bf4 Rxh2! 34.Qxb7??

Losing at least a piece, whereas with more time Al Modiakhi might well have found his way through the complications. Anand offers 34.Bxh2! Nxh2 35.Ne4 Qh6 36.Nd6 Bd5! 37.Nxe8 Nxf1 38.Rc8 Qh2+ 39.Kxf1 Qxg2+ 40.Ke1 Qh1+ when it's perpetual.

This isn't convincing, while 8.Bd3 also appears to be slightly inferior to the main line with 8.a4 . 11.a3 Re8! 12.Qe1 Bb7 13.fxe5 dxe5 ( 13...Nxe5 is also very playable and this recapture is a method which Kosten likes in this line; Black intends to capture on f3, thereby reducing White's attacking chances, while 14.Nd4 g6 leaves Black with a powerful knight on e5) 14.Qg3 (and not 14.Nh4? 8...b5! 9.Bd5?!

Trying to control d5, but White's aggressive play isn't especially convincing. However, after 9.Bb3 Be7 10.O-O O-O 11.Kh1 Bb7 Black's counterplay against e4 is at least as important as the b3-bishop's enjoying an open diagonal down to f7.

9...Rb8 10.fxe5

10.Ng5 Nxd5 11.Qxd5 also forces Black into the slightly ugly 11...Qe7 , but this isn't a problem for him. White can actually do little as Black pushes him backwards, with a combination of ...h6, ...Nf6, ...b4 and ...Bb7 when the black bishop-pair already begins to count for something.

10...dxe5 11.Ng5?!

Pressurising f7, but White should probably prefer 11.Bg5 , although Black remains for choice after 11...Bb4 .;

11...Nxd5 12.Qxd5 Qe7 13.Be3

During the game I was more worried about 13.O-O!? when 13...f6 14.Ne6 Bb7 15.Nc7+ Kd8 16.Ne6+ is a perpetual, but Black should first prefer 13...Nb6! which neatly exploits the weakness of the a7-g1 diagonal ( 14.Qxf7+? Qxf7 15.Nxf7 Bc5+ 16.Kh1 Rf8 ) .

13...b4! 14.Ne2 Bb7 15.Qd3 f6 16.Nf3 Qe6

Without having done anything special, Black has emerged from the opening with a very pleasant position. White must indeed shortly concede a second bishop when he is clearly worse.

17.Ng3 Nc5 18.Bxc5 Bxc5 19.O-O-O O-O!

Black isn't interested in allowing counterplay with 19...Qxa2?! 20.Qd7+ Kf8 21.Nf5 and, in any case, he will shortly gain a strong queenside attack.

20.Kb1 a5 21.Nd2 Rfd8 22.Qf3!?

Desperately trying to drum up some counterplay on the kingside as the bishops would be far too powerful after 22.Qc4 Qxc4 23.Nxc4 a4 24.Na5 Ba8 .

22...a4 23.Nf5 g6! 24.Ne3 f5

Black can already win a pawn with 24...Bxe3!? 25.Qxe3 Rd4 , but I wanted to get the maximum out of the bishop-pair.

25.Qh3 Rxd2! 26.Rxd2 Bxe4 27.Rhd1 Bd4 28.Rxd4

The best try as 28...b3 was a large threat and 28.Ka1 fails to 28...a3 29.Rxd4 exd4 30.Rxd4 Qe5 .

28...exd4 29.Rxd4 b3 30.axb3 axb3 31.Kc1 bxc2 32.Nc4 Ra8 33.Kd2!

Gormally continues to resist well, but I had this one covered and had realised that my queen would cover all the key squares from f6.

33...Qf6! 34.Rd7 Ra1! 35.Qxh7+ Kf8 36.Rf7+!? Qxf7 37.Qh8+ Ke7 38.Qe5+ Qe6 39.Qg7+ Ke8 40.Qh8+ Kd7

11...Nh5 12.Nd4

White can regain his pawn a little quicker with 12.Ne1 Ne5 13.Be2 Nf6 14.Bxf4 , but the e5-knight is well placed and Black certainly isn't worse after 14...Be6 15.Nd3 Ng6 16.Bg3 d5! .;

12...Ndf6!

Holding on to the pawn for as long as possible and hoping to gain full equality once it's been regained.

13.Be2 g6 14.Bxh5 Nxh5 15.Nd5 Bg5

White's only hope for an advantage rests in leaving Black with a slightly problematic bishop. 16...Bd7 17.Ne2 Bc6! 18.Nexf4 Nxf4 19.Bxf4

Play now fizzles out to a draw, but White has no choice by this point as 19.Nxf4 Qf6 would most certainly not leave the black bishop any worse than White's knight.

Game 55
Sokolov,A - Gallagher,J [B93]
Cannes 1998

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.f4 e5 7.Nf3 Nbd7 8.a4 Be7 9.Bd3 O-O 10.O-O Nc5 11.Kh1 exf4 12.Bxf4 Bg4 13.Be3

White can also keep his options open with 13.Qd2 when 13...Bh5 14.Be3 Rc8 15.Nd4 is considered to be slightly in his favour. Black should thus prefer 13...Rc8 when White must decide how to centralise: 14.Rae1 ( 14.Nd4 Ne6 15.Be3 Nxd4 16.Bxd4 Be6 17.a5 , whilst still pretty solid for Black, gave White the edge in J.Arnason-V.Anand, Kopavogur (rapid) 2000; however, Anand should have exploited his control over e4 with 14...d5! , intending 15.exd5 Nxd3 16.Qxd3 Nxd5 and 15.e5 Nfe4 with activity and a good position for Black) 14...Re8 15.Nd4 intends 15...Bh5 16.Nf5 , but the knight is a little loose on d4 and Black should exploit this to improve his remaining pieces with Arizmendi and Moreno's excellent suggestion of 15...Qb6! 16.b3 Nxd3 17.Qxd3 Qc5 18.Bd2 Bd8 , intending to unravel with 19...Ba5 when the position is about equal.

13...Rc8!

WARNING: Always remain vigilant to the opponent's threats! Here 13...Bh5?! allows White to activate his light-squared bishop with some effect after 14.Bxc5! dxc5 15.e5 .

14.Qd2 Re8

Improving the rook, but also possible is 14...Nxd3!? . In general this is an exchange Black prefers to delay making as after 15.cxd3 , White has gained a centre pawn and Black will struggle to get anywhere at all down the c-file. However, here 15...d5! is a good reply because 16.e5 Bxf3! 17.exf6 Bxf6 18.Rxf3 d4 regains the piece. White should thus prefer 15.Qxd3 , although after 15...Be6 , the extra light-squared bishop has found a reasonable square and will exchange itself after 16.Nd5 . Instead 16.Rfd1 Qc7 17.h3 Qc4 18.Nd4 Rfe8 19.Nf5 Qxd3 20.Rxd3 d5! was also fine for Black in K.Arakhamia-A.Collinson, British League 2005.

15.Nd4

Trying to reach f5, whereas the fractionally slower 15.a5 Bh5! 16.Nd4 Ng4 17.Bf4 Bg6 18.Nf5 Bxf5 forced White to recapture with the pawn and gave Black a good game after 19...Bf6 in K.Arakhamia-N.De Firmian, World Team Championship, Lucerne 1997.;

Arakhamia later improved with 15.Rae1 , but after the accurate 15...Qb6! K.Arakhamia-J.Gallagher, British League 2005 was agreed drawn! On the grounds that both players are renowed as fearless attackers, I think we can draw from that, and our main game, that the position after 14...Re8 is fine for Black and dynamically quite even.

15...Qd7!

Exploiting the log-jam down the d-file to find a safe square and to prepare an important manoeuvre, while 16.h3 Bxh3! leads to perpetual. Instead b6 isn't such a good square when the white rook is still on a1 as the queen can be annoyingly hassled by a5.

16.Rf4

Prophylactically covering e4, which would have been attacked after 16.Nf5 Bd8! , but the rook is hardly well-placed here.

16...Bd8! 17.a5 Nh5

White has prevented the bishop from emerging on a5 and Gallagher decides to force a repetition.
