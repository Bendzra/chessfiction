7 Nb3: Introduction and ...h5 Ideas

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3

White's most popular variation

A sign of aggression from White who intends to launch a kingside attack with f3, Qd2, 0-0-0, g4 and h4. Black will be able to gain queenside counterplay, especially with moves like ...b5, ...Rc8 and ...Nb6-c4, but it is important that he doesn't neglect White's play in the centre and even on the queenside. This ...e5 version of the English Attack can indeed quickly become rather complex, with one key white idea being to meet ...b5 with Nd5. Following an exchange on that square, Black must be careful that Na5-c6 doesn't occur and completely stymie his counterplay.

For the past decade the English Attack has been White's main weapon against the Najdorf at grandmaster level. White's play is direct and logical, and so the variation has also become popular at lower levels. Thus Black must ensure that he finds a defence against 6.Be3 with which he is happy and which he understands fairly well.

Why does Black play ...h5?

After 7...Be6 8.f3 , Black can play ...h5, either immediately

Radically preventing g4

or on the following move after 9.Qd2 , with the intention of severely slowing down any kingside attacking ideas White might have. If White insists on still arranging the advance of his g-pawn, Black will use the time that takes to begin active operations on the queenside. Furthermore, his knight won't be quickly driven from f6, as often occurs in the main lines of the English Attack, and so Black enjoys better control over the important d5-square.

When to play ...h5?

7.Nb3 Be6 8.f3

White can also first prefer 8.Qd2 and only then f3, while his alternatives at this juncture are fairly harmless: 8.f4 exf4 9.Bxf4 Nc6 10.Qd2 d5! thematically equalizes, while 8.Be2 Be7 transposes back to Chapter One.

8...Be7

Developing and waiting for 9.Qd2 before flicking in ...h5. This makes sense as Black doesn't need to worry here about 9.g4? due to the strong riposte 9...d5!

Punishing a premature g4

when 10.g5 d4 11.gxf6 Bxf6 sees Black regain the piece with advantage. Black can also play 8...Nbd7 9.Qd2 h5 , but there 9.g4 is much more of a problem (see the next section). Finally, if Black understands the ...h5 positions pretty well, he might wish to play an immediate 8...h5 , not worrying that 9.Nd5 Nxd5 10.exd5 Bf5 11.Bd3 Bxd3 12.Qxd3 sees White save a tempo with his queen which should though be enough for a small edge.

It may, however, appear puzzling that Black doesn't immediately advance with 8...d5?! . Unfortunately that is premature here due to 9.exd5 Nxd5 10.Nxd5 Qxd5 11.Qxd5 Bxd5 12.O-O-O! when White will shortly win the bishop-pair and be left with a rather pleasant ending.

9.Qd2 h5 10.O-O-O

White initially struggled when ...h5 first appeared in the mid-nineties and for a while he concentrated his efforts on castling short after 10.Be2 , as we'll see in Morozevich-Sadler.

10...Nbd7 11.Kb1

Black clearly isn't in a hurry to castle, while White usually decides to improve his king safety with this useful move before deciding on a plan.

11...Rc8

How will White gain some play?

Both sides have developed their pieces to sensible squares and in accordance with their general aims. White can now either make immediate use of the d5-square with 12.Nd5 or still hope to make some kingside progress after 12.h3 as Kramnik-Anand reveals.

Theoretical?

Initially one attraction of an early ...h5 was that it cut down the amount of theory that Black needs to know against the English Attack. However, after much testing some forcing lines have been developed, although Black's play in them is often very natural and so they shouldn't be too tough to remember. Good general Najdorf understanding is very important after ...h5 and so if you prefer to avoid sharp opposite-side castling situations, this may well be the way forward.

Statistics

With an immediate 7.Nb3 Be6 8.f3 h5 , Black has scored an impressive 53% from 250 games, although his score falls to 40% after 9.Nd5 , but 8...Be7 9.Qd2 h5 also picks up 53%. This variation may have never caught on at elite level, where players generally prefer sharper lines which they can spend weeks preparing, but it remains a popular choice among non-super grandmasters.

Game 13
Morozevich, A. - Sadler, M.
European Club Cup, Reykjavik 1999

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.f3 Be7 9.Qd2 h5 10.Be2 Nbd7 11.O-O

NOTE: This variation is fairly comfortable for Black, at least so long as he has understood the key themes of Chapter One.

11.Nd5 is also similar to lines we've considered after 6.Be2 , albeit with the inclusion of f3 and ...h5, and 11...Nxd5 12.exd5 Bf5 13.O-O O-O and 14...Qc7 is fine for Black. There is also 13...h4!? , intending to compromise the white kingside before castling, and 14.f4?! Be4 15.c4 h3! 16.g3 Bg2 17.Rfe1 O-O gave Black a good game in P.Hugentobler-A.Barnsley, correspondence 2002.

11...Rc8 12.a4 g6!?

Intending to bring the king to g7, while leaving the rook on h8 with possible ideas of later generating some kingside activity with it. However, 12...O-O is a good alternative when the position rather resembles a Karpov approach after 13.a5 . Needless to say practice has shown that Black can thus full equalise after 13...Nc5! 14.Nxc5 dxc5 , intending ...c4.

13.a5 h4 14.Nc1

Improving the knight and heading for d5. Sadler later faced the inferior 14.Nd5?! which allowed him to fully justify 12...g6 as 14...Bxd5! 15.exd5 Nh5 16.c4 Kf8 17.Rfd1 Kg7 18.Rac1 Nf4 19.Bf1 Bg5 20.Rc3 Rh5! gave him good play on the kingside and dark squares in M.Feygin-M.Sadler, Dutch Team Ch. 2000.

14...Qc7 15.Rd1 Kf8 16.Bf1!

Slowly improving his pieces and overprotecting the kingside, while aiming to maintain control. Instead an impetuous 16.Nd3 would have allowed 16...d5! when Sadler gives 17.exd5 Nxd5 18.Nxd5 Bxd5 19.Nb4 Be6 20.Nd5 Bxd5 21.Qxd5 Nf6 22.Qb3 Bc5! and Black is fine.

TIP: The bishop-pair is often a dangerous weapon. When opposing it with bishop and knight, an exchange of bishops may well reduce the power of the remaining bishop, especially if one can gain counterplay with the attacking team of queen and knight.

16...Kg7 17.Bf2 Rcd8 18.Qe1 Rde8!

Continuing to wait for White to do something, while Sadler may well already have had in mind ideas of a ...d5 sacrifice followed by ...e4 to seize the initiative.

19.Rd2 Rh7 20.Qd1 Kg8 21.Nd3?!

Morozevich is more renowned for his creativity than his technique and Sammalvuo has suggested that White can improve here with 21.Ra4!? Nc5 22.Ra3 , covering the a5-pawn and preparing Na2-b4-d5. That seems sensible, although Black can cause the white king some discomfort with 22...h3!? when 23.g3 Ncd7 24.N1a2 Rd8 continues to maintain the balance. White might try 25.Nb4!? as 25...d5! 26.Nbxd5 Bxd5 27.Nxd5 Nxd5 28.Rxd5 Bxa3 29.bxa3 enables him to regain the exchange by force, but 29...Kg7 30.Bb6 Nxb6 31.Rxd8 Qc5+ 32.Kh1 Nc4 should be fine for Black as the white king's position is just as problematic as its counterpart's.

21...d5! 22.exd5 Bf5

A strong sacrifice and now White must be careful not to get blown away in the centre and on the kingside.

23.Ra4 e4! 24.Nxe4 Bxe4 25.fxe4 Bd6 26.e5!

The best defence as White keeps his h-pawn and exchanges knights, although now d5 becomes weak.

26...Nxe5 27.Nxe5 Rxe5 28.Rc4 Qxa5! 29.Bd4 Rf5 30.Rc8+ Kg7 31.c3?! Rhh5 32.b4 Qa3 33.Bc4 Rf4!

The black queen is precariously placed, but Sadler has everything worked out and the threat of mate maintains his initiative.

34.Bb3 Rhf5 35.Bf2

White was under severe pressure in any case, but this blockade can be lifted quite easily. Instead he had to avoid 35.Ra2? Qxb3! 36.Qxb3 Rf1# , but retracting his last with 35.Bc4 to cover f1 was a better defence.

35...Ne4! 36.Bd4+ f6 37.Re2 Qxb3!

Game 14
Kramnik, V. - Anand, V.
Monaco [blindfold] 2003

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.f3 Be7 9.Qd2 h5 10.O-O-O Nbd7 11.Kb1 Rc8 12.Bd3

Critical, whereas Black has been holding his own against 12.Nd5 with both captures. 12...Bxd5 13.exd5 Nb6 14.Bxb6 Qxb6 has the merit of maintaining a useful defender in the f6-knight and 15.Bd3 O-O 16.c4 Rfe8! 17.Rhe1 g6 sees Black hold his own on the kingside, whilst hoping to break up any attack after 18.g4 with Sammalvuo's 18...e4! .

12...b5 13.h3 h4!

TIP: It often pays to be consistent by carrying through positional plans in the Najdorf. 9...h5 delayed g4 and with 14.g4 finally threatened, Black continues to prevent it.

14.f4! Qc7!

Refusing to capture on f4 and aiming to keep the centre closed, continuing to challenge White to find an active idea.

15.Rhe1 Nb6!?

WARNING: Black doesn't yet have sufficient control to advance with 15...b4?! as 16.Nd5 Nxd5 17.exd5 Bxd5 18.fxe5 sees the position open as White desires when a6 is dropping off.

Anand's choice threatens to gain serious counterplay with 16...Nc4 and, although it allows the centre to open, Black does so while maintaining control of his position.

16.Bxb6 Qxb6 17.Nd5! Bxd5 18.exd5

Kramnik may have thought that he was getting somewhere at this point, but Anand continues to exploit his early ...h5 by developing his rook to maintain the balance.

18...Rh5! 19.fxe5

This doesn't lead anywhere and a more ambitious try would have been 19.Na5!? . 19...Kf8 prevents 20.Nc6 as then Black can capture on d5, whereas 20.Be2 Rf5 21.fxe5 dxe5! is rather messy. However, 22.d6 Bd8 23.Nb3 e4! (Sammalvuo) blunts the white bishop and appears pretty playable for Black. Indeed White must be careful that his d-pawn doesn't just become weak.

19...dxe5 20.Be2 Rf5! 21.Bf3 Rd8!

Keeping the passed d-pawn under lock and key, while, in view of the opposite-coloured bishops, it is very hard for either side to undertake anything active.
