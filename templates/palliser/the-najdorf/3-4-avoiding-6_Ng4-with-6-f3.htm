Avoiding 6...Ng4 with 6 f3

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.f3

Ruling out ...Ng4

This move-order, avoiding the complex 6.Be3 Ng4 variation, began to catch on in the late nineties and has been especially championed since then by Grischuk and Anand. After 6...e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.Be3 play transposes back to Chapter Two, albeit with White having lost his option of 6.Be3 e5 7.Nf3 . Likewise should Black meet 6.f3 with 6...e6 , White's options have been reduced as he is committed to an English Attack, whereas 6.Be3 e6 also permits him the choice of the sharp Perenyi Attack, 7.g4!? , and of a Classical Scheveningen with 7.Be2 . However, for those happy to meet both ...e5 and ...e6 with the English Attack, 6.f3!? is a canny move-order option.

Must Black counter 6 f3 with e5?

6.Be3 Ng4 Najdorf players clearly have a problem if they don't also play the 6.Be3 e5 lines. Black could try to meet 6.f3 with 6...b5 , but this looks a little suspect after 7.a4 b4 8.Nd5 . Furthermore, 7.Be3 actually leads Black away from the Najdorf and into the Scheveningen as then 7...e6 is best; 7...e5?! being premature due to 8.Nb3 Be6 9.a4! b4 10.Nd5 Bxd5 11.exd5 Nbd7 12.a5 when d5 is secure and White is better.

Fortunately all is not lost as 6...Qb6!?

A tricky response

has established itself as an independent and fully playable variation which 6.Be3 Ng4 expert Judit Polgar frequently makes use of. Black prevents White from immediately developing his dark-square bishop ( 7.Be3? Qxb2 8.Na4 Qb4+ 9.c3 Qa5+ 10.Rb1 Nbd7! is rather unconvincing) and doing so will have to be prefaced by 7.Nb3 and Qe2. Admittedly then Be3 will occur when the black queen must move, but spending a tempo with the black queen to move the white d4-knight back to the less active b3-square is a common Sicilian policy.

7.Nb3

White usually immediately retreats, although he can also use the move-order 7.g4 Nc6 8.Nb3 , but Anand and Ivanchuk have both experimented with the more positional 7.a4!? , intending 7...Nc6 8.Nb3 e6 9.a5 Qc7 10.Be3 to clamp the black queenside. Black mustn't then settle for passivity but should break out with an enterprising pawn sacrifice in 10...d5! 11.exd5 Nb4! .

A promising sacrifice

White must avoid 12.dxe6? Bxe6 when the open central files will cost him dearly, but 12.Bb6 Qe5+ 13.Kf2 Nfxd5 14.Bd4 Qh5! still gave Black quite a reasonable position in V.Ivanchuk-K.Sakaev, European Club Cup, Chalkidiki 2002.

7...e6 8.g4!

Delaying Qe2

Playing aggressively and in true English Attack spirit. This is more accurate than 8.Qe2 Qc7! when Black may be able to effectively delay ...Nc6 as he does in Grischuk-Kasparov. After 8.g4 the knight must be developed though as 8...Qc7?! forgets why the queen went to b6 and allows White to harmoniously complete his development with 9.Be3 b5 10.Qd2 .

8...Nc6 9.Qe2 Qc7

Now play becomes similar to the old Scheveningen lines of the English Attack (with 6.Be3 e6 7.f3 ) and, although the white knight isn't so well-placed here on b3, Black may wish for something quite different. If that is so he should consider another active d-pawn sacrifice with 9...d5!? when A.Motylev-S.Dvoirys, Moscow 2004 continued 10.Be3! d4 11.Bf2 e5! 12.O-O-O Be6 13.Nd5! Qd8! 14.Bh4 . Now, instead of Dvoirys' enterprising 14...h5!? , Motylev feels that Black could have gained a fully acceptable position with simply 14...Be7 15.Nxe7 Qxe7 , when he maintains his extra central space and the b3-knight may find itself misplaced.

10.Be3 b5 11.O-O-O Nd7!?

This has been the choice of the elite. Rather than continue developing, Black ensures that g5 won't come with tempo and increases his control over the important b6 and e5 squares.

12.Qf2

Unravelling, preventing 12...Nb6 and leaving White ready to advance on the kingside.

12...Rb8

Kasparov's choice, but it is noticeable that having suffered with this, Polgar switched to the more active 12...b4!?

Active play from both sides

which may well be Black's best choice:

(a) 13.Ne2 Bb7! 14.Ned4 Nxd4 15.Nxd4 d5

TIP: Following up ...b4 with ...d5 is a good choice in these ...e6 English Attack type positions. Black strikes back in the centre and opens up his bishops.

16.exd5 Bxd5 was already promising for Black in V.Anand-J.Polgar, Cap d'Agde (rapid) 2003.

(b) Leko thus preferred 13.Na4!? , but then Black can and must keep the knight out of b6 with 13...Rb8 . After 14.Rg1 Nce5! 15.f4 Nc4 16.Ba7 Rb7 17.Bd4 Qc6! (Sammalvuo) White can only save the errant a4-knight at the cost of exchanges when Black is fairly comfortably placed.

Continuing to prioritise Black's queenside play with 12...b4!? is tempting, although Black can also choose to simply develop a piece with the 12...Be7 of Grischuk-Ponomariov.

13.Kb1 Nb6

Also possible is 13...Na5!? when 14.Ba7! Ra8 15.Bd4 Bb7 16.Nxa5 Qxa5 transposes to A.Grischuk-G.Kasparov, Russian Ch., Moscow 2005 and White had retained control and the advantage.

14.f4!

White takes the initiative

A useful kingside advance which also takes control of e5. Now 14...Nc4 is well met by 15.Bc1 and Black appears to lack sufficient counterplay.

Theoretical?

This variation is still in its infancy compared to the more established lines of the English Attack after 6.Be3 . Those intending to complement 6.Be3 Ng4 with 6.f3 Qb6 should ensure that they understand the main themes of the variations considered here, and especially that Black should place his queenside activity ahead of completing his development. Having a line prepared against White's best move-order of 7.Nb3 e6 8.g4 Nc6 9.Qe2 would also be beneficial.

Statistics

With 6.f3 Qb6 Black should be happy with his 51% from 190 outings and he does especially well after 7.Nb3 e6 8.Qe2 Qc7! which nets 62% from 34 games. White should thus prefer 8.g4! Nc6 9.Qe2 Qc7 10.Be3 b5 11.O-O-O ; a position from which his score leaps to 64% from 40 games and which Black must be well prepared for, such as with Polgar's 11...Nd7!? 12.Qf2 b4!? .

Avoiding 6...Ng4 with 6 f3: Illustrative Games

Game 28
Grischuk, A. - Kasparov, G.
Linares 2001

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.f3 Qb6 7.Nb3 e6 8.Qe2 Qc7 9.g4

White might be better advised to continue delaying g4 here, although after 9.Be3 b5 10.O-O-O the b8-knight can still avoid going to c6. 10...Nbd7 11.g4 b4 12.Nb1 h6! importantly held up g5 and thus enabled Black to next advance his a-pawn in J.Gunnarsson-J.Stocek, Pardubice 2001.

9...b5! 10.Be3

White can also rule out ...b4 with 10.a3 , but 10...Nc6 11.Bg2 Be7 12.f4 Rb8! 13.Be3 b4 14.axb4 Rxb4 still gave Black good counterplay in R.Ponomariov-K.Georgiev, FIDE World Ch., Moscow 2001.

NOTE: Black is rarely afraid of a3 to hold up ...b4 in the Najdorf. It costs White a tempo and weakens his queenside, thereby making long castling less likely.

10...b4! 11.Na4 Nbd7 12.Qc4!

Grischuk must have been loathe to exchange queens so early, thereby limiting his aggressive intentions, but this was necessary to save the a4-knight.

TIP: A knight on the rim is far from always dim in the English Attack, but it is especially vulnerable when there isn't a knight on c6, enabling Black to quickly target it.

12...Qxc4 13.Bxc4 d5!

The opening has not been a success for White as Kasparov achieves his ideal break and already enjoys a rather comfortable position.

14.exd5 Ne5 15.Be2 Nxd5 16.Bd4 Bd6 17.Bc5?! Be7! 18.a3

Black's last maintained control and settled for a small edge in the event of 18.Bxe7 Nxe7 19.Nac5 a5 20.a3 Bd7 due to his better placed knights.

18...a5 19.Bd4 f6 20.Nac5

Finally the knight rejoins the game, while Grischuk would have had no intention of exchanging on e5, furthering weakening himself on f3 and f4.

20...O-O 21.O-O bxa3! 22.Rxa3!

Necessary as 22.bxa3?! a4 23.Nc1 Rd8 would have left Black exploiting his superior coordination.

22...Nf4 23.Bb5 Rb8 24.Bxe5 Nh3+! 25.Kg2 Rxb5 26.Bg3!

Continuing to defend well, whereas 26.Kxh3? fxe5 27.c4 Rxc5 28.Nxc5 Bxc5 29.Rxa5 Bd4 (Kasparov) would have left Black much better in view of his strong bishops and the ...e4 advance.

26...Ng5 27.Bf2?

Cutting the line of defence to f3, if only for a move, proves fatal. Instead White had to play more actively with 27.c4! when 27...Rb4!? might well be best as 27...Rxc5 28.Nxc5 Bxc5 29.Rxa5 Bd4 30.h4 Nf7 31.b4 (Kasparov) supplies good counterplay and is far from clear.

27...Bb7! 28.Bg1 Rc8 29.h4 Bxf3+! 30.Rxf3 Nxf3 31.Kxf3 Bxc5 32.Nxc5 Rbxc5 33.Bxc5 Rxc5

Black has emerged from the exchanges a pawn ahead and with his rook well placed. Kasparov now makes swift work of the resulting endgame.

34.c3 h5! 35.gxh5 Rxh5 36.b4!? axb4 37.cxb4 Rxh4 38.Ra8+ Kf7 39.Rb8 Kg6 40.b5 Kf5 41.b6 Rb4

Game 29
Grischuk, A. - Ponomariov, R.
Wijk aan Zee 2005

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 Ng4 7.Bc1 Nf6 8.f3 Qb6 9.g4 Nc6 10.Nb3 e6 11.Qe2 Qc7 12.Be3 b5 13.O-O-O Nd7 14.Qf2 Be7 15.g5!?

White can also advance his h-pawn here, but Grischuk realises that the black king might well remain in the centre and so prefers to march with his f-pawn.

15...Nce5

Intending to press forwards on the queenside with ...b4 and ...Nc4; a plan which Grischuk takes steps to prevent.

16.a3!? Rb8! 17.Rg1 Nb6?

Rather slow and Black doesn't need both steeds to converge on c4. Instead he should have continued to logically follow up his thematic last with 17...b4 when 18.axb4 Rxb4 19.f4! Nc6 20.h4 Na5! 21.Nd4 was the sharp course of E.L'Ami-P.Acs, Amsterdam 2004. With White's king more in the firing line than Black's, Black should be doing fairly well here, such as with Kosten's sensible suggestion of 21...Rb8!? , intending 22.f5 Qb7 23.b3 Nc5 when ...Qb4-a3 is on the cards.

18.f4 Nec4 19.Bxc4 Nxc4

Having brought the knight to b6, 19...bxc4 no longer made any sense, but now Grischuk energetically seizes the initiative.

20.Bd4! O-O?

Castling into it and he had to try and grovel with 20...Bf8!? .

21.Bf6! Bb7

After this White's major pieces simply swing into the attack, but Black would also have been mated after 21...Kh8 22.Qh4 gxf6 23.gxf6 .

22.Rd3! Rfc8 23.Rh3! Kf8

Have you ever seen a 2700 player running away with their king like this? Ponomariov now suffers a crushing and rather embarrassing defeat, but 23...gxf6 24.gxf6+ Kf8 25.Rxh7! Bxf6 26.Qg3 was also rather strong and 26...Ke7 (or 26...d5 27.Qg8+ Ke7 28.Qxf7+ Kd6 29.Qxf6! Qxh7 30.Nd4 Re8 31.Nf5+ Kd7 32.Rg7+ which is crushing) 27.Rxf7+! Kxf7 28.Qg6+ Kf8 29.Qg8+ Ke7 30.Qh7+! mates.

24.Rxh7 Ke8 25.Bxg7 Kd7 26.g6! a5

Desperation, but he should really have resigned by now.
