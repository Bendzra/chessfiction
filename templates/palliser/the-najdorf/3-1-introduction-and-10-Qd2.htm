Introduction and 10 Qd2

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 Ng4

Refusing to let White settle

What is Black's idea?

The modern chessplayer is prepared to strive for an early initiative, even as Black. With 6.Be3 White intends to be the player who will quickly gain the initiative with his kingside attack, but 6...Ng4 prevents that and begins an immediate fight for the ascendancy. White usually responds to Black's provocation with 7.Bg5 when Black reveals his main point by expanding on the kingside with 7...h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 .

Black will play on the dark squares

It's not surprising that it wasn't until the early nineties that this idea caught on as Black has irrevocably weakened his kingside. However, in return he gains a dynamic position and is already the proud owner of a rather strong dark-squared bishop. Compare that piece to White's and you will see why this variation is quite attractive for Black, especially if he doesn't want to indulge in a do or die race scenario so typical of the English Attack. 6.Be3 white players are though often after such a situation and so they may well not respond well to 6...Ng4 , as White actually didn't initially at grandmaster level. White will need to play carefully and often quite positionally to restrain Black's early activity, and to prove that ...g5 is a weakness, not a strength. It certainly is a strength in the short-term, buying Black control over f4 and thus enabling the g4-knight to retreat to the excellent central e5-square.

Does White have to allow this?

Some players prefer to only enter the English Attack these days after 6.f3 , which certainly prevents 6...Ng4 but isn't ideal in other ways as we will discuss later in this chapter. White also has two alternatives after 6.Be3 Ng4 to 7.Bg5 :

(a) 7.Bc4?! hopes for a quick initiative after 7...Nxe3 8.fxe3 e6 9.O-O , but 9...Nc6!

The f-file pressure is insufficient

eliminates any sacrifices. Polugaevsky's 10.Nxc6 bxc6 11.Qf3 Qf6!? further neutralizes White's intentions after which Black's superior structure and bishop-pair grants him the better chances.

(b) 7.Bc1 prepares to repeat after 7...Nf6 . Even if Black is determined not to finish so quickly, he should accept the first repetition as White may well choose a different anti-Najdorf system the second time around. However, if White does repeat with 8.Be3 then Black can switch to 8...e5 or opt for 8...Ng4 9.Bc1 Nc6!? ,

Ending the repetition

hoping for 10.f3? Qb6! . 10.Be2 is a superior choice when 10...Qb6!? 11.Bxg4 Qxd4 12.Be3 Qxd1+ 13.Bxd1 Be6 14.f4 g6 15.Nd5 Rc8 16.c3 Bg7 led to an unbalanced position in M.Rose-R.Palliser, Oxford 2002, although 17.Nb6 Rc7 18.O-O would have retained a small edge for White.

How good is 6...Ng4?

Everything so far has sounded quite rosy for Black, but it is worth noting that Kasparov, who had contributed so much to it, gave up the variation in 2000. Whether that was due to the arrival of 6.f3 as an alternative move-order or to the new problems being posed by 10.h3 is, however, not known. 6...Ng4 still continued to be defended by a number of grandmasters, but it did appear that its star was on the wane until it made a comeback at elite level at San Luis. Currently, despite the huge interest in 10.h3!?, Black appears to theoretically be holding his own with 6...Ng4 .

How should White complete his development?

7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 should definitely not be met by 10.f3?? as 10...Ne3 11.Qd2 Bxd4! wins a piece. Instead White usually nowadays prefers one of 10.Be2 or 10.h3 , immediately attacking the pesky g4-knight, whereas 10.Qd2 , which was once a main line, has now rather faded away, largely because it is considered quite comfortable for Black.

10.Qd2 Nc6 11.Nb3

Black has good central control

It may well be having to make this meek move which has put most players off 10.Qd2 . Black's knights will now control a number of central squares from c6 and e5, whereas the white knight achieves little from b3.

11...Be6

For Kasparov's preferred move-order 11...Nge5 see Ivanchuk-Kasparov, but 11...Be6 has been by far the more popular choice.

12.f3

Bringing the bishop back into play which makes positional sense, but is a little slow. Much more challenging is 12.h4! when 12...gxh4 13.Bxh4 Rc8 14.f3! maintains control and should lead to a an advantage for White. Black should thus not fracture his structure, preferring 12...Rg8!? 13.hxg5 hxg5 when Ftacnik's 14.f3 Bxc3!? 15.Qxc3 Nge5 16.O-O-O Rc8 17.Qd2 Qb6! is rather radical, but also very solid for Black.

12...Nge5 13.Nd5 b5 14.Bf2 Rb8 15.Nd4

Exchanging off one of the black knights and centralizing the bishop, but Black is still to be troubled on the kingside.

15...Nxd4 16.Bxd4 f5! 17.h4 f4!

Securing f4

White has finally advanced his h-pawn and his knight is well placed on d5, but Black is still pretty comfortable here as he has maintained control over e5. Furthermore, he doesn't mind an exchange on g5 and his king will be quite safe, once Black begins to advance on the queenside, on e8 or f7.

Theoretical?

In general 6...Ng4 is less theoretical than 6...e5 , although it has been much debated for the past fifteen years and so a body of theory has built up. Black's dark-square play, aiming to fully exploit his strong g7-bishop and e5-knight, is though quite logical, especially in the event of 10.Qd2 . However, as we have seen, 10.Qd2 does contain some awkward ideas and is far from dead and buried, while 10...Nc6 11.Nb3 b5!? could well be Black's best move-order.

Statistics

From our starting position (after 9...Bg7 ), Black has surprisingly scored only 45% from almost 900 games with this positionally rich line. However, if Topalov continues to replace Kasparov in enriching it with new ideas, that percentage should rise. 10.Qd2 has secured a reasonable 56% from 195 games when Kasparov's 10...Nc6 11.Nb3 Nge5 is Black's best scoring defence, netting 48% albeit from just 22 outings.

Introduction and 10 Qd2: Illustrative Games

Game 22
Ivanchuk, V. - Kasparov, G.
Linares 1999

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 Ng4 7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 10.Qd2 Nc6 11.Nb3 Nge5

We've already noted that 11...Be6 12.h4! is a little troubling for Black and so too might be 11...Nge5 12.a4 . To avoid that 11...b5!? is possible, intending to transpose to our main game after 12.f3 Nge5 . More critical is 12.h4!? , although 12...gxh4 13.Bxh4 Nge5 appears quite playable for Black, when the knight may well quickly come to c4, while 12...b4!? is also an option, driving the knight back to d1.

12.f3

Now Black seizes space on the queenside and it's surprising that Ivanchuk didn't prevent that with Leko's 12.a4 , aiming to keep Black's activity under control. Presumably Kasparov was ready for this and had a novelty prepared as Black has scored just 0.5/4 from this position on the database! P.Leko-I.Morovic Fernandez, Cienfuegos 1997 continued 12...Na5! 13.Nxa5 Qxa5 14.Nd5! Qxd2+ 15.Kxd2 Rb8 16.f3 when Leko suggests 16...f5!? 17.Bf2 Nc6 18.exf5 Bxf5 19.c3 and assesses this as slightly favouring White. Black does possess an extra pawn island, but this position appears quite playable for him: his bishops are well-placed, his kingside space will be quite useful in the endgame, ...b5-b4 will be a strong minority attack and he can drive back the white knight with ...e6 if necessary.

12...b5 13.Bf2 Rb8!

Prophylaxis against 14.Nd5 as b6 is now covered and once again we find White lacking a good plan.

NOTE: With 6...Ng4 Black immediately tries to seize the initiative, but White shouldn't meekly let himself be driven backwards. Instead he must aim to either counter on the kingside, with an early h4 or Nf5, or play more positionally by seeking a bind right across the board, as Leko's 11...Nge5 12.a4 intends.

14.Nd4?!

Once again this is too slow and costs the initiative. However, the white position already doesn't inspire confidence and 14.Nd5 e6! 15.Ne3 a5 16.a4! bxa4 17.Rxa4 O-O 18.Be2 , whilst a better try, still allows Black good counterplay with Ftacnik's 18...d5! .

14...b4! 15.Nd1 Nxd4 16.Bxd4 d5! 17.exd5?!

Black has seized the initiative and is extremely active right across the board. Ivanchuk presumably didn't want to cede any more dark square control, but 17.Bxe5 Bxe5 18.exd5 might have been a better try, albeit still rather grim for White after 18...Qc7 .

17...Qxd5 18.c3 O-O 19.Be2 Rd8 20.Qe3 Bb7!

A quick glance at the activity and coordination of the two forces confirms Black's large advantage, whilst poor Ivanchuk can't even castle here due to 21...Ng4 .

21.Nf2 bxc3 22.Bxc3 Qe6! 23.Kf1

Black has continued to generate tactics to keep the white king in the centre as 23.O-O? Nxf3+! demonstrates.

23...Bd5! 24.b3?! Rbc8 25.Rd1 Rxc3!

Kasparov's initiative has flowed almost effortlessly thus far and typically he finishes with a bang.

26.Qxc3 Ng4 27.Qa5 Ne3+ 28.Kg1 Rc8?!

White is playing without his h1-rook and, as Kasparov later pointed out, 28...Nxd1! 29.Qxd8+ Kh7 30.Kf1 Ne3+ 31.Kg1 Qc6 would have been the clinical finish he was seeking.

29.Qxa6

Trying to get the queens off and 29.Rb1!? was the other option, although after 29...Bd4! 30.Qd2 Qb6 31.Rc1 Black's bishops and pressure are so strong that he can simply continue 31...Rxc1+! 32.Qxc1 Qa5 .

29...Nxd1 30.Qxe6 Bxe6 31.Bxd1 Rc1

A rather premature decision as White is a pawn ahead, albeit horribly bound and facing a mighty bishop-pair. However, Black is close to winning here after 31...Rc1 32.g3 (or 32.Kf1 Ra1 33.Ke2 Rxa2+ 34.Ke3 f5! ) 32...Ra1 33.Kg2 Bc3! 34.a4 Ra2 (Kasparov).
