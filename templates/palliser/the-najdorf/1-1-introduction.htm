1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5

Black counters in the centre

White can still play aggressively after 6.Be2 , but this move usually heralds a positional, manoeuvring approach. His aim is often to gain control of d5 and that square must always be at the forefront of Black's mind. In general Black wishes to force White to recapture after any exchange of knights on d5 with his e-pawn. Then d5 is no longer a weakness and Black will mobilize his newly created kingside majority with ...f5, while aiming to hold up White on the queenside. Initially players like Geller scored well by exploiting the extra queenside pawn, but Black has grown wise to the situation as we will see in this chapter. There are a few exceptions in which Black can play around a knight on d5, but in general allowing White an unopposed knight on d5 must be avoided. The game J.Polgar-V.Anand, Wijk aan Zee 1998 should serve as a reminder of what to avoid:

The d5-knight dominates

With so few pieces left Black lacked any counterplay, while the knight completely dominated the bishop. Polgar maintained control and went on to score a crushing victory.

WARNING: Always beware the move Bg5, angling to exchange on f6. After that exchange Black still needs to be covering d5 and so ideally wants to recapture on f6 with his remaining knight. Placing the light-squared bishop on e6 or b7 also importantly contributes to the struggle for the key d5-square.

7.Nb3

By far the most popular retreat, although this knight will take some time to find a good role. From b3 it does, however, support White's queenside play, and especially the advance of White's a and c-pawns, while White also hopes to deter any ideas of ...Nd7-c5, undermining the e4-pawn. The two alternatives promise Black an easy game, although one remains a tricky surprise weapon:

(a) 7.Nf3 is well met by 7...h6! , preventing Bg5 and also enabling the c8-bishop to be developed to e6, without being hassled by Ng5. White's play is reminiscent of the Boleslavsky system ( 5...Nc6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nf3 ), but there Black often spends two tempi improving his knight from c6 to d7. Here the knight can proceed straight to d7 and 8.O-O Be6 9.Re1 Be7 10.h3 O-O 11.Bf1 Nbd7 12.b3 b5 13.Bb2 Qc7 14.a3 Rfc8 15.Qd2 Qc6!

Model development from Black

saw Black develop thematically in V.Gashimov-V.Bologan, Minsk Zonal 2000. Bologan had maintained control over d5 and his queenside play already gave him the superior prospects with White unable for his part to find a good plan.

(b) 7.Nf5!? fails to maintain control over d5 which Black should immediately exploit with 7...d5! . White's idea is that after 8.Bg5 d4 9.Bxf6 Qxf6 10.Nd5 Qd8 11.c4 his knights appear rather impressive and one could believe that Black has gone wrong. He hasn't, however, because 11...g6 12.Ng3 Bg7 13.O-O O-O is actually quite comfortable for him. The d5-knight is well-placed, but it is White's only good piece. Black should thus complete his development with ...Be6 and ...Nd7 after which the ...f5 and ...b5 breaks promise him good chances to quickly seize a strong initiative.
