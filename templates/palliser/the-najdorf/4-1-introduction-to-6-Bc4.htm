Introduction to 6 Bc4

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4

An actively posted bishop

Bobby Fischer honed 6.Bc4 into a formidable attacking weapon early in his career before finding resources against it. This aggressive move then gained a fair amount of popularity in the mid-nineties, following its frequent use in the 1993 Short-Kasparov match. However, with the rise of the English Attack and the establishment of two reliable systems for Black against 6.Bc4 , it is currently not as popular as it has been. Still it would be dangerous to neglect such an attacking move as 6.Bc4 . Black can gain a reasonable position against it, but he must first handle the early stages with some care.

6...e6!

Wisely blunting the bishop and asking what it is now doing. It may try and participate in a sacrifice on e6 once the c8-bishop no longer covers that pawn, but Black usually has to be a little careless to allow a strong version of such a sacrifice.

WARNING: The bishop must be blunted as it stands rather well after 6...e5? 7.Nde2 , and 7.Nf5!? Bxf5 8.exf5 Qc8 9.Bb3 Qxf5 10.O-O Nc6 11.f4! is also a position to avoid. Black must instead aim to deny White the attacking chances he craves with 6.Bc4 .

7.Bb3

A Fischer inspired move-order

Immediately moving the bishop out of the way of the black b-pawn may appear a little strange, but this was how Fischer always continued. His preferred move-order has stuck, partly because the immediate retreat has been considered the most flexible choice: White can still castle short, but he may first prefer to advance his f-pawn or to play Bg5.

After 7.Bb3 , Black has three ways to develop:

(a) 7...Nbd7 intends 8.f4 Nc5 , covering e6, keeping an eye on the dangerous bishop and pressurizing e4, albeit at a small cost in terms of time. Leading Sozin expert Mikhail Golubev considers this Black's best choice, although with it Black must be prepared for some fairly sharp and complex hand to hand fighting.

(b) 7...b5 is the natural Najdorf move, gaining queenside space and facilitating pressure down the a8-h1 diagonal against e4, and remains Black's most popular choice.

(c) 7...Nc6 transposes to the Sicilian Sozin and also occurs via 5...Nc6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 a6 . That complex system is outside the scope of this book, while Najdorf players rarely like to commit their queen's knight to c6 so quickly.

White's other move-orders

White doesn't have to retreat with the prophylactic 7.Bb3 and one or two of his alternatives have gained some grandmaster attention of late with White struggling a little in the main lines:

(a) 7.Qe2?! b5! 8.Bb3 Be7 9.Be3 sees White implement a system which very aggressive players like to use against the Classical ( 5...Nc6 ) Sicilian. However, against the Najdorf this attempted form of the Velimirovic Attack is misplaced and Black gains a good game with 9...O-O 10.O-O-O Bd7! .

Overly ambitious play from White

when the threat of 11...b4 sees him already having seized the initiative.

(b) 7.a3 prepares a retreat square, but after 7...b5 8.Ba2 Be7 , intending 9...O-O and 10...Bb7 , Black has a fairly harmonious position and is ready to meet f4-f5 as Ermenkov-Portisch demonstrates.

(c) 7.a4!? prevents ...b5 but at the cost of a tempo. Black can then consider an immediate exchange on e4; developing with 7...Nc6 8.O-O Be7 9.Be3 O-O , intending to break with ...d5; or he can first develop with 7...Be7 8.O-O O-O . One semi-useful waiting move is then 9.Kh1 , but 9...Nxe4! 10.Nxe4 d5 11.Bd3 dxe4 12.Bxe4 f5! 13.Bf3 e5 (Arizmendi and Moreno) slightly favours Black who has already made good use of his extra central pawn. Instead 9.Ba2 prevents Black's freeing exchange, but costs further time which Black should use to gain pressure against e4 and to advance on the queenside with 9...Nc6 10.Be3 Bd7 11.f4 Nxd4! 12.Bxd4 Bc6 13.Qd3 b5! .

(d) White's failure to find any advantage after 7.Bb3 Nbd7 , has led to some exploration of 7.O-O

Preventing 7...Nbd7

when Black must be careful if he is a 7.Bb3 Nbd7 player, whereas those who prefer 7.Bb3 b5 can transpose to the final section of this chapter with 7.O-O b5 8.Bb3 .

WARNING: Black must always be alert to piece sacrifices on e6 after 6.Bc4 and here 7...Nbd7?! walks right into 8.Bxe6! fxe6 9.Nxe6 .

A monster knight on e6

This promises White a strong initiative and should be avoided as the dominant steed will be anchored on e6 after 10.Nd5 Nxd5 11.exd5 .

Black should also be aware that his equalizing trick no longer works as after 7...Nxe4? 8.Nxe4 d5 9.Bg5 White has far too big a lead in development. That leaves the sensible 7...Be7 ; a move far superior to 7.Bb3 Be7 which permits the dangerous advances, 8.f4 and 8.g4!? .

7.O-O Be7 currently appears to be a good way to exploit White's move-order and the white position has often failed to impress after 8.Bb3 O-O 9.f4 b5! , beginning queenside operations:

(a) 10.f5?! forces ...e5 when White hopes that his light-squared bishop will become a dominant force once again, but it is actually Black who gains the advantage with the careful 10...b4! 11.Na4 e5 12.Ne2 Bb7 13.Ng3 Nbd7! when his queenside prospects and the misplaced a4-knight are the most important features of the position.

(b) 10.e5!? dxe5 11.fxe5 Nfd7

Black is sufficiently solid

also sees White hoping to power his way through. This position may at first appear a little shaky for Black, but e5 is weak and Black may gain strong counterplay down the a7-g1 and a8-h1 diagonals. Furthermore, the black kingside is actually quite resilient as it was in Kulaots-Nisipeanu.

Introduction to 6 Bc4: Illustrative Games

Game 30
Ermenkov, E. - Portisch, L.
European Team Ch., Skara 1980

[ECO "B86"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.a3 Be7 8.Ba2 O-O 9.f4

White can also prefer the move-order 9.O-O b5 10.f4 Bb7 11.f5 e5 12.Nde2 when Black should prefer to allow a transposition back to the game with 12...Nbd7 ( 12...Nxe4?! 13.Nxe4 Bxe4 14.Ng3 Bb7 15.Nh5! begins an attack) 13.Ng3 Rc8! 14.Bg5 (but not 14.Nh5?! Nxh5 15.Qxh5 Rxc3! 16.bxc3 Nf6 and 17...Nxe4 ).

9...b5 10.f5?!

Ermenkov has a simple plan and one which first helped to draw Fischer to 6.Bc4 . White intends to force ...e5 after which he will make use of his bishop's being on a2, and not e2, to gain control of the d5-square. However, White's plan is actually doomed to fail and so he should prefer 10.O-O Bb7 11.Qe2!? , intending 11...Nbd7 12.e5! . Black should prefer to take advantage of the undefended d4-knight with 11...Nc6 , intending to quickly gain counterplay with 12.Nf3 b4! 13.axb4 Nxb4 .

10...e5 11.Nde2 Bb7 12.Ng3 Nbd7 13.Bg5 Rc8!

White has carried out his plan to a tee so far, but Portisch has developed his pieces to their best squares and his last move prepared a key idea.

TIP: When White's entire strategy revolves around just one aim, here controlling d5, it can often be worth a small sacrifice to prevent it, leaving the rest of the white pieces no longer well placed.

14.O-O Rxc3!

Nipping White's strategy in the bud before he exchanges on f6 and plays Nh5. Furthermore, this doesn't really feel like a sacrifice as Black gains more than enough play for the exchange: a central pawn, superior structure and much the better coordinated pieces.

15.bxc3 Nxe4 16.Nxe4 Bxe4 17.Bxe7 Qxe7 18.c4?! Rc8!

White was hoping to improve his structure, but cannot now capture on b5 as that would permit carnage along the second.

19.Qe2 Nf6 20.Rac1?!

WARNING: Try to avoid making passive moves just to defend weak pawns. Sometimes they cannot be avoided, but often the pawn can be sacrificed for activity as Ermenkov should have done here.

White's a2-bishop has become a real problem piece for him, ever since his campaign to seize control of d5 was thwarted. 20.Rad1 bxc4 21.Bxc4 d5 22.Bd3 Qxa3 would still have been good for Black, but with the bishop back in the game, White would have far better chances of putting up some resistance.

20...h5!!

With White rather passive and Black enjoying a colossal bishop on e4, Portisch decides that it's time to attack. The white kingside doesn't currently look especially weak, despite the f-pawn's advance, but just watch how quickly it is undone.

21.cxb5 axb5 22.Qxb5 Qa7+ 23.Kh1 h4 24.Qb3

Preventing ...h3, but White isn't only weak on the kingside light squares as Black exploits his control of the a7-g1 diagonal to move in for the kill.

24...Ng4! 25.h3 Ne3 26.Rg1 Nxf5 27.Kh2 d5!

Cementing the key bishop in place and keeping White frustrated down the a2-g8 diagonal. Ermenkov has been completely outplayed and Portisch now energetically rounds off this fine effort.

28.c4 Rb8 29.Qc3 Qf2! 30.Qxe5 Rb2 31.cxd5 Qxg2+! 32.Rxg2 Rxg2+ 33.Kh1 Ng3+

Game 31
Kulaots, K. - Nisipeanu, L.
World Junior Ch., Medellin 1996

[ECO "B87"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 b5 8.O-O Be7 9.f4 O-O 10.e5 dxe5 11.fxe5 Nfd7 12.Be3!?

Sacrificing the pawn in return for taking the sting out of ...Bc5 and for being able to play Qh5 with gain of tempo. White's problem is that defending the pawn with 12.Bf4 blocks the f-file and Black can unravel fairly easily after 12...Nc5 . However, that is a better course for White than the horribly materialistically 12.Qf3? which is deservedly punished by 12...Nxe5! 13.Qxa8 Qxd4+ 14.Kh1 Nbc6 with great compensation for the exchange and a trapped white queen to boot!

White's main alternative to Kulaots' choice is actually to both attack and defend e5 simultaneously with 12.Qh5 . However, practice has shown that after 12...Bc5! 13.Be3 Bxd4! 14.Bxd4 Nc6 15.Be3 Ncxe5 , White is struggling for sufficient compensation with ...Ng6 on the way to bolster up the black kingside.

12...Nxe5!

The challenge must be accepted to justify Black's play thus far. Furthermore, it's already too late to back out: 12...Qc7? might appear to give Black options over how to capture on e5, but it forgets about the importance of the e6 and f7 squares and sacrifices upon them. Here 13.Rxf7! Rxf7 14.Nxe6 , creating havoc down the a2-g8 diagonal, is rather strong.

13.Qh5 Nbc6 14.Nxc6 Nxc6 15.Rf3

Black is a pawn up and has exchanged a pair of knights, but his kingside is a little low on defenders and he must proceed accurately.

15...Qd6!

An excellent concept and since this game White has pretty much abandoned this variation, unable to find anything against the centralisation of the black queen to assist Black's defence. Instead 15...b4 16.Rh3 h6 may have been used by Kasparov, but it looks a little suspect after 17.Bxh6! , intending 17...gxh6? 18.Rd1! Qb6+ 19.Kh1 Bg5 20.Ne4 (Golubev).

16.Rh3 h6 17.Rg3

Black is well-placed to counter this dark-square attack, although 17.Bxh6 would have allowed Nisipeanu to reveal the main point behind 15...Qd6 with 17...Qc5+! 18.Qxc5 Bxc5+ . Black enjoys an extra central pawn and the better placed pieces, although White does remain fairly solid, albeit still slightly worse.

17...Qe5! 18.Qf3?

6.Bc4 exponents often like to play actively, but this is far too ambitious. R.Levya later improved with 18.Qxe5 Nxe5 19.Bxh6 Ng6 20.Bg5! Bc5+ 21.Kh1 Bb7 22.Rf1 when White currently controls d8. However, if this is his best then it's clear that 15...Qd6 is a good defence and White is still slightly worse here. Black must still watch out for sacrifices involving the b3-prelate, but 22...Bd4 , intending to build up some queenside pressure, looks like a good way to continue.

18...Bc5 19.Bxc5 Qxc5+ 20.Kh1 f5!

Making good use of the mobile central duo to keep White away from e4 and leaving Kulaots desperately struggling to find any compensation at all.

21.Re1 Ra7! 22.Qf4 Kh8 23.Bxe6??

White was in trouble in any case, but evidently miscalculated here as the pin down the e-file will cost a piece.

23...Re7 24.Rg6 Nd4
