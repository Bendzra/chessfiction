Black Plays 7...Nbd7

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 Nbd7

The knight heads for c5

The Najdorf player who thrives in complex and sharp positions should select this as their method against 6.Bc4 . Black brings his knight to a superb multipurpose square on c5 and against this White must be careful not to develop slowly. Indeed he will often aim to exploit his lead in development, although doing so may well require a rather tactical and committal choice.

8.f4

Seizing central space and giving himself the option of two potentially dangerous advances in e5 and f5. However, spurred on by the successes of Karsten Müller and then of Alexander Morozevich, 8.Bg5 has been receiving a fair amount of attention of late. White places rapid development ahead of everything else and will usually going long, aiming to blow Black away. Doing so is unsurprisingly not so easy, but Black must be ready for this system and Sutovsky-Dvoirys fully highlights the danger.

8...Nc5

Guarding some key light squares

9.Qf3

Bringing the queen to an aggressive square while defending e4. However, there is little consensus as to White's best path at this point, partly because he has failed to overcome Black's resources despite exploring a number of different options.

(a) The 9.O-O of Sandler-Danailov once again sees White place a high premium on rapid development and dares Black to capture on e4.

(b) 9.e5 initially brought White a fair amount of success in the early nineties and 9...dxe5 10.fxe5 Nfd7 11.Bf4 can easily quickly leave him with a powerful initiative. However, the ever creative Vassily Ivanchuk thought up the clever response 9...Nfd7! when 10.exd6 Nf6!

Black favourably regains the pawn

11.Be3 Bxd6 , followed by ...Qc7 and ...0-0 leaves Black with a good game, having denied White his attacking open lines.

(c) 9.f5

Light-square pressure

puts e6 under some pressure, but Black has two good responses:

(c1) Black must always be very careful when grabbing the e-pawn after 6.Bc4 and here he most certainly can't get away with it as 9...Nfxe4? 10.fxe6! Qh4+ 11.g3 Nxg3 12.Nf3 Qh5 13.exf7+ Kd8 14.Rg1 left the black king in huge danger in R.Fischer-J.Bednarski, Havana Olympiad 1966.

(c2) 9...Be7 has been by far Black's most popular choice when 10.Qf3 O-O 11.Be3 prepares a kingside attack. White would also, as so often, like to be left with control of d5, but Black appears to be able to get away with 11...e5! 12.Nde2 b5!? . In N.Short-G.Kasparov, World Championship (Game 6), London 1993, White occupied d5 with 13.Bd5 when 13...Rb8 prepared ...b4 and 14.b4!? Ncd7 15.O-O should have been met by 15...Nb6! according to Kasparov, leaving White's queenside weaknesses more important than that of d5.

(c3) 9...Bd7!? is a rare move which Arizmendi and Moreno have recommended. It looks like a good alternative to the much more theoretical 9...Be7 and transposes to our main line after 10.Qf3 b5 .

9...b5!? 10.f5 Bd7

Preparing to complete development

Having seized the opportunity to advance the b-pawn and thus worry White with ...b4, Black wisely covers c6. This permits kingside development to be completed and White must watch out for ideas of an exchange on b3, followed by ...e5 and ...Bc6.

11.Bg5

Aiming to get the maximum out of the bishop, but this is by no means White's only try:

(a) 11.g4?! threatens to blow Black away, but Black is ready for this with 11...e5! 12.Nde2 Nxb3 13.axb3 b4 when 14.Na4? Bc6 cannot be permitted, but 14.Nd5 Nxd5 15.exd5 h5! (Arizmendi and Moreno) is good for Black.

(b) 11.a3 hopes to keep the key bishop which should of course be immediately exchanged.

(c) 11.fxe6 fxe6 12.O-O Be7 13.e5!? dxe5 14.Nc6 wants to exploit the c6-square after all, but following 14...Bxc6 15.Qxc6+ Kf7 Black's king is safe enough. To harrass it at all White must act quickly with Golubev's 16.Ne4!? when Arizmendi and Moreno have proposed 16...Nxb3 17.Ng5+ Kg6 18.Qxe6! Qb6+! (making good use of the b3-knight) 19.Qxb6 Bc5+ 20.Qxc5 Nxc5 and Black's activity compensates for his isolated but passed e-pawn.

11...Be7 12.e5!?

White can exchange on e6 whenever he likes and here 12.fxe6 fxe6 13.O-O-O O-O transposes to Short-Kasparov.

12...dxe5 13.Nc6 Bxc6 14.Qxc6+ Kf8!

The point of White's ambitious play as by delaying the exchange on e6, f7 isn't available to the black king. However, the king is safe enough on f8 and White cannot gain anything concrete with his temporary initiative.

15.fxe6 b4 16.Ne2 Qc8!

Black has good play

Black has responded well to White's initially dangerous looking idea and has a pretty reasonable position. He enjoys good queenside activity and his king will be well placed in the centre should the queens come off.

Theoretical?

Pretty much so and certainly general principles are of less importance than after 7...b5 . The pivotal c5-knight holds Black's position together, but White has a number of dangerous attacking ideas and Black must respond accurately to each of them, although if he can then he should emerge with a good position.

Statistics

Black has scored an impressive 52% from over 1400 games with 7...Nbd7 as Sozin experts have struggled to make an attacking line work for more than just its first few outings. 8.Bg5 has managed to hang around for a while but still only gives White 48%, whereas the main line starting position with 8.f4 Nc5 has the scores level. White has then scored well with the direct 9.e5 (56%) and 9.O-O (52%) but only when Black doesn't respond accurately: 9...Nfd7! and 9...Be7 have netted Black 54% and 55% respectively.

Black Plays 7...Nbd7: Illustrative Games

Game 32
Sutovsky, E. - Dvoirys, S.
Aeroflot Open, Moscow 2003

[ECO "B86"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 Nbd7 8.Bg5 Nc5

Consistent with how Black usually follows up 7...Nbd7 , but here e4 hasn't been weakened and so Black should give serious consideration to delaying ...Nc5. However, he must remain vigilant against e6-sacrifices and so 8...b5?! 9.Bxe6! and 8...Be7? 9.Bxe6! fxe6 10.Nxe6 Qa5 11.Nxg7+ Kf7 12.Nf5 are both best avoided. Recent interest in 8.Bg5 was partly sparked by A.Morozevich-O.Cvitan, Bled Olympiad 2002 where Cvitan copied Kasparov's 8...h6 9.Bh4 Qa5 , but came under pressure after 10.Qe2! Qh5 11.Nf3 Be7 12.O-O-O O-O 13.Rhg1! .

It seems more natural to play an immediate 8...Qa5!? , gaining a tempo and this is probably Black's most reliable option: 9.Qd2 ( 9.Bd2 Qc7 , transposing to the almost unknown 8.Bd2 Qc7 , must be a good gain for Black; however, that leading 8.Bg5 exponent, Karsten Müller, could find nothing better against Naiditsch himself (at the 2004 German Championship), which does suggest that 8...Qa5 is in good theoretical shape and 10.Qe2 Nc5 11.g4 b5 12.g5 Nfd7 13.f4 Nb6 once again led to an unclear position, albeit one with reasonable queenside prospects for Black) 9...Be7 develops and prepares to meet 10.f4 with 10...h6! . White must then cede the bishop-pair as 11.Bh4? permits 11...Nxe4! , taking advantage of the undefended bishop. White preferred 10.f3 in A.Naiditsch-V.Anand, Dortmund 2003, but Black still gained good counterplay with 10...Nc5 11.O-O-O Qc7 12.Kb1 O-O 13.h4 b5 14.a3 Rb8! .

9.f4 Be7 10.Qf3!

This aggressive set-up must be rather appealing to an attack-loving player like Sutovsky. White hopes that he has gained an improved version of the 6.Bg5 variation and Black must certainly proceed carefully.

10...Qc7 11.O-O!?

Dvoirys has wisely covered the e5-square and so Sutovsky introduces the possible idea of f5. More usual though is the very aggressive 11.O-O-O , intending 11...b5 12.Bxf6 Nxb3+ 13.axb3 Bxf6 14.Nf5! exf5 15.Nd5 , a strong piece sacrifice worthy of the 6.Bg5 variation. In A.Sokolov-J.Lautier, French Ch., Aix les Bains 2003 Black was blown away after 15...Qb7 16.Rhe1!? O-O? 17.Nxf6+ gxf6 18.Qg3+ Kh8 19.Qh4 Qe7 20.e5! , but he had to keep his king in the centre and, whilst far from clear, 16...Bd8! 17.exf5 Kf8 would have offered reasonable survival chances.

11...Nxb3?!

Tempting as 12.Nxb3 b5 is fine for Black, but Sutovsky has a much stronger idea in mind. Thus Black should prefer 11...O-O with a reasonable position; 12.f5 Bd7! holds e6 with some ease and ...b5 will begin counterplay.

12.axb3! Qc5 13.Rad1 e5?

Taking up the challenge, but this is rather foolhardy and Black should again have preferred to castle.

14.b4! Qa7 15.Bxf6 Bxf6 16.fxe5 dxe5 17.Nd5 Rf8!?

For his piece White has gained a colossal knight on d5 as well as very serious pressure down the f-file. Dvoirys hurries to cover f8 and offering to return some material was his best try, whereas 17...exd4 18.e5! and 17...O-O? 18.Nxf6+ gxf6 19.Qxf6 exd4 20.Rd3 had to be avoided.

18.Nc7+!? Ke7 19.Nd5+ Ke8 20.Qg3!

Having repeated the position to test Dvoirys' defence and rejected the a8-rook, it's time to introduce White's remaining forces into the attack.

20...Be6

20...exd4 fails to 21.Rxf6! gxf6 22.Qd6 , but Black is powerless against the exchange sacrifice in any case.

21.Rxf6! gxf6

Black never finds time to capture on d4 in the game, but White would also have decisively saved his knight with 21...Bxd5 22.Qxe5+ Be6 23.Rxe6+! fxe6 24.Qxe6+ Kd8 25.Kh1 (Sutovsky).

22.Nxf6+ Kd8 23.Qxe5 Kc8 24.Kh1 Rd8 25.Rd3

Sutovsky has conducted his attack from beginning to end with great energy and the end is finally nigh for Dvoirys' hapless pieces.

25...Bc4 26.Ne6!

Finishing in style as 26.Ne6! fxe6 (or 26...Bxe6 27.Rc3+ ) 27.Rxd8+ Kxd8 28.Qd6+ forces mate.

Game 33
Sandler, L. - Danailov, S.
Adelaide 1990

[ECO "B86"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 Nbd7 8.O-O Nc5 9.f4 Be7

Even those seeking a complex game with 7...Nbd7 are forgiven for sensibly developing, thereby rejecting the critical 9...Ncxe4!? 10.Nxe4 Nxe4 which both Kasparov and Short used against Topalov at Amsterdam 1996. The position is rather sharp and theoretical, but White certainly has dangerous attacking chances: 11.f5 ( 11.Kh1!? is a recent idea which avoids any counterplay down the a7-g1 diagonal; it's too early to give a theoretical verdict, but 11...Be7 12.f5 e5 13.Ne6!? fxe6 14.Qh5+ Kd7 15.fxe6+ Kc7 16.Rf7 Kb8 17.Be3 g6 18.Qh3 supplied an awkward bind and pressure for the piece in E.Najer-K.Kulaots, Cappelle la Grande 2004) 11...e5 12.Qh5 d5! Black must maintain the knight, (whereas Kasparov quickly got into trouble after 12...Qe7?! 13.Qf3 Nc5 14.Nc6! Qc7 15.Bd5 ) 13.Re1 Bc5 14.Rxe4 Bxd4+ was Short's accurate response. However, instead of Topalov's 15.Be3 , White can continue in aggressive mode with 15.Rxd4!? , although the latest analysis on this position by Arizmendi and Moreno suggests that Black has sufficient resources to eventually defend.

TIP: If you decide to adopt a very critical continuation in any sharp line of the Najdorf make sure you prepare and analyse it in some detail.

This position would be pretty scary to play without any preparation and Black must already find the accurate 15...Qb6! as 15...exd4 loses to 16.Bg5 Qd7 17.Re1+ Kf8 18.f6! .

10.e5!?

Critical, but another dangerous continuation is to simply complete development with 10.Qf3 O-O 11.Be3 , before advancing the g-pawn. Such a policy has blown Black away a few times and so he may wish to counter solidly by borrowing an idea from the Scheveningen with 11...Nfd7!? 12.g4 Re8 , intending to bolster the kingside by bringing the bishop around to g7.

NOTE: White would rather not have to advance his f-pawn with a knight on d7. That knight would come to e5 from where it controls a number of key square and greatly assists the defence.

10...dxe5

With the bishop on e7, Black has no option but to exchange, although in comparison with the 8.f4 9.e5 dxe5 10.fxe5 variation, White can no longer castle long which should restrict his attacking prospects.

11.fxe5 Nfd7 12.Qh5

Trying to combine attack with defence of e5, but Black hasn't forgotten about the vulnerability of f7 and e6: he had seen that here 12.Rxf7? fails to 12...Kxf7 13.Nxe6 Nxe6 14.Bxe6+ Ke8! .

As the game continuation appears to leave White with nothing better than a repetition, 12.Bf4 might be preferred, although 12...Nf8 , bringing the knight to g6, is a good riposte, while 12...O-O 13.Qg4 Nxb3! 14.axb3 Nf6! 15.Qd1 Bc5 16.Be3 Nd5 was also fine for Black in N.Kosintseva-A.Kosteniuk, Samara 2005.

12...Nf6!

Silvio Danailov is nowadays well known for being Veselin Topalov's manager, but he is also a noted theoretician in his own right. This important move fully neutralises White's threats as 13.exf6 Qxd4+ 14.Kh1 gxf6! leaves White struggling for sufficient compensation with ...Qe5 imminent to help Black regroup.

13.Qd1

Wisely settling for the draw, rather than be left with a fractured structure after 13.Qh4 Nfe4 14.Qf4 O-O! 15.Nc6!? Nxc3 16.Nxe7+ Qxe7 17.bxc3 .

13...Nfd7 14.Qh5 Nf6 15.Qd1

Game 34
Short, N. - Kasparov, G.
10th matchgame, London 1993

[ECO "B86"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 Nbd7 8.f4 Nc5 9.Qf3 b5 10.f5 Bd7 11.fxe6 fxe6 12.Bg5?! Be7 13.O-O-O O-O

White's play has been very direct thus far, but objectively it is not convincing; something which is not such an unusual situation in the sharpest lines after 7...Nbd7 . As we have seen 12.O-O would have been a superior try, while if Short wanted to go long, he would have allowed fewer resources by not exchanging on e6. However, the position remains tense and far from easy to respond accurately in at the board.

14.e5! Nfe4?

Short described this as a blunder and Kasparov certainly badly underestimated the forced queen sacrifice which follows. Black should instead reject materialism in favour of playing against the b3-bishop with 14...Nd5! 15.Bxe7 Nxe7 16.Qe3 d5 when he was doing rather well in N.Doghri-Dao Thien Hai, Budapest 1996. The b3-bishop has been locked out of play and Black has the simple plan of advancing his queenside pawns. White will probably have to sacrifice a piece in the centre, but that should be unconvincing and this accurate sequence is the refutation of Short's particular move-order in this game.

15.Bxe7 Qxe7 16.Nxe4!

TIP: Do not be scared of material imbalances and strong, experienced players often make good use of them. Here White will gain a rook, knight and strong d6-pawn for the queen which adds up to more than enough compensation.

16...Rxf3 17.exd6 Nxb3+ 18.Nxb3 Qf8 19.gxf3 Qxf3 20.Nec5

The dust has settled, leaving all White's pieces well placed and Black struggling to deal with Delroy, as Jonathan Rowson likes to refer to passed white d-pawns as.

20...Bc6 21.Rhe1 e5! 22.d7 Rd8 23.Rd6 a5

Kasparov has responded well to the situation and has prevented the white knights from wrecking immediate havoc. However, Short now regroups well and maintains control.

24.a3! a4 25.Nd2 Qg2 26.c3 Bd5 27.Nd3 Bb3! 28.Nxe5 Qxh2 29.Nc6! Qxd6 30.Re8+ Kf7 31.Nxd8+ Kg6 32.Ne6?!

The finish of this game was typical of many in the match; Short just couldn't quite get the ball in the back of the net. Here he should have exploited the black king's position to win on the spot with 32.Nf7! .

32...Qh2 33.Nf4+?! Kh6 34.Nd3 Qg1+ 35.Re1! Qg5 36.Ne5?

Short has reacted well to Black's desperate attack, but again missed a clinical finish in the scramble with 36.Rh1+! Kg6 37.Ne5+ Kf5 38.Nc6 .

36...g6 37.Rf1 Be6! 38.Nf7+ Bxf7 39.Rxf7 Qd5 40.Re7?

After this final error, the active black queen is able to force a draw. The win was, however, still present with 40.Ne4! , threatening a nasty check on h7; a theme which also decides after 40...Qd3 41.Rf2! Qxd7 42.Rh2+ Kg7 43.Rxh7+! (Short).

40...Qd6 41.Rf7 Qd3 42.Ne4 Qe3+ 43.Nd2 Qd3
