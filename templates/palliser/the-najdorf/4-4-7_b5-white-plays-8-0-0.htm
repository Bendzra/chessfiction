7...b5: White Plays 8 0-0

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 b5 8.O-O Be7 9.Qf3

The modern preference

The re-emergence of 6.Bc4 as a viable weapon in the late eighties was due to White's relying on piece play and this active queen development. The dangerous idea being to quickly gain launch a kingside attack, but without the use of the f-pawn. If that moves at all it will be to just f3, bolstering the defence of e4. Black for his part must aim to curb White's initiative, but with accurate play it can be fully neutralized, leaving Black with a reasonable position.

After 8.O-O , Black is advised to develop his kingside as 8...Bb7?! 9.Re1! prepares all manner of nasty sacrifices on e6, f5 and d5. In the late nineties the ambitious 8...b4!? did gain some attention, but is rather risky. 9.Na4 Nxe4? 10.Re1 d5 11.Bf4! gives White too strong an initiative, whereas Vega's 9...Bd7 10.Re1 Nc6 11.c3 Rb8 12.Nf3! , preparing to weaken the black structure with 13.Bf4 , slightly favours White.

How should Black develop sensibly?

After 8.O-O Be7 9.Qf3 ( 9.f4 transposes to a variation we considered via 8.f4 ), Black can opt for another provocative choice in 9...Qb6!? , which is the subject of Hamdouchi-Dominguez and which intends to pressurize e4 after 10.Be3 Qb7 . The resulting positions are rather complex and so the majority prefer to develop more solidly.

9...Qc7

Preventing the threat of 10 e5 and preparing to exchange off the powerful d4-knight.

10.Qg3

Forcing Black to castle?

With the threat of 10.e5 parried, the white queen poses new problems, but 10.Re1!?

Hoping for 10...Bb7? 11.Bxe6!

is also possible. However, this doesn't actually prevent 10...Nc6!? ( 10...O-O 11.Qg3 b4! 12.Na4 Bd7 is a good and more common alternative) as 11.e5? Nxd4! 12.Qxa8 dxe5 is a promising exchange sacrifice. However, 11.Nxc6 Qxc6 12.Bg5! , instead of transposing to our main line with 12.Qg3 , is pretty dangerous, but Black can and should sidestep problems on the d5-square with Kosten's 12...Ra7! , as opposed to 12...O-O? 13.Nd5! and 12...Bb7? 13.Bxf6 Bxf6 14.Bd5! .

10...Nc6

Black doesn't have to immediately free his position with this exchange and 10...O-O 11.Bh6 Ne8 12.Rad1 Bd7 is also fully playable:

(a) 13.f4!? is rather aggressive and was met by a promising exchange sacrifice for a huge bishop on e5 with 13...Nc6 14.f5 Nxd4! 15.Rxd4 Bf6 16.Rd3 Be5 17.Qg4 b4! 18.f6 g6 in A.Morozevich-G.Kasparov, Astana 2001.

(b) As White, Kasparov himself has preferred to stick to piece play with 13.Nf3 , although 13...a5 14.a4 b4 15.Ne2 Nc6 16.Nf4 Bf6 saw him gain a reasonable position against his own idea in N.Short-G.Kasparov, World Championship (Game 18), London 1993.

11.Nxc6 Qxc6 12.Re1!?

Beginning a battle of tempi as White starts making useful moves, hoping to be able to develop his bishop in one stroke to h6 after Black castles. Thus he avoids 12.Bg5 Bb7! (but not 12...O-O? 13.Nd5! ), while 12.Qxg7?! is best avoided as it permits Black to trade a flank pawn for a centre one with 12...Rg8 13.Qh6 Nxe4 14.Nxe4 Qxe4 15.f3 Qg6 .

12...Bb7 13.a3

Ruling out a later ...b4, although now is a better time to try 13.Qxg7 which is, however, still a rather risky and greedy choice as 13...Rg8 14.Qh6 O-O-O

A promising gambit

threatens to blast through to g2 with 15...d5 . 15.Qh3 prevents that, but 15...Kb8 16.f3 Rg6 17.Be3 Rdg8 18.Re2 h5! still left Black with good kingside chances and compensation in F.Nijboer-E.Sutovsky, Essen 2001.

13...Rd8!

Another useful waiting move

14.f3

Overprotecting e4 and now Black is out of useful moves and must castle. However, White can also try and aim for activity with the 14.a4!? of Gormally-Sutovsky, but he must avoid 14.Qxg7? Rg8 15.Qh6 d5! 16.exd5? Nxd5 17.Nxd5 Rxd5 when g2 falls.

14...O-O 15.Bh6 Ne8 16.Rad1

Centralization, and this appears more to the point than 16.Kh1 , although that did bring Short his only victory in the 1993 World Championship (Game 16). However, that was not due to Black's fully equal position in the early middlegame after 16...Kh8 17.Bg5 Bxg5 18.Qxg5 Nf6 19.Rad1 and now there was nothing wrong with Kasparov's doubling on the d-file, but 19...Qc5 would also have been rather level.

16...Kh8

Preparing exchanges

Black can also be more ambitious with 16...Bf6!? 17.Rd3!? a5 , but after 16...Kh8 he can claim equality. J.Bosch-R.Palliser, York 2000 continued 17.Bg5 Bxg5 18.Qxg5 Nf6 19.Rd3 (or 19.Qe3 Qc5! ) 19...Qc5+ 20.Qe3 Qxe3+ 21.Rexe3 Rd7! , doubling on the d-file and preparing to bring the king to e7. The game was shortly agreed drawn and the position is rather level, but Black can also play on, aiming to make some use of his extra queenside space.

Theoretical?

Not overly, although this does depend on Black's choice: 9...Qb6!? and 9...Qc7 10.Qg3 O-O being sharper and more theoretical than 9...Qc7 10.Qg3 Nc6 . However, even that sensible latter line can flare up if White is determined not to allow Black to steadily equalize and to emerge with a perfectly satisfactory position.

Statistics

White can be happy with his 53% from just over 1000 games with 8.O-O Be7 9.Qf3 . In response 9...Qb6 has picked up 47% from almost 400 outings when the initial success of 10.Bg5 is still reflected in its 58% from a surprisingly low 60 games. 9...Qc7 fares slightly better with 49% from 570 games; a score which rises to 52% after 10.Qg3 Nc6 11.Nxc6 Qxc6 12.Re1 Bb7 13.a3 Rd8 .

Game 37
Hamdouchi, H. - Dominguez, L.
Wijk aan Zee 2004

[ECO "B87"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.O-O b5 8.Bb3 Be7 9.Qf3 Qb6 10.Bg5!?

A startling response based upon the idea 10...Qxd4? 11.e5! . This initially enjoyed some success, but nowadays White is just as likely to prefer the older 10.Be3 . It would, however, be wrong to view the position after 10...Qb7 11.Qg3 as being less critical and complex than 10.Bg5!? . Black lags in development here and must already be careful with 11...Nbd7?! rather asking for trouble in the shape of 12.Rfe1 Nc5 13.Nf5! . 11...b4! 12.Na4 Nbd7 is far better, covering b6 and remaining happy to trade g7 for e4. After 13.f3 O-O White must find a plan and so usually aims to get his queenside pieces back into play with c3 or to erect a bind with c4. Thus he might try 14.Rac1!? , while 14.Rfd1 ( 14.Bh6? is a standard attacking device, but fails to 14...Nh5! 15.Qh3 gxh6 16.Qxh5 Qa7 when 17...e5 cost White a piece for insufficient compensation in J.Polgar-B.Gelfand, Dos Hermanas 1994) 14...Ne5 15.Ne2 a5! 16.c4 bxc3 17.Nexc3 Ra6! , covering b6 and so preparing 18...Bd7 , was roughly level in A.Zapata-L.Dominguez, Havana 2002.

10...O-O!?

Dominguez decides that he might as well leave his queen actively placed on b6 and keep b7 free for his bishop. However, he had to avoid the immediate 10...Bb7?! due to 11.Be3! Qc7?! 12.Bxe6! .

11.Rad1

NOTE: Black has 11.e5? indirectly covered here as the queen's position on b6 enables him to trap its counterpart with 11...dxe5! 12.Qxa8? Bb7 .

11...Nbd7

It might well be more accurate to prefer 11...Bb7!? when 12.Rfe1 Nbd7 13.Qg3 Nc5! 14.Bh6 Ne8 once again keeps control and leaves White struggling for a good continuation.

12.Qg3 Kh8!? 13.Rfe1 Bb7 14.Be3 Qa5 15.Bd5!?

Dominguez has invested a tempo avoiding Bh6 ideas and Hamdouchi decides that allows him time to open the position.

15...Nxd5?

This fails to quell White's pressure and so Black should have gone in for 15...exd5! . 16.Nf5 Nh5 17.Qh3! regains the piece and leads to a rather unclear situation after 17...dxe4! 18.Nxe7 Ndf6 19.a3! (Dominguez).

16.exd5 Nc5 17.a3

Going after d6 at the cost of complicating the position, whereas 17.Nf5! exf5 18.Bd4 Bf6 19.Bxf6 would have been rather unpleasant for Black who must then grovel with 19...Rg8 .

17...b4 18.axb4 Qxb4 19.Bf4 Qxb2?! 20.dxe6?!

Hamdouchi clearly wasn't having a good day, otherwise he would surely have found 20.Rb1 Qa3 21.Nb3! Rab8 22.Bxd6 Bxd6 23.Qxd6 when the offside black queen costs Black a clear pawn to extradite after 23...Rbc8 .

20...fxe6 21.Rb1 Qa3 22.Bc1?

He should have taken the pawn immediately when Black had no good way to exploit the bishop's position on f4, whereas now the black queen returns to the defence.

22...Qa5 23.Nxe6 Nxe6 24.Rxb7

Enabling the black queen to remain active, but perhaps Hamdouchi was still chasing a by now non-existent advantage. Instead he should have been looking to steer the game towards a draw with 24.Rxe6 Qc7! 25.Bf4 Rf6 26.Rxe7! Qxe7 27.Bg5 .

24...Bf6 25.Rxe6 Bxc3

White's rooks may look impressive, but they are in some danger of being stranded up the board, away from the defence of their monarch.

26.Qd3?! Be5! 27.Qd1 Rac8 28.g3 Qa1 29.Kg2?

Now White is undone along the second, but it's clear that he has already drifted. Dominguez has shown that he could still have defended, however, with 29.Ree7 Bd4 30.Re2 Rxf2 31.Rxf2 Rf8 32.Qf3! .

29...Qa2! 30.Ree7 Rxc2 31.Rf7

A king hunt now ensues, but Black would also have enjoyed a decisive one after 31.Be3 Rcxf2+! 32.Bxf2 Rxf2+ 33.Kh3 Rxh2+ 34.Kg4 Qc4+ 35.Kf3 Qc6+ 36.Ke3 d5! .

31...Rxf2+ 32.Rxf2 Rxf2+ 33.Kh3 Rxh2+ 34.Kg4 Qe6+ 35.Kf3 Qf5+

Taking control of e4 with 35...d5! was more accurate, but Hamdouchi isn't going to escape in any case.

36.Bf4 h5 37.Rb8+ Kh7 38.Qb1 Rc2! 39.Ke3 Bxf4+ 40.gxf4 Qh3+ 41.Kd4 Qc3+ 42.Kd5 Qc4+ 43.Kxd6 Qxf4+

Game 38
Gormally, D. - Sutovsky, E.
Gibraltar 2005

[ECO "B87"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 b5 8.O-O Be7 9.Qf3 Qc7 10.Qg3 Nc6 11.Nxc6 Qxc6 12.Re1 Bb7 13.a3

Instead 13.a4 b4 is fairly comfortable for Black, especially after 14.a5? O-O .

13...Rd8 14.a4!?

A rare continuation which sees White only open the queenside once the black rook has left the a-file.

14...O-O!

Sutovsky isn't impressed by White's queenside demonstration and ignores it. Black can also sharpen the struggle with 14...b4!? 15.Nd5!? exd5 when White has some initiative for the piece. However, Black can defend and should always remember that g2 is rather vulnerable: 16.Qxg7!? ( 16.exd5 Nxd5 17.Qxg7 Kd7 18.Qg4+ Kc7! returns the piece in return for a strong counterattack with 19.Bxd5 Qxd5 20.Rxe7+ Kb8 when White is forced to grovel with 21.Qf3 , but clearly Black has no problems at all after the exchange of queens) 16...Kd7 17.a5 Qc5 18.exd5 reveals why White advanced his a-pawn before sacrificing on d5. This is critical, although Black can still continue 18...Nxd5 19.Qg4+ Kc7! 20.Bxd5 Qxd5 21.Rxe7+ Kb8 and 22.Rxb7+!? ( 22.Qf3?! Qxf3 23.gxf3 Rhg8+ 24.Kf1 Bc6! - Golubev - is a little awkward for White) 22...Qxb7 23.Qd4 leads to a very unclear position.

15.axb5 axb5 16.Bh6 Ne8 17.Ra7 Ra8!

Neutralising any queenside pressure and leaving White without a good plan. Gormally thus tries to force a draw, but Sutovsky is having none of it.

18.Rxa8 Bxa8 19.Bf4 Nf6 20.Bh6 Ne8 21.Bf4 b4!? 22.Na2 Nf6 23.Bh6!

TIP: Black would be delighted to trade pawns with 23.Nxb4 Qb5 24.Nd3 Nxe4 . His extra centre pawns are much more influential and easy to advance in the middlegame than White's queenside duo. They will only become a factor in any endgame, and White must defend rather accurately to reach one!

23...Nh5! 24.Qg4 Qb5 25.f3

Neutralising one bishop, but now the other enters the game. However, White had to avoid 25.e5? dxe5 26.Qxh5 gxh6 27.Rxe5 Qb7 when his own king is actually the more vulnerable, especially with Black enjoying the vastly more active minor pieces.

NOTE: Impeded by the e6-pawn, White's light-squared bishop can remain a problem piece for him all game. He may thus try to improve it with c3, but that comes at the cost of helping Black to open the queenside.

25...Bf6 26.Rb1 Bd4+ 27.Kh1 Bxb2! 28.Bd2! Nf6 29.Qg3 d5 30.Bxb4

Now Black would still be slightly better after 30...Rc8 , but Sutovsky is unable to resist a tempting sacrifice. Practically this was certainly a good choice with move 40 approaching and Gormally having resisted well thus far.

30...dxe4!? 31.c4?!

He could actually have taken the rook, although it's not hard to see why White was rather scared of 31.Bxf8! Nh5! 32.Qd6 exf3 33.Be7 fxg2+ 34.Kg1 g5! 35.Qd8+ Kg7 when the g2-pawn is a large fishbone stuck in his throat and 36...Nf4 a decisive threat. However, with the accurate 36.c3! Qf5!? 37.Re1 Nf4 38.Qf8+ Kg6 39.Qxa8 it transpires that, with the b2-bishop blocked off, Black doesn't actually have enough compensation and that the white monarch can, with some care, slip away to safety.

31...Qf5 32.Bxf8?

Perhaps White thought that this was now playable as the queens must come off, but Sutovsky has some strong zwischenzugs. Instead 32.f4! was correct when 32...Bd4!? 33.Bxf8 Kxf8 34.Ba4! begins a counterattack just in time, while 32...Nh5 33.Qe3 Qf6! 34.Bxf8 Bd4 35.Be7! Bxe3 36.Bxf6 Nxf6 37.Rd1 (Kosten) should also favour White with his extra exchange, although Black retains chances due to his passed e-pawn.

32...Nh5! 33.Qg4

33.Qb8 looks rather strong at least until one notices 33...Ng3+! and 34.Qxg3 ( 34.hxg3 Qh5+ 35.Kg1 Bd4+ leads to mate) 34...exf3 forces White to part company with his queen.

33...exf3 34.Qxf5 fxg2+ 35.Kg1 Bd4+! 36.Qf2 Nf4!

An aesthetic finish, once again rejecting the queen to threaten mate. White's only try would now be to create some luft with. 37.h4 , but 37...Ne2+ 38.Kh2 Bxf2 39.Bd6 g1Q+ 40.Rxg1 Bxg1+ forces an easily winning ending.
