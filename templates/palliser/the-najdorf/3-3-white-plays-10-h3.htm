White Plays 10 h3

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 Ng4 7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 10.h3

Forcing the knight to move

Was the knight so strong on g4?

The short answer is no and the knight is a much more powerful piece once it reaches e5. However, by driving it backwards White gives himself the option of maintaining his own knight on d4 with 10...Ne5 11.f3 Nbc6 12.Bf2 . That way he hopes to gain an improved version of the 10.Qd2 lines, while once again putting the onus on Black to find counterplay, rather than be forced into further unbalancing the position as 10.Be2 h5 does. White's resources after 10.Qd2 and 10.Be2 are far from run out, but 10.h3 has really caught his attention and is now by far his most popular choice.

10...Ne5

The natural retreat and Black's main option, although 10...Nf6 also has its adherents. This may appear a little slow but Black intends 11...Qb6 which he hopes contains the dual threats of 12...Qxb2 and 12...Nxe4 . White is yet to prove any advantage positionally against 10...Nf6 , such as 11.Be2 Qb6 12.Nb3 Nc6 13.O-O Ne5!

The other knight reaches e5

is fine for Black, and so tends to now prefer an aggressive approach as we'll examine in Rowson-De Firmian.

11.f3

This may look a little ugly, but it does usefully bring the dark-squared bishop back into play. However, White can also play more forcefully, aiming to gain a central clamp after the 11.Nf5!? Bxf5 12.exf5 of Svidler-Topalov.

11...Nbc6 12.Bf2

Supporting the d4-knight

12...Ng6!?

Black more usually prefers 12...Be6 , but after 13.Qd2 Nxd4 14.Bxd4 Qa5 15.a3 , White has a superior version of the 10.Qd2 variation and Black can easily be left slightly struggling for counterplay as in Akopian-Kramnik. With 12...Ng6 , Black reopens the g7-bishop's path and also keeps an eye on the f4 and h4 squares. It may be quite a rare move, but it has an excellent pedigree, having been used by 6...Ng4 specialists Judit Polgar and Jonathan Rowson, as well as by Boris Gelfand.

13.Qd2 Qa5!

Exploiting Black's last to gain activity, and preparing to wreck the white structure on c3 in the event of 14.Nb3 .

14.O-O-O Be6

The most active for the bishop, although Polgar recently preferred 14...Bd7?! . P.Svidler-J.Polgar, FIDE World Ch., San Luis 2005 continued 15.Kb1 Rc8 16.g3! when Black decided that she had to enter a forcing line to gain some play: 16...Nxd4 17.Bxd4 Bxd4 18.Qxd4 Qe5 19.Qf2 Rxc3 20.bxc3 Qxc3 was a typical Sicilian sacrifice, but Svidler defended fairly comfortably with 21.Rd3 Qa5 22.Qe3! Be6 23.Ra3

Black lacks enough attackers

23...Qb4+ 24.Ka1 Kd7 25.Be2 .

TIP: Exchange sacrifices on c3 to expose the opponent's king are often very strong in the Sicilian. Do though try to ensure that you have more attacking pieces ready to follow one up than there are defensive pieces of the opponent's.

15.Kb1

Perhaps Polgar didn't like the look of 15.Nxe6?! fxe6 16.Bc4! when Black appears vulnerable on the light squares, but Rowson has shown that his strong knights give him a good position after 16...Bxc3! 17.bxc3 Nce5 18.Bxe6 Nf4 .

15...Rc8

Building up on the queenside

16.Nd5

Wisely avoiding any ...Rxc3 ideas, whereas 16.Nb3 Bxc3! 17.Nxa5 Bxd2 18.Nxc6 Rxc6 19.Rxd2 would have been fine for Black in S.Tiviakov-L.Dominguez, Wijk aan Zee 2004 after 19...Bc4! .

NOTE: A bishop is usually superior to a knight on an open board in the endgame, but here the knight is well-placed to exploit Black's kingside spatial advantage. It will stand well on f4 or e5 and is the equal of the bishop.

16...Qxd2 17.Rxd2 Nxd4 18.Bxd4 Bxd4 19.Rxd4 Bd7!?

A complex endgame

Keeping pieces on and intending ...Bc6 and ...e6 in this unbalanced, if roughly level, ending which Black went on to win in D.Gormally-J.Rowson, British Ch., Scarborough 2004.

Theoretical?

10.h3 is all the rage among grandmasters at the moment and that will probably filter down to all levels of the game. Black should be ready for it and he must especially know his stuff if he intends the rather risky 10...Nf6 . 10...Ne5 appears easier to play so long as Black understands Topalov's ...Qa5+ and ...Nf3+ concept against 11.Nf5 , while 12...Ng6!? looks like a good response to the more solid 11.f3 Nbc6 12.Bf2 .

Statistics

White has scored an impressive 59% from 174 games with 10.h3 when both knight retreats make just 41%. 10...Ne5 has had twice as many outings as 10...Nf6 , but after the latter Black must be prepared for the sharp 11.Bc4 (68% from 14 games). Another line in need of more outings is 10...Ne5 11.f3 Nbc6 12.Bf2 Ng6!? (75% albeit only from 4 games), although Black must beware the critical 11.Nf5!? (70% from 48 outings).

White Plays 10 h3: Illustrative Games

Game 25
Rowson, J. - De Firmian, N.
Selfoss 2003

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 Ng4 7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 10.h3! Nf6 11.Qe2!

Ponomariov's aggressive idea, facilitating queenside castling and intending to blast through with e5. An equally uncompromising alternative to Rowson's choice is 11.Bc4!? when Black must be careful: 11...Nc6? 12.Nxc6! bxc6 13.e5 and 11...Nxe4 12.Nxe4 d5 13.Nb5! (Sammalvuo) are two routes to avoid. That leaves 11...Qb6! when 12.O-O!? continues to develop as quickly as possible and challenges Black's whole concept head on. The restrained and sensible option is 12...O-O (capturing on e4 was risky enough last move, but here it absolutely must be avoided: Black was blown away after 12...Nxe4? 13.Nxe4 Qxd4 14.Nxd6+! exd6 15.Qe2+! in V.Ivanchuk-A.Shirov, Wijk aan Zee 2001) 13.Nde2 (White should consider simply 13.Nb3!? , still with good chances for an edge as a well-timed Nd5 could be irritating as will be Kh1 and f4) 13...Qxb2! 14.Bb3 Qa3! and after 15.f4 Nc6 16.Kh1 , Stohl's 16...Nh5! would have left White struggling in G.Kasparov-J.Polgar, Wijk aan Zee 2000.

Instead 11.Qf3 Qb6 12.O-O-O Nc6 13.Nxc6 Qxc6 14.Be2 Qc5 maintains control over e5 and appears fully acceptable for Black.

11...Nc6!?

Permitting his structure to be split, but relying on the time it will take White to unravel his kingside to compensate. Black could still prefer 11...Qb6 , but after Rowson's 12.O-O-O Nc6 13.Nxc6 Qxc6 14.e5! dxe5 15.Bxe5 and 16.Qe3 White is better due to Black's compromised kingside.

12.Nxc6 bxc6 13.e5 dxe5 14.Bxe5 O-O 15.h4!

Strong and logical play, so typical of modern chess. White doesn't want to have to move his queen again immediately; instead he wants a deadly open h-file and so it is Black who has to keep the kingside closed.

15...g4 16.g3 a5!?

Trying to disrupt White's development, whereas 16...Qb6?! 17.O-O-O Be6 18.Bg2 Rfd8 19.Rhe1 h5 20.b3! Rac8 21.Na4 saw White consolidate his positional advantages in R.Ponomariov-J.Polgar, Benidorm (rapid) 2002.

17.Bg2 Ba6 18.Qe3 Rc8?

De Firmian is a leading Najdorf protagonist and is too active a player to have made such a move just to defend c6. He must have seen that the c-file was about to open when the rook stands quite well on c8. However, this leaves his queen short of squares; a factor which Rowson is quick to exploit. Black later improved with 18...Qd7! when White should consider trying to maintain a small pull with Rowson's idea of 19.Rd1!? Qf5 20.Rd2 . M.Hoffman-K.Kulaots, Budapest 2004 instead continued 19.Qd4 Qxd4 20.Bxd4 e5!? 21.Be3 Rfd8 22.Rd1 Rxd1+ 23.Kxd1 Nd5 and Black gained sufficient activity.

19.Rd1! Nd5!?

Trying to avoid the rather grim 19...Qe8 , but this active attempt isn't fully convincing.

20.Nxd5 cxd5 21.Rxd5 Bxe5! 22.Qxh6!

Fine technique and refusing to give Black a sniff of counterplay, unlike 22.Rxd8 Rfxd8 23.Qxh6 Rd6 24.Qg5+ Bg7 when the white king remains stranded in the centre.

22...Rxc2?

This costs Black a piece, whereas he could have fought on after 22...Qc7!? , intending 23.Qxa6 Qxc2 24.O-O Qxb2 25.Qxa5 Bf6 when the game continues.

23.Rxe5! Re2+ 24.Rxe2 Bxe2 25.Qg5+ Kh8 26.Qe5+ f6 27.Qxe2

Game 26
Svidler, P. - Topalov, V.
FIDE World Ch., San Luis 2005

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 Ng4 7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 10.h3 Ne5 11.Nf5!? Bxf5 12.exf5 Nbc6

Simply developing, whereas Black should avoid the pseudo-active 12...Qa5?! . With 13.Qd5! White exploits his light-square control and 13...Nbc6 14.Qxa5 Nxa5 15.Nd5 leaves leaves him with his desired bind on the position.

13.Nd5 e6 14.Ne3

A rare move; White usually flicks in 14.fxe6 fxe6 before 15.Ne3 . This has caused Black some problems in practice as indeed it did just two rounds before our main game in R.Kasimdzhanov-V.Anand. By analogy with Topalov's play, Black should continue 15...Qa5+!? ( 15...O-O 16.Be2 d5 17.O-O Ng6 18.c4! Nd4 19.cxd5 exd5 20.Bg4! was Kasimdzhanov-Anand when Black lacked sufficient activity to compensate for his vulnerable structure, although Sakaev's 16...Qe7! is probably better, refusing to advance the d-pawn and thereby create further weaknesses) 16.c3 Nf3+! 17.Qxf3 Bxc3+ . After 18.Kd1 Black might try 18...Bxb2!? ( 18...Qa4+ 19.Kc1 Bxb2+ 20.Kxb2 Qb4+ 21.Kc1 Nd4 isn't an immediate draw, unlike in our main game, due to 22.Qh5+ Ke7 23.Bc4! ) 19.Rc1?! ( 19.Qe4!? looks rather critical, but presumably the Topalov team have found something here; after 19...Bxa1 20.Qxe6+ Ne7 21.Bxd6 Qa4+! 22.Nc2 Qd7 23.Qxd7+ Kxd7 24.Bxe7 Kxe7 25.Nxa1 , just like in the game, a rook versus two pieces ending is reached in which Black shouldn't be worse as he has time to activate both rooks while White still has to bring all three of his pieces into play) 19...Bxc1 20.Nc4 Qxa2! and 21.Qf6 was I.Cheparinov-J.Ibarra Jerez, Roquetas de Mar 2004, when Black is better after Kosten's 21...Bb2! 22.Nxb2 Rf8 23.Qg6+ Ke7 . Notably the talented young Cheparinov is Topalov's second - now you know where his idea against Svidler came from!

14...Qa5+!? 15.c3 Nf3+! 16.Qxf3 Bxc3+ 17.Kd1 Qa4+ 18.Nc2!?

A very brave choice as Svidler opts to continue against the always well-prepared Bulgar. Instead he could have settled for an early draw after 18.Kc1 . This appears risky as following 18...Bxb2+ 19.Kxb2 Qb4+ 20.Kc1 Nd4 , there is no check on h5 this time, but 21.Qd1! , intending 21...Rc8+ 22.Nc4 , defends and leaves Black with nothing better than 21...Qc3+ 22.Kb1 Qb4+ 23.Kc1 Qc3+ .

18...Bxb2 19.fxe6 fxe6 20.Qb3 Qxb3 21.axb3 Bxa1 22.Nxa1

Thus an ending has arisen in which Black has a rook and two pawns against two minor pieces.

NOTE: In the middlegame with queens on, two pieces are usually superior to a rook and pawn as they can be used to attack, but the advantage changes hands in the endgame. There extra pawns become more important and the rook is very fast at moving around the board.

22...Ke7 23.Bd3 Rac8

Svidler's two extra minor pieces are at least both bishops but he never really finds a way to fully coordinate his four pieces which he could have begun with 24.Nc2! .

24.Re1?! Nd4! 25.f3 Rc3?! 26.Kd2 Rhc8 27.Rb1?!

Topalov is now allowed to regroup effectively and White could have taken his chances, which shouldn't be too bad, in the piece versus three pawns ending arising from 27.Bc4! R8xc4 28.bxc4 Rxc4 29.Bf2 .

27...R3c5! 28.b4 Rd5! 29.Bf2 Kd7 30.Be3 Nf5 31.Bf2 Nh4!

Exchanging a bishop off and fixing g2.

32.Bxh4 gxh4 33.Nc2 h5 34.Re1 Rg8 35.Kc3?!

Continuing his brave stance, but this was rather risky in the scramble when 35.Re2 Rdg5 36.Ne1 was available as a good and solid alternative.

35...a5!? 36.Bc4?

Blundering into a costly pin, whereas the game would have continued, and with some active counterplay for White, after 36.bxa5! Rc5+ 37.Kb4 Rxg2 38.Nd4 .

36...Rc8 37.Ne3 Rb5!

Intending to win the bishop after 38 bxa5 d5 and forcing the win of the b-pawn.

38.Kd3 Rxb4 39.Bxe6+!?

A last throw of the dice but the rook ending is winning easily enough for Topalov after what was earlier a very complex ending.

39...Kxe6 40.Nc2+ Kd5 41.Nxb4+ axb4 42.Re7 b5 43.Rh7 Rc3+ 44.Kd2 Rc4!

Game 27
Akopian, V. - Kramnik, V.
Wijk aan Zee 2004

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 Ng4 7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 10.h3 Ne5 11.f3 Nbc6 12.Bf2 Be6 13.Qd2!

NOTE: White has no intention of exchanging on e6. The central control which Black then gains far outweighs the theoretical disadvantage of the doubled e-pawns, which there is also no way for White to immediately attack.

13...Nxd4 14.Bxd4 Qa5 15.a3 O-O

Kramnik realises that he won't be mated down the h-file in the short-term, but this is still a rather risky choice. However, the black king isn't especially well-placed in the centre, preventing the king's rook from getting into the game, but 15...Rg8?! 16.h4 O-O-O also failed to solve Black's problems in I.Gaponenko-J.Miloradovic, Belgrade 2001 after the energetic 17.hxg5 hxg5 18.b4! Qc7 19.b5! .

16.h4 Ng6 17.hxg5 hxg5 18.b4!

A deep and impressive concept. As White may go long this appears weakening, but Akopian realises that he can delay castling for a while. Much more important is that he can now regroup his minor pieces and generate pressure by bringing his knight to d4.

18...Qc7 19.Ne2 f6?!

The bishop can now retreat, but this is too passive and also blocks in the g7-bishop. Black must always strive to continue playing dynamically in the Najdorf and so he should have preferred either 19...Be5!? 20.Bxe5 dxe5 21.Qxg5 Rfd8 (Akopian) with some compensation or 19...Rfc8 20.Bxg7 Kxg7 (Sammalvuo) when the g6-knight keeps everything defended for the time being after 21.Qxg5 Qxc2 22.Qh6+ Kg8 .

20.Bb2! Bf7 21.Nd4 d5!

Finally gaining activity, but now Akopian castles queenside when it turns out that Black cannot achieve anything down the a2-g8 diagonal.

22.exd5 Qe5+ 23.Be2 Qxd5 24.O-O-O! Rfc8?!

TIP: If the opponent has an advanced pawn formation in front of their king, always try to further chip away and weaken those pawns.

Kramnik continues to play rather slowly, whereas 24...a5! 25.b5 Rfd8 26.Bd3 Qc5 would have offered Black much better counterplay.

25.Bd3 Ne5 26.Be4 Qa2 27.Nf5! Nc4 28.Qc3 Rc7?

Defending e7, but presumably overlooking Akopian's spectacular finish. Instead Black had to grovel with 28...Qxb2+! 29.Qxb2 Nxb2 , although after 30.Nxe7+! Kf8 31.Nxc8 White's much more active pieces still leave him with a large advantage after 31...Nxd1 ( 31...Rxc8 or 32.Rd7 ) 32.Bxb7 Rxc8 33.Bxc8 Ne3 34.Bxa6 as Akopian has analysed.

29.Rh7!! Qxb2+

This doesn't save the black king, but neither would have 29...Kxh7 30.Nxe7+ Kh6 31.Rh1+ Bh5 32.g4

Even the relatively best 29...Nxb2 30.Rxg7+ Kf8 31.Qxb2 Qxb2+ 32.Kxb2 e6 33.Rxf7+! would have been truly atrocious for Black.

30.Qxb2 Nxb2 31.Rxg7+ Kf8 32.Rh1
