7...b5: White Quickly Attacks

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 b5

Seizing space

This is generally a less ambitious approach from Black against 6.Bc4 than 7...Nbd7 , although 7...b5 can hardly be described as a passive move. Black seizes queenside space and prepares to generate pressure down the long diagonal against e4. He is thus well placed to gain active counterplay when White advances his f-pawn and 7...b5 has been the regular choice of leading Najdorf experts, Boris Gelfand and Emil Sutovsky.

Should White advance his f-pawn?

As we've already learnt, early 6.Bc4 exponents aimed to reopen the long diagonal with a quick f4-f5 and so would meet 7...b5 with 8.f4 . Such a plan has, however, fallen from grace as Black can gain more than sufficient counterplay and so White usually nowadays prefers 8.O-O ; the subject of our next section.

8.f4

Advancing in the centre

Introducing ideas of e5 as well as f5, but Black can respond much more easily against this than he can after 7...Nbd7 . Should White avoid 8.O-O these days, he may well prefer active piece development with 8.Bg5 ; a system which has recently become gained some attention, along with its sister variation 7...Nbd7 8.Bg5 , and which is the subject of Papa-Karjakin.

8...Bb7!

WARNING: Black didn't play 7...b5 just to immediately race ahead with 8...b4 . He wants to gain control of the position before playing ...b4, whereas 8...b4 9.Na4 Nxe4 10.O-O and 11.f5 offers White good attacking chances in return for his pawn.

One must always be careful when moving the bishop away from the defence of e6, but here Black can bring the bishop to powerfully bear on the long diagonal as 9.Bxe6? fxe6 10.Nxe6 isn't convincing after 10...Qc8 11.Nd5 Nxd5 12.exd5 Kf7! (Kosten).

9.O-O

White is still happy to see 9...b4 after this when he will respond with 10.e5! . However, an important alternative is the old main line, 9.f5?! , although those familiar with Ermenkov-Portisch shouldn't have a problem handling the 9...e5 10.Nde2 Nbd7 of Byrne-Fischer.

9...Be7

Which pawn to advance?

10.e5!?

Opening the position, but once again the resulting weakness of e5 is more important than White's attacking prospects. 6.Bc4 author Golubev considers such a strategy to be dubious, but it is not clear what else White can do:

(a) 10.Bxe6? fxe6 11.Nxe6 doesn't work when the a7-g1 diagonal is open as Black gains a key tempo to defend after 11...Qb6+ .

(b) 10.f5 e5 11.Nde2 Nbd7 transposes to Byrne-Fischer.

(c) 10.Be3 is less ambitious, but with the e7-bishop able to recapture on f6, it's time for 10...b4! . 11.Na4 Bxe4! is then rather unconvincing for White, but 11.e5 bxc3 12.exf6 Bxf6 13.bxc3 O-O

Black enjoys a superior structure

is fairly comfortable for Black who should be able to neutralize any pressure against his centre.

10...dxe5 11.fxe5 Bc5 12.Be3 Nc6

Black's main choice, but if he is uncomfortable with the position reached at move 16, even though that is in his favour, he can prefer the Kosten Variation with 12...Bxd4!? , seeking a simpler life:

(a) 13.Bxd4 Nc6

White cannot hold e5

14.Rf4 Qc7! is perhaps not so simple, but does favour Black, whereas 14.exf6 Qxd4+ 15.Qxd4 Nxd4 16.fxg7 Rg8 17.Rad1 Nxb3 18.axb3 Rxg7 19.g3 Rd8 gives Black a promising ending in view of his superior minor piece.

(b) 13.Qxd4!? Qxd4 14.Bxd4 Nc6 15.Bc5 sees White prepare to sacrifice his e-pawn for good play on the dark squares, but it can be neutralized by 15...Nd7! 16.Ne4 Nxc5 17.Nxc5 Na5 (Kosten).

13.exf6 Bxd4 14.fxg7!?

Critical, whereas Black is once again very comfortable with his strong bishop after 14.Qe1 Bxe3+ 15.Qxe3 Qd4! .

14...Bxe3+ 15.Kh1 Rg8 16.Bxe6!? Rxg7!

White is struggling

The position remains quite sharp, but Black, despite his loose position, is a piece up and should be able to unravel while maintaining a large advantage after 17.Bxf7+ Rxf7 18.Qh5 Qd7 , 17.Qf3 Qe7 18.Bd5 Nd4! or 17.Bd5 Qb6 , preparing ...0-0-0.

Theoretical?

Black can counter f4-f5 with a good general understanding of how to prevent White from gaining his desired grip on the position. He must though ensure that he is happy with one of the two possible variations after 8.f4 Bb7 9.O-O Be7 10.e5 . For a long time theory ignored 8.Bg5 , but that is a dangerous weapon and one which Black must be ready for.

Statistics

Black should be happy with his 51% from just under 3000 games with 7...b5 . Unsurprisingly that rises to a huge 70% from 57 games with 8.f4 Bb7 9.f5 e5 , but White's score with 9.O-O Be7 10.e5 (42% from 65 games) is hardly impressive either. Perhaps 8.f4 just isn't too accurate and so we shouldn't be surprised that it has become almost extinct at grandmaster level, unlike 8.Bg5 with which White has made a much better 54% from almost 300 games.

7...b5: White Quickly Attacks: Illustrative Games

Game 35
Byrne, R. - Fischer, R.
Sousse Interzonal 1967

[ECO "B87"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 b5 8.f4 Bb7 9.f5?! e5 10.Nde2 Nbd7

TIP: Remember that control of d5 is all important and so Black should develop, rather than allow 10...Nxe4? 11.Bd5 .

11.Bg5

Once again White is after control of d5 and so there is no need for him to castle just yet, although 11.O-O Be7 12.Ng3 Rc8 13.Bg5 would transpose back into the game.

11...Be7 12.Ng3 Rc8!

Now you know where Portisch gained his inspiration from and counterplay down the c-file is essential. Instead 12...Nc5? 13.Bxf6! Bxf6 14.Bd5 Bxd5 15.Nxd5 is just the sort of dream position White hopes for and which must never be allowed.

13.O-O

This doesn't contribute to the battle for d5 and 13.Bxf6!? Nxf6 14.Nh5 is more critical. However, there aren't any prizes for guessing Black's next move: 14...Rxc3! . After 15.bxc3 Bxe4 16.Nxg7+!? Kf8 17.Nh5 Rg8 18.Nxf6 Bxf6 Black may not yet have a pawn for the exchange, but he possesses two powerful bishops, the safer king and a strong centre after 19.Qh5 d5

Byrne also tried 13.Nh5 at Sousse, although 13...Nxh5! 14.Qxh5 O-O was still comfortable for Black in R.Byrne-S.Bouaziz and 15.h4?! (too ambitious) (although so too was 15.Bxe7 Qxe7 16.Qe2 Nf6 17.O-O-O? Rxc3 18.bxc3 Nxe4 in M.Susnik-T.Kos, Bled 2001) 15...b4! 16.Nd5 Bxd5 favoured Black after 17.exd5 Nc5 , but 17.Bxd5 would have dropped the c-pawn.

13...h5!!

Flank advances may be fairly common today, but back in the sixties this move would have come as a huge shock to Byrne. However, Fischer's idea is very strong as Black both prevents Nh5, thereby maintaining control over d5, and seizes the kingside initiative himself.

14.h4!?

In the years that followed some stubborn White players tried to avoid this weakening advance, preferring 14.Bxf6 Nxf6 . However, this didn't bring them anything but more suffering after 15.Nd5 (or 15.Qf3 Rxc3! 16.Qxc3 h4 ) 15...h4! 16.Nxf6+ gxf6! when e4 falls leaving Black with a rather powerful attack.

14...b4 15.Bxf6

Continuing to complicate the position as 15.Nd5 Nxd5 16.Bxd5 was impossible due to 16...Bxg5 17.hxg5 Bxd5 18.Qxd5 Qxg5 (King).

15...Bxf6 16.Nd5 Bxh4 17.Nxh5 Qg5 18.f6 g6!

18...Rxh5 19.Rf5 Bf2+! would have seen Black emerge a pawn ahead, but Fischer refuses to permit any counterplay and realises that the knight will effectively be trapped on g7.

19.Ng7+ Kd8 20.Rf3 Bg3 21.Qd3

The only defence, although the black initiative will still continue to grow.

21...Bh2+ 22.Kf1 Nc5 23.Rh3 Rh4!

Playing for the attack, although Black also doesn't mind exchanges which further serve to highlight the plight of the g7-knight.

24.Qf3 Nxb3 25.axb3 Rxh3 26.Qxh3 Bxd5

The d5-knight falls and with in the game, albeit a game which Byrne has never really been in ever since Fischer's powerful 13...h5! .

27.exd5 Qxf6+ 28.Ke1 Qf4

Game 36
Papa, S. - Karjakin, S.
Lausanne Young Masters 2003

[ECO "B87"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 b5 8.Bg5!? Be7 9.Qf3

Threatening 10.e5 and Black must move his queen to prepare 10...Bb7 in response.

9...Qc7

WARNING: Black must avoid preventing 10.e5 with 9...Bb7? due unsurprisingly to 10.Bxe6! fxe6 11.Nxe6 and 12.Nxg7+ .

The resulting play after this cannot be described as dull, but 9...Qb6!? is even sharper. 10.O-O now transposes to the 8.O-O Be7 9.Qf3 Qb6 10.Bg5 of Game 37, but White can also prefer 10.O-O-O . W.Hendriks-L.Van Wely, Dutch League 1999 revealed that Black must tread carefully and 10...Nbd7 11.Rhe1 O-O (and not 11...Nc5? when 12.Nf5! is a crushing sacrifice to gain the d5-square and to open the e-file after 12...exf5 13.Bxf6 gxf6 14.Nd5 Qb8 15.exf5 ) 12.Qg3 Nc5 13.Bh6 Ne8 14.Nf5!? was a very creative piece sacrifice. Black should defend with 14...exf5 15.exf5 Bf6! ( 15...Qd8? 16.Rxe7! Qxe7 17.Nd5 Qd8 18.Re1! Ne6 19.fxe6 fxe6 20.Bg5 left Van Wely in serious trouble) 16.Rxe8 Rxe8 which Emms has analysed down to a draw after 17.Bxg7 Nxb3+ 18.axb3 Bh4! 19.Qxh4 Kxg7 20.Qg5+ Kh8 21.Qf6+ Kg8 .

10.e5!?

White insists on 10.e5 in any case and initially this dangerous weapon of Karsten Mller's enjoyed a fair amount of success before Black established some good defences. Instead 10.Bxf6 Bxf6 11.e5 Bxe5 is a promising exchange sacrifice for Black, but White's older move is 10.O-O-O . 10...O-O? 11.e5! Bb7 12.exf6 is a strong queen sacrifice for three pieces and so Black should follow Gelfand's example with 10...Nbd7! , intending 11.Bxe6? Ne5 and to neutralise White's pressure with ...Nc5. 11.Rhe1 O-O 12.Qg3 Nc5 13.Bh6 Ne8 14.h4 Kh8! saw Black defend without too much difficulty in G.Hernandez-B.Gelfand, Merida 2003, and 15.Bg5 Nf6 16.e5 dxe5 17.Qxe5 Qxe5! 18.Rxe5 Bd6 19.Re2 Bc7 20.a3 Bb7 left Black comfortably placed and with two well-placed bishops.

10...Bb7 11.exd6 Bxd6 12.Qe3 Bc5

Eyeing a sacrifice on e6 which Karjakin immediately takes steps to prevent. Black had previously been too casual with 12...h6? 13.Bxf6 gxf6 in D.Stellwagen-A.Naiditsch, Wijk aan Zee 2003 and was punished after 14.Bxe6! fxe6 15.Nxe6 Qe7 16.O-O-O Kf7 17.Rhe1 Be5 18.f4 Qxe6 19.fxe5 Qxe5 20.Qb6 which left White with a crushing attack.

Black can also choose to opt to block the e-file with 12...Be5!? . This may appear a little strange after 13.O-O-O O-O 14.f4 , but Black realises that the extra white bishop on g5 won't be especially active. Furthermore, 14...Bxd4 15.Rxd4 Nbd7 16.Rhd1 Rac8 17.Kb1 Bc6! 18.Qe2 Qb7 (Wahls) successfully keeps the white pieces at bay, leaving Black finally ready to proceed on the queenside by advancing his a-pawn.

13.O-O-O! Nc6!

Wahls' 12...Be5 stems from his dropping a pawn against Mller (in the 1997 German Championship) with 13...Nbd7? 14.Bxe6! when 14...fxe6 15.Qxe6+ Kf8 16.Qxf6+! gxf6 17.Ne6+ would have forced a favourable gain of active rook and two pawns for two pieces.

14.Bxf6 gxf6 15.Nd5!?

Trying to continue playing actively, but it may well be that Karjakin's accurate move-order actually refutes Mller's whole 10 e5 concept. White's problem is that 15.Bxe6 is refuted by 15...Bxd4 16.Rxd4 Nxd4 (whereas 15.Ne4 Bxd4 16.Rxd4 Nxd4 17.Nxf6+ Kf8! 18.Qxd4 Rd8! 19.Qh4 Qe5 saw Black's accurate defence leave White struggling to find compensation for the exchange in E.Lobron-I.Novikov, Bad Wiessee 1999).

15...Qd8 16.c3

Wahls had also suggested this idea for White, but Karjakin isn't at all afraid to inflict an IQP on Papa in place of the previously played 16...Na5 .

16...Nxd4! 17.cxd4 Ba7 18.Nf4 Qd6

The knight has managed to retreat and to defend g2, but Black still enjoys a raking bishop-pair and by castling long will avoid any accidents on e6 or f6.

19.Nh5?

Losing the d-pawn, but quite possibly White's collapse was due to disappointment as to how his aggressive opening had been refuted. 19.Kb1 O-O-O 20.f3 Bxd4 would also have cost a pawn, but at least one bishop could then have been exchanged with 21.Qc1+ Kb8 22.Ne2 .

19...O-O-O! 20.Nxf6? Bxd4

It's not worth playing on an exchange down and White must lose one as 20...Bxd4 21.Qh6 ( 21.Qg5 h6 22.Qg4 Qe5 is also pretty strong) 21...Qc5+ 22.Kb1 Qf5+ wins a piece.

0-1
