The Karpov Approach

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.Be3 Be6 10.Qd2

White develops classically

This classical set-up from White became popular in the early eighties, especially with the then World Champion, Anatoly Karpov. Karpov has always been renowned for his prophylactic play and so it should come as no surprise that this system is all to do with restraining Black's counterplay. After 10...Nbd7 Black will have completed his development and would like to begin queenside operations with ...b5. Karpov decided that 11...b5 must be prevented with 11.a4 when 11...Rc8 prepares ...Nb6-c4 after which Black can recapture on c4 with his rook, generating pressure against e4 and down the c-file. However, that plan in turn can be nipped in the bud with 12.a5 after which Karpov intended to further increase his bind. Thus Rfd1, Qe1 and then Nc1-a2-b4-d5 became his aim.

Why does White now seek other positional paths?

After a number of painful losses Black got his act together against the pure Karpov system and started to hold his own in the manoeuvring battle that ensues. Ideas with an early ...Nc5 have also established themselves as good options and so let's take a look at the early moves after 7...Be7 to see why White often now prefers an early Nd5.

8.O-O

WARNING: Against 8.Be3 , Black should continue to copy with 8...Be6 as 8...O-O allows White the dangerous option of 9.g4!? .

In practice 8.Be3 Be6 will often transpose back to our main line after 9.O-O O-O , although 9.Nd5 has been gaining some attention of late and is dealt with in the notes to Svidler-Polgar.

8...O-O 9.Be3 Be6 10.Qd2

Should White be looking for a queenside majority, following an exchange on d5, he will often now prefer an immediate 10.Nd5 which is the subject Svidler-Polgar. In the unbalanced structures which result Black's kingside majority presents him with reasonable chances, although he must be careful not to neglect White's queenside ambitions by rushing ahead too quickly on the kingside.

10...Nbd7

Continuing Black's harmonious development and avoiding 10...b5 when 11.a4! b4 12.Nd5 Nxe4 13.Nxe7+ Qxe7 14.Qxb4 is slightly in White's favour as his queenside play and bishop-pair is more important than Black's central pawn presence.

11.a4 Rc8

The c8-rook is well placed

Plans involving ...Nc5 are currently very popular against the Karpov approach. Black aims to follow up an exchange of knights on c5 by gaining some essential queenside space with ...c4. He can opt for an immediate 11...Nc5 or implement the plan on one of his next two moves as we examine in Leosson-Gallagher. However, Black shouldn't be tempted to gain the bishop-pair with 11...Nb6?! 12.a5 Nc4 13.Bxc4 Bxc4 when White has traded off his none too active bishop and increased his control over d5. A.Karpov-M.Quinteros, Lucerne Olympiad 1982 was a model display and after 14.Rfd1 h6 15.Nc1 Rc8 16.Nd3 Be6 17.Bb6! Qe8 18.f3 Nd7 19.Be3 f5 20.exf5 Rxf5 21.Nb4 Nf6 22.Nbd5 Nxd5 23.Nxd5

A position to be avoided

White had achieved his goal and clearly controlled the position.

12.a5 Qc7 13.Rfd1

Leko has dabbled in 13.Rfc1 , but this should not be a problem for Black after either 13...Nc5 or 13...Qc6 , fighting for control of d5. 14.Bf3 Bc4! usefully obstructs the white c-pawn in the event of 15.Nd5 Nxd5 16.exd5 Qc7 , but Black has also done well in practice after 15.Ra4 Rfe8 16.Rb4 Qc7 17.Nd5 Nxd5 18.exd5 when both 18...Nf6 and 18...f5 grant him good play.

13...Rfe8!

This is an important move, the strength of which only came to be appreciated after Black suffered in several games with 13...Rfd8 . Now Bb6 ideas are no longer a problem, while the rook is well-placed on e8 to support a later ...e4; either after an exchange on d5 or as the tactical point which may make a ...d5-break work.

14.Qe1 h6!

Black counters prophylactically

Preventing any Bg5 ideas and intending to meet 15.Nc1 with 15...Bf8 16.Nd3 d5! . Black has developed very sensibly thus far and has neutralized White's quest for an advantage as we will see in Zude-Kersten.

Theoretical?

Both the Karpov approach and the early Nd5 ideas are rather positional and Black must thus understand the key ideas for both sides. He must know one or two move-order tricks, such as meeting 8.Be3 with 8...Be6 , but in general understanding is much more important than any memorization here. The main line of the Karpov approach remains positionally quite complex and so many may prefer to copy leading Najdorf expert Joe Gallagher with a ...Nc5 approach.

Statistics

White has scored 53% from just over 500 games with 10.Qd2 , but Black has a good score with ...Nc5 antidotes. That idea is yet to be played in that many games, despite its good reputation, largely because the Karpov system is now rarely seen in grandmaster chess. If White wants a positional struggle then he tends to prefer 9.Kh1 or a line with an early Nd5, although he has only managed 45% from just under 80 games with 8.Be3 Be6 9.O-O O-O 10.Nd5 Nbd7 11.Qd3 .

The Karpov Approach: Illustrative Games

Game 1
P. Svidler - J.Polgar
Dos Hermanas 1999

[ECO "B92"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O

The related 8.Be3 Be6 9.Nd5!? is a fairly recent idea which contains a touch of venom. Black must avoid greed with 9...Nxe4?? due to 10.Bb6 and 11.Nc7+ , whereas after 9...Nbd7 he shouldn't be worried by 10.Nxe7 on account of 10...Qxe7 11.f3 d5 when he has achieved his ideal break. Instead 10.Qd3 O-O ( steering well clear of the exchange on d5 as 10...Bxd5 11.exd5 O-O 12.g4! is White's idea when it is White, not Black as usually occurs after an exchange on d5, who has gained the kingside initiative; here 12...e4!? may well not actually be so bad for Black, but 12...Nc5?! 13.Nxc5 dxc5 14.O-O-O e4 15.Qd2 Bd6 16.g5 Nd7 17.h4 certainly was in A.Khalifman-B.Gelfand, FIDE World Championship, Las Vegas 1999 as Black badly lacked counterplay.) 11.O-O transposes back into Svidler-Polgar, but the other idea behind the 9.Nd5 move order is to have the option of 11.c4 , when it looks as if White's bind promises him an excellent game.

TIP: When it appears that White is set to gain full control of the position and thus a large advantage, always look for active counterplay, even at the cost of a pawn.

11...b5! is an essential but also strong counter which has halted many players' interest in 9.Nd5 . White is loathe to exchange his central e-pawn for the one on b5 and so usually continues 12.cxb5 axb5 13.O-O , although after 13...Bxd5 14.exd5 Nb6! 15.Bxb6 Qxb6 16.Qxb5 Qa7 Black enjoyed full compensation for the pawn in P.Svidler-B.Gelfand, Haifa (rapid ) 2000. He has promising Benko-style queenside pressure and will also be able to tie White to the defence of d5.

8...O-O 9.Be3 Be6 10.Nd5 Nbd7!

Covering b6 and f6 is once again the most flexible move as Black still need not worry about an exchange on e7, after which White cannot both defend e4 and prevent 12...d5. However, White's next does threaten to exchange on e7, after which d6 would become rather vulnerable, and so Black is finally forced to exchange on d5.

11.Qd3 Bxd5 12.exd5 Nc5!

An important concept and one which ensures Black of a good game. Polgar rightly doesn't fear an exchange on c5 as she would then blockade the d5-pawn and be able to utilise her useful kingside majority.

13.Qd2

Trying to retain queenside chances like this fails to impress, but Black is also very comfortable after 13.Nxc5 dxc5 14.Rfd1 when he can do even better than bring his knight to d6. Instead 14...e4! 15.Qd2 Bd6 16.a4 Qc7 17.g3 Rae8 18.a5 Nd7 , intending ...Ne5 and ...f5, saw Black build up a strong attacking position in

D.King-W.Browne, Reykjavik 1990.

NOTE: White may possess a passed d-pawn and the bishop-pair, but after the exchange on c5 he often struggles to find any play, whereas Black has a clear-cut plan on the kingside.

13...Nfe4 14.Qb4 a5!

Gaining a useful tempo as Polgar begins a strong mini-plan to keep the queenside closed against the white pieces.

15.Qb5?!

Svidler later improved here with 15.Qc4 with the idea of forcing Black to recapture on c5 with his knight, but after 15...Rc8 16.Nxc5 Nxc5 17.Qb5 f5 18.c3 White still didn't have any advantage when P.Svidler-A.Grischuk, Poikovsky 2005 was agreed drawn.

15...Qc7 16.Rfd1 b6 17.Qc4 f5 18.Bd3 Qd8

Preparing ...Bg5 and this forces Svidler into an exchange and the white position continues to be far from impressive.

19.Nxc5 Nxc5 20.a3 Rc8 21.Qb5

This has been condemned, but even if White had proceeded immediately with 21.b4 , he would still have come under some serious kingside pressure after 21...Nxd3 22.Qxd3 f4 23.Bd2 f3! .

21...e4 22.Bf1 Bf6!

TIP: Strong players are always looking to bring their pieces to their best squares. Polgar brings her bishop to the dominant e5-square, after which she will be free to improve her queen.

Now White perhaps had to try 23.Bd4 , although after 23...f4 the black initiative would have continued.

23.Rab1?! Be5 24.b4 axb4 25.Rxb4?! Nd7 26.Rc4 Rxc4 27.Qxc4 Qe8

Svidler's kingside was under some pressure even before he weakened his own queenside, and now Polgar's queen finally enters the attack.

28.Re1 Nf6?!

The knight should have remained on d7 to cover f6 with 28...Qg6 being a superior option. However, even after losing two tempi Black retains dangerous attacking chances, while it's notable as to how Polgar immediately recognises her mistake and wisely backtracks.

29.h3 Nd7! 30.Qc7 f4 31.Bc1?

Having been suffering all game, Svidler collapses. He had to try 31.Bxb6! when the position would have been far from clear, although after 31...Nxb6 or 31...Nf6 32.c4 Qg6 32.Qxb6 Bc3 33.Rb1 Qe5 Black's attacking pawn duo would still have left her with excellent practical chances of a decisive kingside breakthrough.

31...e3! 32.Bb5? exf2+ 33.Kxf2 f3!

Further ripping open the white king's defences and 33...f3! 34.Qxd7 Bg3+ 35.Kxg3 Qxe1+ would have been decisive.

Game 2
Leosson, T. - Gallagher, J.
Reykjavik 1998

[ECO "B92"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O Be6 9.Be3 O-O 10.a4

10.Qd2 Nbd7 11.a4 Rc8 (Black can even opt for the immediate 11...Nc5!? as was advocated by Tony Kosten; after 12.Nxc5 dxc5 13.Rfd1 Qc7 , Black has gained a tempo by omitting ...Rc8, although the white a-pawn is less of a target as it hasn't yet advanced on to a5) 12.a5 is an alternative move-order when 12...Qc7 transposes to the game, but 12...Nc5!? has been Topalov's choice against the Karpov approach and looks like a clean equaliser. Following 13.Nxc5 dxc5 White has tried 14.Qxd8 ( 14.Qe1!? c4! 15.Na4! is a more dynamic try when 15...Nxe4 16.Bf3 f5 17.Bxe4 fxe4 leaves the black structure a bit of a mess, but Black has the bishop-pair and a5 is still vulnerable; factors which were exploited by 18.Bb6 Qd5 19.Nc3 Qc6 20.Qxe4 Qxe4 21.Nxe4 Bf5 22.Rfe1 Bxe4 23.Rxe4 Bc5! to force a drawn rook ending in P.Leko-V.Anand, Cologne 2001) 14...Rfxd8 15.f3 but this isn't especially promising and 15...c4! is the key advance to gain space after which White must watch his a-pawn. Following 16.Kf2 Kf8 17.Na4! Nd7 18.Rfd1 Rc6 19.Nc3 Nf6! 20.Rxd8+ Bxd8 21.Ra4 Ne8 neither player was able to make any positive progress in P.Leko-V.Topalov, Wijk aan Zee 2005.

10...Nbd7 11.a5 Rc8 12.Qd2 Qc7 13.Rfd1 Nc5!?

Had this not been pioneered by Joe Gallagher, the likes of 11...Nc5 and 12...Nc5 would never have been seen. Furthermore, if Black wishes to employ such a set-up but also to retain winning chances then this move order must be advocated if only because the queens remain on.

14.Nxc5 dxc5! 15.f3?!

Shoring up e4 but this approach isn't especially challenging. Vlastimil Jansa, a leading advocate of the Karpov approach, has preferred an immediate 15.Qe1 , although it's not clear what he intends against 15...c4! 16.Bf3 Bb4 17.Bb6 Qc6 18.Qe2 Rfe8 19.Na4 Nd7! which maintained control of the key dark squares and full equality in P.Thipsay-J.Gallagher, Kuala Lumpur 1992.

Jansa has also tried 15.Bf3 , but then Black can ease any slight cramp with an exchange of rooks and 15...Rfd8 16.Qe2 Rxd1+ 17.Qxd1 c4 18.Bb6 Qd6 19.Qe1 Qb4! was fine for him in J.Lautier-B.Gelfand, Tilburg 1996.

15...Rfd8 16.Qe1 Rxd1 17.Nxd1

Gallagher had earlier demonstrated that 17.Qxd1 c4! 18.Na4 Bb4! slightly favours Black as a5 will always be weak in the long-term, but it's hard to believe that exchanging off the c-pawn is the way forwards for White.

17...c4!

NOTE: At the risk of repeating myself once again, it is absolutely imperative that Black follows up ...Nc5 with this liberating advance. Otherwise his pieces will simply find themselves passive as White slowly exploits the weakness of d5.

18.Rb1 h6 19.b4 cxb3 20.cxb3 Qc2 21.Rb2 Qc6 22.Bc4

Trying to reduce the pressure through exchanges, but this further weakens Leosson's structure. However, against 22.Qg3 , Gallagher had planned to increase the pressure with the neat 22...Nh5! , based on the point 23.Qxe5? Bf6 24.Qxh5 Bxb2 25.Nxb2 Qc2 .

22...Bxc4 23.bxc4 Rc7 24.Bb6 Rd7 25.Ne3 Bc5 26.Bxc5 Qxc5 27.Qc3 Nh5!

Bringing his final piece into play as Gallagher continues to improve his position. He may be chiefly known as a deadly attacking player - and does shortly finish in style - but thus far this has been a model positional, if also dynamic, handling of the black position.

28.g3 Nf4 29.Rd2?

Allowing a lovely reply, but after 29.gxf4 exf4 30.Kf2 fxe3+ 31.Qxe3 Qxa5 Black would have been a pawn up and still enjoying the vastly superior structure.

29...Rd3! 30.Rxd3 Ne2+ 31.Kf2 Nxc3 32.Rxc3 Qxa5 33.Rc2 Qa1 34.Ke2 a5

The active queen and passed a-pawn quickly prove to be too much for the white pieces to handle.

35.Nd1 a4 36.Nc3 a3 37.Na2 Qg1 38.Kd3 Qd1+ 0-1

Game 3
Zude, E. - Kersten, U.
Hofbieber 1996

[ECO "B92"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.Be3 Be6 10.Qd2 Nbd7 11.a4 Rc8 12.a5 Qc7 13.Rfd1 Rfe8 14.Qe1

The main try which sees White try to reduce the chance of a ...d5 or ...b5 break succeeding. His queen is less exposed on e1, while he can now consider improving his position with Rd2 and Qd1. Instead 14.Bf3 h6 15.h3 Bf8 16.Nc1 was seen in A.Marjanovic-J.Fedorowicz, Clichy 1986/7. The white knight is en route to a good square, but just for a moment isn't well-placed. Fedorowicz realised that and struck with 16...b5! , exploiting the white queen's position in the event of 17.axb6 Nxb6 18.Rxa6? Nc4 . Thus White had to put up with allowing the very useful ...b5 advance, but after 17.Nd3 another powerful move was in store for Marjanovic in 17...d5! . This pawn sacrifice is very thematic and desirable, enabling all the black pieces, as well as the e-pawn, to spring to life whilst White's huddle of minor pieces on the third rank are left looking rather uncoordinated. 18.exd5 Bf5 19.Ne1 Bd6 20.Na2 e4 saw both the white kingside and d-pawn severely weakened and Black had a favourable game.

14...h6 15.Bf3

TIP: When White covers e4 and d5 with Bf3, Black should exploit the bishop's absence from the f1-a6 diagonal by aiming for ...b5.

White can try to delay Bf3, but after 15.Rd2 Bf8 16.h3 Qc6! , challenging the key central squares, he lacks a better move than 17.Bf3 . Black will retreat with 17...Qc7 , threatening 18...b5 and if White repeats and Black doesn't want a draw then adopting the Gallagher plan with 18.Be2 Nc5 (is a good alternative to 18...Qc6 ).

15...Bf8 16.Nc1 b5!

With the knight on c1 and Black having made all his useful moves, Kersten decides that it's time to strike. This break appears very natural, although previously here Black had preferred 16...d5!? , giving up the d-pawn for activity and kingside chances after 17.exd5 Bf5 18.Qd2 Bd6 .

17.Nd3

With the queen on e1, 17.axb6 Nxb6 18.Rxa6 must be critical, but after (probably better is to defend b2 with 18.Nd3 Nc4 19.Bc1 , although Black is still active and can strongly consider 19...d5!? ) 18...Nc4 White lacks a good way to cover b2 and winning back that pawn will always ensure Black of at the very least a reasonable game. One try is 19.Ra7 Qb8 20.Nd3 when the black queen no longer pressurises c2 and 20...Nxb2? fails to 21.Rb1 , but Black still has a strong riposte. It's time for the other break and after 20...d5 21.exd5 Bf5 the threat of ...e4 is very tough to meet.

17...d5! 18.exd5 Bf5

Once again the ...d5 break has left the white position in a complete shambles and Kersten's position is both rather dangerous and pretty easy to play.

19.Be2 Bd6 20.g3?

White was in trouble in any case, but weakening the kingside light squares doesn't aid his cause. After the superior 20.h3 e4 21.Nc1 Black should continue to improve his pieces, slowly increasing the pressure, such as with 21...Ne5 , (whereas 21...b4?! 22.Na4 Qxc2 23.Nb6 begins counterplay).

20...e4 21.Nc1 Ne5 22.Nb3 Qd7!

Zude's knight is all set to find a strong square on d4, but it's arriving too late as Kersten pinpoints the kingside weaknesses.

23.Nd4 Bg4! 24.Ncxb5!?

This rather smacks of desperation, but White had clearly realised that he was in some trouble on the kingside in any case. Thus he decided to gain some counterplay, no matter what the cost, whereas 24.Bxg4 Qxg4 25.Qe2 Nf3+ 26.Kg2 b4 27.Na4 Nxd5 would have been the sort of position Zude clearly didn't want to grovel in. Black has regained his pawn and those kingside light-squares are still rather vulnerable, with ...Re5-h5 just one attacking option.

24...Bxe2 25.Qxe2 axb5 26.Qxb5 Nf3+

Having decided that he can stop the pawns, Black goes in for exchanges, but there was nothing wrong with 26...Qh3! 27.Qf1 Qh5 28.Bf4 Nxd5 29.Bxe5 Bxe5 , intending to keep the attack going with 30.a6 e3 .

27.Kg2 Nxd4 28.Qxd7 Nxd7 29.Rxd4?

Now white loses two queenside pawns, whereas 29.Bxd4 Rxc2 30.a6 Ra8 31.a7 would still have forced Kersten to display some technique.

29...Rxc2 30.a6 Rxb2 31.Rxe4

Continuing to go down without a fight, although after 31.a7 Ra8 32.Rxe4 Nf6 33.Rd4 Rb5 , d5 is dropping off and Black should be able to slowly approach and win the a-pawn, whilst avoiding any tricks.

31...Rxe4 32.a7 Re8 33.a8Q Rxa8 34.Rxa8+ Rb8 35.Ra6 Bc5 0-1
