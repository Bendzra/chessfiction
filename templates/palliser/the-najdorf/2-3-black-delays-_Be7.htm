Black Delays ...Be7

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.f3 Nbd7

Prioritising queenside development

Which king is safer?

By rejecting 8...Be7 , Black places a greater priority on beginning his queenside counterplay than on developing his kingside. This is actually quite natural with White already shaping up to castle queenside. Indeed the black attack can be very fast as 9.Qd2 b5 10.g4 Nb6 11.O-O-O? Nc4! reveals when White must either lose an important bishop or place his knight on the very ugly a1-square after 12.Bxc4 bxc4 . Furthermore, Black shouldn't worry about keeping his bishop on f8 for a little while as delaying castling can actually hinder White's kingside ambitions.

With 7...Be6 8.f3 Nbd7 Black strives for an early initiative and the prudent white player will amend his plan to counter that. Thus accelerating the white attack with 9.g4 has recently replaced 9.Qd2 as White's most popular option, although White can also switch plans and play positionally with 9.Qd2 b5 10.a4 .

7...Be6 8.f3 Nbd7 9.Qd2

9.g4!? is a critical alternative and one which Black must be prepared for. Traditionally he countered with the Kasparov preferred 9...b5 10.g5 b4! when 11.Ne2 is the subject of Ni Hua-Palliser and the slightly more positional 11.Nd5 that of Borriss-Palliser. However, a solid and less theoretical alternative has become popular, namely 9...Nb6!? .

Controlling c4 and d5

Black avoids touching his b-pawn and so the b3-knight will never be able to reach c6 following a trade of knights on d5. Furthermore, 10.Qd2 walks into 10...d5! and so White must prefer 10.g5 when Black unveils another slightly surprising move in 10...Nh5 .

NOTE: A knight on h5 slows down White's kingside pawn advance, whilst the knight isn't so badly placed. It can later come to f4 and Black will often initiate kingside play of his own with ...f5.

After the usual and logical 11.Qd2 Be7 12.O-O-O O-O 13.Kb1 Rc8 14.Rg1 Black has a solid position and White has often struggled. 14...g6 , preventing any g6 ideas and preparing a later ...f5 as well as 15.Qf2 Nc4 , is Black's main continuation, but there also appears to be nothing wrong with 14...Qc7 , intending Topalov's 15.Qf2 Bd8! when 16.Qd2 Be7 repeats and 16.h4 g6 17.Nc1 f6! gives Black good play.

9...b5 10.a4!?

Changing direction as after 10.g4 Nb6! 11.g5 Nfd7 White simply can't rush ahead on the kingside. Furthermore, 12.Nd5 is well met by the waiting 12...Rc8! (but not 12...Bxd5?! 13.exd5 Rc8 14.Na5! ) when Black gains a good game, such as after 13.O-O-O Bxd5 14.exd5 Nc4 . Recently 10.O-O-O!? has gained some attention, intending 10...Nb6 11.Qf2! Nc4 12.Bxc4 bxc4 and then 13.Nc5 or 13.Na5!? . Allowing that isn't so bad, but Black has a good alternative in 10...Rc8!? , preparing an exchange sacrifice to detonate the white queenside in the event of Qf2, and 11.Kb1 Be7 12.g4 Nb6 13.g5 Nh5!

Preventing h4-h5 and g6

again halts White on the kingside, while Black can continue to go forwards on the other flank with ...Nc4.

10...b4 11.Nd5 Bxd5 12.exd5 Nb6!

The weakness of d5 forces White to part with his dark-squared bishop and it is now on the dark squares that Black will often find his counterplay.

13.Bxb6 Qxb6 14.a5 Qb7 15.Bc4

This is the position which White was aiming for. He intends to demonstrate that Black is in trouble on the queenside and must lose the b4-pawn after Ra4 and then Nc1-a2 or Nc1-d3. Indeed the pawn often does drop off, but by then Black will have gained dynamic counterplay by exploiting his trumps of an unopposed dark-squared bishop and extra central pawn.

15...g6!

Beginning kingside counterplay

This novelty of Kasparov's, in place of 15...Be7 , ensures Black of a good game as we'll see in Shirov-Kasparov and has led to White switching his attention away from 9.Qd2 b5 10.a4 .

Theoretical?

Black's play in this dynamic variation is often very thematic, but he must still know some theory. Meeting 9.g4 with 9...Nb6 instead of 9...b5 cuts down the amount that Black must ensure he knows, although 9.Qd2 b5 also possesses quite a large body of theory.

Statistics

Black has scored 47% from almost 500 games with 9.Qd2 b5 , although that rises to 50% from 37 games with the 2000 novelty 15...g6 . With the fashionable 9.g4 and then 9...b5 10.g5 b4 11.Ne2 White has managed 54% from just under 100 games and a huge 65% from 50 games with 11.Nd5 . Kasparov has, however, demonstrated that 9.g4 b5 is fully playable, whilst statistically 9...Nb6 also isn't Black's answer when he has made just 39% from a little over 100 games. These results may well be explained by 9.g4 being a fairly recent development and that the modern-day English Attack employing grandmaster is very well prepared. If Black also is, and has studied the key ideas after 7...Be6 8.f3 Nbd7 , then his statistics should begin to improve as 9.g4 continues to receive yet more outings.

Black Delays ...Be7: Illustrative Games

Game 15
[White "Ni Hua - Palliser, R."]
World Junior Ch., Athens 2001

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.f3 Nbd7 9.g4! b5!

Black must remain true to his policy of delaying the development of the f8-bishop. Indeed 9...Be7 10.Qd2 b5?! runs into trouble after 11.a4! b4 12.Nd5 Bxd5 13.exd5 Nb6 14.a5! when 14...Nbxd5? 15.g5 costs Black material.

10.g5 b4!

Refusing to meekly accept 10...Nh5?! 11.a4! b4 12.Nd5 Bxd5 13.Qxd5 when White is better.

11.Ne2!?

Ni Hua counters with another uncompromising move, whereas Black is delighted to see 11.gxf6? bxc3 12.fxg7 Bxg7 13.bxc3 Bh6! when White's shattered structure outweighs his extra pawn.

11...Nh5 12.Qd2 a5

Defending b4 and continuing Black's active policy. Instead 12...Be7 has been considered too slow ever since M.Adams-P.Svidler, Dos Hermanas, 1999. That game did much to bolster the popularity of 9.g4 , although after 13.Ng3 Nf4 14.h4! h6! 15.Bxf4 exf4 16.Nh5 Bxb3 17.axb3 things wouldn't have been so clear had Svidler preferred 17...Kf8!? .

TIP: This delaying ...Be7 approach contains a number of forcing lines, but there are still many rich and fertile positions to explore. These should contain new ideas to uncover and are certainly enjoyable to analyse.

13.Ng3!

Unravelling and ensuring that Black definitely won't be castling kingside in a hurry.

13...Nxg3 14.hxg3 a4 15.Nc1 Be7?

Having said 'a', Black must be prepared to say 'b' in this variation. Thus it was important to continue playing actively and to delay developing the f8-bishop. 15...Qa5! is the move when 16.Nd3 ( 16.f4 g6 17.Bg2 Bg7! 18.f5?! gxf5 19.exf5 Bxf5 20.Bxa8 Qxa8 is a promising exchange sacrifice for the light squares) 16...d5! sees Black immediately exploit his last and 17.exd5 Qxd5 18.Bg2 Qb5 19.Bh3 Bxh3 20.Rxh3 Be7 21.Rh4 f5! was far from clear, but certainly supplied good counterplay, in V.Anand-G.Kasparov, Linares 2002.

16.f4! Qc8!?

Controlling c4 and ensuring that the final part of White's intended Nd3-b4-d5 won't come with tempo. Black, however, still needs to gain some counterplay to avoid being squashed; hence his next.

17.Nd3 b3 18.cxb3 axb3 19.a3 Qb7 20.Nb4?

Fixing the queenside was understandable, if not White's only good path. However, this premature move allows counterplay against e4 and should have been prepared by 20.Bg2 when Black is in some trouble as 20...Bd8 leads nowhere good after 21.Nb4! Ba5 22.Rc1 .

20...Nc5! 21.Bxc5 dxc5 22.Nd5 Bxd5 23.exd5?!

I was intending to leave the king on d8 and to target whichever flank White's own king went to. Perhaps affected by the new, and too rapid, FIDE time control Ni Hua now plays too ambitiously and underestimated the latent dynamism in the black position, whereas Wells' 23.Qxd5! Qxd5 24.exd5 Bd6 25.Bb5+ Kd8 26.O-O would have maintained a pleasant edge.

23...Ra4! 24.Bg2 Bd6 25.Qe2?! Kd8!

NOTE: Tal once succinctly explained, with regard to king safety: 'if you can't get it then it isn't exposed!'

26.fxe5 Bxe5 27.O-O-O?

The final error which permits a neat finish, although White would also have been under pressure after 27.O-O Re8 28.Qd2 Qd7! .

27...Re8 28.Kb1 Qa7!

23...Ra4 didn't eye up the fourth rank, it also prepared this temporary and decisive doubling on the a-file.

29.Rhe1 Rxa3! 30.bxa3 Qxa3 31.Rd4 cxd4 32.Qb2 Qa5!

0-1

Game 16
Borriss, M. - Palliser, R.
European Club Cup, Rethymnon 2003

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.f3 Nbd7 9.g4 b5 10.g5 b4 11.Nd5 Nxd5 12.exd5 Bf5 13.Bd3 Bxd3 14.Qxd3 Be7

Following the exchanges, a typical race scenario remains and Black must balance his queenside advance with kingside defence and counterplay.

15.h4!?

15.Rg1 is an important alternative when White can meet ...f5 with an exchange as that won't leave a vulnerable h4-pawn behind. However, this approach appears less critical and 15...O-O 16.O-O-O f5! 17.gxf6 Nxf6 18.Nd2 a5 19.Kb1 Nh5 20.Rg4! Qd7 21.Rc4 Qf5! ensured Black of sufficient counterplay to hold the balance in P.Leko-G.Kasparov, Linares 1999.

NOTE: ...f5 is a key resource which both prevents White breaking through with h5 and g6 (or g6 first as a sacrifice and then h5) and gives Black good kingside play in the event of an exchange on f6.

15...O-O 16.O-O-O a5! 17.f4?!

Black won't allow this to reach f5 and now seizes the initiative after getting his knight to c5. Thus White should stick with his usual choice of 17.Nd2 when a second encounter between Leko and Kasparov (at the 2002 Bled Olympiad) saw 17...a4 18.Kb1 f5 19.f4!? (this makes more sense now that Black has had to play ...f5, although 19.Nc4 is also testing when Black should continue with 19...Qc7 20.Rhe1 Rfc8 ) 19...Qc7! 20.Nc4 exf4! 21.Bd4! Nc5 22.Bxc5 Qxc5 23.Rhf1 Rfe8 24.Rxf4 g6 when the bishop wasn't at all inferior to the knight as it would have been a monster on g7, whereas 25.Qd4 Qxd4 26.Rfxd4 h6! began good counterplay.

17...a4 18.Nd2 exf4! 19.Bxf4 Nc5 20.Qh3!

Wisely covering the light squares as 20.Qd4? b3! 21.cxb3 axb3 22.a3 Qc8! would have been superb for Black as 23.Kb1 Ne6! 24.dxe6 Qc2+ 25.Ka1 Rxa3+! is a thematic queenside mating device.

20...Qa5 21.Kb1 Qa6!

Now Black in turn takes control of a key light square (c4) and threatens to break through with 22...b3 23.cxb3 a3! .

22.Rhe1 Rfe8 23.Re3 Bf8! 24.h5? b3! 25.cxb3 Rxe3 26.Qxe3 axb3

Permitting White to close the a-file with a3 which is usually an important defensive device - hence why I was also considering the thematic 26...a3!? here - but that will only be temporary.

27.a3 Nd3 28.Nxb3 Nxb2!

One step to many on the kingside by Borriss has enabled my attack to gain a key tempo and this reaps the reward as the queenside is decisively blown open.

29.Kxb2 Qxa3+ 30.Kc3 Rb8! 31.Qd4

Desperation, but 31.Rb1 Rc8+ 32.Kd3 Qa6+ would have been curtains.

31...Qxb3+ 32.Kd2 Rb4 33.Qe3 Qxd5+ 34.Ke2 Qg2+

Game 17
Shirov, A. - Kasparov, G.
Linares 2004

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.f3 Nbd7 9.Qd2 b5 10.a4 b4 11.Nd5 Bxd5 12.exd5 Nb6 13.Bxb6 Qxb6 14.a5 Qb7 15.Bc4 g6 16.Ra4 Rb8 17.Qd3 Ra8

WARNING: Should Black have to win at all costs this line is fairly unsuitable. If that is so then it is advisable to deviate at move eight or with 9...h5!? .

15...g6 was debuted between the same players at Frankfurt (rapid) in 2000 where Shirov did indeed take the draw on offer here. This time he was clearly prepared and ready to take on his nemesis. Black can admittedly avoid the repetition with 17...Bh6!? , but after 18.Bxa6 Qxd5 19.Qxd5 Nxd5 20.Bd3 he is slightly worse and doesn't have many winning chances in view of the passed white a-pawn and the opposite-coloured bishops.

18.Qd2 Rb8 19.Nc1 h5!

Bringing the bishop to an excellent diagonal and now White faces a tricky dilemma over how to win the b4-pawn and where to place his king.

20.Nd3

20.Na2 Bh6 21.Qe2 O-O 22.Nxb4 transposes, but White has two other options: 21.Qxb4 ( 21.Qd3 O-O 22.Rxb4?! is rather greedy when 22...Qa7! traps the opposing monarch in the centre and 23.Rf1?! e4! 24.fxe4 Nxe4 25.Rxb8 Qxb8 26.Kd1 Nd2 was already pretty horrendous for White in P.Murdzia-A.Kosten, Bundesliga 2004) 21...Qa7 22.Qxd6!? is also quite gluttonous and rather abandons the white king to its fate on the dark squares. However, S.Karjakin-A.Naiditsch, Pamplona 2004 was agreed drawn after 22...Qe3+ 23.Kf1 Rxb2 24.Rb4! Ng4! 25.Qb8+ when White forces perpetual, rather than get mated after 25.fxg4?? Qf4+ 26.Kg1 Qd4+! 27.Kf1 Qd1+ .

20...Bh6!

Black wants to seize the initiative and to attack on the dark squares, rather than suffer after 20...Nxd5?! 21.Bxd5 Qxd5 22.Nxb4 Qb5 23.b3 .

21.Qe2

Also tricky is 21.Qxb4!? when Black must respond with the accurate 21...Qc7! (but not 21...Qa7? 22.Qxd6 Qe3+ 23.Kf1 Rxb2 which fails to simply 24.Ra1 ) 22.Qa3 (it's White's turn to have a plausible blunder in 22.Qc3? which permits 22...Nxd5! 23.Bxd5 Qxc3+ 24.bxc3 Rb1+ and 25...Rxh1) 22...O-O which threatens 23...e4 and 23.Nf2 Bc1! 24.Nd3! Bh6 is another repetition.

21...O-O 22.Nxb4 Qd7!

Deviating from the previously played 22...e4!? which remains a rather dangerous practical weapon, although objectively there may not be quite enough compensation after 23.O-O exf3 24.Qxf3! Ng4! 25.Nc6 Qxb2 26.Nxb8 Qxb8 .

23.Nc6!

Giving up the b-pawn rather than allow Kasparov's main idea with 23.b3 Qxa4! 24.bxa4 Rxb4 . Liquidating the queenside is then drawish, whereas 25.Bb3 Ra8 26.O-O Nd7 (Sutovsky) offers Black good compensation.

TIP: Amongst inexperienced players the queen is often treated with slightly too much respect. Here Black's rook and well-placed knight on c5 offers him full compensation for the queen due to his dark square control and excellent coordination.

23...Rxb2 24.O-O h4! 25.Bb3 h3 26.g3 e4!

Having weakened the white kingside, it is finally time to open the centre. White is under some pressure, but Shirov calculates well.

27.fxe4 Qg4!? 28.Qd3!?

Opting to continue in a very sharp and complex position, rather than allow perpetual with 28.Qxg4 Be3+ 29.Kh1 Nxg4 30.e5 Nf2+ 31.Kg1 Ng4+ .

28...Qg5 29.e5! dxe5 30.Rh4 e4! 31.Qd4 Ng4 32.Rxh6!

Finally settling for a draw as now White can safely capture on b2, unlike with 32.Qxb2?? Qe3+ 33.Kh1 Qe2 when he is mated.

32...Nxh6 33.Qxb2 Qe3+ 34.Rf2 Qe1+ 35.Rf1 Qe3+
