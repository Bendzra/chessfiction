The Old Main Line with 10 g4

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Be7 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.O-O-O Nbd7 10.g4

A powerful kingside advance

White's main move and a very dangerous one. The kingside pawns will now reach f5 and g5 from where they cramp Black on the kingside and herald a strong attack. However, Black remains very solid and it is not so easy for White to undermine e6 and f7, while there may also be some queenside counterplay for White to worry about.

10...b5 11.Bxf6 Nxf6

The usual recapture, although Fischer used to prefer the positionally risky 11...gxf6?! . One good idea then for White is 12.f5! Ne5 13.Qg3!? , keeping h3 free for the bishop and preparing strong pressure against e6. However, 11...Bxf6!? is playable when 12.g5 Be7 returns to the main line, but critical is 12.Bxb5!? when Black gains some compensation after 12...O-O due to the half-open b-file. Instead he shouldn't rush on to it with 12...Rb8?! as Sulskis has shown that 13.Bxd7+! Bxd7 14.g5 Bxd4 15.Rxd4 Qb6 16.Qd3 Qxb2+ 17.Kd2! actually favours White as does 12...axb5? 13.Ndxb5 followed by 14.Nxd6+ and 15.e5 .

12.g5 Nd7 13.f5

Black faces an important choice

This has almost fully superseded 13.a3 which is rather slow and allows Black good play with 13...Rb8! 14.h4 b4 15.axb4 Rxb4

White begins to regret playing a3

16.Bh3 Qc5! 17.Nb3 Qb6 and 18...Nc5 .

13...Nc5

Keeping open chances of a queenside counterattack, while Black must clearly avoid 13...e5? 14.f6! exd4 15.Nd5 . There is though a safer and important alternative in 13...Bxg5+ 14.Kb1 Ne5 when White will regain his pawn, but Black is left with a solid position as Astrom-Hellers reveals.

14.f6

Wedging a pawn in Black's throat and preparing to target f7 and g7, although Black's position remains pretty resilient and he does have the bishop-pair. Instead 14.h4 is another variation rarely seen these days due to Browne's 14...b4 15.Nce2 e5 16.Nb3 Nxe4! , neatly exploiting the tactics down the diagonal. 17.Bg2 Bb7 18.Qe3 d5 then favours Black, but even the superior 15.fxe6!? isn't too problematic due to 15...fxe6! 16.Nce2 g6! 17.Bh3 e5 18.Nb3 Rf8 .

14...gxf6 15.gxf6 Bf8

A very critical position

Black has retained his long-term trumps and threatens 16...b4 , but it is White's move and White is much the better coordinated. We will examine this important position further in Vasquez-Vallejo Pons and especially Perenyi's critical 16.Rg1!? .

Theoretical?

13...Bxg5+ has acquired a certain body of theory, but is generally less critical and theoretical than 13...Nc5 . General positional principles still play a part after 13...Nc5 , but Black must really know his stuff, especially with regard to 16.Rg1!? .

Statistics

White has a good score after 13.f5 (60% from almost 1000 games), but that is largely because it is far easier for Black to lose quickly. 13...Bxg5+ is fractionally the more popular move (445 games as opposed to 439 with 13...Nc5 ), but Black should beware that 14.Kb1 Ne5 15.Qh5 Qd8 may not be best; 16.h4! has then made a huge 78% from 40 outings. Instead the superior 15...Qe7 has scored 45% from 139 games. 13...Nc5 also allows White to make a big score with 14.f6 gxf6 15.gxf6 Bf8 16.a3 (71% from 19 games), but this position should be acceptable for Black so long as he is well prepared.

The Old Main Line with 10 g4: Illustrative Games

Game 41
Astrom, R. - Hellers, F.
Swedish Team Championship 1998

[ECO "B99"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Be7 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.O-O-O Nbd7 10.g4 b5 11.Bxf6 Nxf6 12.g5 Nd7 13.f5 Bxg5+ 14.Kb1 Ne5 15.Qh5!

Ensuring that White regains his pawn and leaving Black a choice of how to defend his bishop.

15...Qe7!?

Staying in touch with f7 and e6, but 15...Qd8 is much more popular when the queen won't be hit by Nd5 after White has regained his pawn on e6. For a long time White continued 16.Rg1 Bf6 17.fxe6 O-O 18.Bh3 g6 19.Nd5 which certainly looks pretty good for him. However, Black is very solid and the e5-knight is especially well-placed. After 19...Kh8 the position is actually roughly level, while the a8-rook may well enter the game via a7.

Realising that Black would like to evict the queen from h5 with ...g6, White has begun more and more to prefer 16.h4! Bf6 17.fxe6 O-O 18.Bh3 , intending to severely weaken the black kingside with 18...g6?! 19.Qe2 Kh8 20.h5! . Black should prefer 18...Kh8 , although after 19.Nd5 fxe6 20.Bxe6! White retains the better chances, such as with 20...Bb7 21.Rdf1! Qe8 22.Qd1 Bxd5 23.Bxd5 Rc8 24.Ne6 Rf7 25.b3! when he controlled the position in M.Pavlovic-Ki.Georgiev, Vrnjacka Banja 1998.

16.Nxe6

White's main move, whereas here 16.h4 is much less problematic as Black has 16...Bf6 17.fxe6 g6! 18.exf7+ Qxf7 .

White should though avoid 16.fxe6?! due to 16...g6 17.exf7+ Kxf7! 18.Qe2 Bg4! , forcing the exchange of queens. After 19.Qf2+ Qf6 20.Qxf6+ Bxf6 21.Be2 Bc8! 22.Rhf1 Kg7 23.Nd5 Bd8 24.Rf4 Ra7! the white knights might look impressive, but they weren't going anywhere while Black enjoyed the bishop-pair and an edge in A.Shabalov-F.Hellers, New York 1993.

Finally, 16.Rg1 is also fine for Black after 16...Bf6 17.fxe6 g6 18.exf7+ Qxf7 19.Qh6 Bg7 20.Qd2 O-O .

16...Bxe6 17.fxe6 g6 18.exf7+ Kxf7 19.Qh3

19.Qe2 has been considered less challenging, but Astrom later preferred it and 19...Kg7 20.Nd5 Qd8 21.Bh3 Ra7!? 22.Rhf1! Re8 23.Qf2 Rb7 24.Qd4 Kh6 25.Nf4! was unbalanced but favoured White in F.Hellers-R.Astrom, Swedish Team Ch. 1999. However, Black should prefer the more solid 22...Rf8 , intending only 23...Re8 after 23.Be6 , or even just 21...Rf8 when he is fine in both cases.

19...Kg7 20.Nd5 Qd8 21.Qg3

The black bishop is actively placed on the c1-h6 diagonal, but it can be hassled by h4. Hellers now elects to prevent that advance and so White might prefer 21.Qc3!? . However, 21...Ra7 keeps White out of c7 and introduces the option of doubling on the f-file. After 22.Qa3 Qb8!? 23.h4! Bh6 24.h5 a5 25.Be2 b4 26.Qg3 Rf7 27.hxg6 hxg6 White may have thought he was getting somewhere on the kingside in B.Vuckovic-H.Nakamura, Bermuda 2002, but the colossal e5-steed kept everything just about together and 28.Bh5 Qf8 29.Rhg1?! Bf4! forced some favourable exchanges for the American prodigy.

21...Bh4 22.Qc3 Ra7!

A key resource and now Astrom is able to target d6, but Hellers is correctly not afraid to sacrifice that pawn.

23.Qd4 Rf7 24.Nb4 Bf2! 25.Qxd6 Qxd6 26.Rxd6 Bc5 27.Rd5 Rhf8 28.Be2 Bxb4 29.Rxe5 Rf2

Black may be a pawn down, but he is very active and continues to enjoy the more energetic bishop. The opposite-coloured bishops and Hellers' useful long-term advantage of a kingside majority now ensure him of an easy draw.

30.Bd3 Bd6 31.Re6 R8f6 32.Rxf6 Kxf6 33.h3 h5 34.a4 bxa4 35.Bxa6 g5 36.Rf1

Game 42
Vasquez, R. - Vallejo Pons, F.
FIDE World Ch. Tripoli 2004

[ECO "B99"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Be7 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.O-O-O Nbd7 10.g4 b5 11.Bxf6 Nxf6 12.g5 Nd7 13.f5 Nc5 14.f6 gxf6 15.gxf6 Bf8 16.Rg1!?

This dangerous move, taking control of the g-file and preparing an exchange sacrifice on g7, has with good reason almost superseded all of White's older tries.

(a) 16.a3 prevents 16...b4 when 16...Rb8 is considered inaccurate due to 17.b4! . This looks ridiculous, but it is far from easy for Black to effectively break through on the queenside after 17...Na4 18.Nxa4 bxa4 19.Rg1 . However, Danny King's idea of 17...Nd7!? is a better try, while in practice Black usually prefers 16...Bd7 . After 17.b4 Na4 18.Nxa4 bxa4 , his rook will be able to come to c8 in one move and White rarely allows that:

(a1) 17.Rg1 h5 transposes to the note to White's seventeenth move in our main game.

(a2) 17.Qh5 Qa5! sees both queens take up aggressive squares and 18.Be2 b4 19.axb4 Qxb4 20.Rhf1 Rb8 21.Qxf7+! Kd8! is very unclear, but Black should have at least sufficient resources on the queenside.

(b) 16.Nf5? is the direct sacrificial try, but fails to convince after 16...exf5 17.Nd5 Qa5! when 18.Kb1 fxe4 and 19...b4 leaves White badly struggling.

(c) 16.Bh3 also prepares a sacrifice, but again not one which Black should fear and, as Fischer has shown, 16...b4! 17.Nd5 exd5 18.exd5 Bxh3 19.Rhe1+ Kd8 20.Nc6+ Kc8 21.Qxh3+ Kb7 is fine for him; the strong c5-knight keeps White at bay for long enough on the queenside, while the king's rook will be developed via g8.

(d) 16.Qh5 is the best of the alternatives to 16.Rg1 and may make a comeback should that ever falter. Due to the pressure against f7, 16...b4 17.Nd5! is less advisable here. Instead Black should prefer the solid 16...Bd7 when 17.a3 is line 'a2' above and 17.Bh3 b4! is now possible as Black can go long after 18.Nd5 exd5 19.exd5 . He also does with 18.Nce2 O-O-O! when 19.Qxf7 Bh6+ 20.Kb1 Rdf8 regains the pawn and holds e6 with rough equality.

16...h5!

This has become established as Black's best defence against a move described by Jonathan Rowson as 'mega-dangerous, abundantly sacrificial and anyone with a heart condition should steer clear of this as Black, even if the position is probably just unclear.' With 16...h5 , Black simultaneously prevents Qh5, gives himself the option of developing via h6 and takes control of g4. Instead 16...b4?! 17.Nd5! is rather risky in view of 17...Qb7 18.Rg7! and Shmuter's 17...exd5 18.exd5 Bd7 19.Rg7 O-O-O 20.Rxf7 Bh6+ 21.Kb1 Rdf8 22.Rxf8+! Rxf8 23.Ne6! .

17.Rg7

Critical, but Black can and must ignore the rook. White can also opt for the slower 17.a3 when Black should avoid 17...Rb8 18.e5! dxe5? 19.Qc6+ , intending 19...Qd7 20.Nxe6! (King). However, 18...d5 is risky, but appears playable as does 17...Bd7 when 18.Bh3 Qb7 (but not 18...Na4?! due to Barnsley's 19.Nxa4 bxa4 20.Kb1 Rb8 21.Rg5 Qb7 22.Qc3 , ganging up on the weak black pawns) 19.b4 Na4 20.Nxa4 bxa4 21.Kb1 a5! 22.c3 axb4 23.cxb4 Rc8 wasn't clear, but appeared to be acceptable enough for Black in H.Ollmann-G.Vetter, correspondence 1998.

Enders' move 17.Re1!? is another important alternative which has been endorsed by Luther. 17...Bd7 18.Kb1 b4 19.Nce2! isn't fully clear, but appears to be slightly in White's favour. However, 17...Bb7!? 18.Bh3 O-O-O might well be fully viable because of 19.Nd5 Qa5 20.a3 Bh6+ 21.Kb1 Qd2! . R.Kalod-D.Navara, Ostrava 2002 saw Black give up an exchange with 22.Rd1 Qxh2 23.Ne7+ Kb8 which led to a rather unclear position, but 23...Kc7!? , intending 24.Qc3 Kb6! may also be quite playable when the plan is to trap the e7-knight with ...Qe5 and ...Bg5.

17...b4!

WARNING: Black shouldn't even want to touch the rook in such a position: 17...Bxg7? 18.fxg7 Rg8 19.Qxh5 supplies plenty of positional compensation, but 19.Ndxb5! is a straight knock-out.

18.Nd5! exd5 19.exd5 Nd7!

Vallejo relies on bringing his knight to a strong outpost on e5 to hold up the white attack. For several years, Black players logically followed up 16...h5 with 19...Bg4?! but 20.Re1+ Kd8 21.Qf4 Kc8 22.Ne6! favours White after 22...fxe6 23.Rxc7+ Kxc7 24.Qxb4 e5 25.Qa5+ Kd7 26.Kb1 .

20.Nc6!

The knight is a monster here and clearly must be removed, whereas 20.Ne6 Qa5! 21.Kb1 Ne5 22.Qe4 Bd7 is considered less problematic.

20...Bb7!

Vallejo Pons has correctly realised that a large number of important battles in this variation are fought in the correspondence world. It is from there that this move comes and it is a big improvement over 20...Ne5? 21.Nxe5 dxe5 22.d6! .

21.Bh3

If White wants to prevent ...Ne5 then he must prefer 21.Re1+!? Ne5 22.Nxe5 dxe5 23.Bh3 , although Black appears to be fine here from a theoretical perspective after 23...Qd6! 24.Qg3 Qxf6 25.Qxe5+ Qxe5 26.Rxe5+ Kd8 27.Rxf7 Bc8! .

21...Bxc6 22.dxc6 Ne5 23.Bd7+ Kd8 24.Qe4 Qb6! 25.Be6! Kc7 26.Bxf7 Nxc6

Vallejo has accurately managed to remove the c6-pawn, whilst keeping his extra piece and already White may well be struggling.

27.Bd5+?

This doesn't convince and a vast amount of analysis has shown that White should prefer 27.Kb1! Rd8 28.Bd5+ Rd7 29.Qg2! when he maintains reasonable compensation and the position is still very unclear.

NOTE: The morals of this game appear to be that just one mistake can be decisive in this complex variation and that it may well be useful to stay up to date with the latest discoveries from the correspondence world.

27...Bxg7 28.fxg7 Rhg8 29.Bxg8 Rxg8 30.Qe6

Despite the black king position this is winning for someone of Vallejo's class without any real worries, but Black would also have been much better after 30.Qh7 Qe3+ 31.Kb1 Qe2 32.Rg1 Ne7 .

30...Rxg7 31.Qxd6+ Kb7 32.Qh6 Qc7 33.Qxh5 Rh7 34.Qg4 Qxh2 35.b3 Qe5 36.Kb1 Re7! 37.Rd7+ Rxd7 38.Qxd7+ Kb6 39.Qc8 Qd6 40.Qf5 Qd1+ 41.Kb2 Qd4+ 42.Kb1 Na7!

The white king is even more vulnerable than its counterpart and the arrival of the black knight on b5 spells the end for Vasquez.
