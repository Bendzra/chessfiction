Black Plays ...Be7

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.f3 Be7

Black prepares to quickly castle

Black classically decides to get his king to safety before beginning queenside operations. This can easily lead to quite a sharp race scenario, although there are also a number of positional nuances; foremost amongst those being White's once again aiming to exploit the weakened c6-square after ...b5. Black meanwhile still has important choices to make after 8...Be7 and indeed he retains the option of a solid set-up or going for adventurous counterplay.

9.Qd2 O-O

Consistent with Black's last and should he not wish to castle then 8...Nbd7 or here 9...h5 are the ways to do that. 9...Nbd7 10.g4 h6?! was once very popular, but has fallen from grace largely because after 11.O-O-O b5 12.h4 Nb6 13.Qf2! Nfd7 14.Kb1 Qc7 15.Nd5! Bxd5 16.exd5 Nc4 17.Bc1!

A solid white queenside

White's queenside is more than capable of standing up to an advance of the a-pawn. Furthermore, White enjoys the bishop-pair and has good prospects on the kingside and with f4.

10.O-O-O

Several options for Black

We've already seen that 9.g4 is premature and so too is 10.g4? when 10...d5! is once again rather awkward for White.

10...Nbd7

This has established itself as Black's most popular move in this millennium. Back in 1993 Danny King was rather enthusiastic about 10...a5 , with which Black aims to attack and to do so without weakening c6. However, the idea wasn't considered to have stood the test of time, but has recently received an injection of new ideas as Anand-Vallejo Pons reveals. Another risky choice is rushing straight ahead with 10...b5!? 11.g4 b4 as we'll see in Sax-Gallagher.

11.g4 Qc7

Completing Black's development and preparing to meet 12.g5 with 12...Nh5 to blockade the white h-pawn and this is more solid than 11...b5 12.g5 b4 13.Ne2 Ne8 .

12.Kb1!

White tends to improve his king position like this, rather than immediately go 12.g5 which is the subject of Van Kempen-Van Oosterom. He knows that Black must shortly advance his b-pawn to gain counterplay when Nd5, followed by Na5, becomes an important possibility.

WARNING: Black should not simply assume that White solely wants to mate him on the kingside. He can attack there, but is also interested in gaining a bind right across the board for which a knight on c6 is very useful.

12...b5

Black has more often preferred 12...Rfc8 , but practice has shown that 13.Qf2! b5 14.g5 Nh5 15.h4 is slightly in White's favour due to the strong idea of following up Nd5 and an exchange there with Bh3-g4.

13.g5 Nh5 14.Nd5 Bxd5 15.exd5 Nb6

Eyeing the c4-square as Black's counterplay arrives just in time.

16.Na5!? Nxd5 17.Qxd5 Qxa5 18.c4

A complex pawn sacrifice

For his pawn White has gained good central control and a useful bishop-pair; factors which he must use to force some weaknesses in the black position. He can also delay this positional sacrifice with 16.Rg1 which also leads to a rather unclear and complex position, as we will further consider in Kramnik-Vallejo Pons.

Theoretical?

This is the main line of the ...e5 English Attack and has been at the forefront of chess fashion for the past decade. Thus quite a body of theory has built up around it, while both sides should also be aware of a number of important nuances after the relatively solid 10...Nbd7 11.g4 Qc7 . Black can try to mainly get by with general principles, but some home analysis is recommended, especially if he intends to use the practically still dangerous, even if theoretically disapproved, 10...a5 or 10...b5 .

Statistics

Black has managed 48% from just over 1000 games with 8...Be7 9.Qd2 O-O . That falls to 45% after 10...a5 and to 43% with 10...b5 11.g4 b4 as White has now got his act together against those variations. With 10...Nbd7 11.g4 Qc7 12.Kb1 b5 Black is up to 48% from 37 games; reminding us that there is still much fertile ground to explore even in this popular variation. On the other hand 12...Rfc8 may make an impressive 57% from 53 games, but that isn't the way to go as Black's score falls to just 41% after 13.Qf2! .

Black Plays ...Be7: Illustrative Games

Game 18
Anand, V. - Vallejo Pons, F.
Monaco [blindfold] 2004

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.f3 Be6 9.Qd2 O-O 10.O-O-O a5!?

This was discredited for quite a few years, but has recently received some grandmaster attention.

11.Qe1!?

A novelty as Anand aims to misplace the black queen, in view of 11...a4 12.Nc5 , before locking the queenside with a4. That White shouldn't allow the a-pawn to advance can be seen from 11.Kb1?! a4 12.Nc1 a3 13.b3 Ra5! 14.g4 d5! when Black has an excellent position.

The theoretically approved method of preventing ...a4 is 11.Bb5 when Danny King's original suggestion of 11...Nc6 12.Kb1 Qb8 is rather unconvincing after 13.Nd5 or simply 13.a3 , preparing to halt and then win the black a-pawn on a4.

11...Nbd7 and this appears not to sufficiently trouble White's grip, but Black's new idea is to break that with an exchange sacrifice on c3. However, further tests are required to see if 12.Qe2 Rc8!? 13.Kb1 Rxc3! 14.bxc3 Qc7 is fully sufficient, although it's certainly easier for Black to improve his queenside pieces here than it is for White and 15.a4 Nb6 16.Bxb6 Qxb6 17.c4 Qc7 18.Kb2 Nd7! maintained good compensation in F.Berkes-R.Wojtaszek, World Junior Ch., Kochin 2004. Thus Black needs an improvement after 13.g4! if he is to make 11...Na6 work, but for the time being Wojtaszek's rare 11...Nbd7 and 12...Rc8 appears quite playable.

11...Na6!? heads for c7 to fight for the critical b5 and d5 squares when 12.Qe2 Qb8!? sidesteps Nc5 and shortly led to a comfortable draw for Black after 13.Kb1 Nc7 14.Bg5 Rc8 15.Bxf6 Bxf6 in P.Leko-F.Vallejo Pons, Linares 2004. However, the move to analyse must be 13.g4! and 13...Nc7 14.Ba4 Rc8 (but not the overly active 14...b5?! when 15.Nxb5 Nxb5 16.Qxb5 Qxb5 17.Bxb5 a4 18.Nd2 Bxa2 19.Bc4! Bxc4 20.Nxc4 Ra6 21.Rd3 gave White a pleasant endgame in E.Inarkiev-M.Freitag, Izmir 2004) 15.g5 Nd7 16.Bxd7! Bxd7 17.Nc5 Be6 18.N5a4 b5?! 19.Nb6 wasn't fully convincing from Black in A.Motylev-E.Inarkiev, Russian Team Ch. 2005, while 18.Nxe6 fxe6 19.g6! , as mentioned by Kosten, appears even better when f4, as well as Na4-b6, is problematic for Black.

11...Qc8

Black's reluctance to go to c7 is understandable, but still 11...Qc7!? could really do with a test, at least so long as 11.Qe1 remains popular, as 12.Nb5 Qc6! 13.Nxa5 Qc8 14.Kb1 d5 (Nunn) is a promising pawn sacrifice.

12.a4 Nc6! 13.g4! Nb4 14.g5

Preventing ...d5 and hoping to demonstrate that both black knights are misplaced. However, 14...d5 isn't yet a threat and so White can consider 14.Kb1!? Ne8 15.Qf2 when 15...b5!? 16.Nxb5 d5 was energetically rebuffed by 17.exd5 Bxd5 18.Nc3! Bxb3 19.cxb3 Qe6 20.Bc4 Qg6+ 21.Ne4! in S.Karjakin-L.Dominguez, Dos Hermanas 2005, intending 21...Nd6 22.Rxd6! Bxd6 23.h4 with a strong initiative. Wojtaszek has thus preferred to meet 15.Qf2 with 15...Nf6 , renewing the threat of ...d5. Marking time thus is not fully convincing and so perhaps Black could try 14...Bc4!? .

14...Nh5?!

The knight isn't well-placed here and so Black later preferred to hold his own on the kingside with 14...Ne8!? 15.f4?! f5! in Y.Quezada-A.Abreu, Yucatan 2004.

15.Kb1 Qc7?

Now White seizes control of the position and Black had to try the active 15...Bxb3! 16.cxb3 d5 . Presumably Vallejo didn't like the look of 17.Rxd5! Nxd5 18.Nxd5 Qd8 19.Bb6 , although Nunn feels that it isn't at all easy for White to further improve his position after 19...Qd7 .

16.Qf2! Nf4 17.Nb5 Qb8 18.Bxf4! exf4 19.N3d4 Bd7 20.Rg1 g6 21.Bc4

Simple chess has netted White a large advantage and now Black can do little but await his fate on the kingside.

21...Qc8 22.Bb3 Qc5 23.Qd2 Qe5 24.h4 Rac8 25.h5 Kg7 26.Qh2 Bxg5 27.Nxd6! Bf6 28.hxg6 fxg6 29.N6f5+ Kh8 30.Qxh7+!

Making excellent use of the raking b3-bishop to finish in style by forcing mate.

30...Kxh7 31.Rh1+

Game 19
Sax, G. - Gallagher, J.
Mitropa Cup, Baden 1999

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.f3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.Be3 Be7 9.Qd2 O-O 10.O-O-O b5 11.g4 b4!? 12.Nd5

A strategy which has grown in popularity in the English Attack in general is to accept a potentially misplaced knight with 12.Na4!? . This blockades the black queenside pawns, which can drop off, while the knight still hopes to later reach d5 via b6. 12...Nbd7! (Svidler's suggested improvement over 12...a5? 13.Nb6 a4 14.Nc5! b3! which looks rather scary for White, but the accurate rejoiner 15.axb3! Qxb6 16.Nxe6 axb3 17.Qc3 left Black in grave trouble in P.Svidler-Cu.Hansen, Groningen 1995) 13.Qxb4 d5 appears to offer Black good compensation and 14.Bc5 ( 14.Nac5 d4 15.Nxe6 fxe6 16.Qc4 dxe3 17.Qxe6+ Kh8 18.g5 Ra7 19.gxf6 Rxf6 is given by Gallagher and is rather unclear, while Black can also prefer to maintain compensation with 16...Qb6!? ) 14...Nxc5 15.Naxc5 Qc8! 16.Qb6!? dxe4 17.Nxe6 fxe6 18.Qe3 was the course of L.Van Wely-S.Van Blitterswijk, Amsterdam 2002. This is assessed as clearly favouring White in Sammalvuo's fine and definitive work on the English Attack, but I'd be surprised if Van Wely was to repeat this as Black appears to be doing quite well here, either with 18...Qc6!? or with the game's dangerous 18...exf3 19.Qxf3 Nd5 20.Qe4 Rf4! 21.Qxe5 Bf6 22.Qe1 Nb4 .

12...Bxd5 13.exd5 a5 14.Kb1 Qc7!?

A favourite of Gallagher's who wants his pieces and pawns to join in a coordinated attack. Black can also first further advance the pawns, but 14...a4 15.Nc1 Ra5!? 16.Bc4! Na6 17.g5 Nd7 18.Nd3 Nb6 19.Bxb6 Qxb6 20.f4! gave White the initiative and superior chances in S.Tiviakov-S.Bekker Jensen, Gausdal 2000 and is far from easy to improve on for Black.

15.g5 Nfd7 16.h4!

Time is clearly of the essence in such a sharp position and 16.Qd3? Rc8 17.Bh3 a4 18.Nd2 unsurprisingly allowed Black a pleasant choice in E.Anka-J.Gallagher, Geneva 1996. Gallagher opted for 18...Na6 19.Ne4 b3 , but also promising was 18...Ra5!? , intending 19...b3 20.a3 Rxd5! .

16...Nb6 17.h5 a4! 18.Nc1 Nc4 19.Bxc4 Qxc4 20.g6!

These sharp positions can be both hard to play and to assess. Hazai even criticised this move, but it is best and, after all, White can hardly defend the queenside for too long. Previously Gallagher had faced 20.Rhg1 when 20...b3! 21.cxb3 axb3 22.Nxb3 gives Black some great open files on the queenside, although before using them he should first secure the kingside with 22...f5! (Gallagher).

20...Nd7 21.gxh7+ Kh8

TIP: If an enemy pawn storm breaks through, the king may well be safest sheltering in front of an enemy pawn. Indeed a white pawn on h7 can prevent to an extent the rest of the attacker's pieces from creating a decisive attack.

General rules are useful in the English Attack, but here some very detailed analysis from Gallagher in 'New in Chess Yearbook 53' suggests that White should actually be better here because g7 is so weak. He thus also analyses 21...Kxh7!? 22.Rhg1 b3 23.cxb3 axb3 24.Rg4! and then a long piece of analysis reveals White to be emerge clearly ahead after 24...bxa2+ 25.Ka1 . However, readers should not give up on this variation which is great fun for Black to play. Firstly White must play rather accurately to get this far, and secondly this position may well be quite playable for Black and 21...Kxh7!? could certainly do with a test. Here 24...Qa6 is possible as is 24...Qc2+!? 25.Qxc2+ bxc2+ 26.Kxc2 Rfc8+ 27.Kb1 Nf6 , ganging up on d5.

22.Rhg1 b3! 23.axb3?

Sax is a vastly experienced player, but finding White's best defensive method was far from easy. Instead he should have aimed for shelter with 23.cxb3 axb3 24.Rg4! bxa2+ 25.Ka1 when his king is safe enough for the time it takes to break through on the other flank. The boot would then have been firmly on the other foot and Gallagher analyses 25...Qb5 26.Rdg1 Bf6 27.Bh6! with a crushing attack.

23...axb3 24.Nxb3 Qa4 25.Qg2 Bf6 26.Bg5?

White was in serious trouble on the queenside in any case, but this permits a beautiful finish.

26...Nc5! 27.Bxf6 Qa2+ 28.Kc1 Nxb3+ 29.cxb3 Rfc8+ 30.Kd2 Qa5+!

Everything comes with check and Gallagher has impressively worked it all out.

31.Ke3 Qb6+ 32.Ke2 Rc2+ 33.Rd2 Rxd2+! 34.Kxd2 Qd4+

Game 20
Van Kempen, H. - Van Oosterom, J.
correspondence 2004

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.f3 Be7 9.Qd2 O-O 10.O-O-O Qc7 11.g4 Nbd7 12.g5 Nh5 13.Nd5 Bxd5 14.exd5

14.Qxd5 is also possible, but controlling d5 with the queen is neither permanent nor worrying for Black and 14...b5 15.Qd2 Rfc8 16.Qa5 Qc6 isn't anything for White.

14...a5!

Now that there isn't a white knight ready to hop into b5, Black can advance his a-pawn to seize the initiative.

15.Kb1!

This should have been played three moves earlier, but it is important to allow the knight to retreat, whereas 15.a4? Nb6 16.Bb5 Nc4 17.Qc3 Rfc8 (Lautier) leaves White under pressure.

15...a4

Lautier had previously employed the slow 15...Rfc8?! , forgetting about White's unopposed bishop which sprung to life with 16.Bh3! Qd8 17.Bg4 in R.Fontaine-J.Lautier, French Ch., Val d'Isere 2002. Van Oosterom, en route to winning the 18th World Correspondence Championship, prefers to keep his rook on f8 and to gain counterplay before the bishop reaches h3.

16.Nc1 f6! 17.g6?!

Too ambitious, although it takes some fine play to show that, and White should have been more consistent with 17.gxf6 Rxf6 18.a3! when 18...Rxf3 19.Be2 Rh3 20.Bg4 Rh4 21.Be6+ Kh8 (Van Oosterom) offers White reasonable compensation for his pawn and is rather unclear.

17...hxg6 18.Qg2 a3! 19.Qxg6 axb2 20.Nb3!

Wisely hiding behind an opposition pawn once again, whereas 20.Kxb2? would have carelessly opened lines when Van Oosterom offers 20...Nc5 21.Nb3 Nxb3!? 22.cxb3 Rxa2+! 23.Kxa2 Qc3 24.Bb5 Ra8+ 25.Ba4 Qxe3 26.Qxh5 b5 , leaving Black with very dangerous compensation for the exchange.

20...f5! 21.Bh3 Nb6! 22.Qxh5?

Van Kempen did well to avoid 21.Qxh5? Rxa2 , but probably felt that he had to take the knight at some point as his queenside will shortly cave in any case. However, he should have preferred 22.Bxb6! Qxb6 23.Qxh5 when 23...Qa7 24.Kxb2 doesn't appear to lead to more than a draw, even after 24...Qa3+ 25.Kc3! Rac8+ 26.Kd2 .

22...Nc4 23.Bxf5 Rxf5 24.Qxf5 Nxe3 25.Qd3 Nc4!

Having played with great verve thus far, Black has no interest in regaining the exchange when he can instead continue the attack.

26.Nd2 Nxd2+ 27.Rxd2 Qa5 28.a3 Qc5 29.Kxb2 e4!

White has finally been forced into removing the b2-shield and is now undone on the dark squares.

30.Qb3 Bg5 31.Re2?

After this White cannot defend against the queenside threats allied to the strong black f3-pawn. He had to thus try and defend by offering to exchange the huge bishop with 31.Rhd1!? .

31...Bf6+ 32.c3 exf3 33.Rd2 Re8 34.Ka2 b5 35.Rc2 Kh7!

Instructively improving his king position before beginning the final wave of the attack to conclude a powerful and impressive performance from Van Oosterom.

36.Rhc1 Re3 37.Rb2 Bxc3! 38.Qxb5 Qd4 39.Rbc2 Kh6! 40.h4 Re2 41.Kb3 Bd2 42.Rf1 Qe3+ 43.Ka2 Bc3 44.Qb3 Qe4! 45.Rf2 Bd4

Game 21
Kramnik, V. - Vallejo Pons, F.
Monaco [rapid] 2004

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.Qd2 Be7 9.f3 O-O 10.O-O-O Nbd7 11.g4 Qc7 12.Kb1 b5 13.g5 Nh5 14.Nd5 Bxd5 15.exd5 Nb6 16.Rg1!?

Preparing to activate the rook along the fourth rank before playing Na5 and challenging Black to find a useful move.

16...Rac8

This rook more usually goes to b8 where it usefully defends b5 should White play a thematic Na5 pawn sacrifice, and 16...Rab8 17.Rg4 g6 18.h4 Ng7?! saw Black improve his knight in P.Leko-P.Svidler, Wijk aan Zee 2005. However, White doesn't have to sacrifice his d-pawn and Leko sensibly gained the advantage with 19.Bxb6! Qxb6 20.Na5 Rfc8 21.Nc6 . Svidler gave up the exchange to gain some much needed counterplay, but never had sufficient compensation. That, along with our main game, suggests that currently White is finding it easier to improve his position than Black does before Na5 is played.

17.Na5! Nxd5 18.Qxd5 Qxa5 19.Rg4 Qc7 20.Bd3 Qc6?

Trying to reduce the pressure, but the white bishop-pair becomes pretty irresistible after this queen exchange. Instead Black had to keep things tight with 20...Qd7! when it is not so easy for White to make progress. Advancing the a-pawn is much more risky with the queens still on, but after 21.Rh4 g6 the black knight keeps the white pieces at bay and an exchange sacrifice on h5 isn't fully convincing.

21.Qxc6 Rxc6 22.a4!

Regaining the pawn, while the white pieces now coordinate much better than their black counterparts.

22...bxa4 23.Rxa4 d5 24.Rxa6 Rc7?

Failing to take sufficient care of his centre, whereas Kramnik offers 24...Rxa6 25.Bxa6 d4! 26.Bd2 Rb8 when it is not so easy for White to make further progress.

25.c3! g6 26.Ra5 Rb8 27.Rxd5!

This may appear slightly risky, but Kramnik has matters under control.

27...Rxc3 28.Rxe5 Ba3 29.Bd4! Rcb3 30.Rd2 Nf4

The knight does a good job on h5 of blocking White's attacking intentions, but has been rather out of play for some time. It does now rejoin the action, but this is rather a case of too little, too late.

WARNING: One misplaced piece may not be felt early in the game, but the longer that the game goes on and the more other pieces which are exchanged, the more the misplaced piece is felt.

31.Bc4 R3b4 32.Rc2 Nd3 33.Ree2! Ra4 34.b3 Bf8 35.Re3!

More accurate play and, having consolidated, White can launch a strong attack of his with his bishop-pair.

35...Nf4 36.Be5 Rba8?

A blunder, but Black's cause was also helpless in the event of 36...Bg7 37.Bxf7+ Kf8 38.Rc7 Bxe5 39.Rxe5 Rab4 40.Bc4 .

37.bxa4
