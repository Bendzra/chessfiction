White Plays 10 Be2

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 Ng4 7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 10.Be2

Driving the knight backwards?

White's queen may well be best developed to d2, but it's clear that an immediate 10.Qd2 doesn't greatly improve the white position. Instead with 10.Be2 , White hopes that Black will retreat with 10...Ne5 when 11.Nd5 Nbc6 12.Nf5! gives White his desired grip on the position. However, we know that Black must aim to cause White early difficulties and to play as actively as possible, and so his next should come as no surprise.

10...h5!

No! Black instead threatens 11...h4

Continuing to try and dictate the pace, rather than retreat. White must now decide how to respond on the kingside. 11.h4 breaks up the black structure and if this worked then Black's last would be revealed as incorrect, but Black has his resources as we'll see in De la Villa Garcia-Hodgson. Another ambitious idea is 11.Nf5!? which should be met by 11...Bxf5 12.exf5 h4! ,

A promising pawn sacrifice

continuing to counter forcefully on the kingside and on the dark squares. After 13.Bxg4 hxg3 14.fxg3 Bxc3+ 15.bxc3 Nc6 , White may have won a pawn, but more importantly his structure is a mess while the black knight will be a monster on e5. White might thus prefer 13.Bxh4 , but 13...Qa5 ensures Black of a good game after the exchange on c3.

NOTE: A common mistake from White is 11.h3? which doesn't prevent 11...h4! . 12.Bxg4 hxg3 13.fxg3? Bxg4 costs him a piece after 14.Qxg4 Bxd4 , but 12.hxg4 hxg3 13.Rxh8+ Bxh8 14.fxg3 Nc6 is also horrendous for White, who is chronically weak on both the kingside and on the dark squares.

11.Bxg4

Rather than radically challenge Black on the kingside, White settles for plan 'b', namely to gain a bind on the position. He realizes that after 11...Bxg4 12.f3 Bd7 Black's light-squared bishop isn't especially useful for gaining dynamic counterplay, whereas White has maintained his knight on d4, unlike in the 10.Qd2 lines. 13.Bf2 Nc6 14.O-O e6 15.Nde2!

Black lacks sufficient counterplay

does indeed leave White better as Black is unable to generate sufficient counterplay on the kingside and is left with weaknesses on b6 and d6.

11...hxg4!

Raising the stakes; the pawn is vulnerable here but importantly prevents White from regrouping and tidying up his position with f3 and Bf2. White must now give up on his positional aims and instead play pretty actively if he wishes to pose Black any problems.

12.Nd5!?

Sacrificing the b-pawn for the initiative, but White more often plays in an equally aggressive vein with the 12.O-O Nc6 13.Nf5 of Ye Jiangchuan-Sutovsky.

12...Nc6

Destabilizing the d4-knight again

13.Nf5 Bxf5 14.exf5 Bxb2 15.Rb1

White has again been unable to keep his knight centralized on d4, but hopes to gain strong pressure down the b- and e-files after 15...Bd4 16.O-O , while he will also regain the pawn on g4 at his leisure. Black's position appears critical, but he has a resource which justifies the decision to recapture on g4 with the h-pawn.

15...Qa5+ 16.Qd2 Bd4!

First played by Topalov, this is an important exchange sacrifice.

17.Qxa5 Nxa5 18.Nc7+ Kd7 19.Nxa8 Rxa8

There is good compensation

Black enjoys full compensation. Not only does he still have a strong bishop on the long diagonal, but White has three weak pawns as well as a rather bad bishop and must play creatively not to stand worse. White has thus turned his attention to 18.O-O!? , but 18...Rc8! 19.Rfe1 Rxc2! 20.Rxe7+ Kf8 21.Bxd6 Kg7 (Sammalvuo) is a good answer when White lacks a way to continue his attack and Black is ready to assume the initiative along the second rank.

Theoretical?

Play is sharper after 10.Be2 than after 10.Qd2 and Black must be aware of some forcing lines so that he doesn't run into trouble against a well-prepared and aggressive opponent after 10...h5 11.Bxg4 hxg4 . However, his general principles remain the same: to play as actively as possible and to make good use of his strong dark-squared prelate.

Statistics

White has scored 53% from 464 games with 10.Be2 , but he is best advised not to meet 10...h5 with 11.h4 which only makes 42% from almost 100 games. 11.Nf5 once packed some surprise value as is reflected from its 57% from 38 games, but Black is generally considered to be fine after 11...Bxf5 12.exf5 h4 . 11.Bxg4 remains White's most popular and highest-scoring move (58% from almost 250 games), although after 11...hxg4! 12.O-O White is down to 51%. He does, however, have a very impressive 73%, albeit from just 15 games, with 12.Nd5 which Black most certainly must be prepared for.

Game 23
De la Villa Garcia, J. - Hodgson, J.
Zaragoza 1993

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 Ng4 7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 10.Be2 h5 11.h4 gxh4 12.Rxh4!

White will hope that the rook is later turns out to be well-placed here. The alternative 12.Bxh4 is too slow to really cause Black any problems so long as he responds actively with 12...Nc6 13.Nb3 Be6 14.Qd2 Qb6! White would like to continue with 15.f3? but that is well met by 15...Bh6 , although even 15.Nd5 Bxd5 16.exd5 Nce5 is quite comfortable for Black.

12...Nc6 13.Nb3 Be6 14.Qd2 Qb6!?

Once again preventing 15.f3 and Bf2, but if readers find this too risky, 14...Rc8 is a good alternative which also contains a fiendish trap. White might well be tempted to exploit his active rook with 15.Qg5? , having seen that 15...Bf6 16.Rxh5 Bxg5 17.Rxh8+ regains the queen leaving White a pawn ahead.

TIP: A quiet move in the middle of a forcing variation can be easy to miss, but can be devastating. Here Black doesn't capture the queen but prefers 16...Kd7!, seizing a rather strong initiative.

A.Morozevich-J.Polgar, FIDE World Ch., San Luis 2005 saw White avoid that with the sensible 15.O-O-O , although after 15...Qb6 16.f3 Qe3 17.Bf4 Qxd2+ 18.Rxd2 Nge5 19.Be3 Bf6 Black had prevented any kingside damage and was left with a reasonable position.

15.Nd5! Bxd5 16.exd5 Nce5!

WARNING: Black must beware exchanges in this variation. They can reduce his activity and leave any weak pawns vulnerable, such as after 16...Nd4? 17.Nxd4 Bxd4 18.Bxg4 hxg4 19.c3 Bf6 20.Rxh8+ Bxh8 21.O-O-O .

17.O-O-O Bh6!

De la Villa has played very actively and Black is forced to prepare a queen exchange. Otherwise his knights might easily have been driven back in disarray, and 17...Bf6 18.Rh3 h4 19.Bf4 sees White maintain control and leaves the black pieces precariously placed.

18.f4 Qe3 19.Qxe3 Nxe3 20.fxe5!

Fighting hard for the initiative. Instead 20.Rdh1 Rc8! 21.Rxh5 Rxc2+ 22.Kb1 Rxe2 23.fxe5 must have been tempting, but following 23...Rxg2 24.Bf4? Bxf4! 25.Rxh8+ Kd7 26.exd6 Be5 (De la Villa) it is once again the unopposed black dark-squared bishop which rules the roost.

20...Nf5+ 21.Kb1 Nxg3 22.exd6! Bg5 23.Rb4 Nxe2 24.dxe7 Bxe7 25.Rxb7

I imagine that Hodgson was rather worried at this point; he is usually on the other side of such positions! White's rooks are actively placed and his passed pawn duo is also rather dangerous.

25...Bd6

Amador Rodriguez later improved with 25...Rg8!? which is a better way of defending against the e-file threats. U.Garbisu de Goni-A.Rodriguez Cespedes, Terrassa 1997 continued 26.Re1 Rxg2 27.Nc1 Rd8! which was fine for Black after 28.c4! Rc8! , whereas 28.Nxe2? would have regained the piece, but left White powerless against the passed h-pawn after 28...Bh4 29.Nf4+ Bxe1 30.Nxg2 Rxd5 .

26.c4! Rg8 27.c5 Rb8! 28.Rxb8+ Bxb8 29.d6!

Continuing to play with the pawns whereas regaining the piece would again have left White in trouble after 29.Re1?! Rxg2 30.Nd4 Bg3! 31.Rxe2+ Rxe2 32.Nxe2 h4 when the h-pawn at least neutralises White's central duo.

29...Rg6?

Preparing to return the piece, but Black should have first brought his king into play with 29...Kd7! . 30.Na5 Nf4 31.c6+ Kc8 32.Nb7 appears scary, but Black can then reach a drawn ending with Sammalvuo's 32...Bxd6! 33.Nxd6+ Kc7 34.Nxf7 Kxc6 .

30.Na5! Bxd6! 31.cxd6 Kd7 32.Nc4 f6 33.Nb6+ Kd8 34.a4?

After such a complex tussle, time trouble was no doubt playing a part. White could have retained a pawn and his large advantage with 34.Rh1 Rg5 35.a4 .

34...Rxg2 35.Kc2? h4! 36.Kb3 h3

Hodgson is affectionately known as 'the grandmaster of disaster' and here another opponent falls victim to his sharp tactical eye and impressive resourcefulness.

37.Rd5? Rg5! 38.Rd2 h2 39.Rxe2 h1Q 40.Rc2 Qd1

Game 24
Ye Jiangchuan - Sutovsky, E.
Shenyang 1999

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 Ng4 7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 10.Be2 h5 11.Bxg4 hxg4 12.O-O Nc6 13.Nf5! Bxc3! 14.bxc3 Qa5

Not rushing to exchange on f5, but first eyeing the vulnerable white queenside.

15.Qxg4

White can also keep the tension with 15.Ne3!? when 15...Be6 16.Rb1 b5! 17.Nd5! led to a complex position in P.Leko-B.Gelfand, Monaco (rapid) 2002.

15...f6!

Refusing to allow White the initiative, as 15...Qxc3?! 16.Qxg5 does, and preparing to keep the white bishop restricted. White can now defend c3, but that pawn should always fall in the long run while Black also has a great square on e5 for his knight.

16.Qf3

This isn't too challenging and much more critical is the attempt to get White's kingside pieces into play with 16.f4!? .

TIP: When preparing to defend a weak pawn, always consider whether it wouldn't be better to sacrifice it to keep or gain the initiative.

Black must tread carefully here and should avoid 16...Bxf5 17.exf5! gxf4 18.Rxf4 Qxc3 19.Rb1 when White is very active. A wiser choice is to continue ignoring the c3-pawn, preferring to bring the knight to a dominant central role and 18...Ne5! 19.Qe2 Qxc3 20.Rb1 b5 21.a4 bxa4 22.Rxa4 a5 (Sammalvuo) appears to repel the attackers and to maintain rough equality.

16...Ne5 17.Qe3

Hardly inspiring, but 17.Bxe5 Qxe5 18.h3 permits Black to break with 18...d5 (although that is superior to 17.Nxd6+ which is an unconvincing sacrifice after 17...Kd7! .)

17...Bxf5 18.exf5 Rc8 19.Qa7!

With his own queenside crumbling, White must go after the b-pawn. However, Black now connects his rooks, while his king is well sheltered on f7.

19...Kf7 20.Qxb7 Rxc3 21.Rfd1 Qc5 22.Rd2?! Qc6! 23.Rb1 Rc8

Ye Jiangchuan has been a little slow to appreciate the danger and now Sutovsky regains his pawn after which his superior rook and central majority give him the advantage.

24.h3?! Rxc2 25.Qb3+ Qc4 26.Rxc2 Qxb3 27.Rxb3 Rxc2 28.Bxe5 fxe5 29.Ra3 Rc6 30.g4 Kf6 31.Kg2 e4?

White has exchanged off his inactive bishop, but must have been regretting his rather passive play over the past ten moves. Ye Jiangchuan is now allowed to force a draw, but he would still have had much defending to do had Sutovsky now found 31...d5! 32.Ra5 Rd6 .

32.Ra5! Rc2 33.Kf1! Rc3 34.Kg2 Rc2 35.Kf1 Rc3
