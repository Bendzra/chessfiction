Changing Tack with 7 Nf3

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nf3

White hopes to control d5

In the mid-eighties White began to meet the Najdorf with 6.Be3 . His unsubtle idea being 6...e6 7.f3 , followed by castling long and a kingside pawn storm, and this became known as 'the English Attack'. Of course 6...e6 transposes to a Scheveningen and so isn't an option we will deal with here, although the provocative 6...Ng4 is the main subject of our next chapter. Noticeably Murray Chandler and John Nunn, two early leading 6.Be3 adherents, didn't meet 6...e5 , like 6...e6 , with 7.Nb3 , but with 7.Nf3 . Perhaps they felt that Black should have sufficient counterplay with the option of ...Be6 in the event of queenside castling or maybe they just felt that White could gain a positional edge with 7.Nf3 .

How should Black handle 7 Nf3?

Today almost everyone follows up 6.Be3 e5 with 7.Nb3 and the description 'the English Attack' is applied to that as well as to 6...e6 7.f3 . However, Black would neglect the positional 7.Nf3 system at his peril and he must prepared to slowly and positionally unravel against it. There is simply no especially dynamic counter which promises Black quick counterplay.

7...Be7

Black sensibly completes his kingside development and waits to see where the white pieces are heading. 7...Qc7 , intending ...Be7, ...Nbd7, ...0-0 and then to meet a4-a5 with ...b5, is an important alternative which will be considered in Gallagher-Nemet. Black's set-up is very harmonious, but in recent years he has been coming under some pressure when White delays a5.

8.Bc4!

One of the points behind 7.Nf3 as the bishop is much better placed here for the fight for the d5-square than on e2.

8...O-O

Preparing to challenge with ...Be6

WARNING: Black should not immediately oppose bishops as 8...Be6?! 9.Bxe6 fxe6 10.Ng5! Qd7 11.Qf3 , heading for h3, is rather awkward.

9.O-O Be6!

Neutralizing the white bishop and fighting for d5 which is an important concept. Once again Black must be careful here as 9...b5?? would be a terrible mistake when 10.Bd5!

Ouch!

10...Nxd5 11.Qxd5 wins the rook in the corner.

10.Bb3

Maintaining the tension like this is a better try than 10.Qe2 when 10...Bxc4 11.Qxc4 b5! 12.Qd3 b4 13.Nd5 Nxd5 14.Qxd5 Nd7 and ...Nf6 re-establishes control over d5 and is fine for Black.

10...b5!?

The true Najdorf move, although the solid 10...Nc6 has received more attention. 11.Bg5 ( 11.Qe2 is also met by 11...Na5 ) 11...Na5! enables Black to remove the white bishop when he needs to and after 12.Bxf6 Bxf6 13.Nd5 Bg5 he aims to play around the d5-knight and to break with ...f5. This is a perfectly valid approach which has been employed by Gelfand, although the weakness of d6 means that Black will often have to play fairly accurately and actively to avoid allowing White his desired positional edge.

11.Qe2 h6!

Remaining alert to Ng5 tricks

Avoiding the final trick ( 11...Nbd7? 12.Bxe6 fxe6 13.Ng5 ) and now Black can harmoniously complete his development with ...Nbd7 as in Smirin-Sadler.

Theoretical?

Unlike the English Attack with 7.Nb3 , 7.Nf3 isn't especially theoretical. Black's play is fairly thematic, but he must take care to avoid the early pitfalls and to get his move-order right. The positions after 10...b5 are fine for Black, but they do contain some tricky positional nuances which Black would be advised to appreciate before playing the line instead of the more solid 10...Nc6 .

Statistics

With 7.Nf3 White has scored a useful 54% from 1040 games. The once popular 7...Qc7 only manages 43% in reply, largely due to 8.a4 Be7 9.Be2! with which White has 62%, albeit from just 37 games. Instead with 7...Be7 Black is up to 48%; a score rising after the accurate 8.Bc4 O-O 9.O-O Be6 to 51% from 67 games. 10.Bb3 b5 then outscores 10...Nc6 (54% to 44%), albeit with both still to receive more than 20 tests.

Changing Tack with 7 Nf3: Illustrative Games

Game 11
Gallagher, J. - Nemet, I.
Basle 2004

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nf3 Qc7

Covering the b7-square and preparing 8...b5 , whereas 7...b5? 8.a4 b4 9.Nd5 Nxd5? 10.Qxd5 drops the rook once again.

8.a4

Also possible is 8.Be2 Be7 9.a4 O-O when 10.O-O! is our next note, whereas with 10.a5 Nbd7! 11.O-O b5! Black ensures himself of sufficient counterplay and J.Ibarra Jerez-A.Naiditsch, Spanish Team Ch. 2004 continued 12.axb6 Nxb6 13.Nd2 Bb7 14.Bxb6 Qxb6 15.Nc4 Qc5 16.Qd3 a5 17.Ne3 Rfb8 18.Ra2 Bc6 .

8...Be6

Rare and the main move is 8...Be7 , but 9.Be2! completes White's development and delays a5. When this system for Black was advocated by Danny King back in 1993, White invariably responded 9.a5 O-O 10.Be2 , but 10...Nbd7 11.O-O b5 is fine for Black as Naiditsch demonstrated above. 9...O-O 10.O-O Be6 ( 10...b6 is the logical way to try and improve, but in N.Short-B.Gelfand, Novgorod 1996, Black was unable to shake off White's grip on the position after 11.Qd3! Nbd7 12.Qc4 Qxc4 13.Bxc4 ; he might thus prefer 11...Bb7 , but then Short has analysed the strong and thematic 12.Nh4! g6 13.Bh6 Rd8 14.Nf5! Bf8 15.Bg5 Nbd7 16.Ne3! which looks pretty convincing) 11.a5 , with the bishop committed to e6, it's time to increase White's bind on the position and we've transposed to our main game.

9.Be2 is supposed to have put black players off a ...Qc7 set-up, but is has been used recently by some young talents. Perhaps Naiditsch and Volokitin have a major improvement ready after 9.Be2 as we will see below that Karjakin didn't.

9.a5 Be7 10.Be2 O-O 11.O-O Nbd7?!

White already appears to be doing well and 11...h6 would prevent his next, but is a little slow and leaves Black worse after the vigorous 12.Nd5! Nxd5 13.exd5 Bf5 14.c4 Nd7 15.b4! , although this was the lesser evil for Nemet.

12.Ng5! Bc4

Black can also permit the exchange, but will still struggle for counterplay after that and 12...Rad8 13.Qb1! Nc5 14.b4! Ncd7 15.Nxe6 fxe6 16.Qb3 Kh8 17.Rfd1 Qc6 18.f3 Ng8 19.Na4! Rf6 20.c4 was horrendous for Black in M.Perez Candelario-S.Karjakin, European Team Ch., Gothenburg 2005.

13.Ra4!

Forcing the exchange after which the rook is usefully placed on a4, enabling ...Nc5 to be well met by Rc4. Gallagher now intends to maintain his bind, while slowly increasing his stranglehold over d5.

13...Bxe2 14.Qxe2 Rac8 15.Nf3 Rfe8 16.Bg5! b5?!

Lashing out and creating new weaknesses. Nemet clearly realised that his position wasn't too attractive, but he would probably have done better to defend solidly with 16...Qc6 , although then White can improve his knight with Nh4 or h3 and Nh2-f1-e3.

17.axb6 Qxb6 18.Ra2 a5 19.Nd2!

Intending to increase White's control with Nc4 and b3, and thereby forcing Black to desperately try and break in the centre.

19...d5 20.exd5 Rxc3?

This never offers anywhere near enough compensation and so Nemet should perhaps have tried 20...Bb4!? , although 21.Bxf6 Nxf6 22.Nde4 is clearly better for White after 22...Nxe4 (or 22...Nd7 23.Qg4! ) 23.Nxe4 Qd4!? 24.d6! .

21.bxc3 Nxd5 22.Bxe7 Nxc3 23.Qd3

Attacking both black knights and now the queens must come off after which Gallagher completes his crushing victory without any difficulties.

23...Qd4 24.Qxd4 exd4 25.Rxa5 Rxe7 26.Nf3 Nf8 27.Nxd4 Rc7 28.Re1 g6 29.f3 h5 30.Re8 Kg7 31.Raa8 Rd7 32.Rad8 Rxd4 33.Rxd4 1-0

Game 12
Smirin, I. - Sadler, M.
Ischia 1996

[ECO "B90"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nf3 Be7 8.Bc4 O-O 9.O-O Be6 10.Bb3 b5

WARNING: Never forget about Ng5 ideas in this line. Here 10...Nbd7? 11.Bxe6 fxe6 12.Ng5 is rather embarrassing for Black.

Black does, however, have a playable alternative to the text move and 10...Nc6 in Lautier's 10...Re8!? . This prepares 11...Nbd7 without Black needing to worry about 12.Bxe6 fxe6 13.Ng5 . It still needs more tests, but White is yet to prove anything against it, such as 11.Ng5 Bxb3 12.axb3 Nc6! 13.Nf3 h6 14.Nd5 Nxd5 15.exd5 Nb8 and 16...Nd7 was fine for Black in I.Smirin-J.Lautier, Cap d'Agde 1996.

11.Qe2 h6!

Now Black doesn't mind an exchange on e6 as then he fully controls d5 and there is no Ng5 to worry about. Instead 11...Bxb3?! 12.axb3 Nbd7 was surprisingly played by Anand. However, after 13.Rfd1 Qc7 14.Bg5! White was in full control in J.Polgar-V.Anand, Tilburg 1998 (a game which reached a good knight versus bad bishop scenario as we saw in the introduction to Chapter One).

12.Rfd1 Nbd7 13.Nh4?!

Heading for g6 or f5, but first 13.a3! worked well for in N.Short-G.Kasparov, Horgen 1994. Rather than Kasparov's artificial 13...Qe8 , Black should prefer 13...Rc8! , intending 14.Nh4 Rxc3! 15.bxc3 Nxe4 , netting an important centre pawn and splitting the white queenside in return for the queen. After 16.Bxe6 Black doesn't even have to recapture on e6 and 16...Nxc3!? 17.Qg4 Nf6 , intending to defend with 18.Qg6 fxe6! 19.Bxh6 Ne8 , was fine for him in R.Alvarez-H.Van Kempen, correspondence 1998.

NOTE: High-level correspondence games are an excellent source of new ideas as the players have usually prepared their openings, and any novelties, with meticulous care.

13...b4! 14.Na4?!

Now Black simply nets a clear pawn, although 14.Nb1!? Nxe4 15.Ng6 Re8 16.Bxe6 fxe6 17.Qg4 isn't too at all worrying after Sammalvuo's 17...Nec5 18.a3 b3! when White must even watch out for ...Bg5 ideas, trapping his knight.

14...Nxe4 15.Bxe6 fxe6 16.Ng6 Rf6! 17.Qg4 d5 18.f3 Ng5!

Sensibly returning the pawn in return for maintaining Black's centre and seizing the initiative, especially with one white knight still marooned on a4.

19.Bxg5 hxg5 20.Qxg5 Bd6 21.c4 Qe8 22.Nh4 e4!

Opening the position and now the black pieces spring to life.

23.fxe4 Bf4 24.Qg4 Ne5 25.Qe2 Qxa4?!

Sadler probably underestimated White's resourceful next otherwise he would have first flicked in 25...d4 .

26.g3! Bg5 27.exd5 Bxh4 28.Qxe5 Qc2! 29.Kh1?

Now White is finished off the kingside and so he should have tried Greenfeld's suggestion of 29.Rf1 , although 29...Qxc4 30.gxh4 Qg4+ 31.Kh1 Raf8 32.Rxf6 Rxf6 33.Qg5 Qe4+ 34.Qg2 exd5 would still have left Black with an excellent rook ending.

29...Bxg3! 30.Qxg3 Rg6 31.Qf3 Rf8 32.Rac1 Qxb2 33.Qh3 Rh6 34.Qg3 Rf2 35.h3 Rg6 36.Qb8+ Kh7 37.Rg1 e5! 0-1
