The Poisoned Pawn Variation

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Qb6

Preparing to grab

Who plays like this?

The Poisoned Pawn Variation is the most common way of meeting 6.Bg5 amongst 2600+ players and it has also been a favourite of Bobby Fischer's. Black doesn't worry about covering the e5-square, but instead develops counterplay by attacking b2. At first in the fifties, White couldn't believe that Black could get away with such an audacious smash and grab raid. However, ever since Black has been holding his own after 8.Qd2 Qxb2 , despite a number of both creative and also very theoretical attempts from White to prove an advantage. Once more that strong black central pawn formation has worked wonders time and time again at keeping its king protected from the hungry white pieces.

Should I play this?

If you want your Najdorf repertoire to emulate Kasparov's then this is the way to go, while it is Black's most critical response to 6.Bg5 . However, playing the Poisoned Pawn does require a lot of knowledge and many positions are viable for Black due to just one resource. Furthermore, it is quite simply impossible to cover the theory of the Poisoned Pawn in a Starting Out guide. We will touch on the main lines and see some important ideas, but many club players will probably feel much more at home, especially considering that 6.Bg5 isn't especially popular (just think how much theory White needs to know to play it!), with one of the variations from the last chapter or with the 7...Nc6!? of this one.

How does White try and bash Black flat?

The main line of the Poisoned Pawn currently runs:

8.Qd2

It should be mentioned that White doesn't have to sacrifice his b-pawn, but 8.Nb3 Be7 9.Qf3 Nbd7 10.O-O-O Qc7 has been considered quite a comfortable version of the Old Main Line for Black now that the white knight is less well placed on b3. After 11.Bd3

White keeps his b-pawn

Black can follow Anand's example and play in Brownesque style with 11...h6!? 12.Bh4 g5 or follow Kasparov's lead with 11...b5 12.a3 Rb8!? . However, after 13.Rhe1 b4 14.axb4 Rxb4 15.Kb1 Bb7 16.Qh3 Nc5 17.Nxc5 , he got into trouble against Leko at Linares 2001 with the unnatural 17...dxc5?! , possibly being too keen to avoid an early repetition with 17...Qxc5 18.Na2 Ra4 19.Nc3 .

Another slow idea for White is 8.a3 , hoping for the rather transparent 8...Qxb2?? 9.Na4 , but Black is fine after 8...Nc6 .

8...Qxb2 9.Rb1

Back in 1996, John Nunn noted that if White is to prove an advantage it may well be instead with 9.Nb3!? because 'the older lines have been analysed to enormous depth without any really convincing possibility emerging for White'. That view still seems very sensible today and 9.Nb3 has begun to receive a bit more attention as we'll see in Luther-Quezada.

9...Qa3

White has several options

10.f5

This, aiming to quickly open the f-file, has by and large superseded White's older tries, but Black should take care not to neglect them in his preparation:

(a) 10.e5 dxe5 11.fxe5 is very direct, but after 11...Nfd7 White lacks a good continuation, although he has certainly not been afraid to throw more wood on to the flames:

(a1) 12.Bc4 Bb4! 13.Nxe6!? fxe6 14.Rxb4 Qxb4 15.Bxe6 gives White a certain initiative for his rook, but Black is able to defend with the accurate 15...h6! 16.Bf7+ Kxf7 17.Qd5+ Kg6 18.Qd3+ Kh5! (Nunn).

(a2) 12.Ne4 h6 , intends 13.Bh4 Qxa2 14.Rb3 Qa1+ 15.Kf2 Qa4! when White finds his pieces rather awkwardly placed. He has thus tried the visually stunning 13.Bb5!? ,

The board is on fire!

but Black can and should gain more than enough material for the queen with 13...axb5! 14.Nxb5 hxg5 15.Nxa3 Rxa3 16.O-O Nc6 .

(b) 10.Bxf6 gxf6 11.Be2 aims to develop first and only then to attack, but is rather slow and if White wants to play like this he should prefer 9.Nb3 as 11...Nc6 12.Nxc6 bxc6 13.O-O Qa5 14.Kh1 Be7 15.f5 h5! (T.Wedberg-L.Portisch, Amsterdam 1984) leaves him struggling for sufficient compensation for his pawn.

(c) 10.Be2 Be7 11.O-O has similar aims and was all the rage in the mid-eighties. However, Black has a number of reasonable defences, including the rare 11...Qa5!? 12.f5 Nc6! and the more established 11...Nbd7 12.e5 dxe5 13.fxe5 Nxe5 14.Bxf6 gxf6! 15.Ne4 f5! 16.Rb3 Qa4

Disturbing White's build-up

when Black has sufficient resources to at least hold the balance, such as with 17.Nxf5! exf5! 18.Nd6+ Bxd6 19.Qxd6 Qe4 20.Re1 Nc6! 21.Kf1 Be6 22.Bh5 Qc4+ 23.Rd3 Nd8 and it's perpetual after 24.Qd7+ Kf8 25.Qd6+ .

10...Nc6 11.fxe6 fxe6 12.Nxc6 bxc6 13.e5!

Beginning a plan to weaken the black structure whereas Timman's 13.Be2 is now believed to leave White with nothing better than to take a repetition after 13...Be7 14.O-O O-O 15.Rb3 Qc5+ 16.Be3 Qe5 with 17.Bd4 Qa5 18.Bb6 or with 17.Bf4+ Qc5 18.Be3 .

13...dxe5! 14.Bxf6 gxf6 15.Ne4

A critical position

Black is two pawns up and has the bishop-pair, but is clearly facing serious problems completing his development and keeping his king protected. On the current evidence White shouldn't, however, have more than a draw as Vallejo Pons-Kasparov reveals.

Theoretical?

Extremely. I could easily have filled this book just with coverage of the Poisoned Pawn.

Statistics

The Poisoned Pawn is not an easy creature to refute as its 52% from over 2,300 games reveals. No wonder so many grandmasters nowadays prefer the English Attack! 9.Nb3 may have only made 47% from just over 300 games, but is currently looking like White's most promising avenue to explore. Instead 9.Rb1 Qa3 is in danger of being played out and Black certainly is in good theoretical shape at the moment, as supported by his 54% from 200 games after 10.f5 Nc6 11.fxe6 fxe6 12.Nxc6 bxc6 13.e5 dxe5 14.Bxf6 gxf6 .

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Qb6 8.Qd2 Qxb2 9.Nb3

Taking control of a5 and c5 and thereby trying to prevent the black queen from rejoining the game in the near future. White also intends to quickly complete his kingside development and only then to begin pursuing any initiative.

9...Qa3

Black's main defence to White's threat.

WARNING: 9...Be7?? would be a huge mistake due to 10.a3 when the black queen is trapped after 11.Ra2 .

There are though two other possible defences:

(a) 9...Nc6 intends 10.a3 Na5 , but has rather fallen out of favour because of the long forcing line 10.Bxf6 gxf6 11.Na4! Qa3 12.Nb6 Rb8 13.Nc4 Qa4 14.a3! b5 15.Nxd6+ Bxd6 16.Qxd6 Qxe4+ 17.Be2 Qd5! 18.Qxd5 exd5 19.O-O-O when White has a pleasant edge. Black has reasonable chances to hold after 19...Be6 20.Bf3 Ne7 21.Rhe1 O-O 22.g4! f5 23.gxf5 Nxf5 24.Bxd5 Rbe8 25.Bb7 , but still has a lot of work to do.;

(b) 9...Nbd7 10.Bxf6 gxf6 11.Be2 usually just transposes to our main game after 11...Qa3 12.O-O h5 , but the rare 11...b5!? has made an excellent score in practice and deserves further attention. After 12.O-O Bb7 , 13.a3? fails to 13...Rc8 14.Rf3 Nc5 and critical is 13.Rab1!? (the forcing 13.Na5 was fine for Black after 13...Rc8! 14.Nxb7 Qxc3 15.Nxd6+ Bxd6 16.Qxd6 Rc6 in K.Streletsky-J.Arnason, Groningen 1980) 13...Qa3 14.f5! when 14...h5! 15.fxe6 fxe6 16.Qe3 ( 16.Nd4? Qc5! 17.Kh1 Ke7! left White struggling in G.Todorovic-S.Marjanovic, Budva 2004) 16...Rc8 ( 16...b4 17.Nd1 prepares to trap the black queen and 17...Qa4 18.a3! is quite strong) 17.Qa7! Rxc3 18.Qxb7 Rxc2 19.Bd3 Rc3 20.e5 d5 21.Bg6+ Kd8 22.Rfc1 gave White a strong attack in T.Luther-A.Vouldis, Bled Olympiad 2002 and 22...Rc4? 23.Rxc4 bxc4 24.Qa8+ Kc7 25.Nd4 was crushing. To make 11...b5 viable, Black thus needs an improvement but finding one isn't easy, such as 20...f5 is refuted by 21.Bxf5! exf5 22.e6 .

10.Bxf6 gxf6 11.Be2 h5

Preventing Bh5 and then bringing the knight to d7 isn't Black's only way to try and develop:

(a) 11...Nc6 12.O-O Bd7 13.Kh1 h5 is an important alternative. 14.Nd1 (Spassky went 14.Nb1 Qb4 15.Qe3 in 1972, but then the calm 15...Ne7! is much better than Fischer's panicky 15...d5? ) 14...Rc8 15.Ne3 Qb4 16.c3 Qxe4 grabbed a key central pawn in N.Short-G.Kasparov, World Championship (Game 4) 1993.

(A) Kasparov lived to tell the tale after 17.Bd3 Qa4 18.Nc4 Rc7! 19.Nb6 Qa3 20.Rae1?! ( 20.Nc4 Qa4 21.Nb6 repeats) 20...Ne7 ;

(B) White might try 17.Nc4!? when 17...Qd5!? 18.Qe3 Ne7! 19.Nb6 Qc6 20.Nxc8 Qxc8 (Kasparov) is a reasonable exchange sacrifice, but 18.Qxd5 exd5 19.Nb6 Rb8 20.Bf3 may improve and is certainly ugly for Black, although quite possibly just about playable.;

(b) Black can also try to do without ...h5 with 11...Nc6 12.O-O Bd7 13.Kh1 Rc8 and now Luther has advocated 14.Rf3!? when 14...h5 and 14...Rg8 are sensible, rather than weaken d6 and allow White some initiative with 14...Bg7?! 15.Rg3 O-O 16.Rd1 .

12.O-O Nd7 13.Kh1 h4!

Seizing some useful space and after 14 h3, White won't be able to swing his rook across to the kingside from f3.

14.h3!

Luther rightly avoided 14.Bg4?! because 14...h3! 15.Bxh3 Rxh3 16.gxh3 b5 is a promising exchange sacrifice.

14...Be7 15.Rad1 b6

The safe move, but quite possibly Black should prefer Nunn's suggestion of 15...b5!? . As 16.Bxb5 isn't especially convincing, that leaves 16.f5 ( 16.Qe3 Bb7 17.f5 Ne5! 18.Qb6 Bc6 was good for Black in R.Pruijssers-M.Senff, Dieren 2005) as a try for the advantage, but after 16...Ne5! 17.fxe6 fxe6 18.Rf4 ( 18.Nb1?! Qb2! 19.Rc1 Bd7 20.Qa5 Nc6 21.Qd2 Qe5 saw the black queen decisively escape in A.Ermolaev-V.Aveskulov, Kharkov 2004) 18...Bd7 19.Qe1 Rc8 20.Rxh4 Black can try 20...Rg8!? or settle for a draw with 20...Rxh4 21.Qxh4 Kd8 22.Qh8+ Kc7 23.Qh7 Kd8 24.Qg8+ as in A.Cela-A.Vouldis, Athens 1997.

16.Qe3 Bb7 17.f5! Rc8 18.fxe6 fxe6 19.Bg4! Qb2

Quezada soon gets into serious trouble and so Black should further explore the alternatives at this point. However, the oft heralded 19...Nc5 only appears to lead to a grim position after 20.Rf3! Nxb3 (or 20...f5 21.exf5 Bxf3 22.gxf3 Rc6 23.f6! Bxf6 24.Bxe6 which is very good for White as Luther has pointed out) 21.cxb3 Qc5 22.Bxe6 Qxe3 23.Rxe3 .;

19...Qb4!? 20.Rd3 f5 may be possible because here 21.exf5 Ne5 22.f6 can be met by 22...Nxg4! 23.Qxe6 Nxf6 24.Re3 Rh7! , clinging on for dear life. There isn't anything immediately crushing though for White and 25.Rxf6 Rxc3! (superior to 25...Qc4 26.Qf5! Kd8 27.Rfe6 Rc7 28.Nd2! Qc6 29.Qg6 Rh8 30.Nce4 when, despite some heroic defence, Black faces an ending a pawn down after 30...Qe8 , albeit with some drawing chances) 26.Rg6 Kd8 makes good use of the powerful b7-bishop to defend with 27.Qg8+ (and not 27.Rg4? Qxg4! 28.hxg4 Rxe3 29.Qg8+ Kc7 30.Qxh7 h3 ) 27...Kc7 28.Rg4 Bxg2+! 29.Kxg2 Rxe3 . White has a resource in 30.Rc4+ Qxc4 31.Qxc4+ Kb7 32.Nd4! , although 32...Bd8!? 33.Qd5+ Kc7 34.Ne6+ Rxe6 35.Qxe6 Rg7+ 36.Kf2 Rg5 appears to set up a fortress.

20.Rd3 f5 21.exf5!

An important improvement over the overly materialistic 21.Rb1?! Qxb1+! 22.Nxb1 fxg4 23.hxg4 of N.Short-G.Kasparov, Riga 1995 when 23...Ne5 would have left White on the back foot.

21...Ne5 22.f6

The key idea which ensures White of a strong attack.

22...Qxc2

22...Nxg4 23.Qxe6 Nxf6 24.Re3 is also rather strong for White.

23.f7+! Nxf7?

With the threat of ...Nxg4 absent, Luther is able to trap and win the black queen. Thus 23...Kf8! should have been preferred when Black must avoid 24.Be2 Nxd3? 25.Nd4! (Luther), but 24...Rc4! isn't at all clear when the black queen scurries back to defend with 25.Nd4 Rxd4 26.Rxd4 Qg6 27.Bg4 Qh6! .

24.Rf2! Qxd3?!

24...Rxc3 would have offered more resistance, although after 25.Rxc3!? (or just 25.Rxc2 Rxc2 26.Rd2 Rxd2 27.Qc3 ) 25...Qe4 26.Qxe4 Bxe4 27.Bxe6 White has good chances to convert his extra material.

25.Qxd3 Ne5 26.Qd4

Now Black can't defend both e6 and b6, and Quezada's position collapses.

26...Nxg4 27.hxg4 Kd7 28.Rf7 e5 29.Qxb6 Rb8 30.Nc5+! dxc5 31.Qxc5 Rhe8 32.Nd5
