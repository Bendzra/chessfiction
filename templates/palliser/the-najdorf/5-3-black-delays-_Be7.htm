Black Delays ...Be7

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Nbd7!?

Gelfand's favourite system

Why is Black avoiding 7...Be7 ?

As in the Old Main Line, Black takes steps to prevent e5, but here he is keen to begin his queenside counterplay as quickly as possible and thereby does not want to develop his king's bishop to e7 in a hurry. This makes sense for the black king will often remain in the centre in any case, but such a strategy is still quite provocative. White must again aim to exploit his lead in development and the position will often quickly flare up.

Black can also prevent e5 with 7...Qc7!? ,

Intending 8...b5

aiming for an improved form of the Polugaevsky after 8.Qf3 b5 . This approach can also lead to serious complications, but was a favourite of the young Kasparov and has recently been recommended for Black in repertoire books by Emms, and by Arizmendi and Moreno. We consider this enterprising choice in Zhao-Xu Jun.

How sharp is 7...Nbd7 ?

The answer to that is very, and potential exponents should make sure that they have studied and learnt some important forcing lines.

8.Qf3

White's usually sticks with his standard and aggressive system of development, but he can also aim to exploit Black's last with 8.Bc4 . Gelfand has shown that Black should then move out of the way of any sacrifices on e6 and begin counterplay with 8...Qb6! :

(a) 9.Qd2 Qxb2 10.Rb1 Qa3 is a Poisoned Pawn in which the white light-squared bishop isn't especially happy, but even 11.Bxe6!? fxe6 12.Nxe6 Kf7 13.f5 Qa5 14.O-O b5! didn't give White sufficient compensation in T.Radjabov-B.Gelfand, European Team Ch., Gothenburg 2005.

(b) 9.Bb3 Be7 10.f5 was Radjabov's improvement against Gelfand the following month in the Spanish Team Championship, but after 10...Nc5! 11.fxe6 fxe6 12.Na4 Qc7 13.Nxc5 dxc5 14.Nxe6 Bxe6 15.Bxe6 Qe5 the game shortly fizzled out.

8...Qc7 9.O-O-O b5

Provocation from Black

10.Bd3

White's main move, but he can also prefer to display immediate aggression:

(a) 10.Bxb5!? is a standard sacrifice for the initiative and the subject of Lintchevski-Negi.

(b) 10.Bxf6 Nxf6 11.e5 Bb7 12.Qh3 is quite rare, but also not without its dangers for Black. However, in M.Carlsen-B.Gelfand, Biel 2005 Black was able to neutralize it with 12...dxe5 13.Ncxb5 Qb6 14.fxe5 Ne4 15.Bc4 Nf2! (but not 15...O-O-O? 16.Nxe6! ) 16.Qb3 O-O-O! when the position was rather messy, but resolved itself to a sharp draw after 17.Nd6+! Rxd6 18.exd6 Qxb3 19.Nxb3 Nxh1 20.Nc5 Bxg2 21.Bxe6+! Kd8 22.Bxf7 g6! 23.Kb1 Bc6 24.Ne6+ Kd7 25.Nc5+ Kd8 26.Ne6+ .

A perpetual in this sharp variation

(c) 10.e5 Bb7 11.Qh3 dxe5 12.Nxe6!? fxe6 13.Qxe6+ Be7

A draw is likely!

is another critical variation, but no longer one considered a good try for the advantage:

(c1) 14.Bxf6 gxf6 15.Be2 h5! prevents a large check after which White usually settles for perpetual, such as with 16.Nd5 Bxd5 17.Rxd5 Nb6 18.Bxh5+ Rxh5 19.Qg8+ Bf8 20.Qe6+ .

NOTE: 7...Nbd7 is not a good choice if Black needs to win and is up against a well-prepared opponent as several variations lead to a perpetual with best play.

(c2) 14.Nxb5?! axb5 15.Bxb5 throws more wood on to the fire, but with 15...Be4! 16.Rd2 Kf8 17.Bc4 Bg6 Black is considered to be doing well albeit in a rather complex position (G.Kamsky-B.Gelfand, Linares 1993).

(c3) 14.Bxb5 axb5 15.Nxb5 Qc6 16.Nd6+ Kd8 17.fxe5 also looks quite dangerous for Black, but after 17...Kc7! White again hasn't anything better than allowing a draw with 18.Qxe7 Rxa2 19.exf6! Ra1+ 20.Kd2 Qd5+ 21.Kc3 Qa5+ 22.Kd3 Qd5+ .

10...Bb7 11.Rhe1 Qb6!?

More uncompromising play

Declining a transposition with 11...Be7 to Kotronias-Shneider and instead aiming to drive back the powerful d4-knight as 12.Nxe6? fxe6 13.Qh3 e5 14.Nd5 Bxd5 15.exd5 O-O-O is insufficient for White. 12.Nb3 b4 13.Nb1!? Be7 14.N1d2 is actually a reasonable choice, but White usually prefers to continue in aggressive vein with the critical 12.Nd5!? of Nakamura-Gelfand.

Theoretical?

Both sides must certainly know their theory after 7...Nbd7 as the key lines are all rather sharp and sacrificial. Black is currently in quite good shape with 7...Nbd7 , but he also is with 7...Qc7!? after which all of White's sharp tries are currently looking rather unconvincing.

Statistics

Black has amassed an impressive 56% from over 850 games with 7...Qc7 , quite possibly because 6.Bg5 players tend to concentrate their knowledge and preparation on the more established variations, like the Poisoned Pawn. 7...Nbd7 only manages a quite respectable 49% from almost 1200 games, but that rises to 53% from 600 games after 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.O-O-O b5 . 10.e5 and 10.Bxb5 have then only picked up 45% and 44% respectively and so White should prefer the main continuation of 10.Bd3 Bb7 11.Rhe1 with which he has obtained 54% from 200 games.

Game 43
Zhao, Z. - Xu Jun
Calvia Olympiad 2004

[ECO "B96"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Qc7 8.Bxf6

Exploiting a possible downside to Black's last, although such doubled f-pawn positions have been known to be playable since the time of Botvinnik. White's problem is that he has been struggling to get anywhere after 8.Qf3 b5! when 9.Bxf6 is again a major, but by no means the only, option:

9.O-O-O is well met by 9...b4! when 10.Nce2 Nbd7 11.g4 Bb7 is pretty comfortable for Black which leaves 10.Nd5 ( 10.e5?! Bb7 11.Ncb5 axb5 12.Bxb5+ Nbd7 13.Qh3 was once considered fairly dangerous for Black, but that changed with the discovery of 13...b3! when 14.Qxb3 Bd5 15.c4 Ne4! leaves White struggling) 10...exd5 11.Bxf6 gxf6 12.exd5 which is a standard sacrifice when 12...Ra7 13.Bd3 Qc5 14.Bf5! Bxf5 15.Nxf5 Rc7 16.Rhe1+ Kd8 (V.Akopian-J.Lautier, Ubeda 1997) is quite unclear and not especially easy to play. Thus Black would do well to consider the 12...Bg7!? 13.Bd3 f5! 14.Rhe1+ Kf8 15.Qe3 Qd8! of G.Ginsburg-J.Moreno Carnero, Bundesliga 2003 when the black king is relatively safe and White's compensation not fully convincing, although unravelling will still require much patience and care from Black.

9.f5!? prevents ...Bb7, but Black is fairly happy to provoke White into 9...b4! 10.Ncb5! axb5 11.Bxb5+ Bd7 12.fxe6 Bxb5 13.Nxb5 Qc5 14.Bxf6 . For a long time play continued 14...Qxb5 15 Bxg7 Bxg7 16 Qxf7+ Kd8 17 Qxg7 Re8 {when White might have an edge, but Black enjoys good drawing chances in practice as he can target the white pawns in any ending. However, Black now tends to prefer 14...fxe6!? because of Noomen's 15.Nd4 gxf6 16.Nxe6 Qc4 17.Qxf6 Nd7! with a strong counterattack. White might thus prefer 15.e5!? Qxb5 16.Qxa8 gxf6 17.O-O-O when Black should avoid 17...dxe5 as I once played and prefer 17...fxe5 18.Qf3 Be7 19.Rhf1 Nc6 20.Kb1 Qc4 (T.Luther-N.Rashkovsky, Sverdlovsk 1989) when the black king will be safe on d7 and Black is better.

9...gxf6 10.O-O-O b4 11.Nd5 transposes to 9.O-O-O above, but also critical is 10.e5!? when Black has a choice: 10...Bb7 ( 10...d5!? 11.exf6 b4 is the way to try and win and has been recommended by Arizmendi and Martinez. It is quite promising and both 12.Nce2 Nd7 13.f5! e5! 14.Ne6 fxe6 15.fxe6 Nxf6! 16.Qxf6 Bg7 and 12.Nxd5!? exd5 13.O-O-O Bb7 14.Qh5 Qxf4+ 15.Kb1 Nd7 (D.Solak-A.Istratescu, Halle 1995) lead to complex positions, but not ones fully convincing for White.) 11.Qh5 b4 12.Nxe6! Qc8! 13.Bb5+ axb5 14.Nxb5 Qxc2 15.Nbc7+ Kd7 16.Qxf7+ Be7 17.exf6 Qe4+ 18.Kd1 Qd3+ is a long variation which finishes in perpetual, so long as White doesn't copy Wedberg with 19.Ke1 Qe3+ 20.Kf1? Be4! .

NOTE: 7...Qc7 is a good choice when Black wants to win at all costs: it leads to unbalanced positions and White can't force a draw against it, unlike against 7...Nbd7 .

8...gxf6 9.Qd2

As Black has the advantage of the bishop-pair, White should avoid 9.Qh5?! Qc5! , while 9.f5 Nc6 10.fxe6 fxe6 11.Be2 also isn't problematic for Black as the black king will be safe in the centre after 11...Qb6!? or 11...Be7 12.Bh5+ Kd8 13.Nxc6+ Qxc6 14.Qg4 Qc5! (M.Mahjoob-Xu Ju, Calcutta 2001)

9...Nc6

A solid choice, but Black wants to go queenside in any case and now the position resembles a Rauzer.

10.O-O-O Bd7 11.Kb1 Nxd4!?

Xu clearly believes in the strength of his position and his bishops in the long run. Instead castling first would be a little more flexible, while Black often opts for 11...h5!? .

NOTE: Advancing the h-pawn is a standard plan in this type of position. Black prevents White from pressurising f7 with Be2-h5 and also clamps down against the white kingside.

12.Bc4 has been advocated in response in 'Experts vs. the Sicilian', with the logical idea of pressurising e6. However, after 12...O-O-O 13.Nxc6 Qxc6 14.Bb3 Kb8 15.Rhf1 Qc5 16.Qd3 , instead of 16...h4?! 17.Qh3! Be7 18.f5! Qe5 19.Rde1! , intending Ne2-f4 when Black instructively came under some pressure in V.Topalov-V.Anand, Dortmund 1997, Black should prefer Leko's suggestion of 16...Be7 17.f5 Rdg8! 18.Qh3 Rg4 preventing Ne2-f4 and ensuring Black of sufficient counterplay to hold the balance.

12.Qxd4 Be7 13.Be2 h5! 14.Rhf1 O-O-O 15.Rf3 Kb8 16.f5

A logical advance, especially now that it can't be met by ...Ne5, but Xu still isn't troubled by it. Perhaps White should thus have preferred 16.Rfd3!? h4 17.Bf3 which is a fairly harmonious set-up, although after 17...Rc8 and ...Qc5 Black is solid enough.

16...Qc5 17.Qd2 Rdg8!

Active and accurate play by Black to prevent White from bringing his knight around to f4.

18.Bd3 h4 19.Re1?! Bd8!

TIP: Black shouldn't rush to activate his dark-square bishop, but as soon as White takes the pressure off d6 he should consider improving it. Sometimes it will regroup to h6 via f8, while it can also help Black seize the initiative with an ...f5 sacrifice to enable ...Bf6.

20.Nd1 Qa5 21.c3?! Qe5 22.h3 exf5 23.exf5 Rxg2! 24.Rf2 Rxf2 25.Nxf2 Qd5 26.Qc2 Re8?!

Black's little trick has netted a pawn, but White still remains pretty well coordinated. However, perhaps in time trouble, Xu Jun doesn't make the most of his advantage, such as he could have begun simplification here with 26...Rh5! 27.Rd1 Bxf5 28.Bxf5 Qxf5 29.Rxd6 Bc7 .

27.Rxe8 Bxe8 28.Be4 Qc5 29.Qd2 Bb6?! 30.Ng4 Bd7 31.b4!

The talented Australian playing White is a pretty resourceful chap and quickly seizes his chance to get back into the game.

31...Qc8 32.Qxd6+ Bc7 33.Qxf6 Qe8 34.Qd4 Bc8

The tables have turned as White is now a pawn up, but he is in danger of being left overextended and the players soon end the game with the position still quite tense.

11...Qb6!?

Rare, but promising, although Gelfand has preferred the more common 11...Qb8 12.e5 Ra5! when 13.exf6 gxf6 14.Bh6! Bxh6 15.Nxd6+ Ke7 16.Kb1 Rd8 was long considered at least equal for Black. However, after 17.Rhe1! Nb6 18.Ncb5 Gelfand got into trouble against Timman (Wijk aan Zee 2002) after 18...Rxb5 19.Nxb5 Rxd1+ 20.Rxd1 Bxf4 21.g3 Be5 22.Qa3+ Ke8 23.Nd6+! . Six months later he improved with 18...Ba6!? and 19.Nf5+ ( 19.Qc3!? 12.e5 Bb7 13.Qe2!

Defending e5; White shouldn't be tempted by 13.Rxd6? as after 13...Bxf3! 14.Rxb6 Nxb6 15.exf6 Bc6 16.Nc7+ Kd7 (Nunn) Black is fairly active and his extra piece (the raking light-square bishop) more important than White's three pawns.

13...h6!

The point of Black's play and superior to an immediate 13...dxe5 14.fxe5 Nd5 15.Nxd5 Bxd5 16.Rxd5! exd5 17.Nd6+ .

14.Bh4

White has also tried 14.Bxf6!? gxf6 15.Nxd6+ Bxd6 16.Rxd6 , but Black appears to be in reasonable shape after 16...Qb4! 17.Qd2 Nb6 18.a3 Qa5 , keeping his queen active. 19.Qd4 Nc8! 20.exf6 Nxd6 21.Qxd6 Qd8 wasn't fully convincing in F.Hellers-I.Ivanov, Philadelphia 1992, whereas 19.Rd1 Nd5 20.Nxd5 Qxd2+ 21.Rxd2 Bxd5 is very unclear, but after 22.exf6 O-O 23.Rb6 Ra5 24.Rb4 Kh7! Black was beginning counterplay, while it wasn't so easy to quickly advance the white queenside pawns in A.Groszpeter-G.Barbero, Hungarian Team Ch. 1997.

14...dxe5 15.fxe5 Nd5 16.Nxd5 Bxd5 17.Rxd5!

A piece down White must continue to pursue the initiative, but the well-prepared Indian talent is ready for this blow.

17...exd5 18.Nd6+ Bxd6 19.exd6+ Kf8 20.Qe7+ Kg8 21.Qxd7

With the pawn back on h7 and the white bishop on g5, Black has continued with 20...h6 here, but then 21.Be7! is strong. Negi has managed to gain a tempo over that variation and that enables him to force a draw before Delroy becomes dangerous.
