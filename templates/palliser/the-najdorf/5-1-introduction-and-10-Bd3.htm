Introduction and 10 Bd3

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5

A very aggressive choice

This has traditionally been the main battleground of the Najdorf, but is now relatively rare with the English Attack far more common. Indeed in 102 Najdorf games between 2700 players in this millennium, 6.Bg5 has been played in just 9 of those, as opposed to as many as 75 outings for the English Attack. However, 6.Bg5 has recently received some attention again from those just below elite-level, whilst it was recommended by one leading exponent, Thomas Luther, in the latest repertoire book against the Sicilian (Experts vs. The Sicilian).

When White wants to refute the Najdorf it is to 6.Bg5 which he turns and countless hours have been spent in analysis by both sides trying to make various incredibly sharp and critical lines work. Black is nowadays considered to have several good defences to this critical choice, but each is pretty theoretical and Black must know his theory here, no matter how rare 6.Bg5 may be at club level. One early slip and, more often than not, it's already effectively game over. However, that also applies to White and playing 6.Bg5 certainly requires a lot of work and an impressive memory.

6...e6

Black's usual response to 6.Bg5 and a good one. He takes control of the key d5-square and will aim to use his d6 and e6 pawn duo to keep White at bay, while catching up in development and beginning queenside counterplay.

7.f4

Preparing e5 and also Qf3

This aggressive pawn advance is White's standard response as he intends to blow Black away with a quick e5. Black must decide whether to hold that up and if so how, but there are also some fairly rare options which he may have to face:

(a) 7.Qd2 would be the normal move had Black played 5...Nc6 instead of 5...a6 , but he hasn't and 7...h6! is an excellent reply.

NOTE: White must cede his dark-squared bishop after 7.Qd2 h6 as after 8.Bh4? Nxe4! Black will emerge a clear pawn ahead.

Thus White must choose between 8.Be3 Ng4 and 8.Bxf6 Qxf6 when Black has the bishop-pair. He has good chances to slowly exploit that positional asset and can even make immediate use of his dark-squared bishop to keep White at bay and to maintain control. H.Kummerow-M.Pavlovic, Biel 1997 continued instructively 8.Bxf6 Qxf6 9.f4 g5!? 10.fxg5 hxg5 11.O-O-O Nc6 12.Be2?! Nxd4! 13.Qxd4 Qxd4 14.Rxd4 Bd7 15.h3 Bc6 16.Bf3 O-O-O 17.Rd3 Bg7 18.Rhd1 Be5 when Black already had all the chances.

(b) 7.Qd3 avoids any ...Nxe4 tricks and prepares to swing the queen over to g3 or to h3. However, the move is a little slow and Black can already seize the initiative with 7...b5! . After 8.a3 Nbd7 9.f4 Bb7 10.Qh3 Be7 11.Bd3 Rc8 he already enjoyed fairly harmonious development in W.Arencibia-V.Ivanchuk, Lucerne World Team Ch. 1997 before 12.O-O-O enabled Ivanchuk to blow open the white queenside with 12...Rxc3! 13.bxc3 Nc5 .

(c) 7.Qf3 is similar, but now 7...b5? is impossible due to 8.e5! . However, White may come to regret having not advanced his f-pawn and 7...h6!? 8.Bh4 Nbd7 9.O-O-O Qc7 is fine for Black, at least so long as he avoids 10.Be2 b5? 11.e5! Bb7 12.Nxe6! fxe6 13.Qh5+!

Brutal if aesthetic

when the queen is immune due to mate and White has a winning attack.

TIP: Black should always be alert to potential sacrifices after 6.Bg5 to destroy his king's pawn shield, especially on d5, e6 and f5.

Instead 10...Be7 11.Rhe1 g5! 12.Bg3 Ne5 kept White contained on the kingside and in the centre in M.Tal-I.Platonov, USSR Ch., Alma-Ata 1969 before 13.Qe3 b5 began counterplay.

7...Be7

Breaking the pin and 8.e5 would now be premature due to 8...dxe5 9.fxe5 Nd5 when e5 becomes weak. However, Black actually faces a major choice after 7.f4 and the ambitious and extremely theoretical Poisoned Pawn ( 7...Qb6 ) and Polugaevsky ( 7...b5 ) variations, in which Black allows the e5-push, will be dealt with in the next chapter. Black can also hold up e5 with 7...Nbd7 and 7...Qc7 , delaying ...Be7 to accelerate his queenside development as we will consider later in this chapter.

8.Qf3 Qc7!

An important move which covers e5 and prevents Bc4, whereas 8...Nbd7?! 9.Bc4! threatens a nasty sacrifice on e6.

9.O-O-O Nbd7

The old main line

White has developed his pieces very aggressively, whereas Black has opted for a well coordinated set-up. Before the Poisoned Pawn became popular this was the main line of the whole Najdorf and it remains an important option against 6.Bg5 . White usually now chooses between piece play with 10.Bd3 , angling to open the centre with a sacrifice, and creating a kingside pawn roller with 10.g4 . We will consider those two important variations below, while another sharp choice is 10.Be2 . However, after 10...b5! 11.Bxf6 Nxf6 12.e5 Bb7 , Black is considered to be fine after both 13.exf6!? Bxf3 14.Bxf3 Bxf6 15.Bxa8 d5 16.Bxd5 Bxd4 17.Rxd4 exd5 18.Nxd5 Qc5 19.Re1+ Kf8 20.c3 h5! (P.Keres-R.Fischer, Bled Candidates 1959) and 13.Qg3 dxe5 14.fxe5 Nd7 15.Bxb5!? axb5 16.Ndxb5 Qc5! .

The Old Main Line with 10 Bd3

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Be7 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.O-O-O Nbd7 10.Bd3

Will the black king survive?

10...b5

Preparing counterplay and to develop the light-squared bishop. However, Black has an important alternative in Walter Browne's preferred 10...h6 , intending to gain control of e5 after 11.Bh4 g5! as we'll see in Angantysson-Petursson.

11.Rhe1

Intending Nd5 to crush Black in the centre. Black should continue to develop in reply rather than provoke White with 11...b4? . After 12.Nd5 exd5 13.Nf5! White has the sort of position 10.Bd3 exponents dream about and, for example, 13...Bf8 14.e5 dxe5 15.fxe5 Nxe5 16.Nxg7+ Bxg7 17.Bxf6 regains the piece with a winning position.

11...Bb7 12.Qg3!

Adding further pressure to the battle for the e5-square and delaying Nd5. However, an immediate 12.Nd5?! is possible and also quite dangerous, but if Black knows his theory he should be happy to see it as 12...exd5 13.Nf5 Kf8! removes the king from the e-file and covers g7. After 14.Qg3 dxe4 15.Bxe4 Bxe4 16.Rxe4 Qc5! White badly needs a good move, but doesn't appear to have one, while a counterattack is brewing. 17.Bxf6 Bxf6 18.Nxd6 Nb6! saw Black defend fairly comfortably in A.Garland Ghio-S.Nenciulescu, correspondence 2002, but 17.Bh6 Nxe4 18.Qxg7+ Ke8 19.Qxh8+ Nf8 20.Bxf8 Bxf8 21.Qxh7 Rc8 (Nunn) is also insufficient.

12...b4!

Having developed his pieces, Black decides that he has little better than to provoke the sacrifice after which he hopes to show that the queen isn't necessarily better placed on g3. However, in the case of 12...O-O-O?! she is and 13.Bxb5 axb5 14.Ndxb5 Qb6 15.e5! is very good for White as is 12...Rc8? 13.e5! .

13.Nd5

Opening the centre

13...exd5 14.exd5!?

This has recently been revived thanks to the sterling efforts of Kotronias and has been further endorsed by Luther. Instead 14.e5 only appears to lead to a draw after 14...dxe5 15.fxe5 Nh5! when White has two tries:

(a) 16.Qg4 Bxg5+ 17.Qxg5 g6 18.e6! Nc5! 19.exf7+ Kxf7 20.Rf1+ Kg8 sees the black king reach some shelter when Black can play on after 21.Nf5 with Dvoirys' promising 21...Rf8!? or just allow perpetual after 21...Ne6 22.Nh6+ Kg7 23.Nf5+ Kg8 .

(b) 16.e6!? Nxg3 17.exf7+ Kxf7 18.Rxe7+ Kg8 19.hxg3 is a radical queen sacrifice for just one piece, but also a huge initiative. However, Black can defend with 19...Qxg3 20.Ne6 Qe5 21.Rf1 Nf8! 22.Bf5! Bc8 when White has nothing more than to take the draw with 23.Re8 Kf7 24.Re7+ Kg8

That's some perpetual!

(F.Hellers-N.De Firmian, Biel 1989).

14...Kd8

The king once more wisely flees the e-file, whereas 14...g6?? 15.Qh4 costs Black a piece.

15.Nc6+!

Kotronias' new idea which has brought life flooding back to 10.Bd3 . Previously White hadn't shown anything after 15.Qe3?! Nb6! 16.Nf5 Nbxd5 17.Qd4 Bf8 , although 15.Rxe7!? Kxe7 16.Nf5+ Kd8 17.Bh4! is possible. However, in G.Garcia-J.Granda Zuniga, Neiva 2005 Black returned his extra material with 17...Qc5!? 18.Qxg7 Kc7 19.Bxf6 Rhg8! 20.Bd4 Rxg7 21.Bxc5 Nxc5 22.Nxg7 Rg8 23.Nh5 Rxg2 24.Bf5! Bc8 when his active rook compensated for the pawn deficit.

15...Bxc6 16.dxc6

Will Black be able to untangle?

This is a critical position, which we'll pick up again in Kotronias-Shneider, in which White is a piece down, but has promising compensation due to his safer king and superior coordination.

Theoretical?

10.Bd3 has made something of a comeback of late and Black must be prepared for it. Responding with 10...b5 requires some thorough preparation and also being ready with a new idea against Kotronias' 15.Nc6+ concept. For a long time 10...h6!? was considered easier to play, but Black must certainly know what he's doing after 11.h4!? . Currently Black is under some pressure in both variations, but that probably won't last. 15.Nc6+ and 11.h4 have only become popular in the last couple of years and within a couple more the leading adherents of the Old Main Line may well, as usual, have worked out some viable defences.

Statistics

That 10.Bd3 has scored 56% from over 700 outings reminds us of its danger. 10...h6 has managed 46% from 300 games in reply and that includes 58% against 11.h4 as until very recently that sacrifice was discredited. After 10...b5 11.Rhe1 Bb7 Black's score is only 43% from 366 games, but that is due to a large number of accidents where Black didn't know his theory with, for example, the dubious 12.Nd5 picking up a huge 68%.

WARNING: Whether you do it by memorization or just by analysing the key lines a fair amount (my preferred method), Black must know his theory when faced with 6.Bg5 .

Game 39
Angantysson, H. - Petursson, M.
Icelandic Championship 1976

[ECO "B99"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Be7 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.O-O-O Nbd7 10.Bd3 h6 11.h4!?

Radical, but on no account must this aggressive approach be taken lightly. White can also refuse to move his bishop with 11.Qh3 , but after 11...Nb6! 12.f5 e5 , 13.Nb3 (or 13.Nde2 Bd7 14.Be3 Nc4 15.Bf2 b5 , J.Plaskett-B.Rechel, British League 2005) 13...Bd7 14.Be3 Na4! (but not 14...Nc4? 15.Bxc4 Qxc4 16.Nd2 Qc6 17.Bg5! and White wins control of d5 - never forget about the d5-square in any line of the Najdorf) 15.Bd2?! Nxc3 16.Bxc3 a5! (J.Timman-L.Portisch, European Team Ch., Plovdiv 1983) gives Black good play on the queenside.

The main line runs 11.Bh4 g5! 12.fxg5 ( 12.e5!? is a sharp alternative, intending an unclear piece sacrifice with 12...dxe5 13.Nxe6! fxe6 14.Bg6+ ; Black can prefer 12...gxh4!? 13.exf6 Nxf6 14.f5 e5 15.Nde2 Bd7 16.Be4 when White has a grip on d5, but Black is very solid and Neverovsky's 16...Rc8!? 17.Bxb7 Bc6 18.Bxc6+ Qxc6 19.Qxc6+ Rxc6 20.Rhf1 Rg8 , intending to bring the king to c6, is promising) 12...Ne5 13.Qe2! Nfg4! 14.Nf3 when Black must decide between: 14...Nxf3 and 14...hxg5 over how to maintain his control of e5: 15.gxf3 ( 15.Qxf3 hxg5 16.Bg3 Ne5 transposes to 14...hxg5 ) 15...hxg5 regains the pawn and leaves White needing to find a way to open the kingside. 16.Bg3 Ne5 17.h4!? ( 17.f4 gxf4 18.Bxf4 Bd7 is fine for Black as the white h-pawn won't become dangerous for some time, and 17.Qf2 b5 18.a3 Qa7! 19.Qe2 Bd7 20.Kb1?! Qb7! 21.Na2 a5 conceded the initiative in J.Bartholomew-A.Wojtkiewicz, Connecticut 2005) 17...gxh4 18.f4 is a logical way to do so, although 18...Nxd3+! 19.Rxd3 Bd7 20.Bf2 Bc6 21.Rdh3 O-O-O still wasn't at all problematic for Black in M.Van Wissen-E.L'Ami, Hoogeveen 2004.

14...hxg5!? intends 15.Bxg5 Bxg5 16.Nxg5 Qc5! when 17.Nf3!? may be enough for a draw, whereas 17.Nh3 Bd7 18.Rd2 b5! leaves Black with the initiative and good compensation for the pawn. Instead 15.Bg3 Nxf3 16.gxf3 Ne5 transposes to 14...Nxf3 , but Luther has preferred 16.Qxf3!? Ne5 17.Bxe5 dxe5 18.Rdf1 Rh7 19.h4!? , playing for an attack. However, neither white minor piece is involved and 19...gxh4! 20.Qg4 Bf8 21.Rxh4 Rg7 22.Qe2 Qe7 23.Rg4 Bd7 was very solid for Black and about equal in G.Souleidis-D.Baramidze, Hamburg 2005.

NOTE: Black is usually happy to see an exchange on e5; his central pawns then control a number of key squares and keep the white pieces at bay.

11...Nc5!

Developing and applying the old adage that the threat is greater than the execution. Black should certainly avoid 11...hxg5?! 12.hxg5 Rxh1 13.Rxh1 Nf8 (and not 13...Ng8? 14.Rh8 Kf8 15.Qh5! ) 14.gxf6 ( 14.Nf5!? is also dangerous) 14...Bxf6 15.Nde2 Bd7 16.g4 which favoured White in D.Navara-A.Adly, Dubai 2005.

12.f5 hxg5

Now Black can and should capture. Otherwise White gains a dangerous initiative without being a piece down, such as with 12...Bd7 13.fxe6 fxe6 14.Bxf6 Bxf6 15.e5! Nxd3+ 16.Qxd3 Bxe5 17.Qg6+ Ke7 18.Rhe1! Rag8 19.Rxe5 dxe5 20.Nf5+ Kd8 21.Nd6 when he regains his exchange with a large advantage.

13.hxg5 Rxh1 14.Rxh1 Nfd7?!

Advocated by theory, but Black should give serious consideration to 14...Ng8! . After 15.f6 ( 15.Rh8? Bxg5+! 16.Kb1 Kf8 17.Qh5 Bh6 defends as Danny King has observed) 15...Nxf6! 16.Rh8+ Kd7 17.gxf6 , Black is just in time to prevent the white light-squared bishop from entering the attack with 17...Nxd3+! 18.Qxd3 Bxf6 . If 11.h4 remains popular then this position will surely finally receive some tests in the near future. Nunn has assessed it as unclear, but Black's strong central pawn shield remains and I can't see a good way for White to dent that. Unless White has something fast then this variation may consign 11.h4 back to oblivion.

15.Rh8+ Nf8!

Avoiding 15...Bf8? 16.fxe6 Ne5 when Nunn wrote in 1996 that 'White has no way to force home his attack'. However, computers have greatly improved since then and, as related by Tony Kosten in his excellent Open Sicilians section of the ChessPublishing website, 17.Nd5! Ncxd3+ 18.Kb1 Qa5 19.Qf1!! covers e1 and wins! David Vigorito has then analysed 19...Nb4 20.e7 Qxa2+ 21.Kc1 Ng6 22.exf8Q+ Nxf8 23.Nb3! Qa4 24.g6 and the white attack is decisive.

TIP: Always be wary of old theory in very sharp positions. There may well be a dangerous improvement lurking which the machine will quickly spot.

16.f6 gxf6 17.gxf6 Bd8?

Extremely greedy and the alarm bells should have been ringing. Black must offer to return the piece with 17...b5! when 18.fxe7 Qxe7 is solid enough for him. White should thus prefer 18.Bxb5+! axb5 19.Ndxb5 Qd8!? (the best defence as 19...Qc6? has been played, but allows 20.e5! Qxf3 21.gxf3 Na6 22.fxe7 Kxe7 23.exd6+ Ke8 24.Ne4 and White wins) 20.fxe7 (but not 20.e5? Bb7! 21.fxe7 Qxe7 22.Nxd6+ Qxd6 23.exd6 Bxf3 24.gxf3 Nb7 ) 20...Qxe7 21.Qf4 Ra6 22.Qh6 and Black is in some trouble: 22...Rc6? 23.Nd4 Ra6 24.Nf5! exf5 25.Nd5 is crushing, and 22...Ncd7 23.Nc7+ Kd8 24.Nxa6 Bxa6 favours White as his active rook and two extra passed pawns outweigh the two black minor pieces.

18.Qf4! Bxf6

Desperation, but this still fails, while recently Black preferred 18...d5 , but after 19.Qh6 Nxd3+ 20.cxd3 Qd6 21.e5 he wasn't able to keep f8 defended in A.Guthrie-D.Stojic, Auckland 2005.

19.Qxf6 Qe7 20.Qh6! Bd7 21.Nf5!

Rounding the attack off in style by overloading the black queen.

21...exf5 22.Nd5 fxe4 23.Nxe7 Kxe7 24.Qh4+

Game 40
Kotronias, V. - Shneider, A.
Korinthos 2004

[ECO "B99"]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Nbd7 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.O-O-O b5 10.Bd3 Bb7 11.Rhe1 Be7 12.Qg3 b4 13.Nd5 exd5 14.exd5 Kd8 15.Nc6+ Bxc6 16.dxc6 Nc5?!

A natural move, but now White whips up a strong initiative which Kotronias never relinquishes. Thus it is here where Black should look for improvements: 16...Qxc6 17.Bxf6 and now both 17...Nxf6 (and 17...Bxf6!? are possible. After the latter, 18.Be4 Qa4! returns a heavy chunk of material for the initiative. The white king is, however, able to run away and after 19.Bxa8 Qxa2 20.Rxd6 Qxb2+ 21.Kd1 Qb1+ 22.Ke2 Qxc2+ 23.Kf1! Qc4+ 24.Rd3 Re8 25.Red1 , Black must find Tischbierek's suggestion of 25...Kc7! . The position is very unclear, but White's slightly safer king after 26.Bd5 Qb5 27.Kg1 (I.Rogers & Z.Zhao) should give him slightly the better prospects. However, Black's queenside trumps must never be underestimated as 26.Qf3? Rxa8! 27.Qxa8 Nc5 28.Qd5 Qxd5 29.Rxd5 Kc6 reveals.) 18.Qxg7 Re8! develops the kingside and has been Shirov's choice, albeit only in a blitz game. White is going to reach an ending the exchange down, but with two dangerous kingside pawns, such as after 19.Rxe7!? Kxe7 20.Re1+ Kd7 21.Qxf7+ Kc8 22.Re6! (which has been assessed as favouring White) 22...Rxe6 23.Qxe6+ Kc7 24.Qxf6 , but with 24...Re8 , followed by ...Qc5 Black begins the counterattack and shouldn't be worse.

16...Nb6!? has only received the one outing, but Luther himself has come unstuck against it. He recommends 17.Bh4 Rg8 18.Bxh7!? Rh8 19.Be4 , but now Black can do better than 19...d5?! . One possible idea is 19...Ne8!? because after 20.Bxe7+ Kxe7 there is no good discovered check. Black's king will then go to f8 and, although unravelling is still far from easy with the pawn on c6, this certainly deserves some testing.

17.Bh4!

Simple and effective as g7 isn't especially easy to defend and 17...g6 18.f5! keeps up the attacking pressure.

17...Rg8

Kotronias had previously faced the ugly 17...Bf8?! when 18.Bc4! Ra7 19.Bd5 left Black badly tied up in V.Kotronias-A.Lesiege, Montreal 2002 and he continued to suffer as White slowly improved his forces with 19...a5 20.Re3! Qc8 21.Rde1 Qf5 22.Bxf6+ gxf6 23.Re8+ Kc7 24.Qf3 .

18.Bxh7!

Shneider probably thought that he had this one covered, but Kotronias' preparation has delved deeper into the position.

18...Rh8 19.Qxg7 Rxh7 20.Qxf6! Rxh4 21.Qxf7 Rh8

Black has avoided being mated and is two pieces up, although it has come at the cost of his entire kingside. The white f-pawn is now very dangerous, while Kotronias continues to make good use of his superior coordination.

22.Re5! Na4

Black's attempts to improve have focused upon 22...Rf8!? , although after 23.Qg7 b3! 24.axb3 Nxb3+ 25.cxb3 Qxc6+ 26.Kb1 Re8 27.Re6! the white king still isn't too exposed and White retained the better chances in R.Hofer-B.Jaderholm, correspondence 2003.

23.Re6 Nc5 24.Re5 Na4 25.Re3! Ra7

Kotronias correctly rejected the repetition, because after 25...Nb6 26.Rde1 Nc8 , White has time for 27.Qg7 Re8 28.f5 .

26.Rde1 Re8!?

Keeping pieces on, while Luther also mentions 26...Qxc6 27.Rxe7 Rxe7 28.Qxe7+ Kc8 29.h4 when the kingside trio will take some stopping, although White must still display a fair amount of technique.

27.f5! Qxc6 28.f6 Qxg2?

Black has worked very hard to stay on the board and to begin to coordinate his pieces, but now all that hard work is undone. Instead he had to try and trouble the white king with 28...Nc5!? 29.fxe7+ Kc8 , intending 30...Rc7 and also a quick sprint of the d-pawn.

29.fxe7+ Kd7 30.Rg3!

Accurate to the end and presumably Black had missed 30...Qc6? 31.Qxe8+! .

30...Qa8 31.Rg6 Nc5 32.Rd1 Nb7 33.Qe6+ Kc7 34.Rg4! a5 35.Rc4+
