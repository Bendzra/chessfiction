The Polugaevsky Variation

A provocative choice

7...b5 is most certainly a risky move; Black accelerates his queenside play at the cost of falling further behind in development. White can and should aim to pursue the initiative with 8.e5 as slower plans give Black a good game, such as with the 8.Qf3 Bb7 9.a3 Nbd7 10.O-O-O Rc8 11.f5 e5 12.Nde2 Qc7! 13.Rd2 Be7 14.h4 Qa5! of J.Fabian-L.Polugaevsky, Marianske Lazne 1959. Those interested in the variation and its development most certainly should try to track down Polugaevsky's chapter, 'The Birth of a Variation', in his Grandmaster Preparation.

The Polugaevsky isn't too common in these days, quite possibly because Black players fear computer preparation and the variation certainly lends itself well to that. However, White players may well have neglected it in their preparation and they do so at their peril; the black position is full of resources and is far from easy to break down.

10...Qe5+ 11.Be2 Qxg5 12.O-O

Will Black survive?

White has a huge lead in development, but can he make it count, especially in the absence of his dark-squared bishop? This question has been hotly debated for the past half century, but the most recent analysis on the Polugaevsky (by Tibor Karolyi in New in Chess Yearbook 58, 59 and 61) suggests that Black is still able to repel the invaders with some very accurate defence. We will return to 12.O-O in Leko-Ivanchuk, while 12.Qd3!?

Another critical try

is also pretty dangerous when Black must choose between 12...Qxf6 and grabbing another pawn with 12...gxf6!? 13.Ne4 Qe5 14.Nf3 Qxb2 .

Theoretical?

The Polugaevsky most certainly is and Black must be ready for White's many sacrificial and critical tries. However, if you don't mind defending on a knife-edge at times and are seeking a new pet variation to defend as Black, then the Polu could be the right choice.

Statistics

An unprepared White player might be scared to play 8.e5 , but his alternatives are less dangerous and with 8.e5 White has made 53% from 550 games. His score rises to 56% with 12.O-O and to 58% with 12 Qd3, and both from just over 100 games. Black players should note that in response to the former, 12...Ra7 (with 52%) has outscored 12...Qe5 (42%), although Black needs to find and analyse improvements against Leko's play in both cases.
