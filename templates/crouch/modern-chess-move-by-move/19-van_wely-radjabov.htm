Game 19
L.Van Wely-T.Radjabov
Biel 2007
King's Indian Defence E97

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7

The King's Indian Defence, a favourite opening for Black, especially if he is looking for sharp attacking and counter-attacking chess. It just so happens that the King's Indian is under-represented in this book, but the two games which are included give a fair indication of quite how complex this opening can be.

3...d5!? , the Grünfeld Defence, is another complicated possibility. After, for example, 4.cxd5 Nxd5 5.e4 Nxc3 6.bxc3 White has a big pawn centre just at the moment, but Black can soon counterattack, with 6...c5 or 6...Bg7 followed by a later ...c5.

4.e4

White sets up a big pawn centre. Other moves, for example, 4.g3 or 4.Nf3 , might allow a Grünfeld with 4...d5 , or Black might continue with a King's Indian and ...d6.

4...d6

Sometimes Black may tease the opponent with 4...O-O , and then a move later ...d6, transposing. Older players will remember R.Letelier-R.Fischer, Leipzig 1960, written up in Fischer's My 60 Memorable Games. After 5.e5?! Ne8 6.f4 d6 7.Be3 c5 8.dxc5 Nc6 9.cxd6 exd6 10.Ne4 Bf5 11.Ng3 Be6 12.Nf3 Qc7 , White may have been a pawn ahead, but his development was slow, and his prematurely advanced pawns left several weaknesses in the centre. Fischer won in just 23 moves.

5.Nf3

For 5.f3, see Game 29, Bu Xiangzhi-Zvjaginsev.

See also comments there on other lines.

5...O-O

With quick development, but what about his pawns?

6.Be2

The most popular line. White keeps his bishop on a safe square, and castles quickly.

6...e5

Finally Black recovers some space in the centre. This is very much the main option, although 6...Bg4 , 6...Na6 , 6...c5 , 6...c6 and 6...Nbd7 are all playable. In the early years of the King's Indian, Black often tried 6...Nc6 , with the idea that after 7.d5 Nb8 , White has loosened his dark squares. There is, however, the problem that Black has lost two tempi.

7.O-O

The main line.

7.d5 is an obvious alternative, gaining space immediately, but after 7...a5 followed by ...Na6, it is difficult for White to gain any more space on the queenside.

7.dxe5 dxe5 8.Qxd8 Rxd8 9.Bg5 (after 9.Nxe5? Nxe4 Black is comfortably equal) 9...Re8!? is probably equal.

7.Be3 has been moderately fashionable, with the idea that after 7...Ng4 8.Bg5 f6 9.Bh4 (or even 9.Bc1 ) Black's kingside has been slightly disrupted.

7...Nc6

This too is the main line, forcing White to decide what to do with his d-pawn. 7...exd4 , 7...Nbd7 and 7...Na6 8.Be3 Ng4 9.Bg5 are all standard alternatives, and there have been experiments with other moves.

There is so much theory to consider, that all we can do in one game is to give a pointer of some of the alternatives, rather than attempt to try to analyse all of them.

8.d5

The most direct move.

There is the final opportunity of an exchange of pawns on e5. It has been known for a long time that after 8.dxe5 dxe5 9.Qxd8 Rxd8 10.Bg5 , the slightly unusual-looking 10...Rd7! leaves Black comfortably equal, so White pushes the pawn ahead instead Here 9.Bg5!? is a possibility, and perhaps an irritation to a tactically-inclined opponent.

8...Ne7

To the uninitiated, this can seem to be extremely passive for Black, but he can usually recoil with great effect with a knight move away from f6, and then ...f5, putting pressure on e4, or maybe advancing with ...f4, and then aiming at a pawn storm with ...g5 and ...g4 There are many examples of this position in Kasparov's play, though only up until a loss against Kramnik in 1997.

9.b4

And this is the move that Kramnik chose.

There are many alternatives, too many theoretical lines to write up in detail here.

9...Nh5

Radjabov goes for the sharpest line, ignoring White's c5 queenside push, and instead ramming through on the kingside with ...f5. As so often, the most direct line leads to complications.

9...a5 10.Ba3 axb4 11.Bxb4 Nd7 12.a4 is a quieter alternative, both sides' attacking chances being slowed down.

10.Re1

A waiting move. Black is going to try ...Nf4 or ...f5, and White wants to continue his development in such a way that either move by Black is useful in reply.

10.c5 is perhaps slightly premature. Black can hit back on the queenside (and the kingside) with 10...f5 11.Ng5 Nf6 12.f3 a5!? .

10...f5

Playing for the thematic kingside pawn roller.

The alternative is to try to spike White's queenside pawns with, for example, 10...Nf4 11.Bf1 a5 12.bxa5 Rxa5 13.Nd2 c5 14.a4 Ra6 15.Ra3 , but in V.Kramnik-G.Kasparov, Novgorod 1997, White still kept some queenside pressure, and his pieces were unusually well placed to defend on the kingside. Kramnik later won.

After 10...a5 11.bxa5 Rxa5 12.Ng5 Nf4 13.Bf1 the position transposes into the last line. Black has tried this a few times recently, mostly with depressing results.

Like it or not, Black's strategy is to play sharp tactical lines here. Radjabov usually does not mind attacking chess! Van Wely too is renowned for highly tactical chess, as well as deep theoretical preparation.

11.Ng5

Immediately focusing on the weakness on e6. 11.Nd2 is possible, but less exciting, for both players.

11...Nf6

11...Nf4 is no longer played at top level. It leads to some unusual pawn structures after 12.Bxf4 exf4 13.Rc1 Bf6 14.Ne6 Bxe6 15.dxe6 Bxc3 16.Rxc3 fxe4 . Unfortunately, when White has recaptured his pawn on e4, he has a dangerous passed pawn on e6. In V.Kramnik-A.Shirov, Tilburg 1997, White later won after 17.Bf1 e3 18.fxe3 fxe3 19.Rcxe3 c6 20.Qd2 d5 21.cxd5 cxd5 22.Qd4 .

12.f3

To bolster the e4-pawn.

Radjabov has had to encounter 12.Bf3 a few times too. For example, 12...c6 13.b5 ( 13.Bb2!? ; 13.Qb3!? ) 13...h6 14.Ne6 Bxe6 15.dxe6 fxe4 16.Nxe4 Nxe4 17.Bxe4 d5 18.cxd5 cxd5 19.Ba3 dxe4 20.Qxd8 Rfxd8 21.Bxe7 Re8 22.Bc5 Rxe6 23.Rxe4 a6 , soon agreed drawn in Y.Pelletier-T.Radjabov, Kerner 2007. This was already well known as a standard drawing line, such as in Y.Pelletier-J.Gallagher, German League 2002 (Pelletier played 23.Be3 instead), and a few other games around the same time. What can Black do, though, if both players know their theory, and both players play correctly?

12...Kh8

It is decision time for Black.

After White's f3 move, it has become clear that if the knight is under pressure, it will have to force an exchange with Ne6. After the resulting pawn is captured on e6, there could be tactics on the a2-g8 diagonal, particularly if White plays c5 at some stage. Therefore the immediate 12...Kh8 has its logic.

A couple of years earlier, Van Wely made excellent use of this semi-long-diagonal after his opponent tried 12...c6 13.Kh1 h6 14.Ne6 Bxe6 15.dxe6 Ne8 16.Qb3 Nc7 17.c5 d5 18.exd5 cxd5 19.Bb2 Qe8 20.a4 a6 21.Rad1 Rd8 22.Nxd5! , L.Van Wely-D.Stellwagen, Dutch Championship, Leeuwarden 2005.

12...Nh5 is worth considering, though, and has been tried a few times. Black's knight is moving backwards and forwards, certainly, but the point is that pawns are not allowed to move backwards, and the h5-square for Black is comfortable. If 13.c5 Nf4 with complicated play.

13.Ne6!?

A natural move, certainly, but also, according to Mikhalevski 'a surprising novelty' when reporting a previous Van Wely-Radjabov game, Khanty-Mansiysk 2005. Thus, there is something of a paradox. The point is that White usually waits for Black to spend an extra tempo with ...h6, before playing Ne6. If, however, the knight move is good, then quite often there is no need, one would think, to avoid it.

After 13.c5 dxc5! 14.bxc5 h6 15.Ne6 White has 'gained' a tempo, but with the extra move he has allowed his queenside pawns to deteriorate. 15...Bxe6 16.dxe6 Qd4+ led to an edge for Black, and later a win in Z.Gyimesi-T.Radjabov, Moscow 2005.

13.Be3 has been tried, then maybe 13...Ne8 , leaving the danger of a hit with ...f4.

13...Bxe6

Of course. Radjabov has pleasant memories in this position, after previous encounters against Van Wely, although as we shall soon see, there was also a resounding win for White earlier.

14.dxe6 Nh5!?

14...fxe4 was tried in their first encounter, L.Van Wely-T.Radjabov, Khanty-Mansiysk 2005. Mikhalevski hinted that this might have been inaccurate, "but right now I can't see anything better'. It was Radjabov himself who first demonstrated the improvement, although Van Wely has, of course, done his own analytical work in reply.

This 2005 encounter continued 15.fxe4 Nc6 16.Nd5 Nxe4 17.Bf3 Nf6 18.b5 Nxd5 19.bxc6 Nb6? (in an extremely complicated game, this is the first serious slip; 19...Nc3! keeps White down to a slight edge) 20.cxb7 Rb8 21.c5 e4 22.Rxe4 dxc5 23.Qxd8 Rfxd8 24.Bg5 Re8 25.Rd1 Bd4+ 26.Rexd4 cxd4 27.e7 h6 28.Bf6+ Kg8 29.Rxd4 Kf7

30.Rd8! Nd7 ( 30...Kxf6 31.Rxe8 Rxe8 32.Bc6 , and White will queen one of the pawns) 31.Bh4 g5 32.Rxd7 Ke6 33.Bg4+ and White duly won the game.

Some incredibly complicated tactical chess, with no real mating threats, but move-by-move attack and counterattack throughout The central theme of the game is, of course, whether White can queen one of the pawns before Black is able to neutralize the pawns, and/or gain material.

I have given the comments without detailed annotation, partly to save space and time, but also as a reminder to the reader of just how complicated top-level chess can be. How many readers could work out what is going on, over-the-board, and without help of computer? Most of us would have fallen by the wayside, whether as White or Black.

We are giving an exceptional win by Radjabov against Van Wely. It is only fair to also mention an exceptional win by Van Wely against Radjabov.

15.c5

In another Van Wely-Radjabov game, Wijk aan Zee 2007 (pre-dating the Biel game), White tried 15.g3 , preventing ...Nf4. Radjabov showed a less obvious way of attacking the advanced but isolated e-pawn with the knight, via 15...Bf6!? 16.c5 f4 17.g4 Ng7 , and after 18.Bc4 Nc6 19.cxd6 cxd6 20.Ne2 Rc8 , it was clear that Black was starting to take over. He later won.

For the next stage of his theoretical battle, Van Wely decided it was better to go for an immediate attack.

15...Nf4

If 15...dxc5 16.Qxd8 Raxd8 17.bxc5 Nf4 18.Rb1 , White is slightly better.

15...fxe4 16.Nxe4 d5 17.Ng5 favours White.

16.Bc4

White's c5-pawn move was not just to play for a queenside pawn attack. He also wanted to open up the c4-square for the bishop, guarding the advanced e-pawn.

16...fxe4

While Black wanted to open up lines on the kingside.

White now has to decide how to recapture the pawn on e4. Which way should he do it? The question is going to be one of tactics, rather than quiet positional play.

17.Rxe4

Van Wely sacrifices the exchange.

A quieter way of continuing would be 17.fxe4!? , trying to stabilize the pawn centre:

(a) The computer suggests 17...dxc5 18.bxc5 Qd4+ 19.Qxd4 exd4 20.Nd5 d3 21.Rb1 Ne2+ 22.Kh1 , but it turns out that White's passed pawns are stronger than Black's. White is better.

(b) 17...Nc6! shows that while White has good control on the light squares, he is weak on the dark squares with ...Nd4 being a possibility. If 18.cxd6 cxd6 19.Nb5 . Black has 19...Nxe6! 20.Nxd6 ( 20.Bxe6? Qb6+ ) 20...Qb6+ 21.Be3 Ncd4 22.Bxe6 Qxd6 23.Bd5 , probably about equal.

17...Nf5!?

No comment on this move by either Van Wely or Radjabov, but Fritz assesses 17...d5 as good for Black. Since Radjabov has already played 17...Nf5 before, and has used this move a second time, we can assume that 17...d5 is probably not quite as good as the computer suggests. Both players have been quiet as to what is actually going on, presumably because the pawn move might be tried on some future occasion.

A plausible line might be 17...d5 18.Bxf4 dxe4 ( 18...exf4? 19.Nxd5 , advantage to White) 19.Qxd8 Rfxd8 20.Bg5 Rd4 21.Bb3 Re8 22.Nxe4 and White has reasonable compensation with knight (on a good and safe square) and pawn (well guarded) for rook, but without any obvious reason why he should be clearly better:

(a) Play might turn out to be around equal after 22...h6 23.Bxe7 Rxe7 24.a3 Kg8 25.Kf2 .

(b) Or 22...Nd5 23.b5 Rxe6 24.Be3 Rd3 25.Bc4 Rxe3 26.Bxd5 . Or here 22...Rxb4!? 23.Rd1!? , with reasonable compensation for the material.

Maybe all this will be examined at some stage over the board.

18.g3!?

The first new move.

L.Van Wely-T.Radjabov, Monaco (blindfold) 2007, continued 18.Bxf4 exf4 19.Nd5 Re8 20.Nxf4 c6 21.e7 Rxe7 22.Ne6 Qb8 23.Nxg7 Rxg7 , possibly close to equal, with Black's knight being slightly better that White's bishop, the knight covering squares of both colours. Black gradually took control as play continued.

18...Nh3+

'Check!!!' - Radjabov Obviously the idea is not so much that Black is going to start a big kingside attack. More it is the case that the check gains a tempo, allowing Black to bring his knight to a dangerous if risky square.

Maybe there are some initial indications that White's king might be exposed, but this will happen later, when Black's pieces are more fully developed.

19.Kg2

He does not want his king on the f-file.

19...Ng5

The knights are dangerous.

20.Rg4

This was still within Van Wely's opening preparation, according to Radjabov's notes. The position is certainly complicated, and White has the basic choice either of simplifying or, as Van Wely chooses, making play even more complicated.

Black's g5-knight is threatening, with the danger not just picking up the exchange on e4. but also various tactics with ...Nxf3. Thus we must also examine:

(a) The simplification route is 20.Bxg5 Qxg5 21.Nd5 (threatening ...Ne3+):

(a1) Radjabov then gives 21...c6!? 22.cxd6 Nxd6 23.Rg4 Qh6 24.e7 cxd5 ( 24...Rfc8 25.Nb6! ) 25.exf8Q+ Rxf8 26.Qxd5 Nf5 27.Re4 Ne3+ 28.Rxe3 Qxe3 29.Rd1 b6 (or alternatively 29...Qc3 ) as unclear. White seems better though. Both queens are active, but White's rooks and bishop are far more flexible than Black's.

(a2) Quite often it is wisest not to uncover one's full analysis when it is possible that the opening may be repeated at some other event. A computer play-through would suggest that 21...dxc5!? 22.e7 Rfc8 is highly promising for Black.

(b) Another possibility for White would be to gain a tempo, at the attempt of sacrificing the passed pawn with 20.e7!? Qxe7 21.Nd5 Qd8 22.Bxg5 Qxg5 23.Nxc7 Ne3+ 24.Rxe3 Qxe3 25.Nxa8 e4! 26.f4 Rxa8 27.cxd6 Rd8 , as noted by Radjabov. All the pieces on both sides, queen, rook and opposite-coloured bishops are active, with also a passed pawn on either side. Neither player has enough to win, and the likelihood is a perpetual after, for example, 28.Rc1 Rxd6 29.Qxd6 Qf3+ 30.Kh3 Qh5+ 31.Kg2 , or 28.Bf1 g5 29.fxg5 Qf3+ 30.Kh3 (not 30.Qxf3?? exf3+ 31.Kxf3 Bxa1 ) 30...Qf5+ 31.Kg2 (but not 31.Kh4?? h6 ) 31...Qf3+ . These lines are given, slightly abbreviated, from Radjabov.

There are many lines on both sides that could be considered, but the general impression is that White at best is aiming only for a draw, with careful play, after an exchange on g5. It is understandable that Van Wely would want to try a more ambitious line.

20...Nxf3!

Black ends the knight manoeuvre with a sacrifice, to bring the king into the open.

The computer at first suggests 20...e4?! , with the idea 21.Bxg5 Qxg5 22.Rxg5 Ne3+ 23.Kh1 Nxd1 24.Rxd1 Bxc3 , but after a few tactics, this is lifeless. After 25.cxd6 cxd6 26.fxe4 Black still has to deal with the passed pawn on e6.

21.Kxf3

Allowing the scary thought of a discovered check.

Naturally there are too many tactical ideas to allow White to give up the queen for various forces with 21.Qxf3? Nh4+ 22.Rxh4 Rxf3 23.Kxf3 e4+ . Radjabov gives 24.Nxe4 d5 25.Ng5 Qf6+ 26.Kg2 dxc4 27.Nxh7 Qxh4 28.gxh4 Bxa1 , Black ending up with a winning endgame.

21...e4+!

Black is not so much interested in the discovered attack on the f-file - that will keep - but is more interested in bringing the King's Indian bishop into play, with a violent attack. He would like to recover the material balance.

22.Nxe4

Black is a clear pawn up, with no particular compensation for White, after 22.Rxe4 Bxc3 23.Bf4 Qf6 24.e7 Bxa1 25.exf8Q+ Rxf8 .

22...Bxa1!

The only move, and play is getting wilder.

Radjabov notes that 22...d5? 23.Bg5! dxe4+ 24.Kg2 Qxg5 25.Rxg5 Ne3+ 26.Kh3 Nxd1 27.Rxd1 gives White a clear advantage, not least because White's passed e-pawn is more dangerous than Black's passed e-pawn.

23.Bg5

The only move, attacking queen and bishop.

23...Ne7+!

Out of all the safe discovered checks, this is in some ways the least expected. The natural reaction is to find something violent, maybe a double check, but Radjabov merely retreats to a safe square where White's passed e-pawn no longer creates an immediate threat.

24.Kg2

Neither Radjabov nor Van Wely mentioned the obvious 24.Rf4!? in their notes. If 24...Be5 , White at the very least may transpose into the game with 25.Kg2!? , with, Radjabov suggests, a complicated draw. Readers might want to experiment in trying for more. Here, though, 24...Rxf4+ 25.Bxf4 Be5 26.cxd6 cxd6 27.Nxd6 Qxd6 28.Qxd6 Bxd6 29.Bxd6 Re8 30.Ke4 Kg7 31.Bb5 Nc6 32.Bxc6 bxc6 33.Ke5 is comfortable for White.

24...Be5

To the most dominating square.

Black could consider hibernating with 24...Bg7?! , but the e6-square is worth protecting. After 25.cxd6 cxd6 26.Nxd6 ( 26.Qxd6?! Qc8! favours Black) 26...Qc7 27.Nf7+ Kg8 28.Qd7 White is in control, his passed pawn being troublesome.

25.Rf4

White is not too bothered about sacrificing the second exchange, so long as he may keep control of the long diagonal.

Van Wely suggests 25.Rh4 as a possibility, but without closer analysis. Black seems comfortable after 25...Qe8 26.Qg4 d5! 27.Qh3 h5 28.Bxe7 Qxe7 29.Bxd5 .

25...Bxf4

So Black is two exchanges ahead, two rooks for two bishops, but those bishops look formidable.

This exchange is so natural, that it could easily not be mentioned. If you can win material, you should take it. Neither Radjabov nor Van Wely give any alternatives here in their notes. This perhaps would be a rather simplified version of events: Radjabov wins after this move, but in later analysis, it becomes clear that the position is only equal after best play. It is possible that this position could be repeated at some stage, and so neither player would want to advertise their analysis prematurely. The computer suggests that there are several good alternatives, with chances of an edge for Black. It only takes one of these lines to be shown as giving a slight edge for Black, for there to be a substantial novelty.

Two promising ideas for Black would be 25...Rxf4!? 26.gxf4 Bg7 , keeping the bishop rather than the knight, and 25...Rc8!? , so that if White exchanges on d6, Black has instant counterplay.

26.gxf4?!

This recapture leaves him significantly worse, whereas 26.Bxf4! holds. Such a conclusion is the result of pure tactics, playing through several strange lines of attack and defence, and in the end showing that in one critical line the position folds, while in the other critical line the position holds. The position is quite simply far too difficult for the players to analyse with certainty over the board, and while both players make a credible stab in their annotations, written shortly after the game, neither player gives the complete picture.

Radjabov, analysing for ChessBase, suggests that White could and should have taken a draw with 26.Bxf4! . It turns out that his assessment is correct This move is instinctively unappealing in that White has released the pin against the knight, but on the other hand White does not face any problems of counterplay along the g-file, as in the game.

Radjabov gives as the main line 26...d5 27.Qa1+ (though not 27.Qd4+?? Kg8 28.Bh6 Nf5 ) 27...Kg8 28.Bh6 Nf5 29.e7 Qxe7 30.Bxd5+ Rf7 31.Nf6+ Kh8 32.Nh5+ with a perpetual.

Or 26...Rxf4!? 27.Qa1+ Kg8 28.gxf4 Qf8 29.Nf6+ , and another perpetual.

Finally, 26...Nf5 27.Qa1+ Ng7 28.Bh6 Qe7 29.Bg5 Qe8 would be another drawing line after 30.Bh6 Qe7 , but if White wants to play on, 30.e7!? keeps live complications, and a genuine chance of an edge after, for example, 30...Rg8?! 31.Bf6 Qc6 32.Qd4 a6 (avoiding White's b5) 33.Kg1! and Black is under great pressure. Here 30...Rf7 31.cxd6 cxd6 32.Nxd6 Qc6+ might well end up as a draw, although 33.Kh3!? would force both players to think.

So the bishop recapture on f4 is good and safe.

26...d5

"The position is simply crazy!" - Radjabov.

27.f5

This is really just a wild waiting move. Van Wely wants to see what is happening with Black's d-pawn. If Black wants to take on c4 or e4. White has a big check with Qa1+. If White checks first with 27.Qa1+ , Black covers the diagonal with 27...d4 . If, however. White plays a 'quiet' move, and Black plays ...d4, White has saved a vital tempo through not moving the queen to a1.

In a highly charged tactical position, often a quiet move adds to the tension, rather than resolving it. The opponent can't capture everything at once in a crazy game, giving an extra sacrificial course to add to the menu. White's pawn thrust is genuinely dangerous, as he is threatening f6, but there is no good way for Black to take the pawn, or even one of the pieces. Dangerous!

We must explore

(a) 27.Qd4+ Kg8 is the obvious start to the attack:

(a1) Then 28.Bh6 Nf5! is a triple knight fork, covering both of the main attackers, and also the opponent's mating threat. It is difficult to do much more with one defending knight. 29.e7 Nxd4 30.exf8Q+ Qxf8 31.Bxd5+ Kh8 32.Bxf8 Rxf8 gives Black a safe extra exchange.

(a2) 28.Nf6+ Rxf6 29.Qxf6 Qf8 also covers the mating threats, again keeping an extra piece.

(b) We return to 27.Qa1+ :

(b1) After 27...d4 Van Wely in Informator simply stated that 28.Nd6 was good, but Radjabov showed that he has the same rejoinder as in the main line, 28...h6! . He continues with 29.Bxh6 Kh7 30.Bxf8 Qxf8 31.Nf7 Nf5 , claiming a slight plus for Black. The suspicion is that this is slightly optimistic, and that after 32.Bd3 Qg7 33.Ng5+ Kg8 34.Bxf5 gxf5 35.Qb1 White should be about level. White is still the exchange down, not unexpectedly given his earlier sacrifices, but he has an imposing passed pawn, and solid squares for the knight close to the opposing king. Here 35...Rd8!? is an attempt to make use of the extra exchange, but White still draws with 36.e7 ( 36.Qxf5 d3 37.e7 also draws, with care) 36...Qxe7 37.Qb3+ Kf8 38.Ne6+ Ke8 39.Nxd8 , and Black is best advised to take a perpetual with 39...Qe2+ .

(b2) This leaves 27...Kg8 :

(b21) Both Radjabov and Van Wely agree on 28.Bh6 Nf5 29.Bxf8 Qxf8 ( 29...dxc4 30.Bd6 cxd6 31.Nf6+ Kh8 is a perpetual) 30.Nf6+ Kh8 as leading to an edge for Black after, for example, 31.Bxd5 c6 or 31.Nxd5+ Qg7 . The advantage of the exchange persists.

(b22) 28.Nf6+ Rxf6 29.Qxf6 Qf8 is also good for Black, as we noted in variation 'a2'.

(b23) 28.f5 gxf5 29.Bf6 d4 was also analysed as good for Black by Van Wely.

(b24) 28.Bb3!? seems best, but was not given by the players. There is, however, an excellent reason for this omission, in that Bb3 a move earlier cuts down a couple of options by Black, before the check on the long diagonal.

(c) So finally, we reach Van Wely's suggested improvement for White. Co back to the last diagram, and we have 27.Bb3!? .

This is a quiet move which means that only one white piece is now attacked. White has more control in his play than after the 'violent quiet move' with f5. If Black takes this piece, 27...dxe4 , White has 28.Qa1+ Kg8 29.Bh6 Qd2+ 30.Kh1 Rf6 31.Qxf6 Qe1+ with a perpetual.

Quite often in chess, the more that one tries to analyse, the more that one recognises that hidden resources come alive, and that it takes many attempts to reach any sort of final analysis. Go back a couple of moves, and try 30...Nf5! (not given by Van Wely) 31.Bxf8! ( 31.e7+? Rf7 leads to nothing, Black wins) 31...h5!! (Black cannot take the bishop) 32.e7+ Kh7 33.Bf7 ( 33.Be6 Nd4 34.Bf7 Qxf4 wins for Black) 33...Qxf4 34.e8Q Rxe8 35.Bxe8 e3 when White has the extra minor piece, but Black's passed pawn is strong, and will win back a bishop. After 36.Bb5 Qf3+ 37.Kg1 Qf2+ 38.Kh1 e2 39.Bxe2 Qxe2 Black has an extra pawn, and White's bishop is very badly placed Black will win.

On the whole, I do not like too many double exclamation marks. A sparingly given single exclamation mark is usually sufficient. If Radjabov's next move, 27...h6!!, is worth giving the double bang, then surely the 31...h5!! in this last variation deserves similar acknowledgement.

Returning to 27.f5 :

27...h6!!

This extra flight square alters the balance. After some extremely complicated play. Black is now winning.

Radjabov does not give any alternative moves, and from his point of view, why should he? Job done, end of question.

It is nevertheless useful for the reader to consider what alternatives, notably grabbing pieces, should be examined. Only then would we be able to see why Radjabov's move was, indeed, worthy of a double-exclam:

(a) The more obvious point is that if 27...dxe4? , Black is shredded on the long diagonal after 28.Qa1+ Kg8 29.Bh6 Nxf5 30.e7+ , winning. One of Van Wely's thoughts about his previous f5 move is that he would have wanted, as here, to prevent any queen check on d2.

(b) A vastly more subtle approach for Black is 27...Rxf5! 28.Qd4+ Kg8 29.Bh6 ( 29.Nf6+ Rxf6 30.Qxf6 Qf8 is also successful for Black) 29...Qf8! , sacrificing the queen. After 30.Bxf8 Raxf8 31.Ng3?! Rf4 32.Qe5 dxc4 33.Qxc7 c3! White is in trouble. Van Wely gives instead 31.Bd3 as unclear. A first impression might be that the two rooks could outrun the white queen, but Black has problems in doing anything active with the knight. Black is not clearly better.

(c) 27...dxc4 28.Qa1+ Kg8 29.f6 gives White an extremely dangerous attack, though with only a bishop for two rooks, there is a danger of running out of attacking pieces. The best reply seems to be 29...h6! 30.f7+! ( 30.fxe7 Qd5 31.exf8Q+ Rxf8 32.Bxh6 Qxe4+ 33.Kg1 Qg4+ 34.Kh1 Qf3+ and Black soon wins) 30...Rxf7 31.exf7+ Kxf7 32.Qf6+ Ke8 33.Qg7 Kd7 34.c6+ bxc6 35.Nc5+ Kc8 36.Bxe7 Qd2+ , and Black escapes with a perpetual.

Radjabov would presumably have noted this line up to at least ...h6, and decided that there was at the very least no clear win. The next stage would have been the jump in imagination, in seeing that ...h6! a couple of moves earlier would have been extremely effective, and indeed would probably save a tempo. In the game line Black does not have to play ...Kg8. but can quickly move with the safer ...Kh7.

28.Qd4+

Quite dizzying. White is two exchanges down, and all three of his minor pieces are under attack. White also has chances of attack against Black's king, although opening up the h7-square gives chances of escape by the king. Finally, White has two potential connected passed pawns, allowing chances of recovering some of the sacrificed material.

The verbal commentary might imply that Black should be better, but it is a case of analysing White's chances move by move, just to see whether Black is covering everything:

(a) 28.Qa1+ is a good start. Then after 28...Kh7! (not 28...d4? 29.Bxe7 Qxe7 30.Qxd4+ Kh7 31.f6 Qd8 32.Qe5 , and White has a winning attack) 29.f6 ( 29.Nf6+ Rxf6 30.Qxf6 hxg5! ) 29...dxc4! 30.fxe7 Qd5 31.exf8N+ Rxf8 Black wins. There has been much sacrifice and counter-sacrifice in this line, but basically, Black starts off well ahead in material, and keeps staying ahead at every stags. When the position is simplified, he wins.

(b) Another attempt to take advantage of the long diagonal is 28.f6 . After 28...dxc4! 29.Qa1 Nf5 , we arrive at the earlier line with 28.Qa1 . As we have seen, Black wins. Here 28...hxg5?! 29.Qa1 is less clear; Black allows White to move dangerously with the knight to g5.

(c) The simple 28.Bxh6! , which threatens Qd4+, seems the best line, although White is still only struggling for a draw.

Radjabov gives 28...Nxf5 29.Qa1+! (his own exclamation mark) 29...d4 30.Bg5 Qe8 31.Kg1 (to avoid checks or pins on the long diagonal) 31...b5 32.Bb3 Qc6 33.Qe1 Kg7 with a big advantage for Black. With the help of the computer, though, and a little bit of nudging, it seems that 34.e7! is a draw, and more if Black makes even the slightest error:

(c1) 34...Nxe7?! 35.Bxe7 Rf3 36.Qh4 Rh8 37.Qg4 ends up as good for White, not desirable for Black.

(c2) 34...Rf7!? is a better try. Then 35.Ng3! Nxg3 36.Qe5+ Kh7 37.Qxg3 Qf3 38.Bxf7 Qxf7 39.Qh4+ Kg8 40.Bf6 Qh7 41.Qxd4 , and White is creating pressure after, for example, 41...Re8 42.Qd5+ Qf7 43.Qc6 .

(c3) 34...Rfe8! seems steadier. If 35.Bf6+ Qxf6! ( 35...Kh7? 36.Ng5+ Kh6 37.Be6 , and the three minor pieces are toxic) 36.Nxf6 Rxe7! 37.Ne4 Rae8 38.Bd5 Ne3 39.Bc6 Rxe4 40.Bxe4 Rxe4 41.h3 with a likely draw in a queen versus rook, knight and pawn endgame.

Can Black improve on this? Return to move 29, and 29...Kh7! 30.Bg5 Ne3+!? ( 30...dxe4!? 31.Bxd8 Raxd8 is given by Van Wely as promising) 31.Kg1 Qe8 32.Bxe3 dxc4 33.Nf6+ Rxf6 34.Qxf6 Qh8 35.Qh4+ Kg8 36.Qxc4 Qf6 is analysed by Van Wely. Is Black better? Substantially so. Black's queen and rook will not allow any stability for White's passed pawn, and if the queens get exchanged, Black's king will cover the passed pawn, and the rook will attack White's queenside pawns.

(d) Finally, if 28.fxg6 , Black must play 28...Qe8! , taking over the g6-square. If 29.Qd4+ Kg8 30.g7 Rf5 31.Nf6+ Rxf6 32.Qxf6 Qg6 33.Qxg6 Nxg6 , and with Black's pawns still attacking two different bishops, he wins material. Black has not had the chance of taking either bishop for many moves, which is an indication of the extreme complexity of the game.

We now return to 28.Qd4+ :

28...Kh7!

Snuggling to the cosiest square. 28...Kg8?? 29.Bxe7 Qxe7 30.f6! would even win for White, with the f7+ threat coming up, and if 30...Qxe6 31.Bxd5 , pinning.

29.fxg6+

There is not much coordination in White's remaining attack, although perhaps this is not too unexpected if White has already sacrificed two exchanges, and has three minor pieces under attack.

29.f6? dxe4 does nothing.

29.Nf6+ Rxf6 30.Qxf6 hxg5! 31.Qf7+ Kh6 allows the king to slip through. This is difficult to visualize far in advance. Before Black's 27th, the h-pawn was on its starting square, blocking the king. Every time the pawn moves, it sweeps open a square for the king.

29.Bxe7 Qxe7 30.fxg6+ Kxg6 transposes to the game.

29...Kxg6

Obviously the only sensible move. Black's king is exposed, but that is only for the short term. Black is material up, and his pieces will soon become active.

30.Bxe7

Otherwise White drops another piece.

30...Qxe7 31.Qxd5

Radjabov gives 31.Bd3 Qxe6 32.Ng3+ Kf7 33.Bf5 Qe8 34.Qxd5+ Kg7 35.Qd4+ Rf6 36.Ne4 Qe7 with a win for Black. Again, winning an exchange for White is not enough when he has already sacrificed two.

31...Rad8

To win simply, he needs to use all his pieces.

32.Qxb7 Qh4 starts a mating attack.

32...Rf5

A tame finish.
