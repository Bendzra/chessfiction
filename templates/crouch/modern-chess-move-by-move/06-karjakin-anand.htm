Game 6
S.Karjakin-V.Anand
Wijk aan Zee 2006
Sicilian Defence B90

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4

A possible disadvantage for Black, in attempting to keep play tense with 2...d6 , would be the quiet 3.Bb5+ . Without attempting to provide detailed analysis of this line, Timofeev has made an impressive plus score as Black after 3...Bd7 4.Bxd7+ Nbxd7!? (instead of the standard 4...Qxd7 ) against 2600+ opposition.

3...cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3

Standard play on both sides. The only likely deviation on these moves is 4.Qxd4 , with Black choosing either 4...Nc6 5.Bb5 Bd7 or 4...a6 .

5...a6

The Najdorf Variation. This pawn move is useful for Black anyway, and he might just as well play it early. He is waiting to see exactly what White plays next move, before deciding whether to try ...e5 or ...e6.

The approximate frequency of play in this position in high-level games would be, first, 5...a6 , the Najdorf; then second, 5...Nc6 , the Classical Variation; then third, 5...e6 , the Scheveningen; then fourth, 5...g6 , the Dragon. Thus Bobby Fischer's great favourite still retains its popularity.

6.Be3

There are many lines here, the most direct line being 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Fischer in many games tried to grab the pawn with 7...Qb6 8.Qd2 Qxb2 , while Polugaevsky concentrated instead on 7...b5 8.e5 dxe5 9.fxe5 Qc7 with great complications. These two leading grandmasters were great pioneers during the sixties, seventies and beyond of detailed and sharp opening analysis. There are many later top grandmasters, including Kasparov, and later Anand, who have carried this process through. The use of computer analysis, predicted at an early stage by Polugaevsky, has led to a major jump in chess understanding at the top.

It has to be recognized that the computer does not automatically provide a detailed list of significant improvements, which players can copy and win. More it is a case of the computer setting up a framework of interesting ideas for the player to examine, and then finding out which moves are worth examining in much greater depth. A recent theme in top-level chess is working out possible ways to find in advance attempted improvements by the opponent, and then trumping these improvements with deeper counter-improvements. This game gives an excellent and attractive example.

Without attempting any deep theoretical analysis of this position, the approximate order of popularity is 6.Be3 , 6.Be2 , 6.Bg5 , then jointly 6.Bc4 and 6.g3 (two radically different approaches), and finally 6.f4 . In the days of Fischer, 6.Be3 was still a rarity. It was later popularized by English grandmasters in the 1980s.

6...e5

The obvious point is that 6...Ng4 hits the bishop, although it is not quite so clear that this is such an obvious advantage for Black after 7.Bg5 . There is, of course, by now considerable theory after 7...h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 10.h3 Ne5 . Black has gained some time, but has also weakened his kingside pawn structure.

6...e6 sets up a Scheveningen approach, and a direct transposition is of course possible. There are many possible alternatives by White.

7.Nb3

An advantage of the Najdorf, when compared with the Sveshnikov, is that White cannot create pressure with Ndb5.

7...Be6

The main line, keeping the option of developing the knight to d7 quickly without obstructing the bishop. There are also questions as to whether Black can play ...d5 sensibly, not just yet but maybe later. Most of the time it doesn't quite work, but both players need to be alert.

8.f3

Keeping the pawn centre solid.

8.Qd2 Be7 9.f3 is a harmless transposition. Not though 8...d5? 9.exd5 Nxd5 10.O-O-O and White wins a piece.

8...Be7

8...d5?! is too early. After 9.exd5 Nxd5 10.Nxd5 Qxd5 11.Qxd5 Bxd5 12.O-O-O White gains the bishop-pair with either 12...Bxb3 13.axb3 or 12...Be6 13.Nc5 Bxc5 14.Bxc5 . White has then made a healthy plus score.

9.Qd2

9.g4?! for once allows the thematic breakthrough after 9...d5! 10.g5 d4 11.gxf6 Bxf6 with advantage to Black.

9.Nd5 Nxd5 10.exd5 Bf5 is equal.

9...O-O

9...d5?! 10.exd5 Nxd5 11.Nxd5 Qxd5 12.Qxd5 Bxd5 13.O-O-O gains a tempo for Black, when compared with the 9...d5 line, but White is still clearly better.

9...Nbd7 is possible, and could transpose into the main line after 10.O-O-O O-O , but White has the extra option of 10.g4!? when 10...d5? is not playable.

10.O-O-O

White continues to keep pressure on the d5-square.

10.g4?! d5 11.g5 d4 12.gxf6 Bxf6 is, as we have already seen, a good line for Black.

10...Nbd7

The usual move, developing.

10...b5 , via a slight transposition, leads into V.Topalov-L.Van Wely, Wijk aan Zee 2007. After 11.Nd5 Bxd5 12.exd5 Qc7 13.g4 Rc8 14.Kb1 b4 15.g5 , Van Wely was able to show why he wanted to delay ...Nbd7. He was able to try 15...Nfd7 . Play continued 16.h4 a5 17.Bh3 a4 18.Nc1 with unclear play:

(a) Van Wely continued 18...Na6 19.h5 Rcb8 20.g6 Bf6 21.gxf7+ Kxf7 22.Be6+ with perhaps a slight edge to White, Topalov later winning.

(b) 18...a3 19.b3 Ra5 20.Rh2 Na6 21.h5 Qc3 22.Qxc3 Rxc3 was tried a couple of times by Maenhoet at Istanbul 2005, but White still looks slightly better.

11.g4

We are still on the highway of theory. White charges through with the pawns on the kingside, while Black pushes hard on the other side.

11...b5

Prioritizing the pawn push.

11...Qc7 is playable, although it is noticeable that in games between top grandmasters, White has a significant plus score.

12.g5

The most direct move, and the most popular.

12.Rg1!? has been tried, and indeed was introduced by Anand in a couple of Melody Amber events. The idea was that if Black pushed the pawn, 12...b4 , White has 13.Nd5 , and if 13...Nxd5?? , the bishop gets trapped by 14.exd5 . Here 13...Bxd5 is of course playable, probably equal, and also, a move earlier, 12...Nb6!? . Anand has experimented on both sides with these lines.

12...b4

Attack and counterattack. Black does not want to concede a tempo.

12...Nh5 has also been tried, occasionally after a slightly different move order, but it seems less effective. There has been a recent improvement for White in P.Svidler-R.Ponomariov, Sofia 2007. After 13.Nd5 Bxd5 14.exd5 f5 15.gxf6 Bxf6 16.Na5 Nf4 17.Nc6 Qc7 18.c4!? (before Black can quietly play ...Nb6) 18...Nb8 ( 18...Nb6?! 19.Qa5 is uncomfortable) 19.Qa5 Rc8 (Ftacnik suggests 19...Qb7 ) 20.Kb1 bxc4 21.Qxc7 Rxc7 22.Bxc4 Kf8 23.Na5! Black fell into difficulties on the queenside, and lost.

13.Ne2

There have been no takers for 13.gxf6?! bxc3 14.Qxc3 Nxf6 , and if 15.Rg1 , then maybe 15...Kh8 . Black has two semi-open files against White's king, and can add to the pressure with ...a6-a5-a4. White has only one semi-open file to attack against Black's king-side, and no obvious pawn push. Black should be comfortable.

13...Ne8

Black keeps the king out of the way, which is the most popular line.

After 13...Nh5 , Topalov had a smooth win in his younger days in V.Topalov-N.De Firmian, Polanica Zdroj 1995. Play continued 14.Ng3 Nf4 ( 14...Nxg3? 15.hxg3 and Black is in trouble on the h-file) 15.h4 a5 16.Kb1 a4 17.Nd4 exd4 18.Bxf4 Qa5 19.Nf5 Bxf5 20.exf5 Qxf5 21.Qxd4 b3 22.Bd3 bxa2+ 23.Ka1 Qc5 ( 23...Qe6 24.Rhe1 Ne5 25.Bxe5 dxe5 26.Rxe5 would not have lasted much longer) 24.Qe4 g6 25.Be3 (Topalov decided against 25.Qxe7 a3 ) 25...Qe5 26.Bd4 Qxe4 27.Bxe4 and White had a dominating bishop-pair. Naturally there must be improvements for Black, maybe on move 18.

14.f4

After 14.h4 , Anand has shown a significant innovation as Black in this line, again at Wijk aan Zee, but a year later. And yet again, the victim was Karjakin. The preliminaries went 14...a5 15.Kb1 Nb6 16.Ng3 a4 17.Nc1 d5 18.Bxb6 Qxb6 19.exd5 Rd8 20.Bc4 Nc7 21.dxe6!? ( 21.Qe2 was quickly drawn in Z.Almasi-K.Sakaev, German League 2004) 21...Rxd2 22.exf7+ Kh8 23.Rxd2 .

In P.Leko-S.Karjakin, Wijk aan Zee 2006, play continued 23...Qc6 24.b3 Nb5 25.Bxb5 Qxb5 26.bxa4 Qxa4 27.Rhd1 Rxf7 28.Rd7 Kg8 29.Ne4 , and a draw was agreed. This looks normal enough, if complicated, but there have been improvements for both sides. Morozevich found an improvement for White with 26.Nf5! (instead of the pawn exchange on a4) 26...Rxf7 27.Nxe7 Rxe7 28.Rd8+ Re8 29.Rhd1 g6?! (Black could have exchanged on b3) 30.R1d5 Qc6 31.R5d6 Qb5 , and now a tactic with 32.Rb6! Qxb6 33.Rxe8+ Kg7 34.bxa4 , and White was eventually able to take advantage of the queenside pawns in A.Morozevich-A.Volokitin, Biel 2006.

Anand meanwhile made his own assessment, and found a way of gaining a tempo a few moves earlier with 23...Nb5! (instead of 23...Qc6 ). After 24.Bxb5 Qxb5 the position was much as in the earlier games, except that this time Black had not provoked the b3-pawn push. Play through the second Karjakin-Anand game, and we see that after 25.Nf5 Rxf7 26.Nxe7 Rxe7 27.Rd8+ Re8 28.Rhd1 Rg8 , White found nothing more he could do than play 29.b3 , as he was starting to be in sight zugzwang.

We could have reached this position in the Morozevich-Volokitin game at move 29, if Volokitin had tried 29...Rg8 instead of 29...g6, except for a small but critical distinction. In Anand's game, it was Black's move!

Anand avoided the exchange of pawns on a4, or indeed on b3. and gained space with 29...a3 . After 30.R1d5 Qf1 31.Rxg8+ Kxg8 32.Rxe5 Qxf3 Anand had reached an endgame with queen versus rook, knight and pawn, level in material, but of course White's knight was heavily constrained, and, even more important, the king in serious danger of being mated on b2. The remainder of S.Karjakin-V.Anand, Wijk aan Zee 2007, is of interest, and can easily be accessed by computer.

These Karjakin-Anand games form a spectacular double against an extremely strong opponent, and show why Anand is the most dangerous of all opening theorists. Also an indication of how extremely deeply Anand has analysed in his specialty lines. It is not just a single innovation in a critical line, it is a whole string of innovations against many of the World's leading grandmasters.

14...a5

An obvious move, but there is also an obvious alternative in 14...exf4 15.Bxf4 Nc5 . This has hardly been played though.

15.f5

White too makes the most direct and aggressive line.

The quieter 15.Kb1 is playable, making an escape square for the knight. Then 15...a4 16.Nbc1 exf4 17.Nxf4 Bxg5 18.Nxe6 Bxe3 19.Nxd8 Bxd2 20.Rxd2 Rxd8 21.Rd4 Nef6 22.Rxb4 Nc5 23.Bg2 Rfe8 24.Nd3 Nfxe4 25.Nxc5 Nxc5 26.Bc6 Re5 ended up as perhaps slightly the better of a draw in Z.Almasi-V.Anand, Turin Olympiad 2006.

15...a4

The most consequential.

15...Bxb3 16.cxb3 a4 17.bxa4 Rxa4 18.b3 should give an edge to White, who can look forward to taking over the a2-g8 diagonal.

16.Nbd4!?

Not so much a genuine capture, more an invitation to take the knight on a different square.

16.fxe6 axb3 17.exf7+ (but not 17.exd7?? bxa2 ) 17...Rxf7 18.Kb1 ( 18.axb3?? Ra1 mate) has been tried and then abandoned, although maybe there could be a later revival:

(a) One of the basic strategic points is that after 18...bxa2+ 19.Ka1 , or 18...Qa5 19.Nc1 bxa2+ 20.Ka1 , the extra pawn on a2 is a liability rather than an asset, and prevents Black from setting up any checkmates on the a-file.

(b) Black therefore tries 18...bxc2+ 19.Kxc2 ( 19.Qxc2? Bxg5 ). In V.Topalov-F.Vallejo Pons, Linares 2005, play continued 19...Nb6 20.Nc1 d5 21.exd5 Nd6 22.Kb1 with a slight edge for White. Black's play was not helped by trying the unnecessary 22...Rf3?! 23.h4 Na4 24.Qe2 , and a later White win. Moreover, it was not clear that the 20...d5 pawn sacrifice was required.

16...exd4

16...b3 17.Kb1 exd4 soon transposes.

17.Nxd4

White's knight sacrifice is only temporary.

17...b3

If 17...Bxa2?! 18.Nc6 , and the more important bishop goes.

18.Kb1

Again, White must not allow a pawn promotion with ...bxa2.

18.cxb3 axb3 19.a3 Rc8+ 20.Kb1 Bc4 21.f6 Nexf6 22.gxf6 Bxf6 23.Bxc4 Rxc4 24.Nxb3 Rxe4 was clearly better for Black in M.Boniss-J.Gallagher, German League 2003.

18...bxc2+

The idea is to force White to take the bishop with a pawn on b3, thereby allowing Black to make a pawn recapture, and open up an attack on the a-file.

18...bxa2+ 19.Ka1 a3 20.b4 is worth considering, and the computer gives this as good for Black. This seems slightly to overestimate Black's doubled a-pawns, but maybe the debate will continue. Then, for example, 20...Bxf5 21.exf5 Ne5 22.Rg1 Kh8 23.Rg3 leads to unclear play.

19.Nxc2

19.Qxc2 Bxa2+ 20.Kxa2 Bxg5 21.Nc6 Qf6 22.Bd4 Ne5 is good for Black.

19...Bb3

The desperado theme. The piece decides where it needs to be sacrificed, and not where the opponent wants to take it.

19...Bxa2+ 20.Kxa2 Ne5 is also to be considered, and Black would seem to be at least equal. In A.Motylev-Bu Xiang-zhi, Khanty-Mansiysk 2007, play continued 21.Nb4 Qb8 22.Qc3 Nc7 23.Bf4 Rc8 24.Bxe5 Nb5 ( 24...dxe5? 25.Nc6 Qb3+ 26.Qxb3 axb3+ 27.Kxb3 Bxg5 28.Bc4 with a clear advantage to White) 25.Qd3 dxe5 26.Qxb5 Bxb4 27.Qxb8 Raxb8 , soon drawn.

20.axb3 axb3

The a-file has now been successfully opened.

21.Na3

And closed again.

21...Ne5

A few months later, Karjakin with the black pieces did not follow the Karjakin-Anand encounter, but instead demonstrated a forced quick draw: 21...d5? (unsound) 22.Qxd5 Bxa3 23.Qxd7 Qa5 24.Qb5?! Qc7 25.Qc4 Qa5 26.Qb5 and the double-queen tango soon ended as a draw in P.Harikrishna-S.Karjakin, Foros 2006.

Harikrishna then did his homework, and found that 26.Qxb3! keeps good winning chances for White:

(a) In P.Harikrishna-A.Volokitin, Cap d'Agde (rapid) 2006, Volokitin was pressed for time (this was a quickplay), and could not find the best line. After 26...Bxb2?! 27.Qxb2 Qc7 28.Bb5 Rb8 29.Rd5 Harikrishna consolidated and won.

(b) 26...Qe5 27.Bc1 Qxe4+ 28.Bd3 Qb4 29.Bc2 still keeps Black under severe pressure.

One gets the feeling that quite possibly Anand had seen all this in advance, such is his mastery, or super-grandmastery, of the opening in chess. Karjakin, himself a superb grandmaster, lost all three theoretical battles in this line, and was fortunate that he did not lose against Harikrishna, as well as twice against Anand.

22.h4

Unexpectedly this ends up as a fatal flaw several moves later, in that White needed a check on h4 with the queen, after sharp combinative play, but the pawn is in the way. In the text line, although the pawn is useful, it does not actually do anything. Could White improve with an incredibly subtle improvement following the Karjakin-Anand idea, but keeping the chance of a critical queen check? In the end, this is likely to end up as engaging fantasy. If White were to play slightly differently, all other intervening points, whether as White or Black, could also be amended.

22.Rc1!? looks a reasonable try, and if Black tries the tactic 22...Bxg5? , the knight would be trapped after 23.Bxg5 Nf3 24.Qd3! Nxg5 25.h4 . There are, however, probably a few ways of equalizing: for example. 22...Ra4 23.Bg2 d5 24.Qxd5 Qxd5 25.exd5 Nd6 .

22...Ra5

The one very slight flaw in what might otherwise have been a perfect game of chess is that there is no totally logical reason why 22...Ra5 should be better than 22...Ra4 . Indeed in later games Black has preferred 22...Ra4 instead of 22...Ra5 .

What is happening here is an extraordinarily deep trap, which Karjakin fell into. Once this trap becomes known, other players will not fall into it, and there is no point for Black in trying this again if other moves provide fractionally better alternatives.

We should consider Black's possible theoretical improvement, 22...Ra4 . This makes logical sense, threatening the pawn on e4, rather than the rook being awkwardly placed on a5. Then the most popular try has been 23.Qg2 Qa8 24.f6 Bd8 25.Bd4 Nc7 , which is sharp but maybe equal. An unusual plan of king-side defence was seen in A.Shirov-L.Van Wely, Wijk aan Zee 2007 (yes, another Dutch encounter here!): 26.fxg7 Kxg7 27.h5 Ne6 28.g6?! (Ftacnik preferred an intervening 28.Bxe5 dxe5 29.g6 Bg5 with equality) 28...Rxd4! (again the rook was more active on a4 rather than on a5) 29.Rxd4 Nxd4 30.h6+ Kf6! 31.g7 Re8 32.Qf2+ Ndf3 , and Black had swamped the position with central defenders, later winning.

23.Qc3?

23.Qe2 had been tried before. In P.Leko-F.Vallejo, Monaco (rapid) 2005, Black broke open the centre with 23...d5 ( 23...Qa8 might be better) 24.Rxd5 Rxd5 25.exd5 Bxa3 26.bxa3 Nd6 . This ended up as a tense draw after 27.Bc5 , but possibly 27.Bf4 would have kept an edge.

The next stage of analysis is to try to set up improvements for White, and a natural idea would be to try to chew up the pawn on b3 with 23.Qc3. It looks good, and no doubt Anand in his analysis was asking why this straightforward move is not good for White. The next stage is to set up the ideal trap, a move which looks to be good for the opponent, but falls into an unexpected reply. These days, 'unexpected' would mean 'not seen by the computer'.

Another try would be 23.Qb4!? , covering White's pawn on e4, as well as Black's pawn on b3.

If 23...d5 24.Qxb3 ( 24.Bb6 Bxb4 25.Bxd8 soon ends up as equal) 24...Bxa3 25.bxa3 Qa8 26.Ka2 Nd6 27.Rxd5 Nxe4 28.Rxa5 Qxa5 , and the bishop-pair will outweigh the knight-pair. In E.Najer-V.Popov, Moscow 2006, Black tried instead 23...Qa8 24.Bb6 Ra4 25.Qxb3 Qxe4+ 26.Nc2 Ra8 27.Qd5 Qxd5 28.Rxd5 . The white bishop-pair, plus an outside passed pawn, seems attractive, while the computer prefers Black, but the third result happened, a draw.

23...Qa8

Doubling the a-file. There may be the occasional threat on a3, but Karjakin was probably still feeling in control.

24.Bg2

Otherwise the e4-pawn drops.

Anand gives 24.Rd4 d5 with advantage to Black. If, for example, 25.exd5 Bxa3 26.bxa3 Rxa3 , and it is difficult for White to cover his pieces before Black has time to play ...Nd6 and ...Rc8, or other winning plans. One possibility is 27.Bc1 Ra4 28.Bb2 Rxd4 29.Qxd4 Nf3 30.Qc5 Nd6 31.Qa3 Nd2+ 32.Kc1 Rc8+ 33.Kxd2 Qxd5+ , and the king is out in the open.

After the text, White looks safe enough, and his pieces are developed, while Black has yet to find a way to bring the knight and the castled rook into play.

24...Nc7!!

Anand has seen this idea.

24...Ra4 would have been level.

25.Qxc7

The one good reason for White to have tried 23.Qc3 is to stop the knight playing to c7. So for better or worse, he might just as well take the knight, 25.Bb6 Rxa3 26.bxa3 Nb5 27.Qxb3 Nxa3+ wins for Black. All his pieces are suddenly on good squares.

25...Rc8!

It is the second sacrifice which sets up the winning attack.

25...Rxa3? 26.bxa3 Qxa3 27.Qa7 easily covers Black's mate threat.

Trying this idea on the computer, this is only about tenth in the line of suggested ideas, each of these being apparently losing moves. It is human imagination which brings about the brilliancy here, and not the machine.

26.Qxe7

He might just as well take the second piece.

The retreat with 26.Qb6 Nc4 27.Qxb3 Nxa3+ 28.bxa3 Rxa3 29.Qb2 Rb8 30.Bb6 Ra6 31.e5 Ra1+! 32.Qxa1 Rxb6+ 33.Kc2 Qxg2+ 34.Kc3 dxe5 gives a likely quick checkmate. This is the line given by Ftacnik, although there are undoubtedly good alternatives.

So what next? Black has successfully diverted the queen away from White's defensive squares, but he has lost two pieces, and must make his attack work.

26...Nc4!

It is only this move that justifies the combination. While the knight move is aggressive, it is not even a violent attacking move, in the sense that there is this no immediate threat of 'take, check, then check and wins'. Black is instead 'threatening to threaten' with 27...Nxa3+ 28.bxa3 Rxa3 , and then threatening a check on a1. This gives White two tempi before he can be checkmated, and he is two pieces ahead. Normally such an attack for Black would not work. Here, amazingly, Black is just in time, even though with White's first tempo he is able to attack against the black kingside.

The computer (at least my computer) suggests that Black's position is losing, and after a trip to summer gardening and later return, the computer still says that White is winning. Quite clearly, the fact that Black wins is not the result of routine electronic suggestions, but instead the result of human endeavour. Anand is probably the greatest opening analyst of all.

Incidentally, 26...Rxa3?? 27.bxa3 Qxa3 28.Qa7 refutes the immediate attack.

27.g6

Karjakin aims for an immediate counterattack, with the hope that his own play arrives first. The problem for the white defender is that his queen cannot help defend the king, and so what else can he do?

27.Nxc4?? Ra1 mate is of course elementary, but on such elementary considerations deeper tactical ideas may be built. The knight on a3 cannot be moved, and may be cracked open.

27.Rxd6?! may also be regarded as elementary. After 27...Rxa3 28.bxa3 Nxa3+ 29.Kb2 Nc4+ 30.Kc3 Nxd6+ any experienced player will conclude immediately that Black is heading for a win. 31.Kd4 Qa4+ 32.Ke5 Re8 33.Qxe8+ Nxe8 may hold out for a while, but perhaps not for very long.

This leaves bishop moves:

(a) 27.Bc1 Nxa3+ 28.bxa3 Rxa3 29.Bxa3 Qxa3 30.Rd2 Ra8 is not difficult to calculate, even though Black has now, in total, sacrificed a rook and bishop.

There is no way for White to avoid checkmate, except the temporary reprieve of a queen sacrifice with 31.Qe8+ Rxe8 32.e5 (preventing ...Ra8), but Black is now material ahead after 32...Rxe5 33.Rc1 h6 34.g6 fxg6 35.fxg6 Kf8 . Earlier, 27...Qc6! wins more cleanly, without allowing White the queen sacrifice.

(b) If 27.Bd4 , Anand gives 27...Rxa3 28.bxa3 Nxa3+ 29.Kb2 Nc4+ 30.Kc3 Qa2! 31.Bc5 Qc2+ 32.Kd4 dxc5+ 33.Kd5 Ne3+ 34.Kd6 Nxd1 . At least White's king and queen have been reunited, but now Black is material ahead, and is about to queen.

(c) Finally, Anand gives 27.Bc5!? . White's idea is to slow down one of Black's pieces by distracting them with the bishop:

(c1) If, for example, 27...Raxc5?! 28.Rxd6! Nxd6 ( 28...Nxa3 29.bxa3 Qxa3?? 30.Rd8+ ) 29.Qxd6 and White is better.

(c2) 27...Rcxc5?! 28.e5! again with ideas of a back-row mate.

(c3) 27...dxc5?! 28.Rd7 , and White now has mating threats himself.

(c4) However, Anand shows there is still a winning attack with 27...Rxa3! . He is not to be distracted, and White has now used up one of his precious tempo moves. After 28.bxa3 Rxc5 29.a4 Na3+ 30.Kb2 Rc2+ 31.Kxa3 Qb8! wins, since if 32.Ra1 b2 33.Ra2 Rc3 mate.

Karjakin's line looks the more natural. He tries to get in his retaliation in first

27...hxg6

Anand needs to create an escape square for the king.

28.fxg6

And Karjakin needs to restore his threat. A glance at the computer might suggest that there are several ways for White to set up an advantage, but these all turn out to be rejected choices from move 27.

28...Nxa3+

28...Rxa3 also wins, and if 29.gxf7+ Kh7 30.f8N+ , then 30...Rxf8 , much as in the main line. If instead 30.bxa3 , Black soon checkmates after 30...Nxa3+ 31.Kb2 Rc2+ 32.Kxb3 Qb8+ .

The one line in which Black needs to be careful, to avoid a trap, is 29.Qxf7+ Kh8 30.bxa3 Nxa3+ 31.Kb2 , and if now 31...Rc2+?? 32.Kxb3 Qb8+ 33.Kxa3 , and unexpectedly Black is running out of checks, while White is vastly ahead in material. The queen on f7 covers so many squares, particularly the hoped-for checking squares on a2, b3 and c4. If 33...Rc3+ 34.Ka4 Qa8+ 35.Ba7 , covering another square with the help of the queen, and then 35...Qc6+ 36.Kb4 , and Black runs out of checks. So Black has to be careful here with 31...Nc4+ 32.Kc3 ( 32.Qxc4 Qa2+ ) 32...Ne5+ 33.Kxb3 Nxf7 , and he still wins.

29.bxa3 Rxa3

This is a highly delicate position, meaning not so much that the position is equal (it isn't), but rather that even the smallest nuance in the position could result either as a win for White, or a win for Black, or even a draw. The position itself is relatively straightforward for Black by now, but evaluating this position several moves in advance would be incredibly difficult over the board.

Look at that pawn on h4, apparently an insignificant pawn making up the numbers. Unfortunately the pawn is in the way. Had the pawn been on h2, White would be winning with 30.gxf7+ Kh7 31.Qh4+. An unpredictable outcome after White's confident pawn push on move 22, but if White had played something different just then, the whole outcome would have been different, for better or worse. Sometimes even the tiniest details have global significance.

30.gxf7+
