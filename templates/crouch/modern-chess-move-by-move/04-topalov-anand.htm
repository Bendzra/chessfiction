Game 4
V.Topalov-V.Anand
Sofia 2005
Queen's Indian Defence E15

1.d4 Nf6

Initiating the Indian Openings. Why 'Indian'? The basic point would seem to be that after 1...Nf6 , Black makes only one-pawn advances in such openings (for example ...b6, ...e6 and ...g6), instead of the 'European' double pawn-advance with ...d5. Chess has evolved over the many centuries, with different innovations in the structure of the game in both east and west. But that is another story.

Black's 1...Nf6 prevents White from setting two central passed pawns on d4 and e4, a good start.

For 1...d5 , see Game 1, Kasimdzhanov-Kasparov 2005, and comments thereafter.

2.c4

White wants to make it difficult for Black to play ...d5. 2.Nc3 d5 is possible, but White cannot quickly put pressure on the d5-pawn with c4.

2.Nf3 is more mainstream, and usually transposes (as in Game 8, Sargissian-Nisipeanu) into one of the Indian Systems, or perhaps the Queen's Gambit. There are extra independent possibilities if White tries 3.Bg5 after 2...e6 or 2...g6 .

2...e6

2...d5?! would be mistimed. After 3.cxd5 Nxd5 4.e4 (or maybe 4.Nf3 followed by e4), the knight will have to wander. Better ...d5 on move 1, or move 3 or beyond.

2...g6 is the King's Indian Defence. Black castles as quickly as possible, without attempting to gain pawn space in the centre, and waits to hit back if and when White tries to set up a large pawn centre. Play can become highly complicated. Kasparov favoured this opening, and earlier Tal and Fischer. See the comments to Game 19, Van Wely-Radjabov; an example of how unbelievably complicated such lines may become.

3.Nf3

3.Nc3 will tend to end up into the Nimzo-Indian with 3...Bb4 , although 3...d5 would lead into a main line Queen's Gambit. For further comments on the Nimzo-Indian, see Game 12, Bareev-Efimenko.

3...b6

Instead 3...d5 would be an Orthodox Queen's Gambit.

Anand here played the Queen's Indian Defence. In many countries, the King's Indian would be described as the East Indian, while the Queen's Indian would be called the West Indian. Back in the 1980s, when the Caribbean countries were dominant in the game of cricket, a booklet gave the intriguing possibility of an idea of the fianchetto system versus the West Indians! It remains unclear whether the fianchetto is a weapon for batsmen or bowlers.

4.g3

And here we have the anti-West-Indian fianchetto, in chess at least.

A standard opening plan, fianchettoing the bishop, quietly castling king-side, and settling down perhaps for a slight positional edge, or steady equality. That would be the normal course of events, but it so happens that White does not manage to complete the fianchetto.

4.Nc3 , 4.a3 , 4.e3 and 4.Bg5 are possible too, but not necessarily dangerous after good play.

4...Ba6

Black disrupts, but only slightly, the natural development plans, forcing White to protect the c4-pawn.

For the standard alternative 4...Bb7 , see Game 23, Carlsen-Tiviakov.

5.b3

For 5.Qc2 , see Game 21, Alekseev-Tkachiev.

5...Bb4+

Black ensures that White cannot create simple and harmonious development with Nc3 and Bb2.

6.Bd2

White would already be under pressure after 6.Nbd2? Bc3 7.Rb1 Bb7 , with threats of ...Bxd4 and ...Be4.

6...Be7

This is maybe a difficult point to understand for the less-experienced player. After all, we are taught about developing as quickly and efficiently as possible, in order to bring the pieces into play without delay, and ...Bb4+ followed by ...Be7 would seem to be a loss of time. Try to look through things from White's point of view, though. His dark-squared bishop ideally belongs on the long diagonal, there is not all that much to be done on g5, f4, or indeed even d2. If the bishop arrives on c3, that is on a useful diagonal, but the bishop also gets in the way of the knight. If White were to set up the natural queenside diagonal - with Nc3. Bc1 and Bb2; or Bc3, Bb2, Nc3 - he would be losing a tempo as a result of Black's bishop check, rather than gaining it. In consequence, White will need to find a different approach.

6...Qe7 7.Bg2 is possible, but White should have a slight edge. Black's pieces are not fully coordinated.

7.Nc3

7.Bg2 is, of course, also possible with 7...d5 or 7...Bb7 being the main responses. As we shall soon see, Topalov has in mind something more ambitious.

7...c6

Black wants to add the security of a recapture with the c-pawn on d5, after ...d5; cxd5. If immediately 7...d5 8.cxd5 exd5 9.Bg2 , the 'minority pawn attack' on the queenside tends here to be in favour of White. He attacks hard against the pawn on d5, and eventually forces Black to play ...c6. Then White puts pressure on the half-open c-file, and eventually tries to attack the c6-pawn with b4-b5. Any exchange of Black's c-pawn will then leave Black with an isolated pawn on d5, and White will eventually try to win that pawn. This is a well-established plan of attack, and usually Black will want to avoid it. The problem is often even worse with ...b6 than with the pawn still on b7, since Black has further weakened the light-squared pawn chain.

8.e4

Three good central pawns, and indeed why not? Black needs to start central counterplay immediately.

8.Bg2 d5 9.cxd5 cxd5 is, of course, possible, but is realistically only a drawing attempt.

8...d5

Finally, the clash of centres opens up after much hedging around. Neither side is fully developed yet, but there is an immediate pawn clash. This often means a particularly violent sequence of play, as the attacker wants to take advantage of the opponent's lack of development. This sometimes means sacrifices, to break through before his opponent develops. On the other hand, everything might well fizzle out as equality, if both players complete their development, and the pawn structures could end up as level. With Topalov playing as White, what do you think will happen?

9.Qc2

White keeps the balance in the centre, for the time being. 9.e5 Ne4 has been tried several times, but it is only about equal.

9...dxe4

Nothing out of the ordinary, one might think. The only peculiarity about this opening is that White's g3 has weakened the long diagonal, and this if anything might be in favour of Black.

10.Nxe4 Bb7!?

As a result of the outcome of this game, Anand gives this as a question mark. It was certainly dangerous, but maybe it was not objectively wrong.

So what else? Since this game, most top-level encounters have continued with 10...c5 , and only then ...Bb7, with good equalizing chances. If, for example, 11.dxc5?! Bb7! , taking advantage of the exposed long diagonal. After 12.Bd3 bxc5? 13.Bc3 Qb6 14.O-O-O White has a slight edge, and later won in B.Gelfand-A.Grischuk, Khanty-Mansiysk 2005; Black never getting the chance to castle after further inaccuracies. However, Gelfand suggests that Black could have improved with 12...Nbd7! , so that if 13.cxb6? Nc5 14.Nxc5 Bxf3 , and Black wins material.

11.Neg5?!

This is marvellous chess, absolutely brilliant, but if Black has an advantage at a later stage, then White must have made a mistake. In the end, the exchange sacrifice was not quite sound, but if it was good enough to beat Anand, then it would have been good enough to beat anyone.

11...c5

Topalov would, of course, have analysed this position in depth at home. There will inevitably be hidden depths, including sacrifices and unexpected manoeuvres, making it extremely difficult for Anand to steer a way through to safety over the board.

Black's move is certainly logical and natural, opening up the long diagonal, and continuing his earlier development plan. Is it good or not? We wait and see. However, could Black have tried to refute or undermine White's attacking plans?

11...h6?! looks rather too drastic. White is clearly intending to sacrifice, so we try 12.Nxf7! (better than 12.Nxe6 ) 12...Kxf7 13.Ne5+ :

(a) If then 13...Kf8 , Anand gives 14.Bh3 Qxd4 15.Ng6+ Ke8 16.O-O Qe4 (any tame rook move would give White excellent compensation for the piece) 17.Qxe4 Nxe4 .

Now White has to continue the attack, and has no time to snatch a rook with 18.Nxh8? Nxd2 .

Therefore 18.Rfe1! , and if 18...Nxd2 19.Rxe6 , and White will clearly win back the bishop on e7, and will win even more material, ending up ahead. Anand continues the analysis with 18...c5 19.Bg2 Bf6 20.Rxe4!! . Material is level now, and all four rooks are under attack, directly or indirectly, by minor pieces; an unusual situation. After 20...Bxe4 21.Bxe4 Bxa1 22.Nxh8 g5 23.h4 Bxh8 24.hxg5 hxg5 25.Bxg5 , White will end up a pawn ahead in a minor piece endgame. Anand gives too 20...Kd7 , and then demonstrates that White can set up another desperado sacrifice with 21.Rxe6 Bxg2 22.Rxf6 gxf6 23.Nxh8 , and eventually win, a pawn ahead.

(b) Alternatively, Anand analyses 13...Kg8 14.Bh3 Qxd4 15.Bxe6+ Kf8 16.Ng6+ Ke8 17.O-O-O! Qa1+ 18.Qb1 Ba3+ 19.Kc2 Qxb1+ 20.Kxb1 , with advantage to White. Here Tyomkin suggests 14...Bc8 15.O-O , with pressure and excellent compensation for the sacrificed knight. Carrying on with possible moves by Black, play could continue with 15...Bd6 16.Ng6 Rh7 (Black needs his extra compensation) 17.Rfe1 Na6 18.Bxe6 Bxe6 19.Rxe6 .

White now has a second pawn in compensation for his knight, and still can put on the pressure. Here 19...Nc7 20.Re3 Qd7 21.Rae1 Rd8 22.Ne7+ Kf8 (better than 22...Kh8 23.Qg6 ) gives White a perpetual, but it is not so clear that he has more: 23.Rf3 Bxe7 24.Qxh7 Ne6 25.Be3!? ( 25.Bxh6 gxh6 26.Qxh6+ draws) 25...Nxd4 26.Bxd4 Qxd4 27.Qh8+ Kf7 28.Rxe7 Kxe7 29.Qxg7+ Ke6 30.Re3+ Kf5 31.h3 h5 32.Rf3+ Ke4 33.Re3+ is a perpetual. Naturally, this is only one line of several possibilities, but it provides a useful indicator that first, Black's position is not about to collapse, and, second, that White's sacrifice on f7 seems sound.

Black should also consider 11...O-O , but White could start attacking with 12.O-O-O h6 13.h4 , and if 13...c5 14.d5 exd5 15.cxd5 with ideas of attack, as in the game, but without forcing White to sacrifice the knight.

Black might just as well try for extra material.

12.d5!

The d4-d5 pawn push, famed by Kasparov in his youthful years, and with several new twists by Topalov and by others. It is a specialty of the Queen's Indian, but there are also similar d5-pawn pushes in the Grünfeld.

On any other move. Black is better.

12...exd5

And Black must take up the challenge.

13.cxd5

The sacrifice is familiar and dangerous, but there is an unusual peculiarity following White's opening set-up, with his pawn being on g3 rather than the more natural g2, and his other pawn being on b3 rather than b2. One might expect that Black could set up counter-play on either of the long diagonals, and certainly there are threats along these lines, but White, maybe unexpectedly, can parry these threats and set up his own. This is the sort of position where no player could try such a gambit purely by instinct over the board Topalov would clearly have looked at this at home, and decided it was good, or at the very least promising.

13...h6

And Black has to make another extremely difficult decision. He has three different ways to take the pawn on d5, could attack the knight (13...h6), or could castle. Five tempting moves, and even if further analysis suggests that, say, two of these moves may be rejected quickly as unsound, it is possible that only one of the remaining moves might prove to be good, or at least clearly better than anything else.

On such assumptions, there may only be a 30 to 40 percent chance of finding a good move. Then after that the defender has to find a string of further correct moves to defend against the attack. Over several moves, the chances of playing ten accurate moves against a strong attacking player may end up to be very slight. All great attackers rely on this arithmetic.

Let us try to whittle down some of the weaker moves just here:

(a) 13...Qxd5? 14.Bc4 leaves Black with no good moves. After 14...Qc6 15.Bxf7+ Kf8 16.Bc4 White is clearly better. In this line, take notice of the way in which the queen and four minor pieces cover so many squares. Coordination is an important part of attack.

(b) 13...Bxd5 14.O-O-O leaves Black's pieces looking rather frayed on the d-file. Anand gives 14...h6 15.Bc3 hxg5 16.Bxf6 gxf6 17.Bc4 with a big plus for White. Play this on for a few moves, and it becomes decisive after 17...Bxf3 18.Rxd8+ Bxd8 19.Re1+ Kf8 20.Qd3 Na6 21.Qd7 .

(c) 13...Nxd5 14.Bb5+ allows two ways to block the check with the knight:

(c1) 14...Nd7 allows White to play the simple tactic 15.Nxf7?! Kxf7 16.Bxd7 , with the idea 16...Qxd7?? 17.Ne5+ , but Black can be more sophisticated: after 16...Bf6! , he has taken the edge. Here 15.O-O-O! is much better, making full use of the various open lines. If, for example, 15...Nc7 16.Bc4 Bxf3 17.Qf5 f6 18.Nxf3 , and Black's play will be uncomfortable.

(c2) The other try for Black is 14...Nc6 . If 15.Nxf7? Kxf7 16.Bxc6 , Black has 16...Bf6! 17.Bxb7 Qe7+ with an edge. 15.Ne5?! also lacks subtlety, and after 15...Bxg5 16.Nxc6 Bxd2+ 17.Qxd2 Qf6 Black is again very comfortable. Sometimes it is wisest to complete one's development, here with 15.O-O-O followed by Rhe1, with highly promising gambit play.

(d) Finally, 13...O-O 14.O-O-O g6 15.h4 gives White excellent attacking chances: for example, 15...Bxd5 16.Bc3 Nbd7 17.h5 , and Black's position is horrible.

Ultimately there is no safe and simple way for Black to try to refute Topalov's play, or even to equalize with any sort of ease. Technically, Anand's 13...h6 is almost certainly best. Sometimes the defender has to ride the storm.

14.Nxf7!?

The art of sacrifice is still alive! Often sacrificial play has been enhanced, rather than diminished, by modern opening theory.

Topalov does not allow Anand the luxury of a quiet equal endgame after 14.Bb5+ Nbd7 15.Ne6 fxe6 16.Qg6+ ( 16.dxe6? O-O wins for Black) 16...Kf8 17.dxe6 Qe8 18.Qxe8+ Nxe8 19.exd7 Bxf3 20.dxe8Q+ Rxe8 21.Bxe8 Bxh1 . Rather he forces his opponent to work harder.

14...Kxf7

15.O-O-O!

Sacrifices can sometimes be almost routine. Not so much, though, when a player is not ahead in development, and castles quietly without a direct attack. White is relying not so much on his own pieces' strength, but rather Black's weaknesses in front of the king.

15.Ne5+ Kg8 16.d6 is the obvious attacking idea. Black can then take the rook: 16...Bxh1 17.Bc4+ Bd5 18.Bxd5+ Nxd5 19.Qg6 Qxd6 20.Qf7+ Kh7 21.Qf5+ Kg8 (not 21...g6?? 22.Qf7 mate) with a perpetual. In this line, 17...Kf8 18.Ng6+ Ke8 19.Nxh8 Bxd6 20.Qg6+ Kd7 21.O-O-O would be extremely risky for Black: 21...Be4 seems the best way of keeping the position under control, with 22.Qxg7+ Qe7 23.Bxh6 Qxg7 24.Bxg7 Ne8 25.Bc3 leading to complicated endgame play, with three connected passed pawns versus knight and another pawn.

After the text, the attack is strong, and very few people would be able to find a way to safety for Black.

15...Bd6!?

Anand displays tough defensive chess, manoeuvring his bishops to the b8-h2 and c8-h3 diagonals. It is fair to say that the vast majority of players would have fallen quickly against Topalov's onslaught. As we shall see later, even Anand gave way at a critical moment.

The simple pawn capture, 15...Bxd5? , has to be regarded with suspicion. Black may have the long diagonal for the bishop, but he has also weakened the d-file and the c4-g8 diagonal, and the pins will soon intensify. After 16.Bc3 Nbd7 ( 16...Nc6 17.Bxf6 Bxf6 18.Rxd5 quickly regains all the material, and the attack continues) 17.Rxd5 Nxd5 18.Qf5+ N7f6 19.Ne5+ Kf8 20.Ng6+ Kg8 21.Bxf6 Bxf6 22.Bc4 , sure enough, White wins by the pin.

A more interesting battle emerges after the line 15...Nbd7 16.Nh4 Nf8 17.d6 :

(a) After 17...Qxd6 18.Bc4+ Ne6 19.Qg6+ Kf8 20.Rhe1 Qd4 21.Bxe6 Qa1+ 22.Qb1 Qxb1+ 23.Kxb1 Black has succeeded in exchanging the queens, but not much else. Material is equal, but White has clearly a winning piece advantage.

(b) 17...Bxd6 similarly does not slow down the attack much: 18.Bc4+ Bd5 19.Bxh6!? Rxh6 20.Rxd5 Nxd5 ( 20...Ne6 21.Rdd1 gives White massive compensation for the knight) 21.Bxd5+ Ne6 22.Qf5+ Rf6 23.Bxe6+ with a winning attack.

(c) Black might just as well take the rook, 17...Bxh1! , and hope for the best. Tyomkin, no doubt pressed by deadlines, suggests only that after 18.dxe7 Qxe7 19.f3 the bad bishop on h1 drops, White is only the exchange down, and still has an attack. If, however, one of the players is still a whole rook down, it is useful to look further:

(c1) 19...Qe5 is a possibility, and White must be careful not to allow ...Qa1+. Maybe then 20.Bc3 Qe3+ 21.Kb2 Ne6 22.Bxf6! gxf6 ( 22...Kg8 23.Qg6 Rh7 24.Bd3 wins, as does 22...Kxf6 23.Qg6+ Ke7 24.Nf5+ ) 23.Qg6+ Kf8 24.Rd7 with a mating attack.

(c2) 19...Ne6 looks reasonably solid at first, but Black still has problems with developing his pieces. After 20.Qg6+ Kg8 21.Bc4 Qf7 22.Rxh1 Re8 23.Re1 Black cannot unravel his pieces, and White should win. Here 20...Kf8 escapes the dangerous diagonal. There is complicated play after 21.Qf5 Nd4 22.Ng6+ Ke8 23.Bb5+ Kd8 24.Bf4! , but White's queen and bishop pair remain dominant: for example, 24...Qb7 25.Nxh8 Bxf3 26.Qe6 Bh5 27.Qd6+ Nd7 28.Re1 , and White wins, or 24...Qd6 25.Nxh8 Nxf5 26.Nf7+ Kc8 27.Nxd6+ Nxd6 28.Rxd6 , which does not provide real safety.

(c3) 19...Bxf3!? 20.Nxf3 g5 returns the bishop quickly, but diverts the knight from covering g6. However, White retains excellent compensation for the exchange and pawn sacrifice, his minor pieces being formidable, while Black's knights have no good squares. Play would continue with 21.Re1 followed by Bc4+.

So it seems that Anand's assessment is absolutely correct in preferring 15...Bd6 .

16.Nh4!

On the edge, but hitting hard on the light squares, with ideas of Qg6+ and Nf5.

16...Bc8!

Steady defence. Alternatively:

(a) 16...Bxd5? opens up the position too quickly. After 17.Bc3 Bxh1 18.Bc4+ White is, of course, winning, and Tyomkin signs off here. The stubborn computer gives 18...Ke7 19.Nf5+ Kf8 20.Rxd6 Nbd7 21.Nh4 Be4 , but then a queen sacrifice 22.Qxe4 Nxe4 23.Ng6+ Ke8 23.Re6+ Kf7 24.Rf6+ wins.

(b) 16...Re8 17.Qg6+ Kg8 18.Nf5 is highly threatening, and helps explain why Anand chose 16...Bc8 instead. Tyomkin gives 18...Qc7 19.Bxh6 Bf8 20.Bxg7! Bxg7 21.Bb5! with far too many threats.

(c) Anand himself gives 16...Na6 17.Bh3 in Informator, but probably without much expectation that Black would survive. At least though Black is starting to develop his queenside. One possibility is 17...Bxd5 18.Rhe1 Nc7 19.Bc3 . The practical player over the board would not have to analyse hard to conclude that White, although a knight down, is almost certainly winning. He has excellent diagonals for his queen and bishops, and strong open lines for his rooks, while Black has several weaknesses. Play might continue 19...Kg8 20.Bxf6 Qxf6 21.Rxd5 Qa1+ 22.Kd2 Qxe1+ 23.Kxe1 Re8+ 24.Kd1 Nxd5 25.Qc4 Re5 26.Bg2 , winning material.

17.Re1!

Just as White's attack appears to be slowing down, Topalov throws a log on to the fire with Re6, sacrificing the exchange to regain control with Nf5.

17.Qg6+ Kg8 18.Bc3 a5!? would have given nothing.

17...Na6

To develop the minor pieces, or to contest the open file - which is better?

After 17...Re8 18.Bb5 Re7 Anand, looking from the defender's point of view, recommends 19.Bc3! . Clearly the idea is to eliminate the f6-knight. The sort of line that Anand would have been thinking of is presumably something like 19...a6 (trying to gain a tempo, and helping to defend with ...Ra7) 20.Qg6+ Kg8 21.Bxf6 Rxe1+ 22.Rxe1 Qxf6 23.Re8+ Bf8 24.Bd3 Qa1+ 25.Kd2 Qb2+ 26.Ke1 Qa1+ 27.Ke2 Qb2+ 28.Kf3 , and Black is running out of checks, while White continues his powerful attack. Here too 19.Bxh6 would have been a possibility, demolishing a pawn, and if 19...Rxe1+? 20.Rxe1 gxh6 21.Qg6+ Kf8 22.Qxh6+ Kg8 23.Qg5+ Kf7 (or 23...Kf8 24.Re8+! ) 24.Be8+! , making attractive use of the pin. However, Black could decline the sacrifice, and 19...Kg8! is probably playable for White, but not better for him.

18.Re6!

The promised sacrifice. Or at least a breakthrough on the light squares, rather than a genuine sacrifice.

18...Nb4?

Of course, Black cannot take, as if 18...Bxe6? then 19.dxe6+ Kg8 20.Bxa6 , so really it is a pseudo-sacrifice, for the moment at least.

With the move played, Anand forces Topalov to exchange his dark-squared bishop for the knight, but as play moves on, it becomes increasingly clear that it is the pressure on the light squares that becomes more important.

Instead 18...Nc7! is critical. The knight stays on a good square, attacking and/or defending the e6-, d5- and e8-squares. Inevitably play will be sharp.

(a) The most direct reply is 19.Qg6+ Kg8 :

(a1) Then 20.Rxd6 Qxd6 21.Bf4 Qd7 22.Bxc7 Qxc7 23.d6 Qf7 24.Bc4 Be6 25.Bxe6 Qxe6 26.Nf5 Rh7 does not quite work for White.

(a2) Therefore the critical line is 20.Nf5 Bf8 21.Bc4 with counterplay with 21...b5 . Now 22.Bc3 Nxe6 23.dxe6 bxc4 24.e7 Qe8 holds fine for Black. Tyomkin therefore recommends 22.Ne7+ Bxe7 23.Rxe7 with a further divide:

(a21) 23...Rh7 24.Bc3 Qf8 25.Rhe1 leads to obvious zugzwang potential. Carrying on with this, White wins after 25...bxc4 26.Bxf6 Nxd5 27.Rf7 Qxf7 28.Re8+ Qf8 29.Rxf8+ Kxf8 30.Be5 .

(a22) Black therefore has to jettison material with 23...Qxe7! 24.d6+ bxc4 25.dxe7 Be6 26.bxc4 Bxc4 27.Bf4 Bf7 .

Having reached this position in his analysis, Tyomkin comments that 'the outcome here is completely unclear'. This is no cop-out. The position is genuinely difficult. White has queen and two pawns for the rook and two minor pieces, about level according to positional theory, but of course such a position is likely to be unbalanced. One of White's extra pawns is passed on the seventh, which is useful, but it cannot win material, and merely ties down Black's pieces. If Black can gradually bring his pieces into better squares, he will be on top. Probably the position favours Black: for example, 28.Qf5 Ncd5 29.Bd6 g6!? (there are several alternatives, but this seems among the most direct) 30.Qe5 Kh7 31.Rd1 Rac8 32.h4 Nd7 33.Qb2 Rhe8 , and while Black's pawn structure has been slightly compromised by his ...g6 pawn push, his pieces have been quickly activated, and are strong.

(b) Anand himself, who after all has tried over the board to defend this position as Black, concentrated on a different line, 19.Bc4 b5 20.Bc3 ( 20.Qg6+ Kg8 21.Nf5 Bf8 transposes to variation 'a2') seeing this as a dangerous:

(b1) If 20...bxc4? , Black's position collapses after 21.Qg6+ Kf8 22.Bxf6 .

(b2) Black's position also folds after 20...Nxe6? 21.dxe6+ Bxe6 22.Qg6+ Kf8 23.Bxe6 Qc7 24.Qf5 threatening Ng6+.

(b3) 20...Nfe8! , avoiding the capture on f6, is the most secure.

If then 21.Bxg7?? Qg5+ with a quick win. The same resource is found after 21.Qg6+ Kg8 22.Bxg7? Qg5+! 23.Qxg5 hxg5 , and White, already a piece down, has four more pieces under attack. Instead the superior 22.Rxe8+ Nxe8 23.Bxb5 Qg5+! is highly thematic, and gives Black a substantial edge after 24.Qxg5 hxg5 25.Ng6 Nc7 26.Bc6 Rh6 .

There is also plenty of entertainment value in 18...Kg8!? 19.Bc3 Nxd5 20.Qg6 Qg5+ 21.Qxg5+ hxg5 22.Rxd6 Nxc3 23.Bc4+ Kh7 24.Bd3+ g6 , which probably ends up as a draw. The author looked at this in depth, but there seems to be little reason to publish the line when 18...Nc7! is almost certainly better, with advantage to Black.

Should we say that objectively Topalov's play was unsound? Or should we congratulate him on winning? There is no clear answer, and there are two opposing views on this point. Broadly, the tactical player will tend to argue that he will win by playing better than his opponent, and if there is a mistake on the way, then so be it, no player will be expected to play absolutely perfect chess in a genuinely complicated game. The positional player, in contrast, will want to keep the position under control, arguing that if he makes no mistakes, then he will not lose, and if his opponent makes a mistake, he will lose. Topalov is clearly one of the tacticians.

19.Bxb4

19.Qg6+ Kg8 20.Nf5 Bf8 does not achieve much: 21.Nxh6+ Rxh6 22.Bxh6 Bxe6 23.dxe6 Qd4 and Black takes over.

19...cxb4 20.Bc4

Ultimately, however great the complications may be, the positional strategy is consistent and clear. White must control as many light squares as possible of those covering Black's king. The only justification of White's early sacrifices was to fracture Black's light squares, making the king open to attack. White needs to be consistent, making further sacrifices if necessary. Strangely the bishop has not been needed just yet, but now needs to pile in. The e6-square is critical, whether for rook or pawn, and that square needs to be bolstered.

20...b5!

And Black needs to divert the bishop. Anand gives 20...Kg8 21.Nf5 Bxe6 ( 21...Bf8 22.d6 ) 22.dxe6 Be7 23.Rd1 Qf8 (or 23...Qc7 24.Rd7 ) 24.Rd7 Re8 25.Nh4 Nh7 26.Qg6 Ng5 27.f4 b5 28.Bxb5 as winning for White. Indeed, it is positional strangulation.

21.Bxb5

There's not much else. If 21.Nf5 Bf8, and Black is safe and solid.

21...Be7?

Even Anand slips after Topalov's fierce attack.

21...Bxe6? loses quickly after 22.dxe6+ Kg8 23.e7! Bxe7 24.Bc4+ Nd5 25.Rd1 Bxh4 26.Rxd5 . Grandmaster commentators such as Anand and Tyomkin may have regarded this too elementary to be worth publishing, but of course the players would need to have analysed this during the game.

The view given at the time was 21...Kg8! with 22.Bc4 regarded as the best reply, and if 22...Rb8 ( 22...Bxe6 23.dxe6 is still too dangerous) 23.Nf5 Rb6 'and Black manages to survive' (Tyomkin). However, it is not so clear that White should continue to concentrate on the diagonal. 22.Qe2! , based on active play on the central files, looks more effective. Of course, if 22...Bxe6?? 23.Qxe6+ White immediately wins. This ensures that for the moment at least White controls the light squares. If, though, 22...Bb7 , White should not try the same trick: 23.Re8+? , hoping for 23...Nxe8?? 24.Qe6+ , as Black instead gives up the queen with fully adequate compensation after 23...Qxe8! 24.Bxe8 Rxe8 , and White runs out of attack. Here 23.Rd1! is better, with good centralization:

(a) 23...Qc7+? 24.Bc4 Rd8 25.Nf5 Kh7 26.Nxd6 Rxd6 27.Re7 is inadequate for Black.

(b) 23...Rc8+ looks best. Then 24.Bc4 is a self-pin, and White makes no progress after, for example, 24...Rc5 . However, 24.Kb2! is better. Then if 24...Qc7 , White invades a further light square with 25.Bc6! , obtaining a clear advantage after 25...Bxc6?! 26.dxc6 Qxc6 27.Rexd6 . Better is 25...Bf8 when there would be no more than equality after the obvious exchange sacrifice 26.Rxf6? gxf6 27.Qe6+ Kh7 , but 26.Bxb7! Qxb7 27.Ng6 wins the battle of the light squares, most convincingly after 27...Rh7?! 28.Re7 Qb6 ( 28...Bxe7 29.Qe6 mate) 29.d6 .

It is, of course, more than possible that somewhere along the many variations Black could avoid being close to losing, but the general run of play would suggest that White is still better. The judgement is that after Anand's mistake, Black is worse, not 'unclear' as in early assessments.

22.Ng6

Now Black has too many pieces under attack.

It is quite possible that Anand had missed that after 22...Bxe6 23.dxe6+ Kg8 24.Rd1 a queen move was no longer defending the bishop. Or, more likely, that after 24...Nd5 25.Rxd5 Bg5+ , he overlooked the winning 26.f4! , instead of the drawing line 26.Rxg5? Qxg5+ 27.f4 Qxb5 28.Ne7+ Kf8 29.Ng6+ Kg8 .

To put this in context, Anand's 'elementary" blunder is about seven moves deep, much deeper than most club players would be able to calculate.

22...Nxd5

Anand changes tack, and hopes for the best.

23.Rxe7+?!

Now Topalov makes a much simpler mistake himself, but one which did not change the end result. One would assume that under the great complexity of the game, both players must already have been in deep time trouble.

There was a simpler win after 23.Re5! Bb7 24.Qf5+ ( 24.Nxh8+ also wins) 24...Kg8 25.Bc4 with a highly thematic pin against the knight on d5.

23...Nxe7

Black does not have to resign yet.

24.Bc4+

And White continues the attack.

24...Kf6!

Maybe Topalov had thought that this was hopeless, the king being well out in the open. Instead 24...Be6 25.Bxe6+ Kxe6 26.Re1+ Kf6 27.Nxe7 Rc8 28.Nxc8 Qxc8 29.Re4 leads to a winning rook and pawn ending for White.

25.Nxh8

Now White is a pawn up.

25...Qd4!

There have been slip-ups in the game, understandably given the complexity of play. Anand has shown great presence of mind, however. He is able to keep the position alive, even when his king is stuck on f6, and so many of White's pieces are hovering.

25...Qxh8? would have won a piece, but lost the king: for instance, 26.Rd1 Nf5 27.Qe4 Rb8 28.Qc6+ Kg5 29.f4+ , and mate in a few moves.

26.Rd1

The king slips round to the other side of the Took. It is the only winning attempt, indeed the only way for White to get out of trouble.

26...Qa1+

The only way to keep the initiative.

27.Kd2

27.Qb1?? Qxb1+ 28.Kxb1 Bf5+ is, of course, a blunder.

27...Qd4+

27...Qc3+ 28.Qxc3 bxc3+ 29.Kxc3 keeps White two pawns up in a safe position. The knight cannot be trapped: for example, 29...Be6 30.Rd6 , winning easily.

28.Ke1

He goes for a win, whereas 28.Kc1?! repeats.

28...Qe5+

The checks must continue.

29.Qe2!

The only good move:

(a) 29.Kf1? Bh3+ loses a piece instantly.

(b) 29.Kd2? is too late to offer a draw; 29...Be6 again wins a piece.

(c) 29.Be2?! sets a vicious trap. After 29...Bg4? 30.f4 Qxe2+ 31.Qxe2 Bxe2 32.Rd6+ Kf5 33.Kxe2 if Black finally takes the knight with 33...Rxh8? , he gets mated. White plays 34.Kf3! h5 35.h3 , and mates with g4. An attractive finish, and many players would fall for it, but Black improves with 29...Be6! 30.f4 Qc3+ , and this time he can win the knight safely.

29...Qxe2+ 30.Kxe2

After all the complications, we have now reached a semi-endgame with Topalov a pawn up. A good result, but presumably he would have liked to have done better.

30...Nf5

Unfortunately Black cannot regain his pawn: 30...Bg4+ 31.f3 Bxf3+ 32.Kxf3 Rxh8 33.Rd6+ Kg5 34.h4+ Kh5 35.Bf7+ g6 36.Re6 and mate soon follows.

31.Nf7

After the recent setback, Topalov is presumably not too interested in playing for combinations, and relies on keeping the extra pawn.

31.Rd8!? was a possibility, and after 31...Nxg3+ 32.Ke1 ( 32.hxg3?? Bg4+ ) 32...Nf5 33.Rf8+ Kg5 34.Nf7+ Kh4 35.Bd5 Rb8 36.Be4 Black is seriously tied up. It would take time to calculate and verify all this, though, and when time is short, it is usually best when the position is already favourable to play simply.

31...a5?!

This makes life easier for White, allowing the chance to coordinate his pieces.

After 31...Be6 , the straightforward 32.Nd6 would have been good to reach the time control, and 32.Bxe6 Kxe6 33.Nd8+ also seems good.

32.g4

Pushing away the knight from a strong square.

32...Nh4

Forced to the edge.

33.h3

Keeping the g-pawn.

33...Ra7

Somehow he needs to bring the rook into play.

34.Rd6+

Forcing the king to a worse square.

34...Ke7 35.Rb6

Now White's pieces are working together, and he has an extra pawn. "The rest is a matter of technique', as they say.

35...Rc7

Still looking to develop the rook.

36.Ne5

But even the knight is on a safe square.

36...Ng2

The knight was not doing anything on h4. Anand makes a last-ditch try, giving up two pieces for a rook, and hoping to pick up the pawn on a2, which may eventually allow Black to set up a passed pawn.

37.Ng6+

Blocking Black's knight.

37...Kd8 38.Kf1 Bb7 39.Rxb7

This still seems best and the most direct, and makes it to move 40 without White having to think.

39...Rxb7 40.Kxg2 Rd7

White now is clearly ahead in material. The only chance for Black to hold is to win White's a-pawn, then create a passed pawn, and then try to pick up a minor piece in return for the advanced passed pawn. However, with reasonable care, White should win.

41.Nf8

White plays with the knight actively.

41...Rd2

Black too must get moving. There is no point in holding on to the g-pawn with 41...Ra7 42.Ne6+ , which would leave several alternative winning plans for White.

42.Ne6+

Continuing his plan.

42.a4?? bxa3 , taking en passant, is a common type of blunder for less experienced players. See further comments to the ending of Game 16, Shirov-Aronian.

42...Ke7

The king needs to rush to the kingside in view of White's 3-1 pawn majority, and 42...Kd7 43.Nxg7 Rxa2 44.Nf5 a4 45.bxa4 Rxa4?? 46.Bb5+ does not help.

43.Nxg7

Now just a few pawns go, mostly Black's.

43...Rxa2 44.Nf5+ Kf6 45.Nxh6 Rc2

A last-ditch attempt to create a passed pawn, supported by the rook, and to force White to sacrifice the bishop.

45...a4 46.bxa4 Rxa4 47.f4 is ineffective for Black: 47...b3 48.g5+ Kg6 49.Bf7+ Kg7 50.Bxb3 Rxf4 51.Kg3 , and White has too much material for Black to cope.

46.Bf7

Naturally White must avoid the sacrifice on c4. After the move played, the bishop and knight at first look fragile, but White's pawns save the day.

46...Rc3

If 46...Kg7 47.g5 , and White will be able to cover the pawn with h4 or f4, while 46...Kg5 47.Nf5 is simple; the knight returns to queenside play via e3 or d4.

47.f4!

Black was threatening 47...a4 , and White can do nothing about this, at least in terms of pure defence.

White will want to push his three passed pawns as quickly as possible, aiming for ultimate promotion, but also taking up a few squares for the minor pieces.

47...a4

The only try.

48.bxa4 b3 49.g5+

The pawns push through.

49...Kg7

This will end up in checkmate, but if instead 49...Ke7 50.f5 b2 51.f6+ Kd7 52.Bg6 Rc1 53.Bf5+ Kd8 , then using White's two passed pawns, with the help of the piece, wins: 54.g6 b1Q 55.Bxb1 Rxb1 56.g7 .

50.f5 b2

He can queen, but he cannot save the king.

51.f6+

Connected passed pawns must be pushed.

51...Kh7

He is also checkmated after 51...Kf8 52.g6 b1Q 53.g7 .

52.Nf5 1-0

52...b1Q 53.g6+ Kh8 54.g7 Kh7 55.g8Q is mate, while Black runs out of checks after 52...Rc2+ 53.Kf3 Rc3+ 54.Kg4 Rc4+ 55.Kh5 (but not 55.Bxc4? b1Q ).
