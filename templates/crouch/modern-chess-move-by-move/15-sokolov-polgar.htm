Game 15
I.Sokolov-J.Polgar
Hoogeveen 2006
Nimzo-Indian Defence E49

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3

Simple enough. White wants to bring his kingside pieces into play. There is a slight problem that the dark-squared bishop cannot develop, and this indeed was a significant factor later in this game.

4.Qc2 , keeping open a later Bg5, is also popular See Game 12, Bareev-Efimenko, and Game 13, Kramnik-Leko.

Among other possibilities, 4.Nf3 d5 is seen in Game 26, Wang Yue-Movsesian.

4...O-O

One of many alternatives. 4...c5 , and 4...b6 are both main lines, but not covered in this collection. If Black wants to try the Nimzowitsch plan of doubling the c-pawns, and playing against them, the best option would be 4...c5 , aiming for 5.Bd3 Nc6 6.Nf3 Bxc3+ 7.bxc3 d6 .

5.Bd3

The most common, although 5.Nge2 is also popular, planning to recapture with the knight, rather than the pawn, on c3. The knight is slightly misplaced after 5...d5 6.a3 Be7 , but in compensation Black has lost a tempo with the bishop. White usually tries 7.cxd5 , and then either recapture is playable.

5...d5

Aiming for a Queen's Gambit type of position. Black's bishop is more aggressive on b4 than on e7, but he will often be forced to exchange, giving away the bishop-pair. However, White no longer has Bg5 possibilities.

6.a3

Sokolov's favoured approach, setting up a Samisch structure.

6.Nf3 , 6.cxd5 and, though less often played, 6.Nge2 are also good.

6...Bxc3+

The natural reply.

6...Be7 is an alternative, and is playable but not very inspiring. Black argues that in comparison with the Queen's Gambit (for example, 1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6 3.Nf3 Nf6 4.Bg5 ), White does not have the Bg5 development. Black has, however, lost a 'semi-tempo', and White's a3 is often useful.

6...dxc4 allows extra transpositional possibilities, for example, 7.Bxc4 Bd6 , but White too has an alternative, with 7.Bxh7+ followed by axb4, with a slight but secure edge.

7.bxc3

So Black has given up the bishop-pair. This is of course standard in the Nimzo-Indian. Black has free development with his, or her, pieces, and has every opportunity of gaining parity in the centre.

7...dxc4

7...e5!? has been tried a few times:

a) The tactical point is that after 8.dxe5 dxc4 9.Bxc4? ( 9.exf6 Qxd3 10.Qxd3 cxd3 is about equal) 9...Qxd1+ 10.Kxd1 Ng4 Black is doing well, and has recovered the pawn. White's isolated queenside pawn pair will become something of a problem as we get closer to the endgame.

b) White usually prefers 8.Ne2 e4 9.Bc2 , and after 9...dxc4 there are some similarities to the pawn structure in our main game.

7...c6 , as played in I.Sokolov-S.Movsesian, Sarajevo 2007, is a quiet alternative, aiming for gradual equality after 8.Nf3 b6 9.cxd5 cxd5 10.Qe2 Bb7 11.O-O Qc8 , followed by ...Ba6. The game was later drawn.

8.Bxc4 c5

8...e5 is again playable, but no longer has the possibility of an ...e4 hit against the bishop. White could perhaps consider 9.Nf3 e4 10.Ne5 Nbd7 11.f4!? exf3 :

(a) 12.Qxf3? is natural, but a serious blunder. Black has a winning attack after 12...Nxe5 13.dxe5 Bg4! 14.Qf4 Qd1+ 15.Kf2 Qc2+ 16.Kg1 Ne4 .

(b) 12.Nxf3! is better, and Black might well have a few problems over the f7-square (0-0, Ng5 or Ne5, etc). Perhaps such thoughts help explain why Black does not try an early ...e5.

9.Ne2

9.Nf3 leads to mainline positions, often reached too via 6.Nf3 .

Sokolov attempts what he hopes will become a more flexible development, covering any problems on the c3-square, and maybe even thinking about gaining pawn space with f3. The trouble is that he no longer keeps control of e5, and Polgar gains a second hit in the centre, with ...e5 as well as ...c5.

9...Qc7

Gaining a minor tempo, with the threatened hit on the bishop after ...cxd4.

The threat is greater than the execution', as the great chess thinker Aron Nimzowitsch noted. Here 9...cxd4?! 10.cxd4 Qc7 11.Ba2 gives nothing for Black. It is better to keep the good pawn in play.

9...Nc6 is a good alternative, keeping the tension. Black will usually play ...Qc7 at some stage, though.

10.Ba2

Keeping the bishop out of harm's way, and reminding Black that if she tries a quick ...e5, White still has pressure against f7.

10.Bd3 is the main alternative. Then 10...e5 is well-timed, and should lead to equal play.

The next stage of the development plan. Black has to decide what to do with the queenside minor pieces.

10...b6

The bishop goes first, either to b7 or a6.

10...e5 has been tried several times. Then the simple 11.d5 might be best. The passed pawn is not a big threat, especially so early in the game, but it demands some respect. Later White may try to bolster the pawn with c4, and possibly f3 followed by e4.

Polgar's move seems more logical, aiming to develop her pieces while giving away nothing.

11.O-O

Or maybe 11.Bb2 , but on the other hand, while we know that White has to castle kingside, we do not quite know whether the bishop is best on b2. It is better to keep flexibility, and to castle immediately.

11...Ba6

Did the reader assume that Black was going to fianchetto immediately on the long diagonal?

Black is not really threatening to attack on g2, not least because White is often quite happy to close the diagonal with f3, if required, and then e4 The a6-f1 diagonal, on the other hand, is more of an irritation. White's doubled c-pawns have gone, but he still has the problem that the c4-square is a slight weakness, making it difficult to play c4, especially if Black's bishop is pressing on this square.

12.Re1

To free the knight.

12...Nc6

The natural square. She completes her development, before pushing the pawn with ...e5.

13.Ng3

13.Nf4 , seemingly more aggressive, has not gained much attention. 13...e5 14.Nd5 Nxd5 15.Bxd5 Rad8 16.dxc5 Ne7! leaves Black comfortable.

13...Rad8

In such positions, it is often difficult to decide which rook should be placed on the d-file. In previous games. Black had tended to play 13...Rfd8 , with the option of placing rooks on the c-file and d-file. Polgar has decided instead that she wants to add extra cover for the f7-square, after she has opened up White's diagonal with ...e5.

14.Bb2

14.Nh5 Nxh5 15.Qxh5 Bd3! , followed perhaps by ...Bg6, covers any attack by White on Black's kingside.

White prefers to complete his development, before attacking.

14...e5

She takes over the initiative in the centre, but loosens some light squares.

14...Na5 15.e4 Nc4 16.Bc1 cxd4 17.cxd4 Qd7 18.Bxc4 Bxc4 19.Bb2 still gives White a slight edge, with the idea of d5 followed by breaking up the pawn structure with Bxf6.

15.Qc2

In Informator, Polgar suggests as better 15.d5! Na5 16.e4 Bc4 . White's connected passed pawn is of course useful, but this is not enough to force an edge of itself. To aim for a realistic plus, White would need to push pieces to the kingside, maybe starting off with 17.Bb1 , taking advantage that Black's knight on a5 is far away from the king-side. Black is not yet equal.

15.Rc1 Na5 16.Qf3 Rfe8 17.e4 Bc4 18.Bb1 Nb3 was tried in M.Illescas-F.Vallejo Pons, Spanish Team Championship 2004. It might look as though Black has gained an edge, attacking the rook, and putting great pressure on the pawn on d4, but White sacrificed the exchange with 19.d5! Nxc1 20.Bxc1 , keeping a strong pawn centre, and being ready to attack on the kingside. Vallejo opted for safety by returning the exchange, with 20...Rd6 21.Nf5 Nd7 22.Nxd6 Qxd6 , draw agreed.

The computer suggests that White may still be slightly better at the time of the draw agreement, but a strong human player would find it difficult to make any progress. It is difficult to activate the bishop-pair, and it is difficult to open up the pawns. Any exchange of the light-squared bishops will leave Black's knight more effective than White's remaining bishop. Also, Black has an extra queenside pawn, in compensation, obviously, for White's extra central pawn, and Black can try to expand with a later ...c4, or a more general push with ...b5 and ...a5. Probably the position is, as the players agreed, just equal.

15...Rd7

With great tension on the d-file, most obviously on the d4-pawn, but also an attempt to restrain the pawn from playing to d5.

The immediate liquidation, with 15...cxd4?! 16.cxd4 exd4 17.Bxd4 , would give White a comfortable slight edge with the help of the bishop-pair.

If 15...Na5 , Polgar suggests 16.dxe5 Bd3 17.Qc1 Qxe5 18.c4 Qe6 19.e4 with chances of attack for White.

15...Bc8 was suggested by the computer, with the idea that if 16.Rad1 Be6!? , Black's isolated pawn structure is no real weakness, and the knight on c6 does not have to reach to a5 to offer a bishop exchange. The simple 17.Bb1! leaves White slightly better.

16.Rad1

16.Ne4! Nxe4 17.Qxe4 looks like an improvement. White has no pin on the d-file, as opposed to the interpolated 16.Rad1 Rfd8 17.Ne4 Nxe4 18.Qxe4 when Black may safely try 18...Ne7 . In the suggested improvement, White might still have a slight edge after 17...Rfd8 18.d5 Ne7 19.c4 .

16...Rfd8

White no longer has an edge on the d-file.

17.h3!?

White's position is fully playable, and remains so for the next few moves. There is no need to criticize Sokolov's quiet move here. Polgar plays dramatic attacking chess later on, and this may easily create the impression that Sokolov's quiet move early on was a mistake of judgement, causing problems much later. Playing through the position move-by-move such an assessment would seem not so clear.

We are in a classic Nimzo-Indian early middlegame, in which both pawns and pieces are finely balanced. There are four possibilities, two on either side, of central pawn exchanges, but neither side would gain from initiating the pawn exchange, and indeed would lose ground. White wants to keep his d-pawn, this being clearly his best and most effective pawn, while Black would not want to exchange with ...cxd4 or ...exd4, when White could recapture with a pawn on d4. The only possibilities of making progress in the centre would either be d5 for White, too early and it would lose a pawn, or ...e4 and/or ...c4 for Black, which would gain space but would release the central pawn tension. Both players will want to keep in mind these ideas.

In the meantime, it is unclear how White should play his next couple of piece moves. 17.Ne4 is a possibility, but so too is 17.Nf5 . It is unclear as yet which would be the better square for the knight. The bishop would be good on the b1-h7 diagonal, but it is also good on the a2-g8 diagonal, and it is not totally clear that an extra bishop move would be worth a tempo. Other piece moves are also possible, but do not improve their positions.

This leaves off-centre pawn moves. The two main candidates are 17.f3!? or 17.h3!? , as played. The 17.f3 idea is fully playable, preventing Black from trying ...e4 or a possible threat with ...Ng4 after White makes a knight move. White is, however, weakening his dark squares. Sokolov instead pushes his h-pawn, cutting out possible weaknesses on the back rank, and also cutting out ...Ng4.

17...c4!?

Polgar takes the initiative in the centre, not perhaps because of aggression intent, but more from a sense that if play drifts on. Black will have fewer good quiet moves than White.

Polgar suggests 17...Na5 18.dxe5 Qxe5 19.c4 Qe6 20.Bb1 as a possibility. Here White could continue the theme of trying to play constructive quiet moves with 18.Bb1 Nc4 19.Bc1 . It is not fully clear that the knight is better on c4 than on c6. The position is probably about equal, or the usual very slight edge for White.

18.a4?!

The first real question mark given by Judit Polgar. It is still more of an indicator that White could have played the line better, rather than any indication that White would be worse. Polgar gives as better 18.Bb1 g6 (otherwise Nh5) 19.Qe2 with much improved centralization.

18...Re8!

Aiming for ...e4.

19.Ba3

Sokolov is still presumably aiming for a slight edge, despite his previous two quiet moves, hoping that the bishop-pair will prevail in the long run. Otherwise, there is a simple and natural repetition with 19.Ne4 Nd5 20.Ng5 Nf6 21.Ne4 ( 21.d5 h6 is at least equal for Black). Here Polgar gives 19...Nxe4 20.Qxe4 g6 , but ultimately White should have an edge after, for example, 21.Qf3 with the bishop-pair starting to become more effective.

19...e4

The next stage of the central pawn structure. Both players may presumably be reasonably happy with the pawn structure, White having a secure passed pawn, and Black having set up a couple of tusks with two advanced pawns close to the centre.

20.Bb1

Sokolov could consider 20.d5!? Nxd5 21.Nxe4 , but presumably he wants to keep his passed pawn. After 21...Ne5 22.Rd4 Nd3 23.Rd1 play could easily become deadlocked, with any active piece move by either side allowing the opponent to find new squares. The position looks tense but equal, one possibility being 23...h6 24.Ng3 Red8 25.Ne4 Re8 with a repetition.

Sokolov holds back his passed pawn, but Black's pieces turn out to be the more active.

20...Rd5!?

Very direct. She aims for attack. There will be tactics, but one cannot calculate everything in advance. 20...Na5 is more solid, and who knows, possibly more accurate, but does not give all that many chances of a win.

21.Ne2

The knight is the piece that can best improve its position.

21...Rg5

Continuing to attack. This is do-or-die chess, and soon Polgar is obliged to drop a pawn to gain any chance of carrying on the attack against the king.

22.Nf4

A good square for the knight, assisting both the defence of h3 and g2, and helping a pawn attack with d5.

22...Bc8?!

Polgar has played aggressively, but so far has not been taking risks. She has allowed White to set up a strong passed pawn, but so long as the pawn is not charging forward, that is acceptable, provided she has reasonable counter-play.

Her next move takes risks, though, as no longer is Black's bishop covering the c4-pawn.

22...Qd7!? , suggested by Polgar in her notes, is safer if less ambitious. Black would probably try ...Ne7, and then one of the knights to d5. Black looks at least equal.

My reading of the position is that Polgar's move is a mistake, overpressing, but that she soon recovers well, by aiming for a full-blown attack. See the notes to White's 25th, below.

23.Qe2

Aiming for the pawn.

Polgar notes the alternative, 23.Ba2!? Na5 24.d5 Bb7 25.d6 Qd7 with unclear play. This may well be Black's best line in this line. Indeed, 23...Nd5?! 24.Bxc4 Nxf4 25.exf4 Qxf4 26.Re3 , also given by Polgar, favours White. After instead 23...Ne7 24.Bxe7 Rxe7 25.d5 Ne8 , with the idea of 26.d6 ( 26.Rd4 looks equal) 26...Nxd6 27.Nd5 Qb7 28.Nxe7+ Qxe7 29.Qd2 Rg6 , Polgar suggests there is compensation for the exchange, but this seems slightly fragile after 30.Qd5! , and if 30...Bxh3 ( 30...Qe8 is possible, when maybe the simplest is 31.Qxd6 Rxd6 32.Rxd6 with an edge for White) 31.Qa8+ Ne8?! ( 31...Qe8 32.Qxe8+ Nxe8 33.g3 Bg4 34.Rd8 Kf8 35.Bxc4 with an edge for White) 32.g3 Qe5 33.Rd5! Rxg3+ 34.Kh1! , and Black's attack fades away after, for example, 34...Bg2+ 35.Kh2 Rh3+ 36.Kxg2 Qh2+ 37.Kf1 .

23...Qd7!

She abandons the c4-pawn. If 23...Ne7 24.Ba2 , and to save the pawn Black would be required to lose two tempi with 24...Ba6 . White can try for an edge with 25.Bxe7 Rxe7 26.Rb1 .

24.Qxc4

Naturally.

24...Na5

Black takes over the light squares, but with pieces rather than pawns.

25.Qb4?

Sokolov is aiming to keep the extra pawn, on a4, but his kingside is too weak.

25.Qe2! keeps White better centralized. White's isolated a-pawn is not significant enough to try to defend. Polgar gives 25...Qxa4 26.Bb4 Nc6 27.Bc2 Qa6 as unclear, but the suspicion is that White is better, with the more effective pawn structure, and a dangerous and very safe passed d-pawn. A good starting point might be 28.Qxa6 Bxa6 29.Ra1 .

This leaves the question of whether Polgar has overpressed. Indeed, if 25...Nd5 , Polgar gives 26.Bb4! Nc6 27.Rc1! , shoring up the defences:

(a) The recapture of the pawn is only temporary, since if 27...Ncxb4 28.cxb4 Nxb4 29.Qc4 a5 , White has 30.Bxe4! ; White's pieces have been carefully set up.

(b) There are ways of playing quietly, a pawn down, such as here with 27...Qd8 28.Ba3 Qf6 , but they are not especially enticing.

(c) Finally, the yahoo attack with 27...Nxf4 28.exf4 Rxg2+? ( 28...Rg6 29.f5 wins more slowly for White) 29.Kxg2 Qxh3+ 30.Kg1 Re6 ( 30...Bg4 31.Qf1 ) 31.Bxe4 does not work. White quickly shifts the bishop from the queenside to the kingside.

There are various alternatives by Black, but centralization by White seems to keep an edge.

25...Nd5

Something of a reprieve for Black. The exchange of knights now leaves White's pawns on g2 and h3 seriously weak, and behind the pawns there is a frightened king.

26.Nxd5

26.Qb5! Qxb5 27.axb5 Nxc3 28.Rc1 Nxb1 (but not 28...Nxb5? 29.Be7! Rf5 29.Bxe4 ) 29.Rxb1 Nc4 30.h4 Rf5 31.Bc1 a5! 32.bxa6 Ra5 is still slightly uncomfortable for White, even with the queens off the board. His bishop on c1 is bad.

26...Qxd5

On 26...Rxg2+ 27.Kxg2 Qxh3+ 28.Kg1 Qg4+ , the simple 29.Kh2 gives Black no more than a perpetual. Black has no time for quiet moves, as White's queen bounds around with Qd6.

Polgar would naturally have analysed the rook sacrifice, and would have wanted to ensure that she had at least a draw, just in case other lines did not work. It would have been in this context that she gave the line, in Informator, 29.Kf1 Ba6+ 30.Bd3 exd3 31.Nc7! Bc4 32.Rd2 Qh3+ 33.Kg1 Qg4+ , and finally it is a draw after various attempts at refutation.

27.h4

White has no defending pieces supporting his king, and this could easily lead to fatal exposure. If, for example, 27.Kh2 , Polgar gives 27...Bxh3! 28.gxh3 ( 28.Qb5 Qd8! ) 28...Qf5 29.f4 Rh5 with a winning attack.

If instead 27.Qd6!? , Black could of course simplify, and recover the pawn with 27...Qxd6 28.Bxd6 Bxh3 29.Bg3 with possibly Black keeping a slight edge after 29...Bg4 30.Rc1 Nc4 , and if 31.Ba2 Nb2 32.Rc2 Nd3 33.Rb1 Bd7 . Black has the more active pieces, but she must be careful about White's passed pawn and bishop-pair.

Polgar instead gives 27...Qf5 , playing for the attack, with a win after 28.Kh1 Nc4 29.Qc6 Bd7! 30.Qxc4 Rxg2 31.Kxg2 Qf3+ 32.Kh2 Bxh3 33.Rg1 Be6 . The counterattack with 34.Rxg7+ Kxg7 35.Qf1 Qh5+ 36.Kg1 Bh3 does not work. There are possible improvements for White, though, for example with 28.Qh2 Qxh3 29.Ba2 Qxh2+ 30.Kxh2 Bd7 31.d5 Bxa4 32.Rd4 Bb3 33.Re2 Bxa2 34.Rxa2 , although then 34...Nb3! is still advantageous to Black, and if 35.Rb4 Nc5 .

27...Rg4

With an attack on the h4-pawn.

27...Rh5 is a possibility, but then White can force the exchange of queens: 28.Qb5!? Qxb5 29.axb5 Rxb5 with unclear play, but no chances of attack.

28.Qb5

Naturally Sokolov would still like to swap the queens.

28...Qd8!

An unusual starting point for a vicious kingside attack. All Black's pieces are on the periphery, with only a solitary pawn in the centre. Black's queen and bishop are, however, in the process of regrouping. Polgar is aiming for a direct attack.

28...Qxb5!? 29.axb5 Nc4 30.Bc1 Bd7 is also good, with a positional edge for Black. But an effective mating attack speeds things up.

29.g3?

This is a weak reply. In a couple of moves time, Black can take the h4-pawn anyway, and White has loosened the pawns in front of his king.

29.Bd6!? at least brings the bishop to the kingside ( 29...Qxd6?? 30.Qxe8+ ), and keeps the play live. Polgar gives 29...Bd7 , without further analysis. After 30.Qb4 Qxh4 31.Bg3 Black has:

(a) She could consider sacrificing the exchange with 31...Rxg3 32.fxg3 Qxg3 , maybe about equal, but it seems sensible to wait for the sacrifice until more pieces are developed.

(b) So maybe 31...Qg5 32.d5 h5 33.Kh1 Nb7 ( 33...h4?! 34.Bf4 is premature) 34.Bc2 (the computer suggests this as best, but it is not so clear that it is better or worse than a few alternatives) 34...Qg6 35.Rg1 (otherwise ...h4 is threatened) 35...Nc5 36.a5 Nd3 37.Bxd3 exd3 38.Qb1 Rxg3 39.fxg3 Qxg3 40.Rge1 Ba4 41.Rxd3 Bc2 42.Qxc2 Qxe1+ 43.Kh2 Re4 44.g3 h4 with a winning attack. Not forced, but White would have to find a considerable improvement to hold this.

29...a6!

Forcing the queen from the b5-e8 diagonal.

30.Qb4

What Sokolov had presumably missed was that if 30.Qh5 Nc4 31.Bc1 g6 32.Qh6 Nd6 , and White's queen is about to be trapped with ...Nf5. It takes four moves by the knight to move from the a-file to the h-file, and sometimes such a manoeuvre may easily be overlooked.

30...Qxh4

Recapturing the pawn, and also reestablishing the rook sacrifice.

31.Bxe4

If 31.Qd6 Nc4 32.Qc6 Rxg3+ 33.fxg3 Qxg3+ 34.Kh1 Qh3+ 35.Kg1 Bd7! 36.Qxc4 Qg3+ 37.Kh1 Re6 (probably the simplest) 38.Qxe6 Bxe6 , and the queen and bishop still create havoc. White's two rooks and extra bishop, in return for the queen, do not coordinate well, and at the very least White will have to sacrifice a bishop to slow down the attack slightly.

31...Rxg3+!

This rook sacrifice is hardly by now unexpected, but the new feature is no longer the complete demolition of the white pawns, but rather the great pressure on the long diagonal.

32.fxg3 Qxe4

Now the queen is fully centralized, and there are worrying mate threats, with ...Bb7 or ...Qf3 and ...Qxg3+, combined perhaps with ...Bg4 or ...Bh3. This is extremely dangerous, and Black has excellent compensation for the exchange sacrifice.

Is Black winning? Maybe the players might have thought so.

33.Rd2?

White has to find tough and resourceful defensive play, but this isn't it. The alternatives were:

(a) Polgar gives 33.d5 Nc4 34.Rd4 Qf3 35.Qxc4 Qxg3+ 36.Kf1 Bh3+ 37.Ke2 Rxe3+ 38.Kd2 Qxe1+ 39.Kc2 Re2+ with a winning attack. As promised, the queen and bishop work successfully together.

(b) 33.Qb2 is a logical defensive plan, making use of the second rank, but the problem is, of course, the knight fork with 33...Nc4 . If then 34.Qa2 Nxa3 35.Qxa3 Bb7 36.d5 Bxd5 37.Rxd5 Qxd5 , and Black has recovered her material, is a pawn up with active play, and her opponent's pawns are all isolated. It will eventually be a loss, as with various positions where White does not take back the knight after ...Nxa3. White slightly prolongs the game, but does not hold it.

(c) 33.Bc1!? , cutting out any ...Nc4xa3 threats, is a sensible reply:

(c1) If, for example, 33...Bb7 34.d5 or 33...Bh3 34.Rd2 , White's position is holding.

(c2) 33...Nc4! is an attractive surprise reply, sacrificing the knight for nothing, and not even attacking anything - a positional sacrifice in the midst of an attack!

(c21) The point is that if 34.Qxc4 Bb7 , White cannot play 35.d5??, as the queen is no longer defended. White could still try 35.Re2 Qh1+ 36.Kf2 Qxd1 , but essentially his defence is broken.

(c22) A more resilient reply is 34.Re2!? Bb7 35.Rf1 , aiming to spirit the king to e1 and beyond. 35...Re6! is the most accurate response, bringing the rook into the attack. If then 36.Qxc4 , Black wins after 36...Qh1+ 37.Kf2 Rf6+ . If instead 36.Kf2 , White is checkmated after 36...Rf6+ 37.Ke1 Rxf1+ 38.Kxf1 Qh1+ 39.Kf2 Qg2+ 40.Ke1 Qg1 mate. It is only a small consolation that the king finally reaches e1.

Is there any further try? It is too early to give up on White just yet.

(d) 33.Qb1! Qf3 34.Qa2! seems to give good chances of holding, provided White does not mind returning his extra exchange. After 34...Nc4 35.Bc1 Black will not be able to force a direct win, but there are two good choices for an endgame edge:

(d1) One idea is 35...Bg4 36.Qf2! Qxf2+ 37.Kxf2 Bxd1 38.Rxd1 f5 39.Ke2 (it is too early to hurry with 39.d5 ) 39...Kf7 40.Kd3 b5 41.axb5 axb5 42.Rf1 Ke6 ( 42...Kg6 43.Rf4 holds) 43.e4 fxe4+ 44.Kxe4 Kd6+ 45.Kd3 and White, with care, should draw.

(d2) Alternatively, 35...Qxg3+! has the merit of setting up two passed pawns, beneficial in an endgame: 36.Qg2 Qxg2+ 37.Kxg2 Bg4 38.Rd3 Bf5 39.e4 ( 39.Rdd1? Bc2 leaves Black a clear pawn up) 39...Bxe4+ 40.Kf2 f6 41.Rg3 Kf7 42.Rg4 f5 43.Rh4 g6 44.Rh5 . White has defended well so far, but one would expect that Black's three connected passed pawns would eventually break through.

So it seems that Polgar did not have a quick winning attack with best play, but that her opponent had to make concessions.

33...Qf3

With a threatened invasion on g3.

34.Kh2

34.Qd6!? sets up a trap. The natural triple knight fork with 34...Nc4 35.Rf2! , and then 35...Qe4 36.Qc7 (threatening the check on f7) 36...Be6 37.Bc1 Bd5 38.Kh2 provides for Black some degree of positional compensation for the exchange, but no dangerous attack. Or 35...Qxf2+ 36.Kxf2 Nxd6 37.Bxd6 Re6 38.Bf4 Bd7 39.Ra1 a5 40.Rb1 , and it would be difficult for Black to force a win in the opposite-coloured bishop endgame.

Black would need to play more carefully to force the win: 34...Bb7! , making use of the long diagonal. Then White has to defend against the mating attack, and only then think about ...Nc4.

Play might continue 35.d5 Nc4 36.Rf2 Qg4 37.Qc7 Bxd5 38.Bd6 Qe4 39.Rh2 Nd2 , and Black wins.
