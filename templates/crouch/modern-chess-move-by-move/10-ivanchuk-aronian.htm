Game 10
V.Ivanchuk-L.Aronian
Russian Team Championship 2006
Ruy Lopez C88

1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 a6

The 3...a6 move is effectively a gain of tempo, and is useful, but it is only a small tempo.

3...Nf6 , the Berlin Defence, is discussed in Game 7, Svidler-Topalov.

4.Ba4

Also there is the Exchange Variation, 4.Bxc6 dxc6 5.O-O , with various similarities to the Berlin Defence. White has given up his bishop-pair, and Black now has doubled c-pawns. Black has not quite equalized yet, and at top level White has achieved a small plus score. At the moment, the most successful reply at top level seems to be 5...Ne7!? , and a pawn gambit after 6.Nxe5 Qd4 7.Qh5 g6 8.Qg5 Bg7 9.Nd3 f5 10.e5 . There are also more traditional lines, such as 5...f6 or 5...Qd6 , protecting the e5-pawn.

4...Nf6

The most direct line, perhaps, is 4...b5 5.Bb3 Na5 , which Morozevich has tried a few times in blitz events. The sacrifice, 6.Bxf7+ Kxf7 7.Nxe5+ Ke7 , is playable, but not wholly convincing. 6.O-O d6 7.d4 exd4 remains possible, if never particularly fashionable. Black picks up the bishop-pair, and does not have to defend the doubled c-pawns, but is behind in development.

5.O-O

Very much the most common move.

White can instead defend the e4-pawn, but it is not necessary yet.

5...Be7

5...Nxe4 6.d4 is the Open Variation.

6.Re1

Time now to defend the pawn. Other moves are possible, but not so common.

6...b5

Since White is now threatening to win the pawn on e5, after an exchange on c6, it is now time to start counterplay with ...b5.

7.Bb3

The bishop is now kicked off the a4-e8 diagonal, but is still on a good diagonal, aiming at f7. Compared with the old and very direct Giuoco Piano, with 3.Bc4 , Black has gained two tempi with ...a6 and ...b5, and in many lines he can make use of this, but, as we shall soon see, the black pawns can be a weakness, and White can hit with a4. The Ruy Lopez, the Spanish Game, is much more popular than the Giuoco Piano, the Italian Game, because it has two lines of attack (b5 and f7), rather than just a single line of attack, f7.

7...O-O

A small but highly significant difference in move order. Both 7...O-O 8.c3 d6 , and 7...d6 8.c3 O-O would transpose into the main line, if both players wish. Aronian is a noted advocate of the Marshall Gambit, and so avoids playing 7...d6 .

8.a4

And Ivanchuk avoids the Marshall gambit, 8.c3 d5. See further commentary on this popular pawn sacrifice in Game 16, Shirov-Aronian.

There are various other anti-Marshall alternatives with, for example, 8.d3 or 8.d4 . Possibly the simplest is 8.h3 , when if Black chooses, he could transpose into the main, non-Marshall, line after 8...d6 9.c3 . Black has also tried 8...Bb7 , but 8...d5?! 9.exd5 Nxd5 10.Nxe5 Nxe5 11.Rxe5 c6 10.d4 is much easier to defend for White. He would have, in effect, an extra tempo.

8...b4

The most common reply.

8...Rb8 9.axb5 axb5 10.c3 d6 has tended to favour White slightly. White's control on the a-file is useful.

9.d4

9.d3 , with the idea of Nb1-d2-c4, is a possibility. Here Ivanchuk tries sharper and more open play.

9...d6

Probably rather more stable than 9...Nxd4 10.Nxd4 exd4 11.e5 Ne8 12.Qxd4 .

10.dxe5

Strangely, there has been little attention paid on the natural space gain with 10.d5 . The first example with this move on the database has been in a game by Aronian, not totally surprising as he has long been a specialist on the Marshall Gambit. This, however, was Aronian with White, and his opponent was trying the Marshall. White demonstrated an edge in L.Aronian-A.Minasian, Yerevan 2001, with 10...Na5 11.Ba2 Rb8 12.Nbd2 c6 13.dxc6 Be6 (a more ambitious attempt at equalizing than the standard recapture) 14.Bxe6 fxe6 15.Qe2 Qb6 16.Nc4 Nxc4 17.Qxc4 d5 18.exd5 exd5 19.Qb3 , although later the game ended up as a draw with equal material in an endgame after complications. Maybe there is the opportunity for other players to explore further? 11...Rb8 might well be slightly irrelevant if he is not going to try ...b3. An alternative perhaps would be 11...Nb7!? followed by ...Nc5.

Earlier, White has also tried various quieter pawn moves, such as 10.c3 , 10.h3 , or occasionally 10.a5 .

10...Nxe5

10...dxe5 has also been tried, but usually Black prefers the knight exchange.

11.Nxe5

11.Bf4 Ng6 has been tried various times, but without making much of an impact on Black's position. White prefers to set up a clear weakness in Black's pawn structure, and attack it.

11...dxe5

The pawn structure has now been fixed, and White can feel reasonably happy with the outcome. Black has three slight pawn weaknesses, leaving White something to work with. White's bishop on the a2-g8 diagonal is an irritation, putting pressure on the f7-pawn. As a consequence, it is difficult to provide full protection for the e5-pawn. Black's rook cannot simultaneously provide full cover for both the e-file and the f-file. As a result, he has to make compromises. On the queenside, Black's b-pawn has been encouraged to advance perhaps more quickly than he would have liked, and in consequence, Black's c-file is weakened. White can play, for example, Nd2-c4 without much opposition.

Having said that, any advantage for White is still slight, provided Black can play actively. Black was careful earlier to exchange knights, giving up his purely defensive c6-knight in return for taking White's active f3-knight. Simplification usually helps the defender, but it cannot solve every problem.

12.Qf3

White sees his queen as an attacker, his opponent's queen as the defender.

12.Qe2 or 12.Bg5 would be less forceful, and 12.Nd2? would be even careless, in view of 12...Bg4 .

12...Bb7

There are some other lines that have been played, but this seems the most direct. Fritz gives lines such as 12...c5 or 12...c6 as equal, but what is Black trying to do next?

12...Be6 covers up White's light-squared diagonal towards f7, but the central tension seems to favour White, for example, after 13.Nd2 Bc5 14.h3!? .

13.Nd2

'Knights before bishops'.

The knight may, though, need some time to manoeuvre itself to the best square, and cannot move instantly from, for example, b1 to g3.

If 13.Bg5 Nd7 14.Be3 Bc5 , Black is equal. Here 13...h6!? 14.Rd1 hxg5 15.Rxd8 Raxd8 is a provocative queen sacrifice. White will have to work out how to develop his knight and rook; his minor pieces do not work together well.

13...Bc5

In J.Ehlvest-V.Kupreichik, Minsk 1989, Kupreichik provided perhaps a little too direct a hint of a threatened capture on e4 with 13...Kh8 . Ehlvest covered the f5-square with 14.g4!? Bc5 15.Nc4 , but Kupreichik persisted with 15...Nxe4?! (Black was already slightly worse anyway) 16.Rxe4 f5 17.gxf5 Rxf5 18.Qxf5 Qd1+ 19.Kg2 Rf8 , but after 20.Ne3! , Black's attack was over. Could Black play with more finesse?

14.Nf1

A standard Ruy Lopez manoeuvre, bringing the knight to g3, and then either defending the e4-pawn, or starting a Nf5 kingside attack. Additionally, White is thinking about Bg5, and of course White no longer has any back-row problems. One senses that Black has not yet equalized.

14...Qc8

After 14...Qd7 15.h3 White will have a later hit with Rad1 (after the bishop move). So the queen shelters to a safer square.

15.h3

One of the less successful games in grandmaster chess continued with 15.Ng3?! Qg4 16.Qd3?! Rad8 , and White later retreated with 17.Qf3 . Ivanchuk naturally prefers to cover the g4-square.

15...Kh8

With ideas of setting up the sacrifice on e4. Ivanchuk suggests, as a possible improvement, 15...a5 16.Bg5 Ra6!? , then possibly 17.Bc4 Rd6 18.Ng3 Qa8 .

16.Ng3

White wishes to consolidate the e4-pawn, or at least partially consolidate.

16.Be3? Bxe4 would, of course, be a mistake.

Ivanchuk gives 16.Bh6 as inaccurate with good play for Black after 16...Nd7 17.Be3 f5 . This seems less than clear after 18.Bd5! , with chances for White for a slight edge. Maybe 16...Ne8 17.Be3 Bxe3 would be more flexible, about equal, after 18.Qxe3 Nd6 , hitting e4 from another direction.

The Bh6 move is not a bad idea, but Ivanchuk has analysed something better and more direct. The next few moves are of themselves straightforward enough, but the resulting play is strategically complicated.

16...Nxe4!?

Clearly the tactic has been in the air for quite a long time.

Ivanchuk gives 16...Qd7 17.Bg5 Qc6 18.c3 bxc3 19.bxc3 Rab8 20.Rab1 as a slight edge for White, but this is hardly surprising given that Black's ...Qd8-c8-d7 has ended up as a loss of tempo.

To try to prove that his previous play was positionally justified, Aronian has to take up the challenge.

17.Nxe4

17.Rxe4? f5 loses the exchange.

17...f5

So Black recovers the knight, unless of course White doesn't mind losing the queen for compensation.

18.Nxc5!

Ivanchuk takes up the challenge. When writing this book, this was one of the first games to select. It is so rare to be able to find positions with queen versus three minor pieces, much rarer than queen versus rook, minor piece and pawn.

I suspect that in home preparation Aronian would have been happy with this position, seeing White's kingside pawn structure as seriously weakened. However, as play goes on, White's pieces start to work together, and Black's play becomes increasingly difficult. Ivanchuk's play is highly original and insightful.

Was dropping the queen forced? Ivanchuk gives as an alternative 18.Be6 Bxe4 ( 18...Qxe6? 19.Nxc5 and White wins a piece) 19.Bxc8 Bxf3 20.Rxe5 Bd4 21.Rxf5 Raxc8 22.Rxf3 Rxf3 23.gxf3 Rf8 24.Kg2 Re8 25.Kf1 , and a possible draw by repetition.

Ivanchuk also gives 18.Bh6 , when the simplest reply is 18...fxe4!? 19.Bxg7+ Kxg7 20.Qg3+ Kh6 (but not 20...Kh8?? 21.Qxe5+ ) 21.Qh4+ Kg6 with a perpetual. White has no room to manoeuvre for more, as Black is threatening ...Bxf2+. Here 18...Bxe4 19.Bxg7+ Kxg7 20.Qg3+ Kf6 21.Qh4+ Kg6 22.Qg3+ is also a drawing line, while Ivanchuk also looks at 18...gxh6 19.Qg3 f4 20.Qd3 , which he gives as unclear.

There are some interesting drawing options, but there is only one real chance of playing for a win.

18...Bxf3 19.gxf3

An unusual material balance. Enjoy it while you can in a game between two top grandmasters.

The material is in theory about equal (9 = 3+3+3), if we assume that Black's e-pawn will drop. If the e-pawn holds, then maybe Black has a slight edge in terms of material, but of course there are many other issues in the position.

Probably White ought to have the better piece structure, if his minor pieces are well guarded, but his bishop on c1 might well be poorly placed. In terms of pawns, Black's queenside pawns are ineffective, but White has weakened kingside pawns, and his king is isolated.

19...f4!?

This is by far the most natural move, with ideas of attacking on h3, and also blocking the bishop on c1. Probably most strong players would want to play this, or at the very least consider it seriously. It may still not be the best. White soon has outposts on e4 and g4, thanks to Black's pawn push.

There are a few alternatives. Ivanchuk gives 19...Re8 20.f4 , and if 20...e4 21.Be3 followed by Kh2 and Rg1, and White's pieces are rock solid Black will have to defend weaknesses on the pawn on g7, adding sensitivity to the king, and also along the d-file, and against threats with either bishop or knight from e6. Similarly, if 20...exf4 21.Bxf4 , and White's minor pieces are excellent.

19...e4!? is an attempt to open up lines for the queen and rooks, although White does not necessarily have to oblige, since 20.f4 closes play down again. Black could of course transpose to Ivanchuk's suggested line with 20...Re8 . There may be improvements, but these are probably not significant. White could also allow the centre to open with 20.fxe4 fxe4 21.Ne6 Rf6 22.Rxe4 Qb7 23.Nc5 Qc6 24.Be3 . Probably White has the better chances, with complicated play, but there are earlier chances to improve for Black.

Pawns may well have been 'the soul of chess', as Philidor may have suggested a quarter of a millennium ago, but the major pieces, the queen and the rooks, are surely the heart of attack. White's king is open, and one could argue that Black should be concentrating on queen and rook moves, rather than pawn play.

Thus 19...Qe8! looks better, and if 20.Ne6 Qh5! 21.Nxf8 ( 21.Bd5 c6 does not help White) 21...Rxf8 22.Rxe5 Qxf3 . Now it is wisest for White to return the light-squared bishop to the kingside with 23.Bc4 Qxh3 24.Be2 Qh4 25.Re6 (or maybe 25.Bf3 ) 25...a5 , and difficult play continues, with Black having a queen and a pawn and a few threats against the white king, and White having rook and two bishops in reply. This is a delicate balance. The computer gives it as equal, but there is no clear perpetual, and the positional balance could easily fall either way after any inaccuracy.

20.Kh2

White needs to defend the exposed h-pawn, and to tuck his king behind the pawn.

20.Rxe5? Qxh3 is much too risky, and after 21.Bd5? c6 22.Bxc6 Rf6 Black is about to mate.

20...Qf5

Black consolidates, and this is indeed natural, stopping White's rook taking control of the e-file, but is it the best? Ivanchuk suggests as an alternative 20...Qd8!? , aiming for counterplay after 21.Rxe5 Qh4 . Black would there be arguing that the pawn on e5 is not so significant. The other pawns, f4 and b4, are more significant, as if Black can hold on to them. White's dark-squared bishop is out of active play.

Ivanchuk gives as his main line the slightly fragile 22.Ne4 Rae8 23.Rxe8 Rxe8 , when it is difficult for White to develop his pieces. If, for example, 24.Bd2?? Rxe4 25.fxe4 Qxf2+ , and Black wins material. White therefore tries instead 24.Kg2 . There are various ways of handling the position, and certainly 24...Re5 is natural, and worth considering, but Ivanchuk suggests, with thoughts of safety for both sides, 24...Qh6 25.Bc4 a5 26.Bd3 Qg6+ 27.Kh2 Qh5 28.Kg2 with a repetition after 28...Qg6+ . Here 28...Qe5!? could be tried, the queen taking advantage of the vacant square of the pawn, but 29.Rb1! Re6 30.Bd2 starts a partial unravelling.

Ivanchuk suggests an alternative choice, 22.Nd3!? (instead of 22.Ne4 ), which would seem to be more attractive. The knight puts pressure on the black pawn on f4, and also guards the weak square on f2. Also White keeps his good rook on e5, since if 22...Rae8? 23.Rxe8 Rxe8 , White simply has 24.Bxf4 . Ivanchuk gives the position as unclear after the preferable 22...Rad8 23.Re2 Qh5 24.Bd2 Qxf3 25.Rae1 a5 .

If White were to find a couple of clear tempi, and bring all his extra pieces into play, then he would be better. Unfortunately, just at the moment his pieces lack coordination, with, for example, his two rooks being immobile (26.Re7?? Rxd3). White has to try something quickly, though, as Black's queen is flexible and mobile, and his pawns can try to open White's kingside with ...g5 and ...g4 One senses that there will be tactics:

(a) There are, for example, some entreating checks, counterchecks and mate threats after 26.Be6?! Qc6! 27.Bxf4 Rxd3 28.cxd3 Rxf4 29.Bd7 . Winning for White? Not quite. Black has 29...Qd6! 30.Re8+ Rf8+ , a vital countercheck. White's king moves, and Black covers his mating threats with 31...Rg8! , with a substantial advantage.

(b) So let us try a more direct threat, 26.Ne5 . There is only one good queen move, as 27.Nf7+ is a threat. He must play 26...Qh5 (and if 27.Nf7+? Rxf7 28.Re8+ Rf8 ). Play continues with 27.Be6 , and if given the chance, White would be happy after Bg4 and Bf3. Again one senses tactics, and they soon arise with 27...f3!? 28.Bg4 fxe2 29.Bxh5 Rxd2 30.Kg2 Rxc2 31.Rxe2 b3! (otherwise White is better), and we have reached a much more normal material balance, with rook and pawn against two minor pieces. Probably the position is about equal after, for example, 32.h4 (preventing Black from thinking about ...g5) 32...g6 33.Bf3 Kg7 34.Nd3 Rd8 35.Re3 , and the position has stabilized for both sides.

In Informator, Ivanchuk gives this line as 'unclear' after White's 25th, and before the tactics come up. In terms of practical over-the-board chess, this seems about right. Neither player can show a clear advantage, while in the alternative line the position is also unclear.

Of course, it would have been beyond the event horizon for Aronian to have been able to decide over the board whether 19...Re8 would have been better than 19...f4 , bearing in mind the additional possibility that he could also have chosen between 20...Qf5 and 20...Qd8 .

21.Bd2!

This is an extremely difficult position to interpret, not just in terms of pure chess, but also in terms of chess psychology. Ivanchuk gives this as an exclamation mark, and this is fully understandable in positional terms, developing his queenside pieces, and waiting to see whether the knight should go to d3 or to e4, both good and logical squares, but cannot both be played.

Ivanchuk gives 21.Nd3 Rae8 22.Bd2 Qh5 , and this could easily transpose into 21.Bd2 Qh5 22.Nd3 Rae8 (see the note to Black's 21st), but as we shall soon see, Aronian does not enter the critical line.

Ivanchuk analyses 23.Re4! Rf6! ( 23...Qxf3 24.Rae1 g5 25.Rxe5 Rxe5 26.Rxe5 g4 27.hxg4 Qxg4 28.Nxb4 with advantage to White; Black's rook cannot add to the attack) 24.Rae1 Rh6 25.Nxf4 Qf5 26.Kg1 g5 with sharp play, including the possibility of good play by Black. Ivanchuk further gives 27.Ne2 Rf6 28.Rg4! Qxf3 29.Rg2 Qxh3 30.Ng3 , citing this as unclear. One possibility is 30...Qg4 31.Rxe5 Qd1+ 32.Nf1 Qh5 33.Ng3 with a perpetual.

One cannot help feeling that there are many variations that need to be considered in such an open position, and that Black has started at last to break up White's kingside pawns. There are possible ideas for Black to try to improve, most naturally perhaps 27...Rxh3!? , and if 28.Nd4? Qf6 29.Bxb4 g4! , and White is suddenly in serious trouble, as if 30.Rxg4 (diverting the rook from the e-file) 30...Qh6 31.Kf1 Rh1+ 32.Ke2 exd4+ , and Black is winning. Probably White has to try 28.Ng3 Qxf3 , but Black is better.

Both 21.Bd2 and 21.Nd3 could lead to these lines, which tend to be favourable to Black. There is, however, an important distinction. After 21.Bd2! , White can avoid all this. See the next note.

21...Rf6?!

If 21...Rae8?! , White does not transpose into the above lines with 22.Nd3 , but tries something else: 22.Rg1! .

After the text move, Ivanchuk again plays the rook to g1 and g4, and Aronian's attacking play looks rather too direct.

21...Qh5 would be another attempt to enter the lines started with 21.Nd3 , and if 22.Nd3?! (not 22.Bd5? Rad8 ) 22...Rae8 , with probably a slight edge to Black, as discussed earlier. 22.Ne4! is better when 22...Qxf3?! 23.Be6 is an irrelevant pawn grab, as White is threatening to trap the queen with Bg4. After 23...Qh5 24.Bg4 , White's position is fully satisfactory. Thus 22...a5! seems best. Then 23.Be6 Ra6 24.Bg4 follows, with tense play, probably less favourable for White than in the actual game, because ideally White would want the rook rather than the bishop on g4.

22.Rg1

Safest White intends to plug the diagonal with Rg4.

22.Bxb4? Rh6 23.Be6 leaves Black too much free space after 23...Qxc2! , and if 24.Rf1 Rxe6 (among others) 25.Nxe6 Qc4 .

The computer suggests early on that 23...Rxe6? 24.Nxe6 Qxe6 25.Bc3 Qc6 is winning, but 26.Rxe5 Qxc3 (or 26...Kg8 27.Re7 ) 27.Rg5 allows White a strong attack with the two rooks and bishop.

22...Rh6

Black forces the opponent to play what he wants to play anyway.

Ivanchuk gives 22...a5 23.Rg4 Rd8 24.Ne4 Rh6 25.Re1 as unclear. Despite Fritz, which suggests that Black is on top, play looks good for White. If, for example, 25...Qh5 26.h4 Rg6 (or 26...g5 27.Nxg5 Qxg4 28.Nf7+ Kg7 29.fxg4 Rxh4+ 30.Kg2 Rxd2 31.Nxe5 , and White is doing very well) 27.Bf7 Rxg4 28.Bxh5 Rxh4+ 29.Kg2 Rxh5 30.c3 , and the bishop and knight will be more active than the rook and two pawns.

The reader might well wonder why Black is trying to play so sharply. There is a simple reason. If Black does not do something quickly, White's pieces will continue to improve.

23.Rg4

Essential. The rook now blocks everything on the kingside, although it helps that Black cannot attack with any minor pieces. White will at some stage want to open up his position for the bishops with c3.

23...a5

A natural and strong move, not just to save the pawn on b4, but more generally to try to lock the dark-squared bishop into passive play.

24.Re1

Some pressure on the pawn on e5 is bound to be useful, cutting down various defensive options for Black, but as we shall soon see, White's plan is not simply a big attack on e5.

24...Rf8

24...Qh5 25.h4 is not fully satisfactory either. Black cannot try ...Rg6 at various stages, in view of Bf7. Ivanchuk gives 25...Rd6 26.Nd3 Re8 27.Bc4 as unclear, but White can gradually consolidate his pieces with b3, Re4, and maybe manoeuvring his bishop to b2.

25.Ne4

The one obvious weakness for White at the end of the combinative phase was that his kingside pawns had been broken up into doubled and isolated pawns. Now with fully protected pieces, with the knight on e4 and the rook on g4, the fractured pawn structure is a positive advantage for White.

25.Nd3 Qh5 26.h4 Rf5 is also promising for White, but it seems simpler to aim for complete security on the king-side, before opening up the queenside.

25...Qh5

He might as well force the pawn to advance, but it is not up to much.

25...Rxh3+?? loses material after 26.Kxh3+ h5 27.Kg2 , and if 27...hxg4? 28.Rh1+ , winning.

26.h4

White's rook on g4 is the hero of the defence.

26...Rg6

And this is the only way to get at the white rook.

27.Rxg6

27.Reg1 Rxg4 28.Rxg4 is also to be considered, and is good, but with the first set of rooks gone. Black finds it more difficult to attack with the second rook.

27...Qxg6

Again, not 27...Qxh4+?? 28.Kg2 hxg6 29.Rh1 .

27...hxg6 28.Kg2 Qf5 29.c3 gives a clear edge for White. It is difficult for Black to attack any of White's minor pieces.

28.c3

The dark-squared bishop is now back in play, finally completing, in the broadest sense, its full development. Soon White will be able to attack.

28...c5?

Effectively the losing mistake, and probably the result of a misjudgement.

Ivanchuk suggests that White is better after 28...Rf5 29.Rg1 Qh6 30.cxb4 Qxh4+ 31.Kg2 Rh5 32.Kf1 axb4 33.Ke2 . The king is safe, and he has an outside passed a-pawn.

28...Qb6 29.cxb4 axb4 30.Rc1 Rd8 is better, and Ivanchuk gives it, perhaps generously, as 'unclear'. White cannot lose this, in the absence of a serious error, but he has chances of playing for a win. White's rook and knight are both well placed, and he has the advantage of a passed pawn, but how can he make progress with the other pieces, more particularly the two bishops? The main problem is the dark-squared bishop, which requires some work. A possible idea would be something like 31.Kg2 g6 32.Bc4 Kg7 33.b3 Kf8 34.Rc2 Ke7 35.Bc1 followed by Bb2, and the bishop reaches fresh air. Black is certainly not yet safe.

Finally, 28...Qh5 seems almost too elementary for Ivanchuk to mention, even though Black has the chance of winning a kingside pawn. 29.cxb4 axb4 30.Kg2 gives White a big edge.

29.Nxc5!

Ivanchuk can quite simply snatch the pawn. Two bishops, a knight and a pawn, in return for the queen, will be enough for White.

29...Rd8

29...Qd6 30.Ne4 Qd3 31.Kg2 is solid for White. He will gradually consolidate. Ivanchuk gives 31...Rd8 32.Rc1 h6 33.Bc2 Qe2 34.Bd1 Qd3 35.cxb4 axb4 36.Bxb4 as winning.

30.Ne4!

30.Bc1 Qb6 31.Rxe5 bxc3 32.bxc3 is genuinely unclear. White has surrendered most of his pawn support for the minor pieces.

30...Qb6

With threats of ...Rxd2.

It is always hazardous to guess exactly where a player has made a serious miscalculation, but here the likelihood would seem to be that when sacrificing with 28...c5 , Aronian had assumed that his queen move to b6 would have been strong. However, Ivanchuk had calculated further.

Ivanchuk gives 30...Qh6 31.cxb4 axb4 32.Rc1 Qxh4+ 33.Kg2 as a big advantage for White. Quite probably the edge is decisive.

31.Rd1!

This consolidates everything.

31...Rd3?!

If 31...bxc3 32.Bxc3! , with a back-rank win after 32...Qxb3?? 33.Rxd8+ . Black loses more slowly after 32...Rxd1 33.Bxd1 Qd8 34.Be2 Qxh4+ 35.Kg2 .

Ivanchuk suggests 31...h6 as the best attempt to hold. There are clearly several possibilities for White, maybe starting with 32.cxb4 . Ivanchuk himself gives 32.Kg2 Rd3 33.Bc2 Rd8 34.cxb4 axb4 35.a5! Qb5 ( 35...Qxa5 36.Bxb4! ) 36.b3 Ra8 37.Nd6 Qxa5 38.Nc4 with advantage to White.

32.Bc4!

Simple and clear. Neither queen nor rook have any good moves.

32...Rxf3?!

32...Rd8 loses a tempo, but at least avoids the quick loss. Ivanchuk gives 33.cxb4 axb4 34.a5 Qxa5 35.Bxb4 , and White has a substantial edge.

33.Be1!

Black's rook is trapped.

33...g5

Black must open up an escape square for the king, otherwise the queen will have no chance of active play.

34.hxg5

Avoiding 34.Nxg5?? Rxf2+ , when Black may even be better.

34...Kg7

Black's queen cannot try anything. If 34...Qg6 35.Rd8+ Kg7 36.Rg8 mate.

35.Rd7+

No doubt White can also find ways to win the rook, but in time trouble there are simpler ways. Check, check, squeeze the queen out of the way, then everything is easy.

35...Kf8 36.Rf7+

Still good, although naturally 36.Kg2 is also effective.

36...Ke8 37.Rf6 1-0

It is time for Black to resign.
