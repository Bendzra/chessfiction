Game 16
A.Shirov-L.Aronian
Moscow 2006
Ruy Lopez C89

1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 a6 4.Ba4 Nf6 5.O-O Be7 6.Re1 b5 7.Bb3 O-O

Varying from the main non-sacrificial line, 7...d6 . See the notes to Game 14, Navara-Socko.

8.c3

White knows, of course, that Aronian is happy to offer the Marshall Gambit. In Game 10, Ivanchuk-Aronian 2006, Ivanchuk side-stepped with 8.a4 .

Kasparov never allowed the Marshall Attack, in recent years preferring 8.a4 or 8.h3 , and earlier, sometimes 8.d4 . Shipov, writing in New in Chess, commented, tongue in cheek, that it was the Marshall that caused Kasparov to give up chess for politics. Actually, he scored very well in the quieter lines. Maybe it was the Berlin Wall, 3...Nf6 , that caused the rift?

8...d5

He could still enter the traditional main line with 8...d6 , but Black is playing for a more direct attempt at equality, rather than trying to manoeuvre quietly until move 20 and beyond.

9.exd5

9.d3 has been tried several times, but after 9...dxe4 10.dxe4 Qxd1 11.Bxd1 it is clear that White is hardly trying for an edge.

9.d4 exd4 10.e5 Ne4 11.cxd4 Bg4 12.Nc3 Bxf3 13.gxf3 Nxc3 14.bxc3 f5 15.Kh1 Na5 16.Bc2 Kh8 has been tried a couple of times at high level. Both games were drawn, although maybe there are chances for White to try for more. White has the solid passed pawn, but Black can defend against the pawn, and White's doubled f-pawns are blocked.

9...Nxd5 10.Nxe5

Often the one way to refute a gambit is to accept it. This was what Capablanca tried close to a century ago, and is still the main line.

10.d4!? exd4 11.cxd4 seems to have been under-examined. If one day there is finally an accepted view that the Marshall sacrifice is completely sound, then attention might shift to this. A recent example is L.Nisipeanu-A.Beliavsky, Pune 2004, which continued 11...Bb4 12.Bd2 Bb7 13.Nc3 Bxc3 14.bxc3 Na5 15.Bc2 Nc4 . Here there are various quiet alternatives for White, the computer suggesting, for example, 16.Bd3 as a slight edge.

Nisipeanu instead went all-out for a sacrificial attack with 16.Bg5 f6 17.Qd3 g6 18.Bh6 Rf7 19.Nh4 Qd7 20.Qg3 Kh8 21.Bxg6 hxg6 22.Nxg6+ Kh7 23.Bf8 Nde3 24.Qh4+ Kxg6 25.Qh6+ Kf5 26.Qh3+ Kg6 27.Qh6+ Kf5 , drawn by perpetual.

10.a4 Bb7 11.axb5 axb5 12.Rxa8 Bxa8 13.Nxe5 Nxe5 14.Rxe5 Nf4 15.d4 Nxg2 has tended to favour Black. Simple reason - White's king is not safe, while Black's pieces are well developed, and his own king secure.

10...Nxe5 11.Rxe5 c6

So here we have the Marshall Gambit.

The original Marshall approach was with 11...Nf6 12.Re1 Bd6 13.h3 Ng4 14.Qf3 , but after a few tactical fireworks with 14...Qh4 15.d4 Nxf2 16.Re2 Bg4 17.hxg4 Bh2+ 18.Kf1 Bg3 , White consolidates with 19.Rxf2 , J.Capablanca-F.Marshall, New York 1918.

In the modern approach, the knight stays on d5. Black is not too worried about the bishop for knight exchange, as the bishop-pair tends to be more important in open attacking play.

12.d4

This is the main line, if White is trying for a win. There are, however, various drawing options. There have been, for example, some painless draws after 12.Bxd5 cxd5 13.d4 Bd6 14.Re3 Qh4 15.h3 Qf4 16.Re5 Qf6 17.Re3 , and a repetition.

Alternatively, 12.Re1 (or 12.g3 , soon transposing) 12...Bd6 13.g3 Bf5 14.d4 Qd7 15.Be3 Rae8 16.Nd2 Bg4 17.Qb1 (or 17.Qc2 , soon repeating) 17...Bf5 18.Qd1 Bg4 , and Black can repeat. This repetition is possible, but is not forced. A recent Aronian win for Black was 18.Qc1 Re6 19.Nf3 Bg4 20.Ng5 Rg6 21.f3 Bxf3 22.Nxf3 Bxg3 23.hxg3 Rxg3+ (two minor pieces sacrificed) 24.Kf2 Qh3 25.Ke2 Qg2+ 26.Kd3 Qxf3 (a minor piece down, in return for an attack) 27.Bxd5 cxd5 28.Kc2 Re8 29.Bd2 Qd3+ 30.Kb3 Rb8 , A.Shabalov-L.Aronian, Calvia Olympiad 2004. If the attack on the king doesn't force a win immediately, the three passed pawns on the kingside will mop things up. White was unable to bring his pieces to good squares.

12...Bd6

12...Bf6?! has been occasionally tried, but not in high-level games. Black needs to attack with the bishop against the weakened kingside, and not against the firmly protected d-pawn.

13.Re1

13.Re2 has also been tried, with the thought that if 13...Qh4 14.g3 Qh3 . White can flick the queen over to f1, either now ( 15.Qf1 ), or as an alternative possibility, slightly later. White could still, of course, transpose into the main line with 14.Re4 .

Alternatively, 13...Bg4 14.f3 Bf5 15.g3 Qc7 16.Kf2 Qd7 17.Bxd5 cxd5 18.Nd2 Bd3 19.Re3 Bg6 (Marin notes that Black could force a draw with 19...Qh3 20.Kg1 Bxg3 ) 20.Nf1 a5 21.a3 , agreed drawn in V.Anand-L.Aronian, FIDE World Championship, Mexico City 2007. Naturally there is plenty of play here. Possibly, one suspects, Black could have played on. He is a Dawn down, but he has the more active pieces, and White's king is slightly open.

13...Qh4

The only realistic move. If Black does not want to attack, he should avoid gambit play!

14.g3

White has to accept the weakness of the pawns, and this of course adds to the prospects of Black obtaining full compensation for the pawn.

14.f4?! Bg4 15.g3 Bxd1 16.gxh4 Bxb3 17.axb3 f5 gives Black a slight edge. He will soon recover the extra pawn, on f4, and he will have the better queenside pawn structure.

14.h3?! Bxh3 15.Bxd5 cxd5 16.gxh3 Qxh3 17.Re5 Bxe5 18.dxe5 does not provide Black an instant win, but after, for example, 18...Rae8 19.Bf4 Qe6 , followed by ...f6, Black has the better prospects.

14...Qh3

Now ...Bg4 is a strong threat.

15.Re4

A critical choice for White. Here he tries to offer the exchange of queens, in order to soften Black's kingside attack, and he attempts to set up a Qf1 exchange.

Another try along similar lines would be 15.Qf3 , followed by Qg2, or alternatively 15.Bxd5 cxd5 16.Qf3 followed by Qg2.

15.Qe2!? is a more recent idea, with Qf1 to follow. This perhaps seems less clumsy than the 13.Re2 and Qf1 idea, when the rook is soon hit, but White has in comparison given up a tempo; Qd1-e2-f1 instead of Qd1-f1. In the game R.Kasimdzhanov-E.Bacrot, French League 2008, play ended up in a perpetual after 15...Bd7 16.Qf1 Qh5 17.Nd2 Rae8 18.f3 Nf4 19.Rxe8 Rxe8 20.Ne4 Nh3+ 21.Kg2 Nf4+ 22.Kg1 Nh3+ 23.Kg2 .

Or quite simply White can try to defend with 15.Be3 , trying to keep his minor pieces as solid as possible. A recent try, with a win for White, is Wang Hao-A.Grischuk, Russian Team Championship 2008: 15...Bg4 16.Qd3 Rae8 17.Nd2 Re6 18.a4 Qh5 19.axb5 axb5 20.Qf1 Bh3 21.Bd1!? (there have been a few draws with 21.Qe2 Bg4 ) 21...Qf5 22.Qe2 c5 23.Nf3 Bf4 24.Qd2 Nxe3 25.fxe3 Bh6 26.Qf2 Rfe8 27.Bc2 Qh5 28.e4! Rf6 29.Bd1 , and White had finally opened up his position, and won without too much trouble. Here an earlier game, P.Leko-V.Kramnik, Monaco (blindfold) 2007, led to only equality after 27.Ne5?! Qxf2+ 28.Kxf2 f6 , though Leko immediately slipped up with 29.Bg4? ( 29.Ng4! is better) 29...Rxe5! 30.Bxh3 Bxe3+ , Black winning a pawn and later the game.

15...g5

This is standard opening theory, even though Black is opening up lines in front of his king. Black wants to prevent White from pushing the queen away with Rh4, and he is arguing that if he can keep good control on the king-side, with queen, pawns, bishops and other pieces. White will not get near Black's king.

Various alternatives have been tried, but the pawn push remains by far the most popular.

16.Qf1

A few players have fallen for 16.Bxg5?? Qf5, forking the rook and bishop.

White has occasionally tried an exchange sacrifice with 16.Qf3 Bf5 17.Bc2 Bxe4 18.Bxe4 , but after 18...Qe6 19.Bxg5 f5 20.Bxd5 cxd5 21.Nd2 f4 22.Bxf4 Bxf4 23.gxf4 Ra7 24.Kh1 Re7 25.Rg1+ Kh8 , V.Topalov-M.Adams, Sarajevo 2000, Black is clearly better, and later won. While White has three pawns and knight for the rook, neither the pawns nor the knight make much impact against Black's major pieces.

So White instead offers a queen exchange.

16...Qh5

Which Black not too surprisingly declines.

Even so, Black is so far ahead in development that the queen exchange is possible. 16...Qxf1+ 17.Kxf1 Bf5 18.Nd2 h6 ( 18...Bxe4 19.Nxe4 allows White to take a second pawn for the exchange, and he will have a lively position) 19.Re1 Rae8 20.Nf3 g4 21.Bxh6 gxf3 22.Bxf8 Rxf8 23.Bd1 Bg4 is a recent try, unclear, maybe in the end equal. A draw was agreed later on in E.Bacrot-V.Anand, Mainz (rapid) 2007, after 24.a4 Rd8 25.axb5 axb5 26.Kg1 Kg7 27.h3 Bxh3 28.Bxf3 Be6 .

17.Nd2

A natural developing move.

17...Bf5

And Black too makes a natural developing move, with the intention of gaining a tempo.

17...f5?! 18.Bd1 Qh6 19.Re1 f4 20.Ne4 Bc7 21.Bd2 , as in A.Shirov-V.Akopian, Carlsbad 2007, leaves Black's kingside pawns too loose. Black was unable to keep an attack going, and lost.

18.f3

White is happy to sacrifice the exchange, if he can keep his extra pawn, he will keep active pieces and the better pawns.

18...Nf6

Taking up the exchange challenge does not seem worth it. 18...Bxe4 19.fxe4 Ne3 20.Qf3! Ng4 21.Nf1 covers White's kingside weaknesses.

19.a4

One of the deepest opening traps in recent years was exposed in a World Championship game, Kramnik-Leko, 8th matchgame, Brissago 2004. Play continued 19.Re1?! Rae8 20.Rxe8 Rxe8 21.a4 Qg6 22.axb5 Bd3 23.Qf2? ( 23.Qd1 Be2 24.Qe1 Bd3 with a repetition) 23...Re2! . This obvious and natural move hardly needs comment, except for the point that the computer gives White's position as winning after 24.Qxe2 Bxe2 25.bxa6 . White is about to queen a pawn, and after the seemingly compulsory sacrifice with 25...Bxa6? 26.Rxa6 , to be followed by Rxc6, White has a substantial, and winning, material advantage.

Checkmate comes first though, and 25...Qd3!! wins.

The critical line is 26.a7 Qe3+ 27.Kg2 Bxf3+ 28.Nxf3 Qe2+ 29.Kg1 Ng4 30.a8Q+ Kg7 31.Qxc6 Qf2+ 32.Kh1 Qf1+ 33.Ng1 Nf2 mate. Kramnik avoided this, but after 26.Kf2 Bxf3 27.Nxf3 Ne4+ 28.Ke1 Nxc3! 29.bxc3 Qxc3+ 30.Kf1 Qxa1 Black had a clear material advantage, and White's a-pawn was by now harmless. Kramnik resigned a few moves later. A superb win for Leko.

This is regarded by some as the greatest win of the decade. At both amateur and professional levels, people talk about it, when relaxing after the game, and talk with great admiration. It is the classic 21st Century win at the highest level. The computer can go far in analysis, but the human analyst, with the help of inspiration, can see things even deeper.

19...Nxe4

19...Bxe4 20.Nxe4 Nxe4 21.fxe4 gives White an edge. See further commentary to Black's 20th.

20.Nxe4

He wants to complete his development, and further to ensure that the knight will become genuinely active.

20.fxe4 Be6 21.Bd1 is about equal.

Pawns without active pieces are not so effective as pieces and pawns together.

20...Qg6!

We have already seen the position before after 20...Bxe4 21.fxe4 . In I.Kumosov-S.Azarov, Kusadasi 2006, play ended up with a perpetual after 21...Kg7 22.Bd2 f6 23.Qf5 Qe2 24.Bc2 Rf7 ( 24...Qxd2 25.e5 Qe3+ 26.Kh1 Kf7 27.exd6 is winning for White) 25.e5 Qxd2 26.Qxh7+ Kf8 27.Qh8+? Ke7 28.exd6+ Kxd6 29.Qxa8 Qe3+ 30.Kh1 Qf3+ 31.Kg1 . White can play for much more than this with 26.exd6! , as Marin notes for Chess-Base. White's king is not in as much danger as it looks, and Black's king, in conjunction with White's excellent passed pawn and dangerous pieces, makes life difficult for Black.

In the game, Aronian is really jettisoning an extra pawn to avoid problems, rather than trying for an edge, although naturally he will be happy to take a chance in the unclear and unbalanced position.

21.Nxd6

The natural reaction.

21.Nxg5?! Rae8 22.Ne4 is not worth giving up two tempi for a not very useful pawn. Marin gives 22...Bxe4 23.fxe4 Rxe4 24.Bc2 f5 25.Bxe4 fxe4 , and Black has considerable pressure in return for the pawn.

21...Qxd6 22.Bxg5

22.axb5 axb5 23.Rxa8 Rxa8 24.Bxg5 releases the tension a little too early. At the minimum, White would want to exchange after Black has already moved the king's rook.

This position had reached before by Shirov, just three days earlier. There was no great excitement in the previous encounter, quickly ending up with perpetual check in A.Shirov-P.Leko, Moscow 2006. Play finished with 22...Rfe8 23.Re1 Qg6 24.Be7 Ra7 25.Bc5 Raa8 26.Be7 Ra7 27.Bc5 . draw agreed.

It is possible, but unlikely, that Shirov would be happy to agree two draws with White in succession. What is more likely is that he would have examined the last few moves, and tried to find an improvement for his game with Aronian, maybe instead of repeating, trying something new like 26.a5!? .

Aronian is a renowned specialist in the Marshall, and while it is possible that he was working hard analysing in the last couple of days in his hotel, it seems more likely that he had seen his novelty a long time before.

22...Qg6!?

Black is aiming to play actively, to hold the balance, in what could easily have been a poor line.

23.Qc1

The natural reply, bringing an extra piece into play.

23...Bd3

The bishop circles around. One idea, as in the game, is to play ...Bc4, blocking off the white bishop on the a2-g8 diagonal. Or, as in the Kramnik-Leko game cited above, Black, if given the option, could set up a ...Re2 attack, supported by the bishop.

24.axb5?!

This may have looked good at the time, but after exchanging the rooks, it turns out that the only remaining rook, Black's, is able to dominate, covering the a- and e-files, and also the opponent's back row. Generally if you are the exchange down, it is best to keep open files closed.

Marin suggests 24.Qd2!? Rae8 25.Rd1 Re2!? 26.Qxd3 Rxb2 27.Qxg6+ hxg6 28.axb5 axb5 . This line involves sacrifice and counter-sacrifice, and Black is about to win a bishop, but before Black can take it, White too has counterplay. Eventually this process could resolve to equality after 29.d5 Rxb3 30.d6 f6 31.Bxf6 Rxf6 32.d7 Rf8 33.d8Q Rxd8 34.Rxd8+ Kf7 35.Rd3 b4 . If 36.Kf2 bxc3 , the safest way to defend is to bring the rook behind the opponent's pawn with 37.Rd6 c5 38.Rc6 Rb2+ 39.Ke3 Rxh2 40.Rxc5 c2 41.Kd3 Rf2 42.f4 .

24...axb5 25.Rxa8 Rxa8

Once the exchanges have started, they must continue.

26.Kf2

One can in fact see the logic for Shi-rev's rook exchange, in that now White's pieces appear to have covered any weaknesses on the e-file, while there is no breakthrough on the a-file, and White's two bishops seem to work well together. The next move changes the balance of play, though.

26...Bc4!

An unexpected reply, perhaps, as normally a player would not want to set up doubled isolated pawns. Black's pawns turn out to be easily protected though, and his two isolated pawns clamp White's three connected pawns.

27.Bxc4

If White meekly withdraws with 27.Bc2?! , Black can start taking over with rook and bishop, with 27...Qe6 28.Be3 Qh3 29.Kg1 Ra2 30.Bf2 h6! , covering the check on g5. The burden of proof is in Black's favour as he is the exchange up, and clearly has the more active pieces, while White's two extra pawns are ineffective. Krnic gives 31.Bb1 Rxb2 32.Bf5 Qxf5 33.Qxb2 Qxf3 34.Qd2 Bd5 35.Kf1 Qg2+ 36.Ke2 Bc4+ 37.Ke3 Qxh2 38.Qd1 Qh3 39.Qf3 Bd5 40.Qf4 Qf1 41.Qg4+ Kf8 42.Qc8+ Ke7 43.Qc7+ Ke6 44.Qc8+ Kf6 45.Qh8+ Ke7 46.Qe5+ Kd7 , and Black should eventually win. after some delicate manoeuvring of king versus queen.

27...bxc4

We now have a complicated endgame to evaluate, with of course the added problem that there are clear middlegame features to the position. If either player becomes careless, he is in danger of being checkmated! Black can add pressure to White's b2-pawn, for example, but it is going to be very difficult to attack the pawn with both queen and Took. White in return will find it difficult to attack the doubled isolated c-pawns, which at first glance might have been a problem.

There is the additional problem that we are starting to draw close to the move 40 time control. It is unlikely that there would be serious time trouble as yet, but equally there would probably not be the luxury of either player having a prolonged think, a quarter of an hour or more, to work out strategic problems.

It is unlikely that either player will have been able to play 100% accuracy, and in the case of Shirov's play he must clearly have done something wrong, as he was rather too quickly in a losing position.

28.g4

White wants to add a bolt square for the king. He will not be checked if the king reaches g3. Also, he would be thinking about pressing forward with the pawns on the g-file. At this stage, Shirov might well be thinking of playing for a win.

28.Be7 is possible, and if 28...Re8 29.Ba3 Qd3 30.Qg5+ , and then to avoid perpetual check he has to play 30...Qg6 , but then White has a draw anyway with 31.Qc1!? .

White's position is solid, rather more so than it looks. Probably Shirov should have held if he had played ultra-solidly. but Shirov has to take calculated risks in order to try for an edge.

White's g4 push allows Black to think of setting up ...f5 or ...h5 pushes.

28...Re8

With the queens on the board, the e-file is far more critical for the rook than the a-file. Aronian is planning to attack with the pieces on the light squares, with ...Qd3 and maybe ...Qe2+ or ...Re2+, following up perhaps with ...f5 or ...h5.

Black could also think of setting up an immediate pawn attack with, for example, 28...h5 when White has to decide whether to hold the position with 29.h3 hxg4 30.hxg4 , with only two pawns on the kingside working together, or, more aggressively, 29.gxh5 Qxh5 , and either 30.Qf4 or 30.Bf4 , and White's pawns have been weakened, but not fatally, and he still keeps a passed h-pawn.

28...f6!? is less direct, and keeps open the possibilities either of pawn attack against g4, or some piece development. After 29.Bf4 , 29...h5? 30.Qf1! , threatening Qxc4+. is premature. Therefore 29...Qd3 , and if White tries to defend with 30.Qe3? , Black wins the b-pawn with 30...Qxe3+ 31.Bxe3 Ra2 32.Bc1 Ra1 33.Be3 Rb1 . White is still under long-term pressure in other lines, though. For example, if 30.Kg3 Re8 31.Qa1 Qe2 32.Qb1 Kh8!? , opening up g8 for the rook, and White will continually have to worry about ...h5.

This line is difficult to assess, but Black seems slightly better. In the end, it is hardly unexpected that the queen and rook have more piece-power than queen and bishop. White's two extra pawns supply compensation, but not quite enough.

29.Bf4

Into more active play.

29...Qd3?!

Possibly the effects of shortage of time creep in. This move is, of course, highly direct, but Black should probably have covered his defence on the dark squares first, with 29...f6! , much as discussed a move earlier.

It is not wholly clear why Aronian keeps rejecting ...f6, covering the important e5- and g5- defensive squares.

30.Kg3?

This is a serious missed opportunity. White needs to attack on the dark squares, with 30.Be5! .

Krnic in Informator suggests the pawn sacrifice 30...f6 31.Bxf6 Qe2 32.Kg3 Re3 33.Qh1 Qxb2 34.Bg5 Re2 ( 34...Rxc3? 35.Qe1! wins) 35.Qf1 . This is a doubtful line for Black, as he has no winning chances, but there are many winning chances for White. Black's rook is pinned, and to unpin with 35...Qa2 , White may play 36.h3 , followed perhaps with Bf4 and Be5. Black has gone backwards, and his f-pawn has gone. There is a simpler escape for Black, though, as Shipov notes in New in Chess. Black has 34...Qxc3! 35.Bxe3 Qxe3 , and his passed pawn is so strong, White must escape for a perpetual with 36.Qa1 c3 37.Qa8+ .

Here 30...c5 exchanges one of the isolated pawns, but is likely to veer towards equality. One line is 31.h4 cxd4 32.Bxd4 Re2+ 33.Kg3 Qd2 34.Qa1 Rg2+ 35.Kh3 Rh2+ 36.Kg3 Rg2+ with perpetual.

It seems that the best that Black can do, if hoping for a win, is 30...Qg6 , repeating. If 31.Qf1 , there is, of course, another draw offer with 31...Qd3 32.Qc1 . Here 31...Qe6 is more ambitious, but 32.g5!? is a promising reply.

Thus with best play, the game should end up as a draw.

30...Qe2?

Again, 30...f6! looks better. It is only when the pawn can stop the bishop arriving on e5 that Black genuinely looks better.

31.Qb1?

The most serious of these inaccuracies, which allows Black a good endgame. Shirov was presumably not worried about the queen exchange, but after delicate endgame play by Aronian, Black ends up with an advantage.

31.Be5! is, yet again, the best and most natural move. Black now has no time to retreat with 31...Qd3? 32.h4! Qg6 33.h5 Qe6 34.Qg5+ , winning. We thus return to 31...f6 32.Bxf6 Re3 33.Qh1 Qxb2 , drawn.

31...Qe1+

With relief, probably from both sides, that the queens are finally being exchanged. We are still only on move 31, so the players will have to play bristly before the time control. Neither player will be able to adjust completely just yet The immediate impression is that this ought to end up as a draw, with accurate play. After the queen exchange, White can protect the weak b-pawn with a bishop on a3, but surely White cannot create a kingside passed pawn?

The position proves to be more difficult than it looks, and by move 40, Shirov is already in a losing position, with problems of zugzwang. After all, if the bishop is stuck on a3, that only leaves the king and pawns for White, and sometimes it may be difficult to find good moves.

32.Qxe1 Rxe1 33.Bd6

To lock his queenside pawn structure with the bishop to a3.

33...Rg1+

Quite often extra random checks in the endgame can prove to be pointless. Here, though, Aronian forces Shirov to decide where the king has to go. Any of the four squares have defects.

34.Kf2

The king is retreating. If though 34.Kf4? Rg2 , White loses a pawn. Similarly, 34.Kh4? Rg2 , and the bishop cannot cover both pawns.

34...Rb1

With a slight but significant gain of tempo.

35.Ba3

Who is trying for a win here? Or to put it the other way round, which player has the ability just to play quiet moves, and keep the draw without difficulty? Or is it just equal, with either player having the opportunity of holding the position?

The closer one examines, the more apparent it is that White still has difficulties. Black always has the option of moving the rook away, maybe with an attack on the h-pawn, while White's bishop is stuck on a3, for as long as the b2-pawn is threatened. This adds to the possibility of zugzwang. The problem is that pawns can never move backwards, and so once a pawn has moved, there are fewer pawn moves available, and White will eventually have to think about having to play a king move.

35...Kg7

Bringing the king into play, an easy decision to make.

36.Kg3

Natural enough.

Generally players would not want to make a committal pawn push, such as 36.h4 , just before the time control. Such a move might not be a positive improvement, if the pawn push could always be made later. It might, however, create a weakness, and once the pawn has been pushed to h4, it cannot retreat. Here after 36...h5 37.Kg3 Kg6 38.Kg2 Krnic suggests that Black is winning by attacking the g-pawn either with 38...f5 or a preliminary exchange. After 38...hxg4 39.fxg4 f5 40.h5+ Kg5 41.gxf5 Kxh5 , for example, White's king will be squeezed out by Black's.

36...Kg6

Aronian too makes a quiet move. Soon, however, one of the players is about to open up their pawns.

37.h3

Probably no better or no worse than any other reasonable move here. Various writers at the time, such as Marin, Krnic, and Shipov, made no comment on this move, no doubt assuming that White was losing anyway.

37.Kf4!? is interesting aiming to pick up some queenside pawns, and creating counterplay with his d-pawn:

(a) Then 37...Rh1? 38.Ke5! Rxh2 39.Kd6 Rf2 40.Kxc6 Rxf3 41.d5 , and suddenly Black is in danger, his king being unable to cover White's passed pawn, and his Took cannot cover White's united pawn, king and bishop.

(b) Clearly this would be too crude for Black. He needs to cover the e5-square. 37...f6?! is not the best option, though, since if 38.h4 h5 39.gxh5+ Kxh5 40.Kf5 , and Black's f-pawn drops.

(c) This leaves 37...Re1 , and to avoid loss of tempo (after, for example, 38.Kg3 Re2 ), White tries 38.h4 . Then 38...h5 , to prevent White playing h5+ himself:

(c1) 39.gxh5+ Kxh5 40.Kf5 Re6! defends Black's pieces with comfort, so White has to try something else.

(c2) 39.Bd6 Re2 40.Ba3 Rg2?! sets up a zugzwang, but White can play on with 41.Ke5! hxg4 42.fxg4 Rxg4 43.Kd6 f5 44.Kxc6 f4 45.d5 f3 46.d6 f2 47.d7 f1Q 48.d8Q . 37...h5

Zugzwang!

Sometimes such zugzwang positions are less than obvious, there being no clear indicator that material is going to be lost. Look closely though, and any move by White is going to be retrograde. If his bishop moves, he is going to lose the b2-pawn. If any pawn moves, his pawn structure will be weakened. Finally his king is on its best square, but that means that any king move will be to a worse square.

38.Kh4

If 38.Kf4? h4! , and Black will pick up the h-pawn, and queen his own passed pawn. White therefore no longer has the option of active play. He has to defend passively.

If the king retreats with, for example, 38.Kg2 , Black can use the thematic 38...Rc1 followed by ...Rc2+, and soon a fresh zugzwang arises. The king will not be able to retreat twice to the second row.

If 38.h4 hxg4 39.fxg4 f5 , and now if White allows the pawns to split with, for example, 40.h5+ Kf6 41.gxf5 Kxf5 , Black will eliminate the passed pawn on the kingside, and will gradually squeeze through on the light squares on the queenside, with a win.
