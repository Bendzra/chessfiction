Game 24
G.Kamsky - M.Carlsen
Khanty-Mansiysk 2007
Petroff Defence C43

1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nf6

For lines starting with 2...Nc6 , see Game 7, Svidler-Topalov, and the many later games.

We are now in the Petroff Defence, or the Russian Defence.

Black's opening is solid, aiming for a symmetrical pawn structure, with quick and steady development for both sides. If White is happy with a steady draw, quite common if he is lower-rated, it is difficult for Black to enliven the game. If, however, White is trying to play for an edge, then chances arise for both sides, and tense games may result. Top grandmasters do not like having quick equality as White.

Kamsky was out of chess during the late 1990s and early 2000s, and Carlsen will no doubt have reckoned that his opponent would be slightly out of practice in certain openings, and could be vulnerable. His strategy worked - to some extent!

3.d4

3.Nxe5 is the normal line. Then all juniors at some stage will have been taught that if Black tries to take the symmetry too far with 3...Nxe4? 4.Qe2 Nf6? , White wins the queen with a discovered check, with 5.Nc6+ .

Black's main line is 3...d6 4.Nf3 Nxe4 . If White is happy with ultra-symmetry, with 5.d3 Nf6 , or even 5.Qe2 Qe7 6.d3 , it is difficult for Black to make play interesting. Sometimes, though, there may be catastrophic results if one of the players suddenly breaks the balance, overpressing and overbalancing. A recent example is N.Short-P.Harikrishna, Montreal 2007: 5.d3 Nf6 6.d4 d5 7.Bd3 Bd6 8.O-O O-O 9.h3 h6 10.Nc3 c6 11.Re1 Re8 12.Rxe8+ Nxe8 . At this stage, it would not be unexpected if the game finished after 19 moves with a draw, but Short went for the jugular with 13.Ne5 Nd7 14.Bf4 Nf8 15.Qh5 Be6 16.Rb1? (planning to defend against ...Qb6; he can still back out to equality with 16.Ne2 ) 16...f6 17.Ng6 Bf7! 18.Bxd6 (as if 18.Bxh6 f5! , and White will lose one of the pawns, without compensation) 18...Nxd6 19.g4? ( 19.Qg4 Nxg6 20.Bxg6 Bxg6 21.Qxg6 Qe7 leaves Black slightly better developed, but without great advantage) 19...Ne4 , and Harikrishna won a pawn.

In symmetrical positions, it is best, when trying to attack, to ensure that the attackers are more powerful than the defenders.

3...Nxe4

3...exd4 has tended to be unfashionable at top levels. White keeps a very small edge after 4.e5 Ne4 5.Qxd4 d5 6.exd6 Nxd6 7.Nc3 Nc6 8.Qf4

4.Bd3

If 4.dxe5 , there is sharp play after 4...Bc5 5.Qd5 Bxf2+ , but Black has usually avoided the challenge with 4...d5 .

4...d5

4...Nc6!? - no misprint, Black is dropping the knight - is one of the most remarkable opening innovations in the last quarter century, at least in terms of paradox. If 5.Bxe4 d5 6.Bd3 e4 , and Black is equal, albeit with play tending to be drawish. Here 5.d5 Nc5 6.dxc6 e4 7.Be2 exf3 8.cxb7 Bxb7 9.Bxf3 is an attempt at a microscopic edge for White, but it should only be equal. Thus, mostly, White usually ignores the pseudo-sacrifice with 5.dxe5 .

5.dxe5

This worked on the day, but it may well be inferior. White's pawn on e5 ought to have been slightly overloaded.

There is some history here. In the sixth game of the match between Karpov and Kamsky in 1996, Kamsky as White tried the standard 5.Nxe5 , play continuing 5...Nd7 6.Nxd7 Bxd7 7.O-O Bd6 8.Nc3 Qh4 9.g3 Nxc3 10.bxc3 Qg4 11.Re1+ Kd8 . Probably play is around equal after the exchange of queens, but Kamsky played ambitiously with 12.Be2 Qf5 13.Rb1 b6 14.c4 dxc4 15.Bxc4 Re8 16.Be3 Bc6 17.d5 Bd7 , and Black started to take over the attack. Kamsky had not reached this line again at high level, and might well have felt nervous.

5...Be7

Carlsen is being careful not to play 5...Nc6 too quickly. He knows where his bishops want to go, and he would like to keep open the option of castling as soon as possible.

6.O-O

Maybe 6.Nbd2 , but Black should be reasonably happy with either 6...Nxd2 or 6...Nc5 .

6...Bg4

Black should have excellent chances of equalizing. The pin allows him to create pressure on White's e5-pawn.

7.h3

A useful move to slip in. He cannot be checkmated on the back row.

7.Bxe4!? dxe4 8.Qxd8+ Bxd8 9.Nd4 might well be best, though. This was tried a few months earlier in A.Shirov-M.Adams, 6th matchgame, Elista 2007, Shirov later winning. Play continued 9...O-O 10.Nc3 Nd7 11.h3 Bh5 12.e6!? fxe6 13.Nxe6 Re8 14.Nxd8 Raxd8 15.Bg5 with a slight advantage to White, Black having to deal with an isolated pawn. Ftacnik suggests instead 12...Nc5 13.exf7+ Bxf7 , and then 14.Nb3 Nxb3 15.axb3 Bg6 with equality. Sometimes such lines never quite equalize fully, and here 14.Be3 Bf6 15.Rfb1!? , a computer suggestion, causes some difficulty. If White's minor pieces can become firmly entrenched in the centre, he does not have to be too afraid of the bishop-pair, hampered by Black's slightly weakened pawn structure.

7...Bh5

It is a little to early to exchange with 7...Bxf3 8.Qxf3 . Black wants to create some more pressure with the pin.

8.Re1

8.c4 Nc6 9.cxd5 Qxd5 10.Bc2 Qxd1 11.Rxd1 Nc5 ends up with a slight edge for Black. The c-pawn push takes up two tempi, while Black makes two developing moves. In some lines, however, the c4 and cxd5 idea could be good.

It is too late to attempt to emulate Shirov's idea with 8.Bxe4 dxe4 9.Qxd8+ Bxd8 10.Nd4 , as Black may now comfortably play 10...Bg6 with equality.

8...Nc6

8...Nc5 also looks fine.

9.Nc3

White cannot grab the pawn. 9.Bxe4? dxe4 10.Qxd8+ Rxd8 11.Rxe4? Rd1+ 12.Re1 Bxf3 wins a piece for Black.

9.Nbd2 Nxe5 10.Nxe4 Nxf3+ 11.gxf3 dxe4 12.Bxe4 Qxd1 13.Rxd1 c6 gives Black a fractional edge. He has the better pawn structure.

Kamsky dropped chess for several years, and he has occasionally since not been fully focused in terms of modern opening theory. Once he gets into the middlegame or endgame, though, he can still play superb top-level chess, as we shall soon see.

9...Bg6?

One senses that Carlsen is suspicious of preparation, and plays quietly. Black has good reasons, though, for playing for an edge:

(a) Even the straightforward 9...Nxc3 10.bxc3 is more than equal for Black.

(b) 9...Bxf3!? is more ambitious. If 10.Qxf3 Nxe5 , Black takes the pawn: for example, 11.Qf5 Nxd3 12.cxd3 Nxc3 13.bxc3 O-O , and he grinds on. The alternative 10.gxf3 Nxc3 11.bxc3 allows a massive gash in White's kingside pawn structure. The queenside pawn gash is less significant, as the king is not being exposed. After 11...Bc5 (threatening ...Qh4) 12.f4 Qh4 13.Qf3 there are several choices for Black, and at least one of these could prove promising. The most straightforward is 13...O-O-O 14.Qg4+ Qxg4 15.hxg4 h5 with good chances for Black.

10.Bd2!

White is not worried about losing his bishop for knight. He consolidates his central piece and pawn structure.

10.Nxd5 Qxd5 11.c4 Qe6 12.Bxe4 Qxc4 13.Bd5 is promising too, but after 13...Qb5 Black is close to equality.

10...Nxd2

After 10...Nxc3 11.Bxc3 Black is yet to prove that he is fully equal. If then 11...d4? 12.Nxd4 Nxd4 13.Bxd4 Qxd4?? 14.Bb5+ , Black loses his queen. On more sensible alternatives, White keeps the positional threat of e6: for example, 11...Qd7 12.e6 fxe6 13.Bxg7 Rg8 14.Bxg6+ hxg6 15.Bc3 , and Black's pawns have been weakened.

11.Qxd2

After the unfortunate 9...Bg6 , Black has quickly moved from a slight edge to a nagging disadvantage.

The bishop-pair is not always an advantage, and here the extra knight is more useful after the bishop exchange. The central point is not so much the pawn structure, but rather the development of the pieces. White is ahead, and can gain space with the minor pieces in the centre. Black has lost time with his ...Bc8-g4-h5-g6 manoeuvre, and will take time to catch up with castling and centralizing the rooks. White meanwhile can move on.

11...d4

Carlsen decides that if the pawn is weak on d5, he might just as well push towards d4. This is not an aggressive pawn push. He is merely trying to stabilize the centre.

11...Bh5 12.g4! Bg6 loses more time for Black, and White should consequently be able to avoid serious weaknesses on the kingside: 13.Bb5 a6 14.Bxc6+ bxc6 15.Nd4 Qd7 16.e6 fxe6 17.Nxe6 is good for White. The knights and rooks work well together, whereas Black's bishop-pair is passive and ineffective. White is attacking.

12.Ne4

12.Bxg6? dxc3 loses material.

12...O-O

Carlsen would not have enjoyed keeping the king in the centre, but now Kamsky stabilizes his centre, and keeps a better pawn structure.

Alternatively:

(a) 12...Qd5? leaves the king stuck in the centre after 13.Nf6+! gxf6 14.exf6 . White has a clear advantage.

(b) 12...Bb4!? 13.c3 dxc3 14.bxc3 damages White's pawn structure, but Black has also lost time. Then:

(b1) 14...Be7 15.Rad1 O-O 16.Qe3 gives White piece pressure in the centre, with Neg5 to follow. If 16...Qd5 17.Nf6+ gxf6 18.Bxg6 Qxa2 19.Be4 , White has a strong attack. The pawn-grabbing is not worth it.

(b2) 14...Ba5 gives livelier play for Black, but also weakens his kingside. There are unclear possibilities after 15.e6 fxe6 16.Nc5 O-O . However, 16.Rad1 O-O 17.Qf4 is a simpler and more effective plan, White thinking either of Nc5 or more probably of Neg5.

13.a3!

This little quiet move with the pawn, cutting out the opponent's attack, is often so important in helping a smooth attack.

If 13.Qf4 immediately, Black is at least equal after 13...Nb4 .

13...Qd5

13...Bh5 14.g4 Bg6 15.Bb5! allows White to win a pawn. After 15...Qd5 16.Bxc6 Qxc6 17.Nxd4 Qb6 18.Qc3 , followed by Nf5, White has covered the kingside weaknesses.

14.Qf4

Building up on the kingside, and in particular making sure that the e5-pawn is heavily protected, indeed 'overprotected', as Nimzowitsch (or, of course, his translator) used to say.

14...Rfe8

Black wants to keep some pressure on the e5-pawn, otherwise White's guardians of the overprotected pawn will be able to switch to dangerous attacking squares.

15.Re2

More overprotection.

15...Bf8

The bishop gets out of the way.

16.Ng3

The knight starts to join in the attack.

16...Bxd3

Black exchanges, before White exchanges on g6, doubling the pawns.

17.cxd3

A glance of the pawn structure may at first look about equal, but in fact Black has considerable difficulties. White's e5-pawn is attacked, but it can be defended without difficulty, and Black's queen, two rooks and knight are all being defended with corresponding force. The remaining minor pieces, the white knight on g3 and the black bishop on f8, strongly favour White, though. White is going to be able to improve his pieces, while Black soon faces his limitations.

17...Re6?

Black has to be careful, but Carlsen seems to have missed Kamsky's positional manoeuvre.

Black should overprotect his advanced but potentially weak pawn on d4. With the Took and knight both covering, Black has the chance to swing the queen around, probably to b3, making it difficult for White to set up an unimpeded attack. Black's position is well balanced, and so is White's. Play might continue something like 17...Rad8 18.Rae1 ( 18.Nh5 Ne7! holds) 18...Qb3 19.Rd2 g6! 20.Ne4 Bg7 (at last, the bishop does something, even if it is only plugging a pawn weakness) 21.Nf6+ 21.Nc5?! Qd5 22.Nxb7? Rb8 naturally leaves the knight in trouble) 21...Bxf6 22.Qxf6 ( 22.exf6? Rxe1+ 23.Nxe1 Qe6 , and Black is better) 22...Qd5 with good chances for Black of holding.

This is an unexpected result, but it all has sound positional logic. Black's bishop has been a weakness, and he would very much like to exchange the bishop for knight, to equalize the number of attackers and defenders of e5. To do this, Black would need to play ...g6 and ...Bg7, to add to the number of attackers of e5. This creates an additional weakness on the dark squares in front of the king, but provided Black keeps attacking on e5, it is difficult to divert the attack against Black's king.

Play might continue with 23.Qh4 h5 (another weakness, but necessary) 24.Rde2 Re6 25.Ng5 (otherwise ...Rde8 and a positional deadlock) 25...Rxe5 26.Ne4 , and then probably a perpetual after 26...Kg7 27.Qf6+ Kg8 28.Qh4 .

Having established that ...g6 is a promising idea, could Black have tried it earlier? The problem would be that White would recapture with the pawn on f6. For example, 17...g6 18.Ne4 Bg7 19.Nf6+ Bxf6 20.exf6! , and White is better, Black facing problems on g7. Or, more drastically, 17...Rad8 18.Rae1 g6?? (instead of the preparatory 18...Qb3 19.Rd2 g6 ) 19.Ne4 Bg7 20.Nf6+ Bxf6 21.exf6! Rxe2 21.Qh6 , and White gives checkmate.

18.Nh5!

Black no longer has the chance to play ...g6.

18...Rae8

The natural move, but White's e-pawn remains stubbornly well protected. Maybe 18...Qb3 19.Qe4 Rd8 as an alternative.

19.Rae1

Four pieces attacking the e5-square, four defending. If White can hold on to this square, he will be better, but can he do this?

19...a5

Black is unexpectedly now in serious trouble, as the alternatives also reveal:

(a) 19...f6? 20.Qg4! (with threats on f6) 20...fxe5 (or 20...Nxe5 21.Nxe5 fxe5 22.Nf4! , with the same tactical theme) 21.Nf4! , winning the exchange through a combination of knight fork and pin.

(b) 19...Rg6 20.Nh4 Rge6 offers a draw by repetition with 21.Nf3?! , but White can do better with 21.Qg4! with knight threats on f4 and f6. After 21...Nxe5 22.Rxe5 Rxe5 23.Nf6+ Kh8 24.Nxd5 Rxe1+ 25.Kh2 White is ahead on the material count, and should win. There are also various ways that Black can sacrifice the exchange for a pawn, not least in this line 21...Qd8 22.Nf6+ Rxf6 23.exf6 Rxe2 24.Rxe2 Qxf6 , but such lines should in general be winning for White.

(c) So how can Black hold this position? One answer is pure Steinitzian grit, with 19...R6e7!? 20.Qg4 Kh8 , avoiding pawn moves for as long as possible, defending pawns as far as possible, and getting away from the knight check on f6. The computer suggests good prospects to hold for Black, but the human player would sense that there ought to be something for White. The answer is to ram the e-pawn with 21.Nf4 followed by e6, breaking open Black's kingside pawns. So 21...Qd7 (or most other queen moves) 22.e6! fxe6 23.Ng6+ hxg6 24.Ng5 , entombing Black's king with the threat of Qh4+.

It becomes understandable that Carlsen in effect does nothing, but Kamsky, of course, can do something.

20.Qg4!

Vacating the f4-square for a knight fork, and pinning Black's g-pawn with another fork on f6. The queen and knight are working smoothly. White is gaining material by force, which must have been an unexpected surprise for Carlsen.

20...Rg6

20...Nxe5? 21.Rxe5 wins.

21.Nf4!

Sometimes there is no need to move the queen when it is attacked. There is generally an almost automatic response to look for checks when the queen is attacked, but on occasion a player might be slow in seeing that it may be possible to counterattack against the opposition queen.

21...Rxg4

The best chance of holding.

22.Nxd5 Rg6

The rook is short of squares, and Kamsky now decides he wants to take advantage of this.

23.g4

Threatening Nf4, and if ...Rh6, then g5 trapping the rook, with the help of the pawns and two knights.

23.Nxc7 Rc8 is, of course, also good, but often it is more effective to win with an extra exchange rather than an extra pawn.

23...Rd8

If 23...Rh6 , then 24.g5 Rxh3 25.Kg2 Rh5 26.Nf4 again snares the rook. If 23...h5 , White must of course avoid 24.Nf4?? hxg4 25.Nxg6 gxf3 , and Black is winning, but 24.g5 Be7 26.h4 is better. If Black makes an escape square with his rook, by moving his knight, then White will win both the c7- and d4-pawns.

24.Nf4

Yes, the rook will fall.

24...Rh6

A tiny hope. He tries to provoke the pawn to g5, allowing the knight to create a none too effective outpost on f5.

25.g5

Of course, winning the exchange is more important than allowing Black the extra square.

25...Re6

Blocking White's e-pawn.

26.Nxe6 fxe6

White is the exchange up. Black's position is not quite resignable just yet, so in an important knockout game he plays on. The rest of the game is not all that exciting, and Kamsky does not loosen his grip. Only brief notes for the remaining moves, therefore.

27.Rc1 Rd5 28.Rc4

Adding pressure to Black's d4-pawn.

28...Bc5

Defending. The trouble is, of course, that the bishop is only defending, it can attack nothing.

29.h4

Over the next few moves, Kamsky brings forward his kingside pawns to add pressure on his opponent's king-side. He also starts to centralize his king. Carlsen attempts to find better moves for his pieces, but he cannot make much impact on his opponent.

29...Bb6 30.Kg2 Ne7 31.h5 Rd8

Black can do nothing on the e5-pawn by now, but maybe he can add pressure to the f-file?

32.Re4

White resumes the squeeze on the pawn on d4.

32...Nf5

If 32...c5 , White breaks open with 33.b4 .

33.Nh4

Aiming to exchange Black's most active piece.

33...Ne7

33...Nxh4 34.Rxh4 Rf8 35.f4 should not cause much technical difficulty. If 35...c5 , White has 36.b4 cxb4 37.axb4 a4 38.Kf3 a3 39.Rh2 Ra8 40.Ra2 , etc.

34.Kg3 g6 35.Kg4

Two more king moves to add to the pressure.

35...Rd5 36.hxg6 hxg6

36...Nxg6 37.Nxg6 hxg6 38.a4 is no better, no worse.

37.a4

Avoiding having to think about ...Rb5.

37...Kf7

Extra cover, to give the knight the chance to move.

38.Rc1 Rd8 39.Rh1

Without this plan, making use of the open h-file, it could still have been difficult for White to break through.

39...Kg7

Black cannot afford the exchange of rooks after 39...Rh8 40.Ree1 followed by Ng2 and Nf4.

40.Ng2 Nf5

Covering the h6 option, but of course White has several alternatives here.

41.Nf4

White's knight is even better than Black's.

41...Re8 42.Ree1

To double on the h-file.

42...c5 43.Rh3

1-0

The end. Reh1 crashes through.
