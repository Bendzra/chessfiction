Game 9
P.Leko-T.Radjabov
Morelia 2006
Sicilian Defence B33

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.d4

3.Bb5 is a quieter 'more positional' alternative, doubling the pawns after 3...g6 4.Bxc6 dxc6 5.d3 . It is noticeable that Leko, when playing as White against Van Wely, has chosen this line twice, with a draw at the Melody Amber Rapidplay 2005, and a powerful win at Dortmund 2005. Many other players have tried to avoid Van Wely's deep theoretical analysis.

It is perhaps equally noteworthy that Leko has faced problems with Black in this line, in Shirov-Leko, Monaco (rapid) 2005, Svidler-Leko, Dortmund 2005, and Ivanchuk-Leko, Mukachevo (rapid) 2007; three straight wins against Leko. This suggests that the 'Sicilian Lopez' still provides significant dangers for Black.

3...cxd4 4.Nxd4

Best, and natural play on both sides.

4...Nf6

There are, of course, many different ways of handling this position. One of the older ideas is the Löwenthal Variation, 4...e5 5.Nb5 a6 6.Nd6+ Bxd6 7.Qxd6 Qf6 , and then various possible queen retreats by White, which should keep a slight edge. This is very much a mid-19th Century rendering of the Sicilian, with no hedgehog style set-ups with ...d6 and ...e6, but instead moving the pawn quickly in the centre and bringing the pieces into play just as quickly. There is a slight loss of subtlety in Black's play.

5.Nc3

The natural reply. 5.Nxc6 bxc6 is only equal.

5...e5

An improved Löwenthal Variation, the Pelikan. Jorge Pelikan was one of many European players (he was originally Czech) who played at the Buenos Aires Olympiad in 1939, and stayed in Argentina after the announcement of the War. He experimented with this line in the mid-1950s, but it was regarded as slightly suspicious in view of the weakness of the light squares. The line was revitalized in the mid-1970s by Evgeny Sveshnikov, who showed that there were considerable tactical and positional complexities, which force White to take care. An old idea becomes modern.

6.Ndb5

Alternatively:

(a) 6.Nxc6 bxc6 is only equal. Black keeps a solid pawn structure.

(b) 6.Nf5!? is worth considering, avoiding much of the complicated main line. White wants to try Nd6+ and also Ne3, covering rather more efficiently the pressure on d5. Black should probably try 6...d5 7.exd5 Bxf5 8.dxc6 , when the queen exchange after 8...Qxd1+ 9.Nxd1 bxc6 10.Ne3 gives White a slight edge in view of Black's isolated pawns. There are many who would very happily grind away for White in such an opening. Thus 8...bxc6 9.Qf3 ( 9.Qxd8+ Rxd8 is less impressive this time, Black being ahead in development) 9...Qd7 10.Bg5 has been tried, maybe with a slight edge for White, but seems under-researched. Something to be tried for the adventurous? Or even for the less adventurous?

6...d6

6...a6 7.Nd6+ Bxd6 8.Qxd6 is now far less effective than the Löwenthal Variation, as Black no longer has ...Qf6: 8...Qe7 9.Qxe7+ followed by Bg5 leaves White positionally better, the weaknesses on d6 and d5 again being a concern.

7.Bg5

The main line these days, although Pelikan's opponents in the 1950s did not like the idea of White being stuck with knights on a3 and c3, when Black can retaliate with ...b5. Therefore 7.a4 was a natural reply. Play might continue with 7...a6 8.Na3 Be6 9.Bg5 Rc8 , with strategies not too different from the main line, but it is not all that clear that a4 gains that much.

7.Nd5 is very direct. After 7...Nxd5 8.exd5 Nb8! , White has no more than the usual slight edge. The knight retreat looks paradoxical, but 8...Ne7 gets in the way of the bishop, and would not give Black the opportunity of manoeuvring the knight to c5.

7...a6

Time to push the knight away, before White has the chance of playing Nd5 followed by Nbc3.

8.Na3

8.Bxf6 gxf6 9.Na3 used to be popular, but allows counterplay with 9...f5 .

8...b5

8...Be6 has been tried many times, but after 9.Nc4 Nd4 10.Bxf6 Qxf6 11.Nd5 White's d5-knight is more secure than Black's corresponding d4-knight, and White should have a slight edge. There are no recent examples in top-level games.

9.Nd5

Varying from a game they played at Linares two years earlier. There have been man recent tries with 9.Bxf6 gxf6 10.Nd5 Bg7 11.Bd3 Ne7 12.Nxe7 Qxe7 13.O-O f5 14.c4 O-O 15.Qf3 , and now a startling pawn breakthrough with 15...d5! .

Much of the theory of the Pelikan depends on whether or not Black can squash the light squares on d5 and f5, and here Black takes the radical suggestion of making pawn breakthroughs on both of these squares. White cannot hold the pawn on e4, and if Black can push with ...e4 himself, then he will have excellent attacking diagonals on the dark squares, h8-a1 and f8-a3.

The main line continues with 16.cxd5 fxe4 17.Bxe4 , and now 17...Rb8 , escaping from White's h1-a8 diagonal. White has then tried various rook moves. We continue with the Leko line after 18.Rad1 Rb6 . Then 19.Qd3 Qd7 20.g3 f5 21.Bg2 Rh6 22.f4 exf4 23.gxf4 , drawn before too long in P.Leko-T.Radjabov, Linares 2004. But surely, one might think, there is a simple pawn snatch earlier? After 20.Bxh7+? Kh8 21.Qe3 Rh6 22.Bc2 Qd6 White's position collapsed with remarkable speed on 23.g3 Bh3 24.Rfe1 f5 25.f4? (speeding the crash) 25...exf4 26.Qe7 Qb6+ 0-1, P.Smirnov-T.Radjabov, FIDE World Championship, Tripoli 2004. One may feel reasonably certain that Radjabov had analysed this well in advance.

9...Be7

9...Qa5+ 10.Bd2 Qd8 is an implicit draw offer, which White does not have to accept Apart from 11.Bg5 , there is the complicated line with 11.c4 Nxe4 12.cxb5 , or the quieter 11.Nxf6+ Qxf6 12.Bd3 .

10.Bxf6

This gives White a dominating d5-square for the knight, making Black's position look dubious, at least according to much earlier theory. That view existed until Sveshnikov, in the second half of the 1970s, showed that Black had compensation for the d5-gap. Black has, of course, gained the bishop-pair.

Otherwise:

(a) 10.Nxf6+ gxf6! followed by ...f5 is comfortable for Black.

(b) 10.Nxe7 Nxe7 11.Bd3 Bb7 12.Qe2 Nd7 13.c4 b4 14.Nc2 a5 15.O-O Nc5 16.f3 Qc7 with a small edge for White, later drawn, in A.Morozevich-T.Radjabov, Kemer 2007. Possibly better is 12...Ng6!? 13.c4 h6 14.Bd2 O-O 15.O-O Rb8 16.Rfd1 bxc4 17.Nxc4 d5 18.exd5 Qxd5 19.f3 e4 , equal, later drawn, in K.Georgiev-P.Eljanov, Sochi 2007.

10...Bxf6

10...gxf6 is possible, but usually Black prefers to bring the bishop back into play via g5.

11.c3

The knight has to re-emerge, and if it can arrive on e3, it can be strong.

11.c4 b4 12.Nc2 is a possibility, but the general preference is to keep the c4-square open, with the help of a later a4 push, rather than to block c4 with a pawn. Morozevich is the main current advocate of this line: for example, 12...Rb8 13.b3 Bg5 14.g3 O-O 15.h4 Bh6 16.Bh3 Be6 17.Kf1 a5 18.Kg2 with a slight edge for White, later drawn, in Morozevich-Leko, FIDE World Championship, San Luis 2005. There are some obvious similarities in development patterns with the Leko-Radjabov game, but also a substantial contrast. The Leko game as White leaves the light squares on the queenside open, and able for use by the pieces, whereas the Leko game as Black ends up with a closed queenside.

11...O-O

Here 11...Bg5 12.Nc2 O-O often transposes, although Black could also experiment with delaying castling, with, for example, 12...Rb8 .

Independently 11...Ne7 12.Nxf6+ gxf6 breaks off White's knight dominance on d5, but Black's pawns are still slightly weakened. White usually tries 13.Bd3 or 13.Qf3 . Black has a slightly weakened pawn structure. If, for example, ...f5 followed by a pawn exchange on d4, Black has three pawn islands (a+b; c+d+e; h), whereas White has two bigger and safe islands (a+b+c; f+g+h).

12.Nc2

Preferring the knight-pair, rather than the bishop-pair. Also, White is planning to break up Black's queenside pawns with a4.

12.Nxf6+ Qxf6 is only equal. Then 13.Be2 or 13.Bd3 are rare. We shall see why on move 15, when White makes a bishop jump to c4 in one go, instead of using up a tempo with Bf1-e2-c4. Another example of the theme, 'knights before bishops'.

12...Bg5

Black wants to keep his bishop-pair.

13.a4

In the pre-Sveshnikov days of the Pelikan, White tried 13.h4 Bh6 14.g4 Bf4 15.Qf3 , but Black's play was comfortable after 15...Be6 16.Nxf4 Qf6 17.g5 Qxf4 18.Qxf4 exf4 19.Bh3 Ne5 20.Bxe6 fxe6 , eventually winning in D.Minic-K.Langeweg, Budva 1963. If White could split Black's queenside first, then maybe the h4 idea would seem more promising, as in the Leko-Radjabov game.

13...bxa4

13...Rb8? 14.h4 Bh6 15.axb5 axb5 allows White a clear open a-file, and after 16.g4 Bf4 17.Qf3 Be6 18.Nxf4 Qf6 19.Ra6 White wins a pawn.

14.Rxa4

The exchange has improved White's pawn structure, two good queenside pawns versus one isolated pawn. It is not just a case of trying to win a pawn. White now has useful outpost squares on c4 and a4. He is aiming for control of the light squares.

14...a5

If 14...f5 15.exf5 Bxf5 , White happily plays 16.Bxa6 . Black instead wants to secure his pawn, and makes it more difficult for White to try Ncb4.

15.Bc4

Finally the bishop emerges, in just one go. 15.b4 Bb7 16.b5 Ne7 does not do that much for White.

15...Rb8

Later Radjabov was to prefer 15...Bd7!? . For example, in V.Anand-T.Radjabov, Wijk aan Zee 2007, play carried on with 16.O-O Rb8 17.Ra2 Kh8 18.Nce3 g6 19.Qd3 f5 20.b3 Bh6 , and Black was far more active than in the Leko game. One main point is that Black does not want to allow the rook to stay on a4 when the position is opened.

16.b3

White continues to build up his pawns.

16.Ra2 Kh8 17.Nce3 Bxe3 18.Nxe3 Ne7 has been tried, and is equal. Leko prefers to keep the rook active a little longer.

16...Kh8

16...Be6 was of considerable theoretical and historical interest in A.Karpov-E.Sveshnikov, USSR Championship, Moscow 1973. After 17.Qa1 g6 18.O-O Qd7 19.Rd1 f5 20.exf5 gxf5 21.b4 axb4 22.cxb4 Kh8 23.b5 Bxd5 24.Rxd5 Ne7 25.Qxe5+ dxe5 26.Rxd7 Nc8 , the knight threat on b6 led to equality.

This line, with 16...Be6 , quickly died out Quite a few non-theoreticians tried 17.O-O , which at the very least cuts down the Leko-Radjabov line, but there is no reason at all for Black to avoid this.

So why has the Karpov-Sveshnikov line been abandoned? Maybe there is nothing seriously wrong with Sveshnikov's move in the position; it is quite simply that 16...Kg8 is more flexible, allowing Black to think of ...f5, and deciding later where the bishop should go.

Sveshnikov himself showed the way in E.Geller-E.Sveshnikov, USSR Championship, Tbilisi 1978, with 16...Kh8 17.O-O f5 18.exf5 Bxf5 19.Qd2 Qd7 ( 19...Bg6 is nowadays more usual) 20.Nce3 Be6 21.Rd1 Bd8 , equal, but Sveshnikov later demonstrated a spectacular sacrificial kingside winning attack.

17.Nce3

17.Qe2 is the chief alternative at top level. Black has made various replies ( 17...Bd7!? , 17...f5!? ), with reasonable chances of equality.

For 17.O-O , see the previous note on Geller-Sveshnikov.

17...Be6

A natural developing move, but there is also an argument to be made that by this stage of the game, it is more important to concentrate on attack, defence, counterattack, and useful exchanges, rather than just bringing the bishop out Leko has reached this position both as White and as Black. A year earlier he tried 17...g6 , the reply being 18.h4! Bxh4 19.g3 Bg5 20.f4 exf4 21.gxf4 Bh4+ . This had been known before, but after 22.Kf1?! f5 23.Ra2 fxe4 24.Rah2 g5 25.Ng2 Rb7 Black had covered his weaknesses, and White's kingside had been opened up a little, in M.Stanga-S.Kindermann, Altensteig 1987.

In V.Topalov-P.Leko, Linares 2005, White brought his king to the other side with 22.Kd2!? Ne7 23.Kc1 Nxd5 24.Nxd5 Be6 25.Qd4+ Kg8 26.Ra2 (there have also been experiments with 26.Kb1 , avoiding any checks from c3) 26...Bxd5 27.Qxd5 Qf6 28.Qd2 Bg3 29.Rf1 with complicated play. This ended up with a draw, but with queen, rook and opposite-coloured bishops, there were perhaps not surprisingly some slip-ups.

Radjabov, in a later encounter against Leko, tried to keep play simple with 17...Bxe3 18.Nxe3 Ne7 19.O-O f5 20.exf5 Bxf5 , P.Leko-T.Radjabov, Morelia/Linares 2008. Leko showed, yet again, his great understanding on both sides of the board in the Sveshnikov system, and found a way of setting up serious pressure on d6 after 21.Ra2 Be4 22.Rd2 Rb6 23.Re1 Qb8 24.Qa1 Qc7 25.Red1 h6 26.h3 Bb7 27.Qa3 Rd8 28.Be6! Qxc3 29.Rxd6 , and Black's d-file soon collapsed.

Two wins by Leko in the same line against the same top-rated opponent. A result!

18.h4!?

Leko would, of course, have remembered his game as Black against Topalov with 17...g6 18.h4 , and would have recognized this as being at least promising after 18...Be6 .

18...Bf4?

Missing an extremely deep idea. 18...Bh6? 19.Ng4 is also strong for White.

The simplest line, exchanging, is 18...Bxe3 19.Nxe3 Ne7! . It is far from clear that White has an edge, his king not yet being safely developed. If, for example, 20.Bxe6?! (doubling the pawns, but opening up the f-file for Black) 20...fxe6 21.Nc4 d5 22.exd5 exd5 23.Nxe5 Qc7 24.Qd4 Rxb3 and Black wins a pawn. There are of course safer options, such as 20.O-O Nc8 21.Bxe6 fxe6 22.h5 , but it remains to be proven that White has an edge.

19.Nf5!

A natural enough move, taking advantage of Black's dark-squared bishop not having been exchanged. White has two knights on light squares, and it is very difficult for Black to exchange the light-squared bishop for one of the knights, since then the other knight dominates.

Radjabov's idea had been that if White had played 19.g3 Bxe3 20.Nxe3 Bxc4 21.Nxc4 f5 , Black is comfortable, White having weakened his kingside pawn structure. Also 19.Nxf4 exf4 20.Nd5 Ne5 21.Nxf4 Bxc4 22.bxc4 Re8 is comfortable for Black.

Leko had foreseen his opponent's innovation, and found a bigger counter-innovation. Such things happen in top level chess.

19...g6

Radjabov was, of course, relying on this. Anything else, and White is comfortable, making full use of his light squares.

20.Nfe3!

A sensational idea, giving away two tempi with the knight, just so that Black has to weaken his pawn structure, slightly but significantly.

Leko's move makes a great impression, but sadly from the purely technical point of view, there is also an equally good alternative: 20.Nxf4! Bxf5 ( 20...exf4? 21.Nxd6 ) 21.exf5 exf4 . Leko gives this as unclear, but after 22.Bd5!? (there are other promising moves), White has better control in the centre, despite initial appearances. Once Black has tried ...Ne5, he would of course have excellent use of the e5-square, but White would have the remaining three central squares. Play might continue with 22...Re8+ ( 22...Qf6 23.Rh3! makes good use of the ranks, as well as the files) 23.Kf1 Ne5 24.fxg6 fxg6 25.Rxf4 Qc7 26.Rh3 , and again White uses the files effectively.

Black quite simply has too many pawn weaknesses.

20...Kg7

It proves to be difficult for Black to equalize. The pawn weakness on g6 is a severe problem. Black could, of course, try 20...h5 , to prevent White playing h5 himself, but extra pawn pushes to try to conceal other pawn weaknesses tend to have a tendency to create even further weaknesses.

20...Bxe3!? 21.Nxe3 Bxc4 22.Rxc4 Ne7 23.h5 g5 , and if 24.O-O f5 , might keep White down to a slight edge, but Black is not yet equalizing. This seems a much smaller edge than in the lines starting with 20.Nxf4 , so with regrets, the impression is that Leko's line is not totally the best. It is still good, though, and refreshingly paradoxical.

21.g3

Or maybe 21.h5 immediately, also with active play.

21...Bh6?!

Radjabov wants to keep the tension with the bishop, but it soon turns out that it is White who is keeping the tension on the bishop, and in the end White can force the exchange for knight against bishop on a much more uncomfortable square.

21...Bxe3 22.Nxe3 looks better, but White has a definite slight edge. Black has weaknesses on the d-file, and has to pay attention to possible h5-pushes. Here 22...Qb6 keeps White down to a slight edge, as if 23.Qxd6?! Rfd8 24.Qa3 Bxc4 25.Rxc4 Qxb3 , and Black is now slightly better.

22.Ng4!

Superb flexibility by the knight, before Black can cut out this knight option with ...f5.

Even if Black goes just slightly wrong in the Sveshnikov Variation, White can have excellent opportunities of exploiting light-squared manoeuvring.

22...f5

Black finds a way of activating his pawns, but his king is soon stuck on h6, and Leko plays vigorously.

22...Bxg4 23.Qxg4 Qc8 24.Qxc8 Rfxc8 cuts out any weaknesses against the king, but Black's pawns and minor pieces are under severe pressure. After, for example, 25.g4 (squashing the idea of ...f5) 25...Bf4 26.Ke2 , Black's bishop can attack nothing on the dark squares, and of course has absolutely no influence on the light squares.

23.Nxh6

The bishop-pair is worth getting rid of. One bishop gone means that the defender cannot cover the remaining squares.

23...Kxh6

Radjabov's position would be comfortable had his king been on h8 or g7, rather than the unusual h6-square. Leko must move quickly before his opponent may consolidate.

24.h5!

Leko notes that 24.Ne3 is also possible, with slight edges after either 24...Bxc4 or 24...f4 . The move that Leko chooses is more direct. If the opponent's king is on a strange square, he might just as well take advantage of it.

24...g5

Ugly, but Radjabov is uncomfortable about the thought of opening the h-file: 24...Kg7 25.hxg6 hxg6 26.exf5 Bxf5 27.Be2 (or 27.Bf1!? - Korotylev) 27...Rh8 28.Rxh8 Qxh8 29.Rh4 Qf8 30.Bc4 with advantage. White has successfully opened and closed the valve on the fourth rank, and with much more space, White's second rook takes over the attack on the fourth rank, while Black's second rook cannot defend so successfully.

25.exf5

Breaking open Black's kingside pawns.

25...Bxf5

25...Rxf5? 26.Ne3 Rf6? 27.Bxe6 Rxe6 28.Nf5 mate is a quick finish.

26.Ne3

Another punchy move by the knight.

26...Bc8

26...Be4!? is more active, with perhaps the implication that it is not so good in defence. Black would ideally like to keep some control on the c8-h3 diagonal:

(a) 27.Bf7?! Nb4! (or 27...Rxf7 28.Rxe4 with a slight edge for White) 28.Qf3?! is spectacular, but not quite sound. Instead of 28...Bxf3?? 29.Nf5 mate, Black has 28...Nc2+ 29.Kd2 Nxe3 30.Rxe4 Nd5 , and he is better.

(b) 27.O-O Bf3 28.Be2 Bxe2 29.Qxe2 is Leko's suggestion as White's best, with a clear advantage. Rogozenko puts this even stronger by suggesting that 'Black is strategically lost'. Fritz in contrast gives this as equal, but is the computer always to be trusted?

Further examination suggests that after 29...Kg7 30.Qc4 , Black still has problems. He faces the standard pawn weaknesses with the a- and d-pawns in this opening, and his kingside light-coloured squares have been opened up rather more than is comfortable.

A line might be 30...Rc8 31.Qe6 Qf6 32.Qd7+ Kh8 33.Nd5 Qh6 34.Rc4 Rfd8 35.Qb7 Qg7 36.Qa6 , and White continues to keep pressure on. Alternatively, 30...Qd7 31.Rd1 , or 30...Ne7 31.Rfa1 d5 32.Qg4 Nc6 33.Qe6 Qf6 34.Qxf6+ Rxf6 35.Nxd5 Rd6 36.Ne3 , and White keeps the extra pawn. So White keeps an edge.

27.Ra2!

We have already seen this rook retreat, in the other Leko-Radjabov game (note to Black's 17th). White is ready to defend on f2, and to attack from d2, while still keeping in touch with the attack on Black's a-pawn.

27...Ne7

Leko in Informator gives 27...Kg7 28.Rd2 Rf6 29.h6+ Kh8 30.Qh5 as winning for White. It is comfortable for him, but after 30...Rf8! there is no immediate winning plan. One wonders whether there might be a typo, or whatever the term is of a lazy hit on the mouse. It is possible he was intending 30.Rh5 Rg6 ( 30...Qf8? 31.Rxg5 Qxh6 32.Rg8 mate) 31.Bd3 Re6 32.Be4 , with a bind.

This would be yet another example of the rooks using the a- and h-files to their advantage, then maybe changing ranks and files (Ra4-a2-d2; Rh5 threatening Rxg5) to attack various weaknesses in Black's pawn structure. White's dominance of the minor pieces is significant, but something else needs to be added. What is unusual here is that there are no completely open files, and only two closed files (g- and h-files). There are six semi-open files, three on either side. White's a-, c- and f-pawns are safe enough, and cannot be attacked. Black's e5-pawn is also safe, but his a5- and d6-pawns are weak. In addition, White's push with the h-pawn to the sixth leaves White's pawn being slightly weak, but Black's g5-pawn is also slightly weak. It is possible that there might be an exchange of weaknesses. Even here, it is White who is likely to initiate the exchange of pawn weaknesses.

28.O-O

White keeps his flexibility. If Black's king does not move, and does not allow White to push with h6, then the rook is more effective elsewhere, maybe on d1 or a1, again attacking pawns on half-open files.

28.Rxa5? , intending 28...Qxa5?? 29.Qxd6+ , is careless because 28...Bb7! 29.O-O d5 gives Black counterplay.

28...Rf6

Black concentrates on trying to defend his d-pawn, rather than immediately trying to extricate his king. If 28...Kg7 29.Qd3 Bf5 30.Nxf5 Rxf5 31.Rd1 Rf6 32.Qe3 h6 33.Rad2 , Black is under pressure. White has, by this stage, complete centralization.

29.Qe2

His first chance to castle on move 28, then his first queen move on move 29. Soon it will be time to bring the rooks together. The completion of the opening phase may have been slow, but the late middlegame phase is quick.

29...Qb6

29...Qe8 30.Rxa5 Qxh5 is a pawn exchange that is likely to favour White, allowing him a good passed b-pawn. Leko gives 31.Ra7 Bb7 32.Ng4+ Kg7 33.Rxb7 (not 33.Nxf6?? Qh1 mate) 33...Rxb7 34.Qe4 Rc7 35.Nxf6 Kxf6 36.Ra1 as favouring White; indeed, one would expect, a forced win.

30.Rfa1

But now there is the danger of taking the a-pawn for nothing.

30...Qc6

Black must find counterplay on the long diagonal.

31.Rxa5

There is no reason to delay.

31...Bb7

A threat!

32.Bd5!

It is the knight that needs to be retained, while the bishop may be exchanged.

32...Nxd5 33.Rxd5

He wants to keep the knight on its best square, on e3, where it cannot be attacked, and where it is threatening checks and attacks on g4 and f5.

33...Qxc3

33...Kg7 34.Ra7 Kh8 35.Qb5 Qc7 ( 35...Qc8? 36.Rxd6 ) 36.Qb4 Qc6 37.c4 grinds slowly on.

34.Ng4+

Leko could have set up a trap with 34.Ra7!? Bc8 35.Qf3! , and if 35...Rxf3 36.Rxd6+ Kxh5 37.Rxh7 mate. Here 34...Kg7 35.Ng4 would have transposed into the game, and 34...Qxe3!? , followed by ...Bxd5, may look slightly desperate, but such a sacrifice of queen versus rook and bishop can be difficult to break down.

Leko's immediate check cuts down the opponent's options.

34...Kg7 35.Ra7

The finish is enlivened by various pins and other tactics, all of which favour White.

35...Re6

Leko notes the checkmate in mid-board after 35...Qc6 36.Nxf6 Kxf6 37.Qf3+ Ke6 38.Rxb7 Qxb7 39.Qg4+ Kxd5 40.Qd4 mate. A battle on the long diagonal. This would have been an attractive finish.

By contrast, White wins slowly, probably on the dark squares, after 35...Qc1+ 36.Kh2 Rf7 37.Rxb7 Rfxb7 38.Rxd6 . Black's extra exchange does not have much impact when White's queen, rook and knight are drilling away at Black's kingside pawns and exposed king. After 38...Qc3 39.h6+ Kh8 40.Nxe5 Rf8 41.Nd7 , even if there is objectively a win for White, he will have to work hard: 41...Rxd7 42.Rxd7 Qf6 43.Kg1 Qxh6 44.Qe4 might at first look long and technical, but again Black's king is exposed, and his pieces cannot do anything other than defend. 44...Rc8 45.Kg2! is close to zugzwang. If Black does not lose immediately, the b-pawn will decide.

35...Qf3 36.h6+ Kf8 37.Qxf3 Rxf3 38.Rb5 Rf7 39.Ne3 is given by Leko as a win for White, Black being squeezed, but White still has to be careful. After 39...Rc7 40.Nc4 Ke7 White does not quite have the luxury of creating an escape square for the king after 41.g4?! Kd7 42.Rb6 , as Black can jump out of prison with 42...Bc6! . Even if White were to find a winning try in the endgame, this would be long-winded. Once the problem is seen, the solution is easier: 41.Rb6! g4 42.Kh2 places Black in zugzwang.

36.Qc4!

Forcing favourable simplification, or a mating attack.

36...Qe1+

36...Qxc4 37.bxc4 Kf8 38.Rb5 Re7 39.Ne3 will win for White.

37.Kg2

White is not scared of the long diagonal, as he is intending to sacrifice the exchange to eliminate the bishop.

37...Re7

The only move.

38.Rxb7
