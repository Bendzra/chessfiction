Game 28
P.Eljanov-R.Ponomariov
Kharkiv 2008
London System D00

1.d4 d5 2.Bf4

One of those reasonable but obscure openings that has not been given a clearly recognized name. How about the Mason Variation after the American (formerly Irish) player from the 19th Century? And then there is the Steinitz Counter-Gambit, 2...c5. These were the names given by the late Hooper and Whyld, co-authors of The Oxford Companion to Chess. Presumably in other countries, other names have been allocated, but 2.Bf4 is often regarded under the 'London' umbrella: 2.Nf3 Nf6 3.Bf4 is the London System proper.

There is nothing wrong with this move, if we take the slightly negative sense that White cannot be worse yet. Whether White can be better remains to be seen. The bishop move is a developing move, and does not get in the way of anything, but it creates no pressure against the opponent. Even 2.Bg5 at least pins the e-file, and obtains pressure when at some stage Black develops with ...Nf6.

The psychology of such an unusual move is not too difficult to appreciate, at least in broad terms. Eljanov naturally has knowledge and good grasp of the opening, but Ponomariov is, of course, also extremely well prepared. Does Eljanov feel it is a good time to try something new for his opponent?

2...c5

And so we reach the Steinitz Counter-Gambit. This is, of course, the only direct way to try to punish White's centre, but other moves are, of course, reasonable.

2...e6 3.e3 Bd6 4.Bg3 is a steady line briefly explored a century ago, and in a much more recent top-level blitz game, V.Kramnik-S.Rublevsky, Moscow 2007. Did the players make a mistake, as early as move 3? It seems not. The computer gives 4.Qg4 as an advantage for White, but playing though the moves, it becomes clear that after 4...Nf6 5.Qxg7 Rg8 6.Qh6 Rg6 7.Qh4 Rg4 8.Qh6 Rg6 the position is heading for a perpetual.

3.e4

This could conceptually be regarded as an 'Albin Counter-Counter-Gambit', in recognition of Albin's idea of the late 19th Century, 1.d4 d5 2.c4 e5!? (or perhaps '?!') 3.dxe5 d4 . Presumably both players were trying to work out the implications of the additional Bf4 in the main line. On the whole, the extra bishop move seems useful, but we shall see...

The J.Mason-W.Steinitz, London 1883, game continued with 3.dxc5 Nc6 4.Nf3 f6 followed by ...e5, and Black recovered his pawn with good chances for an edge.

3.Bxb8!? Rxb8 4.dxc5 Qa5+ 5.Nc3 e6 6.e4! Bxc5 7.exd5 Nf6 8.Bb5+ Ke7 has been tried a few times about a century ago. Mason scored one of his greatest scalps, admittedly after a blunder by his opponent, with 9.Nf3 Nxd5 10.Qd2 Nxc3?? 11.Qg5+ f6 12.Qxc5+ Kf7 13.Be8+ , and Black resigned in J.Mason-M.Chigorin, New York 1889. Chigorin later played a World Championship match against Steinitz in 1892. Here 10...Bb4! gives Black a small plus after 11.Nxd5+ exd5 12.c3 Qxb5 13.cxb4 Re8 , while 11.Qg5+ Kf8 14.O-O is unclear. There are no recent examples in this line. Explorers might also wish to consider 9.Qd2 Bb4 10.Nge2 , aiming for a slight edge.

Or White could quietly play 3.c3 or 3.e3 .

3...dxe4

3...Qb6 4.Nc3 e6 is reasonable and also unexplored, but grabbing the 'poisoned pawn' with 4...Qxb2? 5.Nxd5 takes too many risks.

4.d5

4.dxc5 Qxd1+ 5.Kxd1 is deeply uninspiring, but should be about equal. Naturally there are many lines in which a player can press through for an edge with even the smallest of opportunities, but this does not seem to be one.

4...Nf6

4...e6 leads to a memorable trap, known from the Albin Counter-Gambit. After 5.Bb5+ Bd7 6.dxe6 Bxb5?? ( 6...fxe6 seems playable) 7.exf7+ Ke7 White wins with an under-promotion - 8.fxg8N+!! .

5.Nc3

There is no time to set up pawn barricades with 5.c4 .

5...a6

To cover any threats on b5.

Rudolf Spielmann, an exponent and theorist on the art of sacrifice in chess, found himself on the wrong end of an attack after 5...g6 6.Nb5 Na6 7.d6 Bg4 8.f3 exf3 9.gxf3 Bf5 10.Qd2 Qb6 11.a4 . White won quickly in B.Toupalik-R.Spielmann, Prague 1912. This was a simultaneous game, and Spielmann would presumably have been able to find improvements in proper tournament time limits.

6.Qe2

6.f3 exf3 7.Nxf3 b5 is pure gambit play, with the hope for White of being able to develop quickly and start an attack in return for giving up a pawn. Black's ...b5 and ...b4 sets up good countermeasures.

The text move, advocated by the Be1 gian Grandmaster, Luc Winants, forces Black to work harder for the extra pawn.

6...g6

Black returns the pawn, in the hope of a quick and safe equality with the possibility of playing for more.

6...e6 or 6...Bf5 may tend to be more speculative.

7.Nxe4

Material is level, and White may even entertain hopes of playing for an advantage after quick play.

7.O-O-O is also possible, and led to a transposition after 7...Bg7 8.Nxe4 Nxe4 9.Qxe4 O-O in the McShane-Illescas game cited later.

7...Nxe4

Avoiding the beginners' traps, 7...Qxd5?? 8.Nxf6+ and 7...Nxd5?? 8.Nd6+ , in either case with a winning pin on the e-file.

8.Qxe4 Bg7

Or 8...Bf5 , but there is no need to hurry with this. Ponomariov prefers to threaten the queen with ...Nd7 and ...Nf6.

9.O-O-O

If one were to take the view that this aggressive queenside castling creates too many chances of counterplay for Black, then 9.c3!? is worth considering.

9...O-O

It would be unwise for the king to stay for too long in the centre.

10.Ne2

10.Nf3 or 10.Be5 are other possibilities, maybe equal, maybe with a slight edge for White. One cannot assume just from the quick win for Black in the game that White is automatically worse.

10.Bc4!? provokes Black into attacking the bishop:

(a) However, after 10...b5 11.d6 Ra7 12.Bg5 Bf6 13.Bxf6 exf6 14.Bd5 Rd7 15.Qf4 Kg7 16.Bf3 White is better.

(b) 10...Nd7!? , suggested by Wells, seems a good response for Black.

(c) Instead, L.McShane-M.Illescas Cordoba, European Team Championship, Gothenburg 2005, continued 10...Bf5 11.Qf3 b5 , and now the strange and interesting 12.Bf1!? . White gives up two tempi with his bishop, but he has lured Black's bishop to f5, and will soon hit back with g4. After 12...Qa5 13.g4 Qxa2!? 14.gxf5 Qxb2+ 15.Kd2 , Black could have drawn by perpetual after 15...Qb4+ 16.Ke2 Qc4+ , but Illescas played for more with 15...Nd7 16.Ne2 Nb6 , and later lost after unclear and complicated play. Wells suggested 13...Bc8! , as Black too gives up two tempi with the bishop. Black's idea is to follow up with 14.a3 Bb7 , and White has problems on the long diagonal.

10...Nd7

10...Bf5 is also possible, again with complicated play.

11.Qe3

White is going to be hit with ...Nf6 anyway, and so he decides to move the queen out of the way first. The hope is that White can hit the c5-pawn.

Again, there are choices here. This line is still unexplored in what is an unclear position. Two other possibilities, to cut out counterplay from Black's light-squared bishop, might be 11.h3!? and 11.Ng3!? .

11...Nf6

11...Nb6?! sets up the trap, 12.Qxc5? Na4 , but this is hardly difficult for White to avoid. After 12.Nc3 Bd4 13.Qf3 Black's knight is misplaced.

Ponomariov decides it is time to gambit a pawn.

12.Nc3

12.Qxc5 Bf5 provides good attack ing lines against White's king. Eljanov decides not to take the bait.

12...b5!?

With some sharp gambit play. It is not necessarily the best move, but it is playable, and offers the chance of surprise. Maybe Eljanov reached the previous position, and tried to analyse it but probably he had not seriously considered Ponomariov's gambit idea. If White grabs the pawn, Black can take control of the c-file, and this is far from negligible.

There is a strong element of psychological pressure. Eljanov must clearly have decided beforehand that he wanted to mix the position up in the opening, trying to find unusual plans and sacrifices, and all that happens is that Ponomariov himself decided to take over the initiative through sharp counterplay.

The result of Ponomariov's enterprising play is that Eljanov soon looks disorientated, misses a not too difficult sacrifice, and loses in 22 moves. Ponomariov is generally a solid grinder in terms of chess, but like other top grandmasters, he can attack on occasion like the late Mikhail Tal.

What were the alternatives for Black? It would have been tempting to have tried 12...Bf5?! , with the idea that if 13.Qxc5?! Rc8 Black can sacrifice the c-pawn without the unnecessary ...b5. White sidesteps, though, this with the simple 13.h3! , aiming to push the bishop out with g4. Here 12...b6 is dull, allowing White a slight edge after 13.Be2 , but 12...Ng4!? deserves serious consideration. If then 13.Qxc5? b6 , and the queen is pushed away, and the knight will make a fork on f2: 14.Qc6 Bd7 does not help. Maybe best is 13.Qe1!? Bxc3 14.bxc3 with unclear play. This seems the best of the alternatives.

13.Qxc5

'Prove it!'

13.d6 is the main alternative, with play possibly fizzling out to equality after 13...Re8 14.dxe7 Qxe7 15.Qxe7 Rxe7 16.Rd8+ Re8 17.Rxe8+ Nxe8 . White can pick up a pawn instead with 14.Qxc5 exd6 15.Bxd6 (or 15.Rxd6 Qa5 16.Bd3 Be6 ) 15...Be6 , but Black has clearly the more active piece play, and it is difficult for White to force any simplification with exchanges. Neither is White likely to be able to push forward with his pawns: 16.Bc7 Qc8 17.Bb6 Qb7 18.Qc7 Qxc7 19.Bxc7 b4 would be too simplistic an option. Black has by far the superior development, and stands better after 19...b4 .

Earlier, Black could also consider 13...e5?! , but White can avoid complications after 14.Qxe5 , instead trying 14.Bg5! , cutting down Black's diagonals, and keeping White's advanced pawn.

13...Bf5

The natural reply. Once Black has played ...Rc8, the bishops will create pressure with the rook, with attacks on c2 and c3.

14.Qb4?!

Eljanov is hesitating, both on this move and the next. There are probably a dozen reasonable moves for White to consider, and it would be difficult to have the chance of considering more than a few of these to analyse over the board. Indeed, it would be difficult enough to find the best move at home with the computer. The wider the choice, the more difficult it is to find a convincing choice.

There are many ways in which White can shuffle around with the pieces, and Eljanov chooses one of them. A pawn push, to open up his own pieces, is another idea; 14.d6!? is at the very least worth considering:

(a) 14...Rc8 15.dxe7 Qxd1+ 16.Kxd1 Rxc5 17.exf8Q+ Bxf8 18.Be3 followed by Be3 gives Black some slight compensation for the sacrificed pawn, but not as much as he would like.

(b) 14...Nd7!? attempts to improve:

(b1) 15.dxe7 Nxc5 16.exd8Q Rfxd8 17.Nd5 Be4 gives Black compensation for the pawn, but more to hold the balance of play, rather than to try for an edge.

(b2) White could try instead 15.Qa3!? exd6 16.Bxd6 ( 16.Bd3 is about equal) 16...Rc8 17.Bxf8 Bxf8 18.Qxa6 b4 , but after the exchange sacrifice Black's position looks promising. One line is 19.g4 Bxg4 20.Rg1 Bxd1 21.Nxd1 b3!! 22.axb3 Ra8 23.Qd3 Bh6+ 24.Ne3 ( 24.Kb1 Qa5 gives checkmate) 24...Bxe3 25.fxe3 Qf6! , and this turns out to be a winning attack for Black with threats of ...Qf2 or ...Ra1+.

This is of course only scratching the surface, and if any strong grandmasters would be interested in testing this line, new ideas would undoubtedly emerge.

14...Rc8

An important half-open file.

15.Rd2?

Overly-defensive, although it is not easy to find good alternatives:

(a) White might want to try to kick out Black's light-squared diagonal, but if 15.Be2 Ne8 16.g4? , Black has 16...Bxg4 17.Bxg4 Rc4 .

(b) 15.h3 seems logical, but Black now has attacking chances after 15...Bxc2! 16.Kxc2 Nxd5 17.Qb3 e6 . If then 18.a3 Qf6 19.Bd2 Qxf2 20.Kb1 Nxc3+ 21.Bxc3 Bxc3 22.bxc3 Rfd8 , and Black has ample compensation for the sacrificed piece. Here 18.Bd2 is even better for Black after 18...b4 19.Bxa6 Rc5 20.Be3 Nxe3+ 21.fxe3 Qa5 .

(c) White needs to improve on this. 15.Be5! is best, blocking Black on the long dark squares, rather than trying to cover the light squares. If 15...Bh6+ 16.Kb1 Nd7 17.Bd4 , and White is comfortable, but 15...Qb6! is the most dangerous reply. Then there are a few tactics to beware:

(c1) If, for example, 16.Bd4? Nxd5! 17.Nxd5 Rxc2+ 18.Kb1 Rc4+ 19.Bd3 Rxd4 , and Black is winning.

(c2) Or 16.Qxe7? Qxf2 17.Bd3 Nxd5 18.Nxd5 Rxc2+ 19.Kb1 Rxb2+ 20.Bxb2 Qxb2 mate.

(c3) 16.Qd4 is natural, but after the queen exchange Black keeps a slight advantage: 16...Qxd4 17.Rxd4 ( 17.Bxd4? b4 18.Bxa6 Ra8! ) 17...Bh6+ 18.Kb1 Nd7 19.Bf4 Bg7 20.Rd2 Bxc3 21.bxc3 Rxc3 allows level material, while White's pawns have been damaged.

(c4) Perhaps the best try for White, but not easy to visualize, is 16.Rd2! Bh6 17.f4 Qe3 18.Kd1 Nd7 ( 18...Ng4? looks threatening, but 19.Re2! holds everything together) 19.Qd4 Qxd4 20.Bxd4 Bxf4 21.Rf2 with equality.

The mistake, it seems, is not so much the Rd1 move itself, but rather in playing it a move too early.

15...Ne8!

This is vicious, and very easy to underestimate. One expects players when attacking to move pieces forward, rather than backward. The knight is moving towards a significant defensive square on d6, but the more significant point is that Black's two bishops now have clear and effective diagonals, and work very well with the rook. The queen and knight are also ready to enter the attack, and there could be some dangerous prods with the queenside pawns. Black is better.

16.Bd3?

A blunder, but Eljanov was under pressure. Two slips in consecutive moves can turn a position from being comfortable to being in danger, but the third slip can often be quickly decisive.

16.Nd1? , which may have been his intended move, would allow Black to saw through the queenside after 16...Qxd5! 17.Rxd5 Rxc2+ 18.Kb1 Rxb2+ 19.Kc1 ( 19.Ka1 Rb1 mate) 19...Rc2+ 20.Kb1 Rc4+ .

White's best would be 16.Be2 , and then hope for the best. The computer might suggest that White is comfortable after, for example, 16...a5 17.Qxb5 Nd6 18.Bxd6 Qxd6 19.Nd1 Rc5 20.Qa4 Rb8 , but a good human player would find White's position extremely uncomfortable. Black can attack, while it is difficult for White to coordinate. For example, if 21.Bf3 Bh6 22.Ne3 Qe5 23.Qa3 Qc7 , and Black is pushing.

16...Bxd3

As simple as that. Black is now clearly winning.

17.cxd3

If 17.Rxd3 Rc4 , winning the bishop.

17...Qxd5

Pawns are now level, and the queen is forking the pawns on a2 and g2. If that is not enough, Black also has the more active pieces.

18.Kb1

18.f3 Qxa2 is horrible.

18...Qxg2 19.Qe4

This speeds things up. A decent Took move keeps the position just about alive, although Black should win.

19...Qxe4 20.dxe4

If 20.Nxe4 , then Black rolls through with 20...e5 followed by ...f5.

20...Nf6

Completing his development!

20...Bxc3 21.bxc3 Rxc3 22.Rd7 makes life more difficult.

21.Rhd1?

Dispirited, Eljanov falls for another trap. 21.f3 Nh5 22.Bg5 Bxc3 23.bxc3 f6 24.Bh6 Rfd8 might allow White to struggle to the time control.

21...Rxc3! 22.bxc3 Nxe4

0-1

Winning a second pawn in a simplified endgame.
