Game 12
E.Bareev-Z.Efimenko
Turin Olympiad 2006
Nimzo-Indian Defence E32

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3

For 3.Nf3 , see Game 4, Anand-Topalov, and subsequent games.

3...Bb4

The Nimzo-Indian.

4.Qc2

Alternatively, for 4.Nf3 , see Game 26, Wang Yue-Movsesian, and for 4.e3 , see Game 15, Sokolov-Polgar.

The Qc2 idea was the original 'classical' variation. White does not want doubled pawns on the c-file, and wants to play a3, while recapturing with the queen after the exchange on c3. It is all very logical, as White gains the bishop-pair, but it also stows down his development. The queen spends two moves, and will quite likely have to move again in view of a possible hit with ...Ne4.

4...O-O

Not the only move, but Black sorts out the kingside quickly.

5.a3

Carrying on with the plan.

5.e4 is the most obvious move, and indeed who would not be tempted by this? There is, however, the danger that with his aggressive pawn rush in the centre, White is also creating weaknesses on d4 or e4.

One possibility for Black is the restrained 5...d6 , and if 6.a3 Bxc3+ 7.bxc3 e5 followed by ...Nc6, then perhaps ...b6 and ...Ba6. If White were to try d5, his central pawn structure would now be rigid, and one possibility for Black is to set up a pawn break with ...c6, and with due timing, to open up the half-open c-file. White has to be careful to play actively. In I.Sokolov-V.Bologan, Sarajevo 2006, play continued 8.Bd3 Nc6 9.Ne2 b6 10.O-O Ba6 11.f4 Nd7 12.Rf3!? Na5 13.c5 Bxd3 14.Rxd3 with a likely edge for White, who later won.

The alternative for Black is immediate counterplay with 5...d5!? . An indication of the sharpness of play is given by V.Ivanchuk-D.Navara, Antalya 2004: 6.e5 Ne4 7.a3 Bxc3+ 8.bxc3 c5 9.Bd3 cxd4 10.cxd4 Nc6 11.Ne2 Qa5+ 12.Kf1 Nb4!? ( 12...f6 and 12...f5 have also been tried) 13.axb4 Qxa1 14.f3 f5 15.Qb1 Qa4 ( 15...Qxb1 16.Bxb1 leaves the knight trapped) 16.Ke1 a5! 17.fxe4 fxe4 18.Bc2 Qxb4+ 19.Qxb4 axb4 , and after 20.cxd5 exd5 21.Bb3 Be6 , Ivanchuk eventually had to struggle for a draw.

Golod suggests 20.Bb3!? dxc4 21.Bxc4 , assessing this as favourable to White. Black has rook and two pawns versus two minor pieces, normally a slight material edge if Black's pawns are well coordinated. Unfortunately Black also has two sets of isolated doubled pawns, which require careful guarding. Also, as Golod notes, White can make use of his own advanced pawns with 21...Ra1 22.d5! . This is one of several complicated lines in this opening. Both players will need to be well prepared in the main line after 5.e4 d5 .

5...Bxc3+

If you dislike giving away the bishop-pair, you should not play the Nimzo!

6.Qxc3 b6

The main line. Black quickly fianchettoes on the remaining long diagonal.

6...b5!? , popularized by Adorjan, looks like a complete finger-slip, but if White takes on the extra pawn, Black has annoying compensation with pressure on the light-coloured squares. A recent attempt to hold on to the extra pawn was A.Motylev-A.Naiditsch, German League 2006: 7.cxb5 c6 8.e3 cxb5 9.Bxb5 Ne4 10.Qb3 Ba6 11.Qa4 Qg5 12.Bf1! Bxf1 13.Kxf1 Nc6 14.f3 Nd6 15.Ne2 Rab8 16.Kf2 Qh4 17.g3 Qh3 18.Qd1 , and White was able to consolidate, and later won.

6...d6 is fully playable.

7.Bg5

White in return sets up an awkward pin. One of the drastic ways for Black is to play ...h6 and ...g5, but there is the danger of weakening squares in front of his king.

7.e3 Bb7 8.Nf3 is less aggressive. After 8...d5 White has forfeited his pressure with the bishop on the h4-d8 diagonal.

7...Bb7

For the moment he quietly develops.

The drastic 7...Nd5? 8.Bxd8 Nxc3 9.Bxc7 merely wins a pawn for White, but quite often both players need to watch this idea for several moves. If, for example, White plays an early Rd1, Black can attack the rook after ...Nxc3 in this sequence.

8.e3

For 8.f3 , see Game 13, Kramnik-Leko.

8.Nf3 d6 9.Nd2 is a possibility, but White has not made much impact with this variation.

8...d6

Black can flick in 8...h6 9.Bh4 , but there is no particular reason to do so. If anything, there is more counterplay for Black if the bishop is on g5, rather than the less exposed square on h4.

9.Ne2

Bareev's favoured line.

9.Nf3 is more common. The problem is that Black has good control of e4, and may even consider at some stage ...h6; Bh4 g5; Bg3 followed by ...Ne4. Sometimes White has played 8.Nf3 a move earlier, to give the chance of playing Nd2 quickly.

Bareev's idea is to swing his knight to c3 instead, covering e4. It takes time, not least because the queen has to move yet again, but Bareev could argue that if his pieces can consolidate, without Black being able to create counterplay in time, he will have chances of being better.

9...Nbd7

Completing his piece development. Now he has to decide what to do with the pawns.

10.Qc2

The less than obvious 10.Qd3!? has been tried several times, the queen being on a more aggressive square, but also being more likely to be hit after a pawn exchange in the centre. Bareev played this a couple of times against Karpov, in both cases offering a pawn sacrifice on g2.

E.Bareev-A.Karpov, Wijk aan Zee 2003, continued 10...h6 11.Bh4 c5 12.Nc3 Qe7 13.Rd1 Rfd8 14.Be2 cxd4 15.Qxd4 Nc5 16.Bxf6 Qxf6 17.Qxf6 gxf6 18.Nb5 d5 19.cxd5 Bxd5 20.Nc7 Bb3 21.Nxa8 Bxd1 22.Bxd1 Rxa8 23.Bc2 with a slight edge for White in a rook and minor piece endgame, but Karpov successfully held the draw.

There were a couple of opportunities for Black to snatch the g-pawn, with some degree of risk. For example, 14...Bxg2?! 15.Rg1 Bb7 16.e4 allows White definite attacking chances on the kingside. A move later, 15...Bxg2?! (instead of 15...Nc5 ) 16.Rg1 Bb7 allows the previous gambit line with 17.e4 , or White could simply recover the pawn with 17.Qxd6 with a slight edge.

Going back even earlier, in E.Bareev-A.Karpov, Cap d'Agde (rapid) 2002, White omitted Rd1, pressing on d6, and tried to sacrifice too early with 13.Be2?! cxd4 14.Qxd4 e5! (no Qxd6 attack for White) 15.Qd1 Bxg2 16.Rg1 Bc6 , and the pawn sacrifice was rather more speculative, although the game again finished as a draw. Quite clearly Bareev will have experimented at home with slightly different sacrifices on the g2-square, aiming for improvements.

10...c5

A natural reaction for Black, exchanging a bishop's pawn against a central pawn with the likelihood of gaining an extra pawn in the centre. Indeed, with Black's c-file being half-open, one can even wonder whether it was wise for White to place his queen on c2. Bareev has, of course, evaluated such positions well in advance, and demonstrates his plan.

11.Rd1

Immediately creating pressure on the d-file, a sensible reaction.

In V.Ivanchuk-V.Topalov, Linares 1999, White was in trouble, Black winning the c-pawn, after 11.Nc3?! cxd4 12.exd4 Qc7 13.Bd3 Rac8 14.O-O Ba6 . The game was eventually drawn, though, after long rearguard play.

11...Rc8

Continuing the main plan.

11...Qe7 is probably playable, but it is useful to place pressure on the opponent.

12.Nc3

A good square for the knight, but of course it is slow. White has gained the bishop-pair, but to set up the knight on c3, he has to make three moves with the queen (Qc2, Qxc3 and another queen move), an a3 pawn push, and an extra move with the knight (Ng1-e2-c3). Indeed, White has not even found time to castle, and soon has to take drastic measures.

12...cxd4

Not as automatic as it looks. Black has ideas with his queen, and so he disregards such moves as 12...Qe7 .

13.Rxd4

Improving on the older plan with 13.exd4 - compare with the Ivanchuk-Topalov game, above.

13...Rc5

A hit on the g5-bishop, and finally helping explain why Black did not particularly want to throw in an earlier ...h6; Bh4 insertion.

14.Bh4

White keeps his hard-won bishop.

14.Bxf6 Nxf6 15.Nb5 Qa8 16.Nxd6 Bxg2 is possible, but only equal.

14...Qa8

Famed as the 'Reti manoeuvre', startling almost a century ago, but standard now. What is White going to do on the long diagonal?

15.Be2!

White is prepared to sacrifice a pawn on g2, and will even, as the game shows, allow the pawn to drop after he has castled. White has lost time with his slow and methodical manoeuvring, and he does not want to lose any more.

15.e4 , setting up a Hedgehog structure, is the main alternative, but 15...d5! seems to equalize. After 16.b4 Rc7! (more accurate than 16...Rcc8 17.cxd5 exd5 18.Bb5 ) 17.Bxf6 Nxf6 18.e5 Nd7 19.Nb5 Rc6 20.f4 ( 20.Bd3 Nxe5! ) 20...f6 Black is at least equal. Here 17.Bg3 e5 18.Nxd5 Nxd5 19.exd5 exd4 20.Bxc7 Rc8 followed by ...Bxd5 is good for Black. Again, White's lack of development creates problems if the central squares are quickly opened.

15...d5

It is too early to snatch the pawn. After 15...Bxg2?! 16.Rg1 it is Black's king that is in danger. 16...Bb7 17.Rxd6 gives White an edge.

16.b4

Pushing Black's rook to a less effective square, but also weakening the squares on the c-file.

16.O-O dxc4 is equal, but Dreev looked for an improvement with 16.Qd1!? e5 17.Rd2 d4 18.exd4 exd4 19.Rxd4 Bxg2 20.Rg1 Bh3 21.Rd6 , A. Dreev-D.Jakovenko, Russian Championship, Moscow 2007, and an advantage to White, who has the more active pieces. This may well be an improvement, although Black may also consider 16...Re8!? .

Naturally Dreev will have played through this earlier game, seen the original idea as interesting, and looked for slight but significant modifications. This is how chess theory develops.

16...Rcc8

The natural move, but other retreats could be considered.

17.O-O

The only good move, but he has to be prepared to allow his opponent to take the long diagonal.

17...e5

17...dxc4 is to be considered, and helps explains why Dreev later chose the 16.Qd1 idea instead. Dreev would have two major pieces to cover the knight on d7. 18.Rfd1 attacks the knight, even so, and the position is perhaps best regarded as level, but tense. A quiet way of playing would be 18...Ne5 19.Bxf6 gxf6 . A more aggressive idea for Black is 18...Bxg2 19.e4 Bh3 20.Rxd7 Nxd7 21.Rxd7 e5 , and who plays better, wins. The computer suggests that Black is better, but this is probably usual the point that computers tend to overestimate a slight material advantage.

White instead tried 18.f3!? in R.Kasimdzhanov-A.Grischuk, Moscow 2007, and Grischuk won after 18...Ne5 19.Bxf6 gxf6 20.Nb5 Qb8 21.Nd6 , but this was a blitz game, and one should not make too many conclusions from the result. White was slightly better before he later lost.

18.Rd2

The only sensible retreat White needs to have both rooks on the d-file.

18...d4

Taking the long diagonal by force.

If 18...dxc4 19.Rfd1?! , Black has gained a useful tempo after 19...Bxg2 20.Rxd7 Nxd7 21.Rxd7 Bh3 , and the rook is already attacked. Black is better after 22.Rd5 f6 . However, 19.f3 would attempt to hold the balance after, for example, 19...a6 20.Rfd1 Bc6 21.Qf5 b5 22.Bxf6 Nxf6 23.Qxe5 . Black's passed pawn might look dangerous, but it is difficult to dislodge the knight from c3.

19.exd4

The only move. If 19.Nb5? , then simply 19...Bxg2 .

19...Bxg2

This looks dangerous! Black has now taken the long diagonal, and is threatening to threaten checkmate. But, to adapt a comment by Tarrasch a century ago, if you are worried about a threat of checkmate, you might just as well give up chess.

20.Rfd1

The rooks will now have a dangerous open file.

20...exd4

Otherwise White will block the diagonal with 20...Bh3 21.d5 . White would have an advanced protected passed pawn, and Black's minor pieces get in each others' way.

21.Rxd4

The end of the tactical skirmishes, and the players must work out what is going on, positionally.

The most obvious point is that Black has taken the long diagonal, with queen and bishop, and White's king is exposed through the loss of the g-pawn. Black is not, however, in complete control, and White's pieces are otherwise well placed.

Once it is clear that Black is not about to give checkmate, White has various plus points, which may tip the balance. White has excellent control with the rooks on the d-file, which, with the help of his bishop on h4, places a strain on Black's knights. Indeed, despite the mate threat on g2, White's minor pieces are overall much more imposing than Black's.

And the pawns? We shall see. Imagine several pieces being exchanged, though, and it is easy to work out that White's queenside pawns are much easier to convert for a passed pawn than Black's kingside pawns, despite the pawn isolation of the f- and h-pawns.

White is, in total, slightly better.

21...Bh3

If 21...Bh1 , then 22.Bf1 covers the g2-square, essential but also good. White has one weak square, but the rest of his piece structure is good. Here 22.f3 Bxf3 23.Rxd7 Nxd7 24.Rxd7 Bxe2 25.Qxe2 is far less clear.

21...Ne5 22.Bxf6 gxf6 23.Qf5 Nf3+ 24.Bxf3 Bxf3?? is a race for one of the players to check on the g-file, but White wins after 25.R1d3 . 24...Qxf3 25.Qxf3 Bxf3 26.R1d3 is better, but White still has a clear advantage, with the better pieces and a better pawn structure.

22.Ne4

Simple chess. White does not have to worry about complicated lines, such as 22.f3 followed by Kf2 and Rg1, when there are straightforward excellent winning chances by taking control of the centre, and aiming for a good endgame.

22...Nxe4

Maybe Efimenko is a little too eager to acquiesce for simplification.

22...Kh8!? 23.Qc3 Rce8 24.Qxh3 Rxe4 25.Bf3 Rxd4 26.Rxd4 Qc8 (with possible threats of ...Ne5) is not so good for White as at first appears. If Black can play ...Re8, with thoughts of protecting his knights on the e-file, he can take advantage of the weaknesses of the isolated pawns on the kingside, with the absence of the g-file making it difficult to cover the f-file. 24.f3!? instead is to be considered. After 24...Nxe4 25.fxe4 , the isolated pawn is slightly uncomfortable, given that Black can place the knight on e5, but White's overall piece structure is slightly better, and he has chances of making pressure with the extra queenside pawn.

23.Qxe4

Endgame coming up.

23...Qxe4

Simplest. 23...Ne5?! is too elaborate, and White gains time after 24.Qxa8 Rxa8 25.f4 .

24.Rxe4 Rfe8

Black does not want to give away control of both central files.

25.Rxe8+

White may not be worse after various alternatives, but this again is the simplest and most direct Black keeps the e-file, but White keeps the d-file, and with nurturing the bishop-pair will gradually slightly overrun the bishop and knight.

25...Rxe8

26.Rd2!

Maybe Efimenko had underestimated this quiet move? White's bishop remains on a good square, and although it is temporarily pinned, Black cannot attack the bishop. After f3, at an appropriate stage. White unravels his pieces with for example Kf2, and he will have the extra queenside passed pawn, well away from Black's king, and a useful and effective bishop-pair.

26...Re4

Black wants to stay active.

27.Bg3

White is still slightly better. Sooner or later, Black will experience pressure from the fragile queenside pawns.

27...g5

Playable, and indeed probably the best, but on his next move Efimenko seems to change his mind, and loses a tempo as a result.

27...f6 28.a4 Kf7 29.f3 Re3 30.Kf2 Ra3 31.Bd1 Ne5 saves a tempo for Black, but even here White still has winning chances after, for example, 32.Bxe5 fxe5 33.c5 bxc5 34.bxc5 Rc3 35.Rc2 Rxc2 36.Bxc2 Ke6 37.Be4 . White's plan of attack would be Ke3, then Bb7. If Black were to try ...Bf5, then Bc8+ would be a win for White. If ...Bf1, then White can push the king away from the critical square on e6 with Ke4 followed by Bc8+.

28.a4

If 28.f3 Re3 , and the rook can attack behind White's queenside pawns.

28...f6?

Changing his mind.

28...g4! is better and more consistent. Black can no longer move his bishop again, unless White gives a hand, but White's pieces are equally constrained. In the end, it is going to be more important for White, rather than Black, to unblock the kingside. 29.f3 gxf3 30.Bd3 Re8 31.Kf2 will probably end up with White having very slightly the better of a draw:

(a) 31...Bg4?! 32.c5! bxc5 33.Bb5 cxb4 34.Bxd7 Rd8 35.Rd5 Rxd7 36.Rb5+ leaves Black in trouble though.

(b) 31...Ne5 32.Bxe5 Rxe5 33.Kxf3 still requires Black to take care. If the rooks get exchanged, for example, White will probably win. The safest defensive option would seem to be 33...Be6 34.Re2 Rh5 35.Ke3 Rh4 , then Black centralizes the king. Play should end up as a draw.

29.Bc7

Now the bishop is activated.

29...Ne5

If Black were thinking of playing 29...g4?! , he has left it too late: 30.f3 gxf3 31.Bxf3 Rxc4?? 32.Bd5+ , and White wins a rook.

30.f3

White must deal with the ...Nf3+ threat, but he wanted to play f3 anyway.

30...Re3

Aiming for a counterattack.

30...Rf4 31.Kf2 ( 31.Bxe5 fxe5 draws) 31...Kf7 32.Rd8 allows White to break through on the kingside: for example, 32...Nc6 33.Ra8 Rd4 34.b5 Rd7 35.Bxb6 axb6 36.bxc6 Rc7 37.Ra6 Rxc6 38.a5 .

31.Kf2

King to the centre. If in doubt, this is often not a bad idea in the endgame.

31...Rb3

Black would like to force the b-pawn to advance.

31...Ra3 32.a5 bxa5 33.Bxa5 Nc6 34.Rd6 Nxa5 35.Ra6! gives White a substantial edge.

32.b5

The best chance of playing for a win. 32.Bxe5 fxe5 33.Rd8+ Kg7 34.Ra8 Rxb4 35.Rxa7+ Kf6 36.Rxh7 Be6 37.Rb7 Bxc4 38.Bxc4 Rxc4 39.Rxb6+ Kf5 40.a5 Ra4 allows Black to hold, even though a pawn down. For example, 41.a6 Ra2+ 42.Kg3 Ra3 43.h3 e4 44.Rb5+ Kf6 45.Kg4 exf3 46.Rf5+ Kg6 47.Rxg5+ Kh6 , draw, or 41.Ra6 e4 42.fxe4+ Kxe4 43.Kg3 Kf5 44.Ra8 Ra3+ 45.Kf2 Ra2+ 46.Ke3 (otherwise, if Black wants to keep the pawn, he has to allow a perpetual) 46...Rxh2 47.a6 Ra2 48.a7 Kg4 , again with a draw.

32...Rb4

32...Be6 33.Rd8+ Kf7 34.Bxe5 fxe5 35.Ra8 should win for White.

33.Rd8+

A necessary part of White's plan of attack.

33.a5?! Nxc4 34.Rd4 Be6 35.a6? ( 35.axb6 draws) 35...Rb2 36.Bb8 Na3! turns out to be good for Black.

33...Kf7

Black prefers to bring the king to the centre. Maybe there is some argument for trying 33...Kg7 , to avoid any bishop checks on the a2-g8 diagonal, but this is hardly of any great relevance, as the main checking line will be after a later Rxa7+.

34.Ra8

Behind the pawns.

34...Nxc4

This ends up as a win for White.

34...Rxa4!? leads to some interesting play, but does not quite seem to hold The natural 35.Bxb6 would lead to a quick win for White after 35...Nxc4? 36.Rxa7+ Rxa7 37.Bxc4+! , picking up a piece after a zwischenzug. However, Black can try for startling cheapo sacrifices with 35...Be6 36.c5 Nxf3!? 37.Bxf3 ( 37.Kxf3 Bd5+ ) 37...Ra2+ , which might occasionally work just before the time control. Then, not immediately obviously, 38.Kg3! is best, keeping the extra piece. If 38...g4?! , then simply 39.Bxg4 , and the king protects the bishop.

35.Rxa7

With threats of discovered checks, cutting down Black's defensive options.

35...Ke6

35...Bd7 36.Bxc4+ Rxc4 37.Bxb6 will eventually win for White, maybe with a later b6 advance, ignoring the a4-pawn.

36.Bxc4+

White is not too bothered about the supposed drawing effects of the bishops of opposite colours when he has a forced win by aiming to queen a pawn. Black's bishop on h3 is out of place when compared with White's a- and b-pawns.

36...Rxc4 37.a5

Aiming for a touchdown. 37.Bxb6 is possible, but fiddly. Bareev wants to keep the pawn on the board, so he can threaten to attack with axb6.

37...Rc2+

37...bxa5 38.b6 Rb4 39.b7 queens with the other pawn.

38.Ke3

Maybe 38.Ke1 is fractionally more accurate, but both moves win. Usually there is no time to think on move 38. If you see a reasonable move, play it.

38...Bf1

38...f5 39.Kd3 Rf2 40.axb6 Rxf3+ 41.Kd2 Bg2 42.b7 Rf2+ 43.Ke1 Rf1+ 44.Ke2 wins for White.

39.axb6

39...f5

A more delicate mating threat is 39...Kd5!? ( 39...Bxb5 will transpose) 40.b7 Bxb5 (there are no good discovered checks after 40...Re2+ 41.Kd3 ) 41.f4 ( 41.b8Q?? Re2 mate) 41...g4 42.f5! , and the king escapes and the pawn queens.

40.b7

Winning, but there is one last trick to try.

40...f4+

A last-breath attempt. Naturally,

40...Rxc7 41.b8Q f4+ 42.Kd2 wins easily for White.

41.Bxf4

Avoiding 41.Ke4?? (or 41.Kd4?? ) 41...Rc4 mate.

41...gxf4+ 42.Kxf4 1-0

White queens a pawn.
