Game 5
E.Bacrot-S.Rublevsky
Khanty-Mansiysk 2005
Sicilian Defence B46

1.e4 c5

The first example of a Sicilian. Black covers the d4-square with a pawn away from the centre. The idea that if White later plays d4, then an exchange of pawns will give Black theoretically the better pawn structure, with an extra pawn in the centre. White in return has quicker development with his pieces, and more squares for his pieces.

Play can often become extremely complicated, as we shall see in later games. The usual rule of thumb is that White scores well in short games, with the help of rapid attacking chances, while Black tends to do well in the long positional games, once he has covered White's attack, then making use of his better pawn structure. There are, of course, many exceptions.

2.Nf3

White generally plays for an Open Sicilian, with Nf3 followed by Nxd4, at least at top levels. The problem for White, at anything below professional level, is that Black has so many different possibilities, often ending up in vastly different, yet still complicated opening structures, that it takes enormous time and effort for White to understand in detail what is going on in the Sicilian.

A quieter way of playing the Sicilian is 2.c3 , keeping a pawn in the centre with d4. At the moment, Tiviakov is the main advocate of this at top level. Play tends to end up with a high proportion of draws, which can be frustrating for Black if he is aiming for a win.

2...e6

There are various possibilities, often involving inter-transpositions later on.

2...d6 can lead to the late Bobby Fischer's favourite, the Najdorf Sicilian after 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 . See Game 6, Karjakin-Anand, an outstanding win for Anand. Depending on how White plays, Black may consider either ...e5, as in Anand's game, or ...e6. Here 5...g6 , with a bishop fianchetto, is a popular line, although not included in this book. This is the Dragon Variation. Black has a splendid diagonal for his bishop, but he has lost some pawn control in the centre.

2...Nc6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 e5 6.Ndb5 d6 would have looked extremely anti-positional a couple of generations earlier, leaving the hole on d5, a weakness, and also the backward pawn on d6. The Pelikan Variation is popular, though, and Black argues that if White's knight reaches b5, then no problem. Black can force it away with ...a6. Difficult play follows for both sides, as in Game 9, Leko-Radjabov.

2...e6 has the merit of avoiding the most complicated attacking lines, while still keeping positional tension.

3.d4

3.c3 would still return to the quiet 2.c3 line, and indeed this is Rublevsky's favoured line for White, including a good win against Topalov in 2004.

3...cxd4 4.Nxd4

The natural replies on each side.

4...Nc6

4...a6 could easily transpose into the game after 5.Nc3 Nc6 , but Black could also consider 5...b5 , or 5...Qc7 , and White could consider, a move earlier, 5.Bd3 or 5.c4 .

4...Nf6 5.Nc3 d6 leads to the Scheveningen, a trusted defensive measure by Kasparov over many years.

5.Nc3

5.Nb5 d6 6.c4 (or maybe 6.Bf4 e5 7.Be3 ) is also possible, but tends to be drawish.

5.Nxc6 is too early. White should at least wait until Black has lost a move with ...a6.

Similarly, White would want to delay c4 until Black has played either ...a6 or ...d6. Just here, it is not worth playing cat-and-mouse. Simple development has its virtues.

5...a6

With designs of a Paulsen Variation, with an early ...Qc7, or perhaps a Scheveningen, setting up the barricades with ...e6 and ...d6.

Black does not activate his development with ...a6, but it is a good waiting move, forcing White to decide what he is going to do next In most lines, the ...a6 move is in any case useful.

For 5...Qc7 , see Game 20, Ni Hua-Jakovenko, which leads to a more classic Paulsen/Scheveningen structure.

6.Nxc6!?

White deviates, and so finds the one way in which ...a6 is something of an irrelevance. White is hoping for a slight plus, but the exchange of knights suggests that he has no real ambitions of a forcing attack.

6.Be2 , as in Game 17, Shirov-Illescas, leads to a Scheveningen structure, with the twist that Black then delays ...Be7, and unusual complications result.

6.Be3 and 6.g3 are alternatives.

6...bxc6

6...dxc6?! 7.Qxd8+ Kxd8 8.Be3 gives White an edge. Black's problem is not so much that the king has been moved, usually only a slight irritation when the queens have been exchanged, but rather that he has weakened his dark squares, as a result of his early ...a6.

7.Bd3

Quiet development.

7.e5 Qc7 8.f4 is playable, but is perhaps premature. After 8...d5 9.exd6 Bxd6 , White has induced a slight structural weakness on Black's isolated a- and c- pawns, but White also has weaknesses on the kingside and in the centre.

7...d5

Black could also set up a more defensive formation with 7...d6 8.O-O Nf6 .

8.O-O

8.exd5?! cxd5 allows Black too easy equality. White's pawn on e4 is more effective in the centre than Black's pawn on c6, so why should White exchange?

8...Nf6

Natural development.

Black has occasionally experimented with 8...Bd6 9.Re1 Ne7 , but White has chances of an edge, and good kingside freedom, after 10.Qh5 .

9.Re1

Still keeping the light-square tension in the centre.

9.Qe2 Be7 , and then maybe 10.b3 , is an alternative.

9...Be7

9...d4 10.Nb1! e5 11.Nd2 weakens any chance of influence by Black on c4.

9...Bb7 is also possible, with the point being that if 10.e5 Nd7 . Black still has the g7-square guarded by the bishop, so that 11.Qg4?! is pointless. The strongest grandmasters tend to prefer instead 11.Bf4 .

10.e5

Now it is time for White to take over the initiative.

10...Nd7

The only sensible square.

11.Qg4

11.Na4 Nb6 12.Nxb6 Qxb6 13.c4 has been tried a couple of times recently, and arguably White's queen might be better centralized than on g4. However, Black can play more directly with 11...O-O! 12.c4 a5 (aiming for ...Ba6) 13.Qc2 g6 14.Bh6 Re8 , and in comparison with the Bacrot-Rublevsky game, the king and the rook are at least the right way round. In V.Bologan-S.Rublevsky, Poikovsky 2006, Black set up a favourable bishop exchange after 15.cxd5 cxd5 16.Bb5 Rb8 17.Qe2 Bg5! 18.Bxg5 Qxg5 , which was equal, with Black later winning.

11...g6

Black cannot castle safely, as if 11...O-O? 12.Bh6 g6 13.Bxf8 , and White wins the exchange.

This is of course an elementary tactic, but sometimes such small points may influence the positional outcome of a whole game.

12.Na4

At the moment, as a result of Bacrot's win in this game, this knight move has become the centre of attention among top-level players. At first sight, it looks strange. The knight is stuck on the edge, and cannot attack anything. It covers defensive squares on b6 and c5 though, and arguably Black would like to use those squares himself. Perhaps more importantly, White is opening up a nibble with c4, starting to undermine Black's central pawn structure.

In slightly earlier games, White tried 12.Bh6 . J.Polgar-V.Anand, Sofia 2005, continued 12...Rb8 13.Qh3 Rb4 14.Bg7 Rg8!? (sacrificing the exchange; 14...Rh4 has previously been tried) 15.Qxh7 Rxg7 16.Qxg7 Bf8 17.Qg8 Qg5! , leaving White's queen in great peril. Polgar baled out, trying for a draw with 18.g3 Nxe5 19.f4 Nf3+ 20.Kf2 Qh5 21.Bxg6 fxg6 22.Rxe6+ Bxe6 23.Qxe6+ Be7 24.Qxc6+ Kf8 25.Qa8+ Kg7 26.Qxd5 Nxh2 27.Qxh5 gxh5 . Black maybe had an edge, but it was later drawn. Some sharp tactics.

12...Qa5

12...c5 is possible, but weakens his pawn structure. 13.c4 O-O (or 13...Qa5 14.Qd1! and Black too will have to retreat his queen before long) 14.Bh6 Re8 15.Rad1 Rb8 16.b3 d4 17.f4 Qc7 18.h4 , and White has good chances on the kingside. White later won in G.Sax-G.Franzoni, Thessaloniki 1984. Black has a visually impressive passed pawn on d4, but he has not got much purchase on the other dark squares.

13.Bh6

Obviously the bishop was going to have to move, as White's rook on e1 was under attack. Clearly the bishop is also an irritation on Black's kingside, stopping Black from castling that side. What is slightly unusual is that more than 20 moves later, Black's rook found itself unable to enter into open play, and his king unable to castle. The bishop proved to be more than an irritation, it strategically dominated the whole game.

In an earlier game, Black soon equalized and later drew after 13.c3 c5 14.Bc2 Bb7 15.Bh6 Bc6 , A.Eismont-O.Romanishin, Biel 1995.

13...Qb4

Clearly Eismont wanted to avoid this queen exchange, but Bacrot was able to show that White is able to attack Black's various slight weaknesses without needing the queens.

13...Rb8 14.b3 Rb4 15.Qe2 went seriously wrong for Black in J.Benjamin-L.Piasetsky, Thessaloniki 1988. It is as if he had forgotten the white bishop can return to d2. Black sacrificed the exchange with 15...Rxa4 16.bxa4 Bb4 17.Red1 Bc3 , but it was clear this would be insufficient after 18.Bd2 .

13...c5!? is an attempt at counterattack, rather than merely holding the position. After 14.b3 c4 15.Bf1 Bb7 , it would seem that White is under pressure. In M.Carlsen-S.Mamedyarov, Moscow 2006, Carlsen had to throw everything into his attack on f7 with 16.Re3 Bc6 17.Qf4 Bxa4 18.Rf3 .

Here there are plenty of dangerous tactics, mainly favouring White, after 18...f6 (or 18...f5 19.exf6 , transposing) 19.exf6 Bxf6 20.Qd6! :

(a) 20...O-O-O 21.Rxf6 Nxf6 22.Qxe6+ will give excellent compensation, and an attack, for the exchange.

(b) After 20...Bxa1 21.Bg5 Black cannot castle kingside, but then 21...Bf6 22.Rxf6? would allow Black to castle safely after 22...O-O-O! .

(c) Another option for Black is 20...Be5 21.Qxe6+ Kd8 22.bxa4 Bxa1 23.Bg5+ , and while it is easy to see that White, a rook down, has perpetual, the question is whether he can play for more:

(c1) 23...Kc7 24.Bf4+ Kd8 25.Qc6! proves to be impossible for Black to hold After 25...Rc8 26.Bg5+ Bf6 27.Bxf6+ Nxf6 28.Qxf6+ Kc7 29.Qe5+ White wins.

(c2) 23...Kc8 24.Qc6+ Kb8 25.Qxd7 Be5 26.Rf7 Bc7 27.Bf4 Ra7 28.g3!? leads to good zugzwang possibilities for White.

Mamedyarov avoided these extremely dangerous tactics, showing that sharp attackers are also sharp defenders. Play was still on the edge after 18...O-O-O 19.Qxf7 Rhe8 20.bxa4 Bc5 ( 20...Bf8 21.Qxh7 Re7 22.Rf7! , and White is pressing) 21.Rb1 Qxa4 22.Bg5 Qc6 23.Bxd8 Nxe5 24.Rb8+! Kxb8 25.Bc7+ Qxc7 26.Qxe8+ Qc8 27.Qxc8+ Kxc8 28.Rh3 Ng4 29.Rf3 Ne5 30.Rh3 Ng4 31.Rf3 , and drawn. All very complicated, and one can be sure that there are hidden resources for both sides.

14.Qxb4

The simplest. There is no need to allow doubled pawns with 14.h3 Qxg4 15.hxg4 .

14...Bxb4 15.c3

Again the simplest, with gain of tempo, although possibly White might be worried that the knight is unable to run away if it were to be attacked (rather than exchanged). Black cannot get there though, and White's pieces and pawns are safe.

15...Ba5

Black aims to keep some pressure on the pawn on e5.

Black has also tried simplifying, aiming to getting rid of the annoying bishop with 15...Bf8 16.Bxf8 Rxf8 in M.Carlsen-G.Vescovi, Wijk aan Zee 2006, but remains under pressure. After 17.c4 Ke7 18.cxd5 cxd5 19.Rac1 Ra7 20.b4 White's pieces are clearly more active. In terms of pawns, Black has a passed d-pawn, certainly, but this is no serious advantage, especially if White can blockade with king or, as happened, knight on d4. In compensation for the passed d-pawn, White has an extra outside pawn on the queenside, and Black is stuck in the game with a weakened isolated pawn. Also, White was able to create pressure on the kingside. Carlsen duly won.

15...Be7 does not quite seem to equalize. After 16.b4! a5 17.a3 Ba6 18.Bc2 White keeps pressure with his pieces.

16.b4

Mainly to prevent Black from gaining space with ...c5.

16...Bc7 17.f4

Covering the advanced pawn.

17.Bc2!? is also to be considered, so that if 17...Bxe5? 18.Rxe5 Nxe5 19.Bg7 White gains material. The tactic is obvious, but the positional point is that if White can avoid or delay the f4 push, he keeps the option of swinging the bishop from h6 to the queenside. Here 17...a5 18.a3 Nb6 gives White a slight edge, but nothing special.

17...a5

Somehow Black must loosen his pieces.

18.b5

18.a3 is still possible, but White is aiming for a more active breakthrough.

18...Nb6?!

This was at one stage seen as a drawing line, but Bacrot can play for an edge.

18...c5! seems better. Then after 19.Rab1 c4 20.Bc2 Rb8 White has a good passed pawn. It is, however, difficult to see how White could improve the rest of his pieces or pawns, with the three minor pieces remaining ineffective. After 21...Bd8 and a few quiet moves, Black should be able to consolidate for equality quite comfortably.

19.Nxb6!

An innovation, strangely enough. Sometimes a new move is played in a blaze of glory, but sometimes, as here, it is just the obvious follow-up, but a new interpretation to the follow-up.

In L.Hazai-O.Romanishin, Sochi 1982, the players agreed a draw by repetition after 19.Nc5 Nd7 . Unexciting, but as David Bronstein used to say, twenty years later players will find improvements. Or five years, or ten years, or a day.

19...Bxb6+ 20.Kf1

White has lost a tempo as a result of the check, but does that really matter? The king is moving towards a more centralized square, which is going to be a help for the endgame. In the meantime, Black has to prove that he genuinely has equality. In the subsequent games that resulted, it was clear that there were unexpected difficulties.

20...cxb5?!

Black is hoping for some simplifica tion, but it does not quite work out. White gains some time.

Ftacnik suggested 20...c5 21.c4 Bb7 22.Rad1 a4 23.a3 Rd8 24.Bg5 Rd7 25.Ke2 with a slight edge for White. This has not resolved Black's basic problem, that he has not been able to castle kingside, and his rooks therefore remain unconnected. Here the obvious solution is to castle queenside with 22...O-O-O!? . Black keeps the tension of whether to exchange pawns on c4, whether to create a passed pawn with ...d4, or whether to delay until the time is right. In view of this, maybe White could put the other rook on the d-file with 22.Red1!? , placing rooks on the c-file and the d-file, keeping some tension himself.

21.Bxb5+ Bd7

21...Kd8 22.c4 keeps an edge for White.

22.Rab1

Is this really the sort of position that a player would want to try as Black a second time? Rublevsky wanted to prove that his defence was sound.

22...Rb8

Rublevsky questioned this move in his annotations in Infomtator, and suggested that 22...Bxb5+ 23.Rxb5 Bc7! led to equality. Unfortunately his analysis was not quite watertight, and Ponomariov, an excellent grinder in the endgame, was able to show that White still had chances of playing for an advantage.

Rublevsky's analysis continued with 24.c4 (he gives 24.Rd1 Kd7 25.c4 Kc6 26.a4 dxc4 27.Rd4 Rhb8 28.Rxc4+ Kd7 as equal) 24...dxc4 25.Rc5 O-O-O 26.Rxc4 Rd7 27.Bg5 Kb7 , and now 28.Bf6 Rb8 with level play. It is not so clear what White has gained with his Bf6 move, which merely forces the rook to a better defensive square. Ponomariov no doubt had the opportunity in his preparation to sort out weaknesses in his opponent's analysis, even as late as move 28, and found a possible improvement. In R.Ponomariov-S.Rublevsky, Poikovsky 2006, play went on with 28.Rb1+ Bb6 29.f5!? (Ftacnik suggests that the quieter 29.Ke2! was even better) 29...Ka7? 30.Rc6! and White was winning material with a decisive attack, only three moves away from Rublevsky's analysis.

Ftacnik points out that Black has good chances of holding after 29...Rc7! 30.Rd4 Rb8 31.Rd6 Rc6 32.Be3 Kc7 33.fxe6 fxe6 34.Ke2 Ba7 , the point for the defence being that after 35.Rxc6+ Kxc6 36.Rc1+ Kb7 37.Bxa7 Kxa7 38.Rc7+ Rb7 39.Rxb7+ Kxb7 the king and pawn endgame is only a draw. Black oscillates with the king between b6 and c6, and the h-pawn can play ...h6 to cover any kingside weaknesses.

Unfortunately, not Rublevsky's finest day.

23.c4

Aiming to exchange White's isolated pawn for Black's best pawn.

23...Ba7

If 23...dxc4 24.Bxc4 Black is close to zugzwang:

(a) His position does not fold immediately, but after, for example, 24...Bc7 25.Rxb8+ Bxb8 26.Rb1 Bc7 27.Bg7 Rg8 28.Bf6 White has a long and leisurely squeeze.

(b) Rublevsky gives 24...Ba4 25.Re4!? as a possibility, with the idea that if 25...Bc2 26.Rd4 Bxb1 27.Bb5+ , and checkmate after 27...Ke7 28.Rd7+ Ke8 29.Ra7+ Kd8 30.Bg5+ Kc8 31.Bd7 . Here Black would be able to force the opponent to work much harder with 25...Kd7 26.Bxe6+ Kxe6 27.Rxa4 Rhc8 when White has won a pawn, but Black's pieces are by now the more active.

However, 23...d4? loses a pawn without compensation after 24.Ba6 Bc6 25.c5 Ba7 26.Rxb8+ Bxb8 27.Rd1 .

24.Ba6

Quite a symmetry of the White bishops, on a6 and h6. Of course, they threaten nothing, but they cut out important defensive squares. Even now, Black cannot castle.

24.Rec1?! allows Black to escape for equality with 24...Bxb5 25.cxb5 Kd7 26.a4 ( 26.Rc6 Rhc8! ) 26...Rhc8 27.Ke2 Bc5 , as Rublevsky notes. An advanced passed pawn is useful, but if the pawn cannot queen, and the other forces give nothing, the end result is a likely draw.

24...Bc6

Rublevsky gives 24...dxc4? 25.Rxb8+ Bxb8 26.Rb1 Bc7 27.Rb7 Bd8 28.Bxc4 as a zugzwang for White. Perhaps this is not quite clear just yet, as after 28...Bc6 29.Rb8?! Kd7 Black will be able to disentangle the back row. However, a quieter approach is 29.Ra7 a4 30.g3 Be7 31.Rc7 Bd7 32.Bg7 Rg8 33.Bf6 Bxf6 34.exf6 Kd8 35.Ra7 with a winning bind.

25.cxd5

This is the sort of position where it is easy enough to say that White is slightly better, but very difficult to find a clear way of improving his position. Given the chance of ...Kd7, Black would have excellent chances of consolidating for equality. After, for example, 25.Rxb8+ Bxb8 26.Bg7 Rg8 27.Bf6 Kd7 28.Rb1 Bc7 29.cxd5 exd5 30.Bh4 Rb8 31.Rxb8 Bxb8 32.g4 White's edge is slight. White can, of course, attempt to play for a win, as he has a slightly better pawn structure.

It is equally understandable that Bacrot would wish for more, and he finds an inspired pawn breakthrough. The trouble is that the breakthrough weakens his own pawn structure, as well as his opponent's. Black has to play very carefully, avoiding a couple of traps, to hold the position.

Instead 25.f5 gxf5 26.Be3 Rxb1 27.Rxb1 Kd7 ( 27...Bxe3? 28.Rb8+ ) 28.Bxa7 Ra8 29.cxd5 exd5 ( 29...Bxd5?? 30.Bb5+ ) 30.Bd3 transposes into the notes to Black's 27th, below, where Black has chances of holding.

25...exd5

25...Bxd5? 26.Rxb8+ Bxb8 27.Rc1! cuts through on the back rank. If 27...Kd7 28.Bb5+ , the bishops help as well.

26.f5!?

Given an exclamation mark elsewhere, but while it seems good it is not the most accurate.

Instead 26.Rxb8+ Bxb8 27.Rc1! Kd7 28.Be2!! is a highly subtle try, with zugzwang ideas.

If 28...Bc7?? or 28...Rc8?? , then White has immediate wins with 29.Bg4+ (and, for beginners, don't forget the en passant after 29...f5 30.exf6+ ).

Tactically this is elementary, but positionally this is significant, as it eliminates two of Black's best chances of bringing his pieces into play.

28...Ba7 activates the bishop, but can no longer put pressure on the e5-pawn, so White has 29.f5! . If 29...Re8 , White has a pawn breakthrough close to the queening square with 30.e6+ fxe6 31.f6 , winning. Alternatively, Black has 29...gxf5 30.Bd3 , and we now reach the winning line in the game, but bypassing an improvement for Black on move 27.

Black could also have tried developing the rook with 28...Re8 , but even here the rook hits a mine. After 29.Rc5! a4 ( 29...Bc7? 30.Bg4+ ) 31.Bg4+ Kc7 32.Bd1 White wins at least a pawn, as if 32...Kb6 33.Rxc6+ Kxc6 34.Ba4+ , skewering the rook.

A blemish for White, perhaps, but it is difficult to imagine how a player would be able to calculate over the board why 26.f5 is less accurate than 26.Rxb8+ .

26...Rxb1

If 26...gxf5 27.Be3! Rxb1 28.Rxb1 Bxe3 29.Rb8+ , winning the exchange. A simple but attractive combination, using the whole range of the board.

27.Rxb1

27...Kd7?

Black has at long last brought his king into play, no doubt with some relief, but the timing still seems to be incorrect.

Rublevsky suggests 27...gxf5 28.Be3 Kd7! ( 28...Bxe3? 29.Rb8+ ) 29.Bxa7 Ra8 30.Bd3 Rxa7 31.Bxf5+ Ke7 32.Rb6 ( 32.Bxh7 Ke6 33.Re1 Rb7 is level) 32...Rc7 33.Ke2 with an edge to White. However, Ribli gives it as a substantial edge. Black's pawns are all isolated, and White's pieces are active, with the king threatening to end up on d4.

Can we have any clear indication that White is winning, or is close to winning? Or can we show that Black's position is tenable? Quieter moves for Black tend to lead to an uncomfortable defence, but 33...f6!? is more challenging:

(a) If 34.exf6+ Kxf6 35.Bxh7 Rxh7 36.Rxc6+ Ke5 37.h3 Rb7! , and Black has excellent counterplay in return for White's extra pawn. White will need to cover Black's advanced passed d-pawn, and so he will not have any opportunity of pushing his g- and h-pawns. In rook and pawn endgames, active pieces make all the difference.

(b) 34.e6 looks dangerous, but with accurate play by Black, it is difficult for White to set up a check on the seventh rank. Then 34...d4! brings the bishop into play. If 35.Kd3 Bxg2 36.Kxd4 Bf1! , and Black can use the c4-square with rook or bishop (after 37.Bxh7 Bc4 ), just about holding the balance, and 35.g4 Bd5 36.Kd3 ( 36.a3? Rc3 with strong counterplay) 36...Bxa2 37.Kxd4 a4 also holds the balance.

This is the deep defensive resource that White could have avoided by playing 26.Rxb8+ Bxb8 27.Rc1! Kd7 28.Be2! Ba7 29.f5 earlier, instead of 26.f5 .

28.Rc1!

And this is an easily missed quiet rook move, the tactics shifting from the b-file to the c-file. White is now thinking of threatening e6+, and almost any active Black move makes things worse. 28.e6+ fxe6 29.f6 e5 30.f7 Bc5 would have been more aggressive, but offers no clear way of taking advantage of the seventh rank pawn. 31.Bb5 Bxb5 32.Rxb5 Bf8 33.Bxf8 33.Rb8? Ke7 33...Rxf8 34.Rxd5+ Ke6 35.Rxa5 Rxf7+ is, for example, a draw.

28...gxf5

Black makes the most straightforward reply.

28...Ba4 29.Bc8+! ( 29.e6+ fxe6 30.f6 Bb8! ) 29...Rxc8 30.e6+ fxe6 31.fxe6+ Kxe6 32.Rxc8 , as Rublevsky notes, favours White, possibly decisively.

29.Bd3

We have seen this position before. See the notes to move 26.

Even without White's advanced f-pawn, he still has generated considerable attacking chances, with just rook and two bishops versus Black's more passive rook and two bishops. Black has not yet fully completed his development, even though we are at the early stages of the endgame. Had the rook been on a better square, he would have been able to equalize comfortably, but just at the moment he lacks the coordination of his pieces.

29...Re8

Alternatively:

(a) If 29...Ba4? , White has a mating attack after 30.Bxf5 Ke7 31.Bg5+ Kf8 32.Rc8+ Be8 33.Bf6 .

(b) If 29...Bd4? 30.Bg7! , then 31.e6+ , and the underdevelopment of the rook again causes a problem.

(c) 29...Rb8 30.Bxf5+ Kc7 31.e6! followed by Bf4+ picks up the rook on another diagonal.

(d) 29...Bb6 avoids immediate trouble, but after 30.Bf4 Bb7 31.Bxf5+ Ke8 , Black has still been unable to bring the rook into play.

Rublevsky decides to try to complete his development.

30.Bf4

30.Bxf5+ Kc7 31.Bf4 Kb7 32.Rb1+ Ka8 33.Bxh7 is no doubt also strong, but Bacrot prefers to keep a hit against the king in the centre.

30...Bd4

In their notes, Ribli and Rublevsky both suggest that 30...Re7 31.Bxf5+ Kc7 32.e6+ Kb7 33.Rb1+ Ka8 34.Bd6 Rxe6 35.Bxe6 fxe6 36.Ke2 gives more resistance, but it is difficult to see any chances for Black to hold White's 2-1 kingside pawn structure seem decisive, not least because of the danger that Black could lose his final h-pawn.

31.Bxf5+

This wins even more comfortably.

31...Kc7

Otherwise he loses material.

32.e6+

The pawn is pushed. Had this not been close to the time scramble, when sometimes strange accidents happen, Black would probably have resigned shortly.

32...Kb6 33.Rb1+!

Bacrot is unlikely to fall for 33.exf7?? Bb5+ when Black wins. Others might.

33...Kc5

33...Bb5+ 34.Bd3 wins for White. Black's bishop is pinned.

34.exf7 Rf8 35.Rc1+ Kb6 36.Be6

He safely keeps the pawn on f7, and Black has no more than a couple of inconclusive checks.

36...Bc5 37.Bh6 Bb5+ 38.Ke1 Bb4+ 39.Kf2 Rd8 40.Rc8 1-0
