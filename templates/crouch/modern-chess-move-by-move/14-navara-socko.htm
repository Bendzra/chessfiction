Game 14
D.Navara-B.Socko
Polish Team Championship 2006
Ruy Lopez C95

1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 a6 4.Ba4 Nf6 5.O-O Be7 6.Re1 b5 7.Bb3 d6

Black is not interested in the Marshall Gambit with 7...O-O 8.c3 d5 , see Game 16, Shirov-Aronian, and also the anti-Marshall system with 8.a4 , as in Game 10, Ivanchuk-Aronian.

8.c3

The main line.

8.a4 is possible, but perhaps slightly less effective for White than in the Anti-Marshall proper. 8...Bg4 is a reasonable reply.

8...O-O

8...Bg4 is just about playable, but after 9.h3 Bh5 10.d3! Black's bishop loses cover on the light squares on the queenside. White's basic development plan is to swing the knight from b1 to f1, then maybe gain space with the pawn with g4 and/or play the knight to e3 or g3, setting up a light-squared bind.

9.h3

Whereas here White decides to prevent ...Bg4.

The difference is that after 9.d4 Bg4 , White's central pawns are less solid than in the rock-hard d3 formation, and Black can set up counterplay after 10.d5 Na5 11.Bc2 c6 or 11...Qc8 .

9...Nb8!?

Which was the most sensational new system of the Ruy Lopez in the early 20th century? The Marshall Cam-bit or this, the Breyer Variation?

If we can demonstrate that the Marshall Gambit is fully and completely equal, then it may be given the top slot. The Breyer Gambit is tough and extremely difficult to beat, but White still has chances of playing for a slight, maybe very slight, edge. There have been times, though, when players with White, such as Stein have quite simply given up on 1.e4 , because of the Breyer. White has found a few ways since then to improve his play.

Breyer's knight retreat intends to redeploy the knight to d7, keeping the e5-pawn safe, and allowing Black the chance to advance his queenside pawns effectively, with ...c5, so that the pawns become strengths rather than weaknesses.

For 9...Na5 , see Game 33, Karjakin-Inarkiev. There are many other alternatives for Black, not surprisingly when White's last move was a quiet h3. One curiosity is 9...Re8!? 10.Ng5!? ( 10.d4 Bb7 leads to sharp play) 10...Rf8 11.Nf3 , offering a repetition. If Black wants to play on, he has to try something else, with 11...Nb8 , 11...Na5 , 11...Bb7 , 11...h6 , or a few others.

10.d4

10.a4 Bb7 gives Black no great problems.

10...Nbd7

The continuation of the Breyer manoeuvre.

11.Nbd2

11.Nh4 used to be popular up to the early 1970s, with the idea that Black's knight blocks the bishop's defence of the f5-square. It soon became appreciated that 11...Nb6 and 11...exd4 12.cxd4 Nb6 gave Black full equality.

11.d5 Bb7 , followed by ...c6, does not give White much either.

11.c4 c6 sets up Black's barricades well. Black has good chances of equality.

11.Bg5 Bb7 12.Nbd2 h6 13.Bh4 Re8 also seems comfortable for Black.

All of these lines were tried many times in the early 70s, and proved to be extremely solid for Black. Almost by a process of elimination, 11.Nbd2 survived as the only promising plan.

11...Bb7

Black has to develop. 11...Re8?? would be careless, as White traps the queen after 12.Bxf7+ Kxf7 13.Ng5+ followed by Ne6.

12.Bc2

White needs to provide extra cover to the e4-pawn, before manoeuvring his knight to the king's file.

If one is getting worried about Black spending three tempi to manoeuvre the knight from b8 to d7, remember that White has spent four tempi manoeuvring the bishop from f1 to c2.

These offset each other.

12...Re8

Now that White's bishop is off the a2-g8 diagonal, the rook move is much better.

13.Nf1

A standard Ruy Lopez development plan. White puts pressure with the pawns on the queenside, and sets up possible attacks on the kingside, while keeping his pawns intact in the centre. It is up to Black to set up some counter-play.

13...Bf8

In 1983 when Spassky was content at being just a highly respected former World Champion, he made some quick draws with 13...d5!? . Tal too was happy with a quick draw against both Spassky and Psakhis. The various pawn exchanges, and also 14.Ng3 , all seemed equal.

Then Kindermann showed, against Spassky in the 1984 Bundesliga, that 14.Nxe5! Nxe5 15.dxe5 Nxe4 16.f3! can still create problems. Spassky tried 16...Nc5 17.b4 Nd7 , but after 18.f4! , White created pressure, and later won. Spassky then tried improving with 16...Ng5 17.Ng3 f6 , but Chandler demonstrated an edge with 18.Bxg5 fxg5 19.Qb1 , M.Chandler-B.Spassky, German League 1985.

This line soon dropped out of fashion, but maybe Black can improve? Black quietly continues to develop his pieces before setting up the central explosion with ...d5.

14.Ng3

Carrying on with his main plan.

14.d5 c6 is probably not too dangerous.

14...g6

This looks like a simple re-fianchetto move, although in fact the bishop does not make it to g7. Black's other idea is more to prevent White from moving his knight to f5.

14...c6 is an alternative. Then 15.Nf5 Qc7 ( 15...g6?! 16.Nh6+ Kg7? 17.Ng5 is unplayable for Black) 16.dxe5 dxe5 17.Nh2 c5 18.Qf3 c4 19.Bg5 has been played a couple of times:

(a) Black's play soon collapsed after 19...Qc6?! 20.Rad1 h6 21.Bc1 Nc5? 22.Bxh6! in A.Volokitin-E.Bacrot, German League 2007. The point is that if 22...gxh6 23.Rd6! Bxd6 24.Qg3+ , checkmate follows, so Bacrot struggled on with 22...Nfxe4 23.Ng4 , but White's minor pieces were swarming alarmingly, and helped to win quickly.

(b) Black avoided the immediate danger in Anand-Morozevich, Morelia/Linares 2007, playing instead 19...Re6! 20.Rad1 Nc5 :

(b1) Maybe White is slightly better after, for example, 21.Ng4 Nxg4 22.hxg4 .

(b2) Anand tried instead the tempting 21.Bxf6?! Rxf6 22.Ng4 Re6 , but for much of the time he would have liked to have kept his dark-squared bishop. After 23.Nge3 (Marin suggests 23.b4!? as an improvement) 23...Qa5 24.a3 g6 25.Nd5?! Rd8!? (ignoring the sacrifice) 26.Qg3 Rd7 27.Nfe3 Qd8 Morozevich looked to have a good position, but Anand found the better moves, and later won.

14...d5!? is relatively unexplored, although Black usually delays it for the next move. There is no obvious refutation. White could perhaps try 15.Bg5!? h6 16.Bh4 g5 17.Nxg5 , the same sacrifice as in the main line, except that White has not played the extra b3 move. Is this relevant for either side? I leave it to the reader.

15.b3

White too aims at a fianchetto. except that again neither player completes the development of the long diagonal. Events intervene.

15.a4 is not quite as fashionable at higher levels, but has achieved good results for White, after either 15...c5 16.d5 c4 17.Bg5 h6 18.Be3 or 15...Bg7 16.Bd3 c6 17.Bg5 Nf8 18.Qd2 Ne6 19.Be3 .

In either case, the pawn structure is mainly blocked, quite a contrast with the current main line.

15...d5!?

A central pawn explosion!

Quite often in such positions, and also in such lines with white pawns on c4 and d4, and black pawns on c5 and d5, play can lead to extremely complicated positional calculations, as each player on the new move has to calculate two 'pawn takes pawn' captures, work out whether either of them is good, bad, level or premature, and then has to foresee whether the opponent has the chance of making a good response in reply. Sometimes there is no immediate pawn capture by either side, and the tension increases, no doubt leading to severe time pressure. Play through the next few moves in the game, and there is no central pawn exchange on either side, until Black's 19th, four extra moves on either side. During this time, play has not been quiet, but White has made a violent piece sacrifice.

The aggressive central pawn thrust has only been a relatively recent try, with 15...Bg7 having a much longer history, Tal and more especially Spassky having scored with good wins in this. Also, more recently, Socko has reverted to the more traditional lines, in response to his loss against Navara. For example, M.Carlsen-B.Socko, German League 2006, three months after the loss to Navara, continued 16.d5 Qe7 17.c4 Qf8 18.Be3 Bh6 (Black is desperate to exchange the bad bishop) 19.Bxh6 Qxh6 20.cxb5 axb5 21.b4 , but Black was under pressure, and later lost. There have been many tries for Black after 16.d5 , and indeed many defensive attempts by Socko. Black is slightly worse, hence the reason to try 15...d5 .

16.Bg5

He doesn't take anything, but adds to the tension instead. Quite often an extra pin can make all the difference.

Are there any good 'pawn takes pawn' options immediately?

16.exd5 Nxd5 does not make much pressure against Black, as White's c3-pawn is under threat. Play is level after 17.dxe5 Nxc3 18.Qd3 Nd5 . This of course helps explain why Black did not try 14...d5 a move earlier, when White's pawn was still on b2.

16.dxe5 dxe4 gives White nothing, and indeed forces him to play carefully:

(a) If, for example, 17.Nxe4 Nxe4 18.Bxe4 Bxe4 19.Rxe4 Nxe5 20.Bf4 Qxd1+ 21.Rxd1 Nxf3+ 22.gxf3 c5 , and Black has the better endgame pawn structure.

(b) Or 17.exf6 exf3 18.Be4 Bxe4 19.Nxe4 fxg2 20.Kxg2 Nc5 with good play for Black.

(c) 17.Ng5! appears to be the safest option, with a few small tactics ending up in a drawn opposite-coloured bishop endgame after 17...Nxe5 18.N5xe4 Nxe4 19.Nxe4 Bxe4 20.Bxe4 Nd3 21.Re2 Nxc1 22.Rd2 Ne2+ 23.Rxe2 Qxd1+ 24.Rxd1 Rad8 .

The final central exchange capture looks equal after 16.Nxe5 Nxe5 17.dxe5 Rxe5 18.Bf4 Re8 .

16...h6

Now it is Black's turn. Again, he must consider initiating pawn exchanges in the centre. The problem is that the pin on the knight on f6 is so painful for Black that he has to try to break it.

16...exd4?! 17.e5 dxc3 18.exf6 Rxe1+ 19.Qxe1 h6 is too wild. Black is sacrificing his minor piece for a few pawns, but his opponent is now attacking, and Black's pawns attack little. A return piece sacrifice with 20.Bh4! Nxf6 21.Qxc3 g5 22.Nxg5 hxg5 23.Bxg5 d4! 24.Qd2! Qd5 25.f3 keeps the initiative. Black suffers serious gaps in his king-side pawn structure.

16...dxe4?! 17.Nxe5 puts intense pressure on both black knights. If 17...Nxe5 18.dxe5 Qxd1 19.Raxd1 Nd5 20.Nxe4 , White wins a pawn.

17.Bh4

Of course, Navara will have prepared this piece sacrifice. Indeed, it has already been tried before, but here he adds a couple of extra details.

In any case, 17.dxe5 Nxe5 18.Nxe5 Rxe5 19.Bf4 Re6 is only equal, as is 17.Bxf6 Qxf6 18.exd5 exd4 19.Qxd4 Qxd4 20.Nxd4 Bxd5 .

17...g5

Socko takes up the challenge.

Former World Champion, Boris Spassky, played more cautiously with 17...dxe4 18.Nxe4 ( 18.Bxe4 Bxe4 19.Nxe4 g5 20.Bg3 transposes into a later line) 18...g5 in J.Polgar-B.Spassky, 8th matchgame, Budapest 1993:

(a) Polgar might well have considered 19.Bg3 , and if 19...exd4 20.Nxf6+ Qxf6 21.Qd3 Bg7 22.Nxd4 with a positional advantage to White, because of Black's light-squared weaknesses on the king-side. Black improves though with 19...Nxe4 20.Bxe4 Bxe4 21.Rxe4 f5 , with unclear play.

(b) Polgar, then only a teenager, showed great flair for setting up a sacrificial attack with 19.dxe5! Nxe4 20.Bxe4 Bxe4 21.Rxe4 gxh4 22.Rd4 Re7 23.e6! fxe6 24.Ne5 . She had recovered her material, and Spassky's pawn structure proved to be seriously damaged, resulting later in a loss.

On the other central pawn capture, White stands positionally better after 17...exd4 18.e5 g5 19.Bxg5 hxg5 20.exf6 Qxf6 21.Qd3 .

18.Nxg5

He has to sacrifice, and he needs to keep the bishop.

18...hxg5

18...exd4? 19.Nf3 does not give any material compensation in return for the kingside pawn gash.

19.Bxg5

Two pawns for the knight, and good attacking chances against an exposed king. This seems like promising compensation.

19...exd4

19...dxe4 is an attempt at simplification, but White still kept strong king-side pressure after 20.Bxe4 Bxe4 21.Rxe4 Qc8 22.Rh4 exd4 23.cxd4 Bg7 24.Nf5 in A.Sherzer-A.Lesiege, Biel 1993. After 24...Nf8 25.Qf3 Re6 26.d5 Re5 27.Nh6+ Bxh6 28.Bxf6 Bg7 29.Bxe5 Bxe5 30.Re1 Ng6 31.Rg4 White was winning.

20.e5

So that both bishops are on good attacking diagonals, and his other pieces are also in the attack.

20.cxd4? dxe4 21.Nxe4 Be7 is ineffective.

20...Rxe5

Black also faces problems after 20...Nxe5 21.Nh5 Bg7 22.cxd4 Ned7 23.Qf3 . The basic plan of attack is Nxg7 followed by Qg3.

21.Rxe5 Nxe5

22.cxd4!

A year earlier, Navara had got caught off-guard against the same opponent with 22.Qxd4? . He soon found out that he had no real compensation for his sacrificed pawn after 22...Qd6 23.Re1 Ne4 24.Bf4 Re8 , and a later win for Black in D.Navara-B.Socko, Creek Team Championship 2005.

One can be sure that Navara had prepared this position in detail. White's pawn capture on d4 adds an important centralized pawn, and kicks Black's knight from another central square, e5. There is no deep tactic, rather just a natural positional attack, taking over some dark squares, and adding pressure on the light squares leading up to h7.

22...Ned7

A position which has interested the analysts. Where should the knight move? Socko's retreat, helping defend the other knight on f6, seems just about to hold, but requires great accuracy. In other games, Black chose 22...Nc6 instead. This was played by Inarkiev in April 2005, with a later win for Black, given below. This predates the earlier Navara-Socko encounter, which dates from July 2005. Therefore, neither Navara nor Socko covered entirely new ground, and a diligent player could be expected, especially in the days of the Internet, to have had chances of preparing in detail.

22...Nc6 is a more tactical line, putting pressure on the pawn on d4, rather than pure defence:

(a) White suffered a quick collapse a month after the second Navara-Socko game in J.Polgar-S.Mamedyarov, Essent 2006. After 23.Nf5?! ('an unfortunate novelty', according to Marin), Black fought back with 23...Bc8! 24.Rc1 Bxf5 25.Bxf5 Qd6 26.Bh4 Ne7 27.Bg3 Qb4 , Black later consolidating and winning.

(b) 23.Qd3!? Nb4 24.Qf3 has also been tried.

White lures the knight from the centre, but he will soon have to give a tempo with his bishop:

(b1) If then 24...Nxc2? 25.Bxf6 Qxf6 26.Qxf6 Nxa1 27.Qg5+ Kh8 28.Nh5 , and White's queen and knight are much more effective than Black's scattered pieces.

(b2) 24...Be7 is far safer, and it could end up as another draw by repetition in a few moves time:

(b21) Lukacs and Hazai give 25.Nf5 Nxc2 26.Nxe7+ Qxe7 27.Bxf6 Qe4 28.Qg3+ Qg6 29.Qh4 Qh7 30.Qg5+ as a draw.

(b22) Instead, A.Grischuk-S.Mamedyarov, Moscow 2006, continued with 25.Bb1 Bc8 26.Qf4 Ne4 27.Bxe7 Qxe7 28.a3 Nc6 29.Bxe4 dxe4 30.Nh5 Qd6 31.Nf6+ Kf8 32.Qh6+ Ke7 33.Ng8+ Ke8 34.Nf6+ Ke7 , draw. Analysis for Chess-Base, by Ftacnik and by Lukacs/Hazai, suggests possible improvements for both sides, with 26.Bf5! looking promising for White. Black could instead try 25...Ne4! a move earlier: 26.Bxe7 Qxe7 27.a3 Nc6 28.Nxe4 Nxd4 29.Qg4+ Kf8 30.Qh5 Kg7 . Clearly there is now an obvious draw. The computer suggests that the unexpected 31.Nd6! might well be an improvement for White, though. If 31...Ne2+ 32.Kh1 Qxd6 33.Qxe2 Qf4 34.Bd3 Rh8 35.Kg1 d4 36.Re1 , White, maybe contrary to initial appearances, is doing well. Black has the passed pawn in the centre, but White controls the other central file with queen and rook, and Black's king is exposed.

There is clearly plenty of play to be analysed in this whole variation, but maybe the quiet positional grind, after the tactics and sacrifices, might be the most effective way of handling things.

(c) There is another sharp line, though: 23.Nh5 Be7 24.Bxf6 Bxf6 25.Qd3 Bxd4 26.Qh7+ Kf8 27.Qh6+ Ke8 28.Re1+ .

After some natural and logical play, some recent games diverged. Naturally Black must block the diagonal, with either 28...Ne7 or 28...Ne5 ; 28...Kd7?? 29.Bf5 mate is not bright:

(c1) The earlier game was A.Fedorov- E.Inarkiev, Sochi 2005, where Black chose 28...Ne7 :

(c11) Play continued with 29.Ng7+ Kd7 ( 29...Bxg7 30.Qxg7 Kd7 31.Qxf7 Qe8 32.Bf5+ Kd8 33.Rxe7 Qxe7 34.Qg8+ Qe8 35.Qg5+ Qe7 is a perpetual, but Black will want to do more than that) 30.Nf5 Nc6 31.Nxd4 Nxd4 32.Qf4 Nxc2 33.Qxf7+ Kc6 34.Qg6+ Kc5 35.Qxc2+ Kb6 , and the king was safe, and Black kept an extra bishop.

(c12) If White wanted to force a draw, 29.Bf5!? would sort this out quickly. Black cannot allow the bishop to stay there, so 29...Bc8 and a perpetual after 30.Qc6+ Kf8 31.Qh6+ Ke8 . Therefore there is no realistic prospect of Black refuting White's play in this line.

(c13) The most startling line is 29.Qg5 Kf8 30.Bh7 (avoiding the repetition with 30.Qh6+ ) 30...Bc3 31.h4 Qd6 32.Rxe7 Re8! (not 32...Qxe7?? 33.Qg8+ ), and a draw was agreed some moves later in K.Landa-V.Kosyrev, Internet 2005.

This is a wonderfully strange position. Black is not in fact threatening the rook on e7, since 33...Kxe7 would be illegal, and 33...Qxe7?? and 33...Rxe7?? would allow checkmate with 34.Qg8 .

Black cannot even push the queen away with 33...f6?? in view of 34.Qg7 mate. So far, this all looks promising for White, except that he cannot make any further advance, with either rook or queen. There are many chances on either side to break though, but if the opponent is alert, the likely result is deadlock.

(c2) In a game Mamedyarov-Shirov, Moscow 2006, play continued 28...Ne5 29.Bf5 Qd6 30.Qg5 c5 31.Nf6+ Kd8 32.Nxd5+ Ke8 33.Nf6+ Kd8 34.Nd5+ , and a draw by repetition. There are other possibilities here too: for example, 31.Bg4 (opening up the f5-square for the knight after Ng7+) 32...Qe7 32.Qg8+ Qf8 33.Qg5 Qe7 , with another repetition. Instead Lukacs and Hazai suggest 31.Kf1!? as a possibility, with the idea of f4. Play is complicated, but there are many draws by repetition if White wants. There is a suspicion, though, that with best play, White could try for more. For example, after 31...Kf8 32.Nf6 Bc6 33.f4 b4 34.Bd3 , White will capture the knight (as if 34...Nxd3?? 35.Qh6 mate), with chances of an edge.

We now return to 22...Ned7 : 23.Qf3

Back to 'normal' chess. We are at the end of the combinational phase, and now it is a case of positional pressure against the black pieces. All White's pieces, apart for the king, are already attacking, or just a move to attacking, while some Black's pieces are quiet defenders. Much depends on whether Black can uncover his pieces.

23...Qe7

Probably the best defensive option.

An initial computer suggestion is 23...Bg7 24.Nh5 Bh8 , keeping firm control of the pinned knight, but unfortunately Black has little support for the king. 25.Bh6 Ne4 26.Qg4+ Kh7 27.Bg7 Qe7! ( 27...Qg8?? 28.Qf5 mate) 28.Bxh8 f5 29.Qg7+ Qxg7 30.Bxg7 Kg6 31.g4 keeps Black's alive, although a pawn down. White should eventually win.

If 23...Be7 24.Nf5 , and Black's position creaks. Playing through the computer options for Black would give 24...Kh8 25.Re1 Bb4 26.Nh6 Kg7 27.Re3 Bd2 28.Nf5+ Kh8 29.Ne7 Kg7 30.Qg3 Bxe3 31.Bh6+ Kxh6 32.Nf5+ Kh5 33.Bd1+ and mate next move.

If the bishop cannot help, the queen has to take on the burden.

24.Bf5

White threatens Bxe7.

The alternative is 24.Nh5 Bg7 25.Nxg7 ( 25.Bf5 Qf8! holds) 25...Kxg7 26.Qg3 Qe6!? 27.Bxf6+ Kxf6 28.Qf4+ Ke7 29.Bf5 Qd6 30.Re1+ Kd8 31.Qh4+ Qf6 32.Qg4 Nf8 33.Qg8 Qd6 34.Qxf7 Bc6 35.Re5 , and Black is still under pressure, but not as much as in the game.

24...Qd6

Socko accepts that the best he can do is to allow a repetition. Navara's piece sacrifice has at least held the balance in this game, but can he try for more? One would expect so.

25.Bf4

The bishop is able to use another diagonal. Indeed, both white bishops are very strong.

25...Qe7?

Both players would have had the opportunity of analysing their previous encounter some months ago, but it is Socko whose game faltered. He tries to keep the extra minor piece, but his defence crumbles.

A more active way is to make a return sacrifice, with the view of active counterplay. The choice is between 25...Qb6 and 25...Qb4 . Either way, White recovers the knight with 26.Bxd7 Nxd7 27.Qg4+ , and then 27...Kh8 28.Qxd7 (White can, of course, accept a draw with 28.Qh4+ ) 28...Qxd4 forks the rook and bishop.

The obvious continuation is 29.Rc1 , giving up bishop rather than rook. After 29...Qxf4 30.Rxc7 Ra7 31.Nf5 it looks at first as though White has a winning attack. But then there is a wonderful tactical resource with 31...Bc6!! , threatening the queen, threatening the rook, and blocking the defence of the queen check on c1. White can just hold out for a draw with 32.Qd8! Qc1+ 33.Kh2 Qf4+ . Some superb tactics, but with the help of computer Navara could well have analysed this line in advance. If I can find it, so could he! Try to imagine how difficult it would be for the over-the-board player finding the draw from move 25. Difficult.

Here White also has a relatively quiet line, with 31.Rxb7 (instead of 31.Nf5 ). After 31...Bc5 , there is a perpetual check on 32.Nh1 Bxf2+ 33.Nxf2 Qc1+ 34.Kh2 Qf4+ 35.Kg1 Qc1+ . Play is still equal after 36.Nd1 Qxd1+ 37.Kh2 Rxb7 38.Qxb7 Qh5! . Alternatively here, White has 32.Qc8+ Kh7 33.Qxc5 Rxb7 34.Qxd5 Rc7 . After all the wild complications, we reach a relatively stable early endgame with queens, and for White, knight and two pawns versus rook. White is better, and certainly should not lose this, but it would be a long endgame grind to play for a win.

These are the critical main lines, but it remains to be seen whether White can find good alternatives, after either 25...Qb6 or 25...Qb4 .

The weaker 25...Qb6?! 26.Rc1! Bc6 27.Nh5 Nxh5 28.Qxh5 Nf6 29.Qh4 Bg7 30.Be5 puts Black under pressure. Therefore he needs to consider the other queen move.

25...Qb4! places the queen on a livelier square.

Then White could, as suggested earlier, have tried 26.Bxd7 Nxd7 27.Qg4+ , with an edge for him after complicated play.

Or he could try 26.Rc1 Bc8 27.Be5 Nxe5 28.dxe5 Bxf5 29.Qxf5 Ne4 . Then 30.Rxc7 Qe1+ 31.Nf1 Qxf2+ 32.Qxf2 Nxf2 33.Kxf2 Re8 is level, or perhaps slightly better for Black in an endgame. White also has 30.Qg4+ Kh8 31.Qh5+ Kg8 , and then 32.e6!? , avoiding the repetition. The safest reply is then 32...Qe7! , and then White should take a draw with 33.Qg4+ Kh8 34.Qh5+ Kg7 : 35.Qxd5? is unwise, as Black has 35...Nxg3! , and if 36.Qxa8 Ne2+ , forking king and rook.

Complicated, but it seems that 25...Qb4! keeps White down to a slight edge.

26.Rc1!

Maybe Socko was hoping for a draw anyway with 26.Bg5 , repeating.

Navara shows that he can add to the pressure.

26...c6

Marin gives 26...c5 27.Bg5 Qd6 as an improvement, but Black's position is unconvincing. After 28.dxc5 Qc6 29.Nh5 Nxh5 30.Qxh5 Bg7 31.Qh7+ Kf8 32.Bxd7 Qxd7 33.Bh6 White has a winning attack. Alternatively, 29...Re8 30.Be3 Ne5 ( 30...Rxe3 is better, but unsatisfactory) 31.Qg3+ Kh8 32.Qh4 Nxh5 33.Qxh5+ Kg7 34.Qh7+ Kf6 35.h4! Ng6 36.Bxg6 fxg6 37.Bd4+ leads to a win for White.

Black's mistake was on move 25, not on move 26.

27.Bg5

Returning to the pin, and threatening the Bxd7 capture.

27...Qd6

And so the queen returns.

28.Nh5

He is not interested in a repetition with 28.Bf4 .

White has only three minor pieces, but they have much greater impact than Black's four.

28...Bg7

The queen breaks through after 28...Nxh5 29.Qxh5 Bg7 30.Qh7+ Kf8 31.Bxd7 Qxd7 32.Bh6 , and if 32...Bxh6 33.Qh8+ .

White also has a fairly standard attacking win after 28...Re8 29.Bf4 Qe7 30.Qg3+ Kh8 31.Qh4 Kg8 32.Qg5+ Kh8 33.Nxf6 Nxf6 34.Be5 Bg7 35.Rc3 . So many attacking pieces for White, so few defenders for Black.

29.Nxg7

Forcing the king into the open.

29...Kxg7 30.Bf4

The bishop hits the queen yet again.

30...Qe7

Black's queen is stuck on the a3-f8 diagonal, and from White's point of view, it does not really matter where the queen should go. By now, White is winning.

31.Qg3+

A strong check.

31...Kh8

If 31...Kf8 32.Bd6 , pins the queen.

32.Qh4+

Zigzagging through.

32...Kg8

32...Kg7 33.Bh6+ wins more quickly.
