Game 23
M.Carlsen - S.Tiviakov
European Team Championship, Crete 2007
Queen's Indian Defence E17

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nf3 b6 4.g3 Bb7

The most natural move, placing the bishop on the long diagonal, and challenging the white bishop on g2. This move is, of course, sound, logical and solid, but the general impression is that at top level there have been many draws, a few wins for White, and not many wins for Black.

For 4...Ba6 , see Game 4, Topalov-Anand. This tends to lead to much livelier play.

5.Bg2 Be7

Also possible, and transposing into the Bogo-Indian is 5...Bb4+ , but Black's results have not been very successful recently. 6.Bd2 , in this particular line of the Bogo, is the most popular.

6.Nc3

6.O-O O-O 7.Nc3 could transpose to the main line in this game after 7...Na6 . Quite often, though, White does not castle immediately.

6...c5 7.d5 exd5 8.Nh4 keeps an edge for White.

6...Na6

The old main line, 6...Ne4 , and then 7.Nxe4 Bxe4 8.O-O O-O 9.Ne1 (or 9.Nh4 , soon transposing) is generally regarded as boring and drawish. 7.Bd2 f5 is the current main line. White is not too worried about the knight versus bishop exchange, with 7...Nxd2 8.Qxd2 , as he has more control in the centre.

6...O-O is also standard main-line play if White castles immediately, but 7.d5!? attempts to play more aggressively. Tiviakov has played 7...Bb4 a few times, giving up a tempo, but arguing that White too has made concessions with his early pawn advance. He has also played 7...Na6 .

7.O-O O-O

Standard castling by both sides, but where will White develop his pieces? Or, indeed, should he move a pawn?

Tiviakov's favourite knight move in this opening looks slightly unnatural, perhaps. The knight is on the edge, and it will take some time for it to get back into play, maybe with a timely ...c5. Even so, all his minor pieces are off the back rank, he has castled, and he is not under immediate attack. In other words, he has completed his development. Soon he will have to sort out his central pawns.

8.Bf4

This too is just a developing move to get the minor pieces out of the back rank. It does not really attack anything, but at least the bishop is active if the centre opens up. Carlsen is aiming for a quiet edge, trying nothing spectacular.

8.d5 is more combative, pushing the pawn to the other side of the board, but one senses that this is slightly anti-positional. White is allowing the knight to escape to c5, which can create pressure on the e4-pawn. Maybe Black can try 8...exd5 9.cxd5 Re8 with equality.

8...Ne4

A standard defensive idea of this opening, but usually played without ...Na6 and Bf4. Going through Tiviakov's games on the database, it is clear that he is careful to have tried different moves, here and earlier, just to create a little bit of variety. It is useful not to allow his opponent to know exactly what he is going to try in the opening. Tiviakov has, for example, tried 8...c5 and 8...d6 , with different ways of playing on.

Here he tries possibly the steadiest and safest plan. Black wants to exchange White's knight on c3, making it difficult for White to create pawn pressure with d5 and/or e4. If 9.Nxe4 Bxe4 , it is difficult to activate White's king-side pieces for attack.

9.Re1

Maybe White can still claim a micro-advantage, even so, after 9.Nxe4 Bxe4 10.a3!? , trying to prove the knight is misplaced. If 10...c5 11.dxc5 Nxc5 , for example, White is doing well after 12.b4 . Here 10...c6!? provides a different escape route for the knight.

9...d5

9...Nxc3 10.bxc3 looks fully playable, doubling the c-pawns, and then maybe 10...f5 or 10...Be4 .

Tiviakov's move is also good. Whatever else Carlsen achieves in this game, he has not managed to dent Tiviakov's pet opening.

10.cxd5

If 10.Nd2 Nxc3 11.bxc3 , the computer suggests a stonewall-type defence with 11...g5 12.Be3 f5 , and maybe 13.Nf3 f4 14.Bc1 . The experienced human player would tend to be suspicious of this, seeing the e5-square as a serious weakness for Black, not helped by the knight being on the edge on a6. Also, are Black's forward pawns starting a genuine attack, or are they merely creating weaknesses for his own king?

11...c5 12.cxd5 exd5 is a standard-enough idea for both sides.

10...exd5

There is no need to offer the knight exchange first. The knight is still strong on e4.

11.Rc1

11.Ne5 c5 12.Nxe4 dxe4 13.dxc5 Nxc5 14.Qc2 is probably also about equal.

11...c5

Black could have tried blocking White's c-pawn with 11...Nxc3 , since if 12.Rxc3?? Bb4 Black wins the exchange.

Now we have reached the characteristic Tarrasch pawn structure in the centre. Black will probably have to endure an isolated d-pawn, which is a weakness in the pawn structure, but so long as his pieces remain active, as here, the pawn can survive. It follows that Black would want to allow the isolated pawn quickly, if at all, rather than trying to attempt any slow manoeuvring.

12.dxc5

It is difficult for White to develop his pieces further, and still maintain tension in the centre, and so Carlsen decides it is time to exchange pawns, and force Tiviakov to declare his intentions.

After 12.Qa4 , the computer suggests another Stonewall with 12...g5 13.Be3 f5 . Right or wrong, it is certainly different, and there is far less likelihood of a quiet draw.

12...Bxc5

The one recapture that may be considered and rejected quickly is 12...bxc5?! 13.Nxe4 dxe4 14.Ne5 . White has active pieces, and Black has two uncomfortable isolated pawns, while the e4-pawn is also a problem. If Black needs to play ...f5 to defend the pawn, then he is wholly open on the a2-g8 diagonal.

12...Nexc5?! should barely even be considered too. Black's pieces are moving backwards, and getting in the way.

Of the other moves, 12...Bxc5 at least attacks something, and gains a tempo, although the knight on a6 is still a slight problem, and 12...Naxc5 gets in the way of the bishop, and it is the bishop that needs priority at the moment.

13.e3

Carlsen cuts off the diagonal immediately. After 13.Nd4 , White will probably have to play e3 anyway, so he might as well do it now, without loss of time.

13.Nxe4 dxe4 14.Ne5?! ( 14.Ng5 Qe7 might give Black a fractional edge) 14...g5! runs into tactical problems: 15.Bd2 Bxf2+ 16.Kxf2 Qd4+ wins a pawn, while 15.Be3 Bxe3 16.fxe3 , with doubled isolated pawns, is not inspiring.

13...Nxc3

Simplest, relieving the tension on e4 and d5.

14.bxc3

14.Rxc3? Bb4 wins the exchange.

14...Qe7

The computer suggests 14...Qf6 , but Tiviakov would have wanted to avoid a Bg5 fork after ...Rad8.

15.Nd4

Still trying to squeeze an edge. Other moves are equal.

15...Rad8

It would be unwise to trap the bishop with 15...g5? . After 16.Qg4 h6 17.Nf5 Qf6 18.h4 Bc8 19.e4 dxe4 20.Bxe4 it is Black's attacking pawn that falls, not White's defender.

16.h4

But now it is useful for White to secure the g5-square.

16...Rfe8

Standard development by Black, bringing both rooks into play. It is unclear as yet whether the other underdeveloped piece, the knight on a6, should play to c7 or, after a bishop move, to c5. He therefore waits.

17.Bf1

Carlsen gains some slight pressure on the f1-a6 diagonal, but loses pressure on the long diagonal. Does he gain anything overall with this? Who knows?

The important point is that White's play remains solid both before and after his move, while Tiviakov's play remains equally solid A level position. It is only when one player falters, and either plays indecisively, or overpresses, leaving behind weaknesses, that the other can start to take advantage.

17...Ba3

Black decides he must bring the knight into play before being pressed on a6.

18.Rc2

At first it seems there is no real difference between this move and 18.Rb1 , both looking about equal. Keeping the protection of the pawn on c3 is useful though in case play speeds up.

18...Nc5

The knight finally emerges, either to e6 or to e4.

19.Nb5

White attacks something, but play is still equal. Once White's knight moves, Black's knight can move to e6, a safe square.

19...Ne6

And so it proves.

20.Be5

If 20.Nxa3 Qxa3 21.Be5 , Black may transpose into the main line with 21...Bc6 , or there may be other more active choices. However, here 20...Nxf4?! would be a case of over-pressing. After 21.Nb5 White has a better bishop, and has an edge. The pawn sacrifice with 21...d4?! 22.cxd4 Qe4 23.f3 Qg6 24.Kf2 is unconvincing.

20...Bc6

Asking the knight to make a decision.

Maybe 20...Nf8!? could be slightly more accurate. If 21.Nxa3?! Qxa3 , Black is even slightly better, while if 21.Bd4 Ba6 , Black is holding satisfactorily.

21.Nxa3?!

An obvious move, gaining the bishop-pair, but a slight inaccuracy.

21.Qg4! is better, with pressure against the king.

21...Qxa3 22.Qg4

The right idea but mistimed.

22...Qa4!

Equalizing.

23.Qxa4

He cannot avoid the queen exchange, as the rook was under attack.

23.Qf5?! Nc5 is promising for Black.

23...Bxa4

After the exchange White has achieved nothing in terms of an edge. If anything, Tiviakov as Black should have slightly the easier position.

What is interesting about this game is not so much any in-depth strategy, from opening through to endgame, but rather the way in which quite suddenly Carlsen was able to overrun his 2600+ opponent from an apparently easily equal endgame. A few slight slips, and Carlsen is on the way.

Quite often it is the younger player who succeeds. He will have the energy to keep playing, and carry on keep playing, hour upon hour, waiting for the slightest slip-up by the opponent. The older player is more likely to relax, seeing the opportunity of aiming for a clear draw and a half-point without extra effort Then slight mistakes start to slip in, and suddenly the older player has to work hard to hold the position, and then maybe he will make further slight mistakes, and increasingly there is a danger of losing.

24.Rd2

The rook has to move, and he might as well keep some pressure on the isolated pawn.

24...Nc5

Black can force an exchange of knight for a bishop, if required. To maintain complete security, he would like to exchange the knight for a light-squared bishop, keeping the excellent drawing prospects of bishops of opposite colour.

Black could also exchange the other bishop with 24...Ng5!? 25.hxg5 Rxe5 . Then 26.c4 d4! 27.Rb1 d3 , and perhaps 28.Bxd3 Rd7 (White threatened Bxh7+) 29.Rbb2 Rxg5 30.Be2 gives Black even slightly the better pawn structure. Instead 26.f4 Ree8 27.Kf2 might well end up as equal, although it is possible that White can create pressure with his kingside pawn mass. Who knows?

Tiviakov plays it cautiously.

25.Bd4

Centralizing, and keeping the bishop-pair.

White has slightly more chance of drifting into a worse position, but with reasonably strong play on both sides, one would normally expect a draw.

25...Rc8

Putting pressure on the c3-pawn.

26.Rb2

Now Black no longer has a chance to hit d2 with tempo after ...Ne4.

White could also have added extra defence to the pawn with 26.Rc1 .

26...Rc7

All good moves are presumably equal here. Tiviakov creates the option of doubling on the c-file.

27.Rb4

27.Bb5 Bxb5 28.Rxb5 Ne4 29.Rxd5 Nxc3 ends up as a drawn rook and pawn ending.

27...Bc2

Black's bishop is now on a better diagonal.

28.c4

Leading to an 'obviously drawn' opposite-coloured bishop endgame. To play for a win, sometimes one has to exploit the most micro of micro-advantages.

28.Ra1!? avoids the opposite-coloured bishops, and maybe this too can lead to microscopic pressure. White's hope would be to play c4 at some stage, while still keeping the bishop-pair.

28...Nd3

The natural move, though other replies are not necessarily bad.

29.Bxd3 Bxd3 30.cxd5

The pawn gain is only temporary.

30...Rd7

And Black wins it back.

31.Rc1

A slight sense of an initiative. White controls the open c-file, and Black's d-pawn is blocked because the bishop on d4 is securely guarded by the e3-pawn.

31...Rxd5 32.Ra4!?

Many players, and Fritz, might prefer 32.Rc7 Ra5 33.a4 , but Carlsen prefers to avoid spending a tempo with a4. To be able to do something effectively with his tiny edge, he needs to move quickly.

32...Rd7

Time for a general assessment, and in particular to answer why the players did not simply agree a draw.

We start with the bishops. White's bishop is slightly more active than Black's because his bishop on d4, already strong by itself, is supported by a solid potential passed pawn on e3. Black cannot attack this bishop with any of his pawns. Black's bishop is not quite so secure, though. Naturally it can run quickly when attacked, but Black cannot rely on the security of counterplay with the bishop on a central square.

The bishops by themselves would not give White any chances of playing for an edge, were it not for White having the more active rooks. White has control of the open c-file, and he creates pressure on the a7-pawn. Black, in contrast, keeps rooks on the d-file and the e-file, but cannot attack any pawns.

His rooks and his bishop then slightly outweigh his opponents', so there is no particular reason for White to agree a draw. What about his pawns, though? We shall soon see.

33.g4!

Carlsen is pressing forward, and if Tiviakov plays quietly, perhaps lulled by the seeming dullness of the position, then Carlsen can start to take the initiative.

White wants to stop Black from playing ...h5, which would allow his opponent to create, if not exactly counterplay, then at least some solid squares for the bishop on the light squares.

33...h6?!

Probably this was intended as a quiet and natural move, played quickly to help reach the time control. Tiviakov creates an escape square for the king, and prevents for the moment a g5-advance. He needs, though, to defend more actively.

33...Be2! is equal. If, for example, 34.g5 h6 35.gxh6 gxh6 and Black has broken up White's kingside pawn phalanx. The isolated pawns for Black are of no great weakness. White can try instead 34.Kh2 Bxg4 35.Bxg7 ( 35.Rg1? f5 ) 35...Kxg7 36.Rxg4+ Kf8 37.Kg3 Re6 , but Black should have no trouble in holding the rook endgame.

34.f3

Now White is gaining ground. A 4-3 pawn initiative often creates many more problems for the defender than a 3-2 initiative. Even more importantly. Black's 2-1 queenside initiative provides less punch.

34...Kh7?!

Black is just stalling. Clearly the king is going in the wrong direction, but any other piece move could have possibly worse consequences.

It is often a difficult judgement to decide whether to defend passively a slightly inferior position, or whether to change the balance of the position. Here 34...f5!? looks tempting. After 35.gxf5 Bxf5 White has a passed e-pawn, but the opposite-coloured bishops help to defend the weaker player; White will not have much opportunity to advance the pawn beyond e5. Meanwhile, Black's pieces have rather more freedom of action than in the actual game.

It is quite likely, especially given time shortage, that Black would have been worried about 35.Ra3 Be2 36.Kf2?! , but he has a tactical resource, 36...fxg4! 37.Kxe2 Rxd4 . White should still hold with 38.Rxa7 , but this is not really what he wanted.

35.Kf2

This king move, closer to the centre and the advancing pawns, makes more genuine progress.

35...Ree7

Allowing Black to advance with ...b5, without losing the pawn.

It is unexpectedly difficult for Black to coordinate his pieces now. His bishop has few squares, while his rook on d7 is tied down to his pawn, and his other rook does not coordinate well. Also, his king is tied down to the pawn on g7.

Maybe it is time for Black to play 35...f6 immediately, rather than shuffling around with the rooks. White still has some slight pressure after 36.h5 Kg8 37.Ra3 Bh7 38.Rac3 Kf7 39.Kg3 f5 , but Black should be able to hold. He is not fully equal though.

36.Ra3

Forcing the bishop to a worse square.

36...Bb5

36...Bg6? 37.h5 loses the bishop.

37.Rac3

White has taken control of the c-file, whereas Black's rooks do not press against anything. White's bishop is again more secure than Black's. White is better.

37...Rd8

Understandable in that he does not want his opponent to control the back rank.

38.Kg3

The king needs to advance, but only when the pawns have started to push. It is all a question of proportion - kings and pawns need to work together. White's bishop will soon work well with the king and pawns.

38...f6

Belatedly, but Black will not want to have to keep the king guarding the g7-pawn.

39.f4

More pressure from the pawns.

39...h5

Black seeks counterplay, breaking open the connected pawns on the fourth rank, and giving some chance of making use of the light-squared pawns, but in the end, he is just moving closer to a possible zugzwang. Black is not losing yet, but any small error will lead to trouble.

39...Kg6 is an alternative, with a slight edge for White.

40.g5

40.gxh5 Be2 holds.

40...Kg6

The time control. Carlsen has worked hard for his edge, but he has not broken through, and the position still looks close to equal.

41.Rb1!

This apparently simple move adds pressure on Black's bishop.

41...Rd5

Alternatively:

(a) If 41...Bd7? , aiming to prevent f5+, the unfortunate bishop runs into a pin with 42.Rc7 , probably winning.

(b) If 41...Ba4 42.f5+ Kxf5 43.Rf1+ Ke6 44.gxf6 gxf6 45.Rxf6+ Kd7 46.Ra3 , and the driftwood of Black's pieces ends up facing a strong ocean current. Black can survive, just about, with 46...Kc8 (??) 47.Bxb6 Rg7+ (but not 47...axb6? 48.Ra8+ Kc7 49.Ra7+ , winning the exchange) 48.Kf3 Rd1 49.Rc3+ Kb7 50.Bd4 . He is a pawn down though.

(c) 41...Ba6 covers any rook ideas on the f-file ( 42.f5+? Kxf5 43.Rf1+?? Bxf1 ), and it is difficult to find anything for White by direct means. For example, after 42.gxf6 gxf6 43.Rc6 Rf7 44.Rg1 Bb7 45.Kh3+ Kf5 46.Bxf6 Bxc6 47.Bxd8 Rd7 Black should be able to hold. Perhaps the best plan for White would be to drill away on the queenside with 42.a4!? : for example, 42...Rd6 43.a5 bxa5 44.Ra1 fxg5 45.hxg5 Bb5 46.Rxa5 a6 47.Rc5 Rf7 48.Kf3 , and the pressure continues. If 48...Bd7 49.e4 , and if then 49...Rxd4? 50.Rxa6+ , winning.

One cannot imagine that Tiviakov had an easy choice to make, but here he probably chose correctly.

42.Rc8

Into the back rank.

42...Be8

Trying to seal things off. With this and the next few moves, at first sight Tiviakov's plan looks absurd, moving into passivity. In fact though, it takes only a good move for Black to hold the position together.

43.Rbc1

43.gxf6 gxf6 does not help White.

43...f5

Black continues to close the drawbridge.

44.Rb8

White doubles up on the end rank.

44...Rd6

And Black doubles on the e-file.

45.Rcc8 Rde6

Both players continue their plans.

46.Be5

It looks close to zugzwang, but fortunately Black's position is not so desperate.

46...Kf7?!

Tiviakov seems so fascinated about setting up a barricade, keeping the rooks and bishop unmoved, that he forgets about the need for activity.

The simple 46...Bc6! holds the balance comfortably. Carlsen has done as much as he can to try to play for a win, and undoubtedly he has handled his play more creatively than the opponent in the endgame, but one of the basic rules of chess strategy is that one cannot force a win in chess from a level position, unless the opponent makes a mistake. Often there will be a large margin of error in a level endgame, but here the margin is about to slip.

47.Rd8

Waiting for something to happen.

47...Kg6

Tiviakov sees himself as safe. Dangerous!

After 47...Bc6 there are a couple of attempts by White to aim for an edge, but Black holds: 48.Rh8 Kg6 49.Rbg8 Rxe5 50.fxe5 Rxe5 51.Rh6+ Kf7 52.Rxg7+ Kxg7 53.Rxc6 Rxe3+ 54.Kf4 Re2 55.Kxf5 Rf2+ 56.Ke5 Rxa2 57.Rc7+ ends up as a draw, and, similarly, 48.e4 Bxe4 ( 48...fxe4?? 49.Rf8 wins immediately) 49.Bd6 Rb7 50.Rxb7+ Bxb7 51.Rd7+ Ke8 52.Rxb7 Rxd6 53.Rxa7 leads to another level rook endgame.

48.Kf2

Carlsen continues his quiet manoeuvring.

48...Kf7?

And Tiviakov loses his sense of danger.

48...Bc6 was still playable.

49.e4!

Time for a breakthrough. The pieces are now on their optimum squares for both sides, either attacking or defending, and White's king has quietly centralized but can do no more, so the only way to make progress is with the pawns.

49...Kg6

If 49...fxe4 , White now has a pawn break with f5. Play continues with 50.Bd6 e3+ 51.Ke1 Rd7 52.Rxd7+ Bxd7 53.Rf8+ Kg6 54.f5+ , winning the rook.

The exchange sacrifice with 49...Rxe5 50.fxe5 Rxe5 should ultimately be unsuccessful: 51.g6+ Kf8 pins the pieces down, and after 52.exf5 Rxf5+ 53.Ke3 Re5+ 54.Kf4 , one idea for White is 54...Re6 55.a4 Re1 56.Rb7 Re7 57.Rxe7 Kxe7 58.Ra8 a5 59.Kg5 Bxa4 60.Rg8 , and while Black has two connected passed pawns with the bishop, White's passed pawns, with the help of the rook, will be quicker.

49...g6 looks extremely unappealing, taking away the g6-square from the king, and weakening the dark squares. After 50.Bd6 Rd7 51.e5! (more accurate than 51.Rxd7+ Bxd7 52.e5 Re8 when Black will be able to activate some of his pieces) 51...Rxd8 52.Rxd8 Bc6 53.Rf8+ Kg7 54.Rc8 Be4 55.Rc7+ White has a comfortable extra pawn.

50.Bd6

The only sensible move. 50.exf5+? Kxf5 activates Black's pieces.

50...Rd7

Now Black has broken up his defensive e-file structure, and White has made further progress.

51.Rxd7

There is still the chance of a blunder.

After 51.Rxe8? Rexd6 . Black has at the very least perpetual check on the d-file.

51...Bxd7 52.e5

Through sheer stamina, and of course excellent chess understanding, Carlsen has somehow performed some magic in a 'completely drawn' opposite-coloured bishop endgame.

52...Bc6

52...Re8 53.Rb7 Bc6 54.Rxa7 wins a pawn, much as in the game.

53.Rc8

Into the seventh rank.

53...Be8

Back to passive defence. 53...Be4 54.Rc7 Re8 55.e6 Rxe6 56.Be5 threatens mate, and after 56...Rxe5 57.fxe5 White has a second advanced e-pawn, which with care should lead to a win.

54.Ra8

The pawn goes.

54...Bf7

To cover the g7-pawn.

55.Rxa7

Further progress.

55...Re8

At last the rook can move somewhere.

56.a3

Securing the pawn, so he can move his rook.

56...Bd5

Black can do nothing constructive.

57.Ke3

While White gradually improves his position.

57...b5

Maybe the pawn is a little safer?

58.Kd4

But what about Black's bishop?

58...Bg2

After 58...Bc4 59.a4 White has a clear run with the pawn.

59.e6

We have seen this idea before. See the note to Black's 53rd.

59...Rxe6

Not much choice.

60.Be5

With a collapse on g7.
