Game 13
V.Kramnik-P.Leko
Dortmund 2006
Nimzo-Indian Defence E32

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Qc2 O-O 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.Qxc3 b6 7.Bg5 Bb7 8.f3

The main line. White is thinking about playing e4, but this is only a subsidiary point. White is more interested in stopping Black from playing ...Ne4, and in blocking the long diagonal.

For 8.e3 , see Game 12, Bareev-Efimenko 2006. Readers will note that Bareev had to give up the pawn on g2, both in the game against Efimenko, and against Karpov.

8...h6

Slightly loosening the bishop's grip on the h4-d8 diagonal.

9.Bh4

He would like to keep the pin, reckoning that a later ...g5 would open up too many kingside pawns.

9...d5

9...d6 is also possible, and if 10.e4 c5 ( 10...Nxe4? 11.Bxd8 Nxc3 12.Bxc7 makes a break much too early) 11.d5 ( 11.e5 cxd4 12.Qxd4 dxe5 is equal), Black must be careful this time to avoid 11...Nxd5? 12.Bxd8 Nxc3 13.Be7 Re8 14.Bxd6 Na4 15.b3 Nc3 16.e5 . The knight has no escape. Instead 11...Nbd7 is about equal, but 11...Nxe4? does not quite work either: 12.Bxd8 Nxc3 13.Bc7 Na4 14.b3 Nc3 15.Bxd6 exd5 16.Bxf8 Kxf8 17.Kd2 with an edge. It is troublesome for both sides to have to calculate the breakout from the pin every time.

10.e3

10.cxd5!? could be explored further:

(a) The breakthrough this time fails after 10...Nxd5? 11.Bxd8 Nxc3 12.Bxc7 Nb5 13.Bxb8 Raxb8 14.e3 , winning a pawn.

(b) 10...g5?! 11.Bf2 Nxd5 12.Qc1! leaves Black, unsurprisingly, too weakened.

(c) This leaves 10...exd5 :

(c1) Then 11.Bxf6 Qxf6 12.Qxc7 Ba6 13.Qe5 Qc6 has been tried a few times, although tellingly not at very top level. White is a pawn up, but is a long way behind in development, and Black can create pressure on both the c- and e-files.

(c2) There are various untried and slightly exotic ideas, starting, for example, with 11.g4 Re8 12.Nh3 c5 13.Bf2 followed by g5. In such a line, the idea is to avoid weakening the e-pawn with an early e3, and concentrating on attacking Black on the g-file. Whether this works remains to be proven.

(c3) The quieter 11.e3 has been tried several times, with probable equality. After 11...Re8 , if White will need to cover the e3-pawn, with 12.Bf2 , this reduces any possibility of making use of the bishop-pair, but 12.Bb5 c6 13.Ba4 allows Black several ways to equalize, not least the thematic 13...Ne4 14.Bxd8 Nxc3 15.bxc3 Rxd8 16.Ne2 Ba6 with equality, later drawn, in L.Van Wely-A.Grischuk, Biel 2007.

10...Nbd7

The natural developing move.

11.cxd5

There are, of course, reasonable alternatives, such as 11.Bd3 or 11.Nh3 , but Kramnik had something he wanted to try.

11...Nxd5

Finally the breakout works! Or at least it is semi-successful, better than any of the alternatives for Black.

What often happens in such lines is that tension builds up, then there is a quick release of pressure, pieces get exchanged and the result is a semi-endgame, without any real middlegame. Kramnik has been the great specialist of such semi-endgames. It is enough to remember, for example, the way he avoided any loss as Black, and indeed as White, against Kasparov, with the Berlin Defence, and other lines, during his 2000 match in London.

Many writers, more general journalists rather than chessplayers, have commented, on the basis of such a match, that chess is about to dry up, with leading grandmasters being able to equalize at will, with the help of the growth of theory. This is an old theme, dating back until at least the 1920s, but it hardly seems convincing. There must be at least 50 years until chess will dry up because every good player knows everything. One of the central themes in this book is that chess creativity has greatly expanded in recent years, with more and more players reaching super-super-grandmaster level (2700+ level), and correspondingly an increasing number of players reaching higher levels of creativity and of technical accuracy.

11...exd5 12.Bd3 Re8 13.Ne2 has also been tried, the tactics not quite working for Black after 13...Rxe3? 14.Bxf6! Qxf6 15.Bh7+ Kxh7 16.Qxe3 , leaving White an exchange for a pawn up.

12.Bxd8 Nxc3

Naturally the exchanges are forced for both sides.

13.Bh4

But can White grab a pawn? This was tried quite a few times in the late 1980s and early 1990s, with the hope that the far-flung knight might become a weakness after 13.Bxc7 Nd5 14.Bd6 Nxe3 15.Kf2 Nc2 16.Rd1 Rfc8 , but Black equalizes after 17.Bb5 Nf6 18.Ne2 a6 19.Bd3 Nd5 20.Rc1 Nce3 . The knights are extraordinarily busy.

So White plays the opening quietly, but only the most determined player will try it for a win. Still, in this game, Kramnik shows just how difficult it can be for the defender to hold a minuscule disadvantage against a top technician.

13...Nd5

The only safe move. 13...Na4?? 14.Bb5 wins a piece.

14.Bf2

14.e4 Ne3 leads to nothing. White will have to relinquish the bishop-pair, in view of the threat of the ...Nc2+ knight fork, and then he has no chances of playing for an edge.

14...c5

This at the time looked like a safe bet for a steady draw. The current game, however, opens up a couple of doubts.

14...f5 is a good alternative, making it difficult for White to expand in the central squares, but also slightly weakening his own light squares. Play is level after 15.Bb5 c6 16.Bd3 e5 17.Ne2 Rae8 , most games ending up in draws. Here in a blitz game, Kramnik experimented with 16...c5 , but White probably has a slight advantage, helped by the bishop-pair, with 17.Ne2 Rac8 18.O-O cxd4 19.Nxd4 Ne5 20.Be2 , G.Kasparov-V.Kramnik, Moscow 1998. Presumably the position was level at various stages in a rook and bishop ending with a symmetrical pawn structure, but Kasparov squeezed through.

15.e4

After 15.Bb5 , there have been a couple of encounters between Kasparov and Kramnik, the colours reversed second time. Both games ended up as draws. There have, of course, also been encounters between other players. On 15...Rfd8 16.e4 , Kasparov aimed to give up a pawn in return for active play with 16...Nc7!? 17.Bxd7 Rxd7 18.dxc5 f5! 19.cxb6 axb6 , and Kramnik returned the pawn in order to complete his development with 20.Ne2 fxe4 21.fxe4 Bxe4 , later drawn, V.Kramnik-G.Kasparov, World Championship (Game 8), London 2000.

Entertaining and imaginative defensive play by Kasparov, of course, but the computer suggests that Black might even have a slight edge after 16...N5f6!? . If then 17.Ne2 a6 , White's bishop move would seem to be premature: 18.Bxd7 ( 18.Bc4 Rac8!? ) 18...Nxd7 19.dxc5 Nxc5 20.Bxc5 bxc5 21.Rac1 Rac8 forces Black to cover a slight positional weakness, but with a well-timed ...f5, opening up the diagonal, he should stay equal.

And the other Kasparov-Kramnik game? This was again in the aforementioned blitz match, Moscow 1998. Kramnik tried 16...Ne7 , then 17.Ne2 Bc6 . If 18.Bxc6 Nxc6 the position is broadly level, but Kasparov played for more with 18.Ba6 . Then 18...b5!? , a useful move for blitz, but not especially accurate. A computer with a couple of seconds would give 19.Bh4 f6 20.Bg3 e5 21.a4! , with threats of trapping the bishop, with either d5 or axb5; White is much better. However, even the best humans cannot calculate so quickly at blitz speed, and it was Kramnik as Black who came out ahead after 19.a4 bxa4 20.dxc5 . Now 20...Rab8! is good for Black, and the author naturally recognises the help of the computer in seeing this so quickly. Kramnik played the weaker 20...Ne5? , leaving the knight exposed, and after 21.Nd4 Rab8 22.Bg3! , so that if 22...Rxd4? (Kramnik tried 22...f6 and later lost) 23.Bxe5 , unexpectedly there is a bishop fork in the middle of the board.

I am not sure what this proves in terms of technical and theoretical chess over the last few moves, but it seems like gold-dust in terms of the psychology of chess.

15...Ne7

Black is aiming to deploy his knight to c6, putting pressure on the d4-square.

Naturally other moves may be considered:

(a) Postny, writing for ChessBase, suggests that 15...Nf4 16.Be3 Ng6 allows the knight to be misplaced, but this seems ungenerous. Here the point is not that this knight should now be moved to a more active square, but rather that one knight is going to be protecting the other. After 17.Rd1 cxd4 18.Bxd4 Nde5 19.Nh3 Rac8 20.Bc3 Rfd8 21.Be2 Rxd1+ 22.Kxd1 Nc4! Black threatens ...Ne3+, and so White has no realistic option to keep the bishop-pair. Following 23.Bxc4 Rxc4 in V.Kramnik-A.Naiditsch, Dortmund 2003, the game eventually ended up as a draw, Kramnik pressing but not breaking through. There are clear similarities with the Kramnik-Leko game a few years later at Dortmund, with both sides having rook, knight and opposite bishops, and a close resemblance in terms of pawn formations.

We are dealing with microscopic pawn differences. In the Kramnik-Leko game, Leko found his pawn ending up on e5, seemingly not so important if there were bishops of opposite colour, but he lost some degree of flexibility. Naiditsch was able to keep his three pawns united: e6, f6 and g6, and held the game.

16.Ne2

16.Bb5 , and an exchange of bishops, led to a quick and level draw after 16...Bc6 17.Bxc6 Nxc6 18.Ne2 cxd4 19.Nxd4 Nxd4 20.Bxd4 Rac8 21.Kd2 Nc5 in V.Topalov-P.Leko, Cannes 2002. Here Leko had done his homework, since a couple of months previously, he had allowed White a slight edge after 18...Rfd8?! 19.O-O-O! cxd4 20.Nxd4 Nde5 21.Kb1 , still later ending as a draw, in B.Gelfand-P.Leko, Wijk aan Zee 2002.

16...Rac8

The natural reply, avoiding the isolated pawn after, for example, 16...Ba6 17.dxc5 Nxc5 18.Bxc5 bxc5 19.Nc3 with a slight edge for White.

Maybe in view of the result of the game he could have tried to open the position before White could consolidate with 16...f5!? , and if 17.exf5 Nxf5 18.dxc5 Nxc5 19.Bxc5 bxc5 , and although Black has conceded three isolated pawns, his pieces are the more active, and should probably hold the balance. Of course, it is only in retrospect that one might consider such a line as necessary. Beforehand, players might well have thought that the position was equal with quiet but direct play.

Here the attempt at refutation with 17.Nf4?! does not work. After 17...fxe4 18.Nxe6 Rf6 19.Bc4 Bd5 20.Nc7 Bxc4 21.Nxa8 exf3 22.gxf3 Re6+! 23.Kd1 (the king is forced into a skewer) 23...Bd5 24.Nc7 Bxf3+ 25.Kc1 Re2 Black is better. Entertaining tactics, but one cannot be wholly surprised if there are threats if the opponent is spending time with the knight chasing after the far-off rook.

Leko himself later tried 16...f5! , with success. M.Gurevich-P.Leko, 4th match-game, Elista 2007, continued 17.Ng3 fxe4 18.fxe4 cxd4 19.Bxd4 Rac8 20.Rd1 Rfd8 21.Bc3 Ng6 22.Bb5 Nc5 23.O-O Ba6 , probably about equal, but a few minor slips by White gave Leko a chance to play for a win.

17.Nc3

After this, White is ready to complete his development, and make use of the bishop-pair. There is also the niggling threat of Nb5, which continues to cause problems even into the endgame.

17...cxd4

Black simplifies, at the risk of losing a tempo. Normally he would want to wait until White exchanges with dxc5, when the pawn disappears and Black gains time with the knight after ...Nxc5, but perhaps this is not so practical.

17...Rfd8 completes his development, but 18.Nb5 is annoying. Black's idea is 18...cxd4 19.Nd6 Rc7 20.Nxb7 Rxb7 21.Bxd4 Nc5 22.Be3 when he is better developed, and has no pawn weaknesses, but White has the bishop-pair against two knights. The computer suggests that Black is fully equal, but good humans might well be suspicious of this. Indeed, it's probably a slight edge for White.

18.Bxd4

The computer is optimistic in suggesting that Black is a touch better. Two of the three suggested moves, 18...a6 and 18...e5 , create weaknesses in the pawn structure, which are likely to cause problems later.

18...Nc5

With a threat of ...Nb3, but the rook was planning to develop anyway, so Black does not gain a tempo.

19.Rd1

White should not allow the bishop for knight exchange. His dark-squared bishop is the best minor piece on the board.

19...Rfd8

Except, of course, that White can think of an exchange with Bxc5 followed by Rd7.

20.Be3!

White was only level after 20.Bb5 Nb3 21.Be3 Rxd1+ 22.Kxd1 Nc6 23.Bxc6 Bxc6 24.Kc2 Na5 , later drawn, in L.Van Wely-P.Leko, Wijk aan Zee 2005, and Leko was presumably happy enough to settle for a draw with Black against Kramnik as well.

There was only a peaceful draw too after 20.Bxc5 Rxd1+ 21.Kxd1 Rxc5 22.Kd2 Nc8 , play soon fizzling out in E.Bareev-A.Grischuk, Sochi 2004. Moreover, in another top-level game in 2005, the players agreed a draw before White even got round to playing on move 20.

Kramnik's innovation was not a brilliant move, but rather the view that because he keeps the bishop-pair, as opposed to bishop and knight, he still has a slight edge, and he is justified in seeing whether he can keep his edge, or whether his opponent can hold.

20...Rxd1+

If immediately 20...e5 , then White can damage Black's pawn structure with 21.Rxd8+ Rxd8 22.Bxc5 bxc5 . Black will have to suffer a long and often uncomfortable defence to try to draw.

21.Kxd1

There is no point in decentralizing with 21.Nxd1?!. It is the king that needs to move.

21...e5

Black gains space, but also weakens his light squares.

21...Ng6 is to be considered, avoiding any pawn gaps in the centre, and preparing for ...Ne5, but White is better after 22.b4! . The knight-hopping in the centre favours White after 22...Nd3 23.Nb5 Rd8 24.Kc2 Ne1+ 25.Kb3 a6 26.Bxb6 .

22.b4!

In view of the last comment, the idea of b4 is not quite as unexpected as it looks, but it is still good and imaginative, and typical of Kramnik's extremely high technique.

After 22.Kc2 Na4 23.Bb5 Nxc3 24.bxc3 Bc6 25.a4 f5 26.Bxc6 Rxc6 27.Rd1 fxe4 28.fxe4 Rc7 , Z.Gyimesi-Z.Almasi, Szekesfehervar 2006, White has no serious chances of an edge in view of his isolated pawns, and the game ended as a draw.

In New in Chess, Kramnik related that Bareev had tried 22.Kc2 , with another draw against Almasi, in Monaco 2006, and that Bareev afterwards suggested that 22.b4!? might have been an improvement. So big innovations do not automatically arise from deep computer work. A more traditional method can be through going though a postmortem and learning from it.

22...Ne6

After 22...Nb3 23.Kc2 Nd4+ 24.Kb2 White keeps a small edge, one point being that he can threaten a bishop for knight exchange on d4 at an inconvenient time, while the knight cannot initiate a bishop exchange.

Leko decides that it is the other knight that should move to d4, via c6.

23.Kc2!

Running into a pin, but Kramnik has worked out, as Bareev previously noted, that he can bring the king to b2, unpinning, and that now he has chances of playing for an edge. The king is only there on c2 for one move, and can run on the next move.

23...Nc6

23...f5 24.Kb2 threatens little, and leaves the e5-pawn extremely weak.

24.Kb2

The king moves on.

Kramnik has moved straight from the opening into the endgame, bypassing the middlegame. He does this because he feels that he is better. Recent theory may suggest that this should be equal, with a symmetrical pawn structure, and that White's bishop-pair does not achieve that much, given that Black's knights are solid and active, but Kramnik wants to test the waters.

24...Kf8!

Kramnik himself gives this an exclamation mark, centralizing the king for the endgame. He notes that White is slightly better after 24...Ncd4 25.Bd3 Nf4 26.Bxf4 exf4 27.Rd1 , and if at any stage the knight returns to c6, White has Nd5, attacking the weak pawn, and generally consolidating on an advanced square.

It is far easier to play 'bishop takes knight', rather than 'knight takes bishop', and the player with the two bishops has the leisure to decide whether to exchange one of his bishops for a knight, or whether to ignore it, or wait and keep up the tension. White is not so much aiming for the bishop-pair, but rather playing for the better bishop after a timely trade of bishop for knight.

Postny, writing before he had the chance of seeing Kramnik's own annotations, gave instead 25.Nb5 (rather than 25.Bd3 ). White is no doubt slightly better after, for example, 25...Nxb5 26.Bxb5 Nd4 27.Bxd4 exd4 28.Rd1 a6 29.Bf1 Rd8 30.Bc4 Rd7 (avoiding the Bd5 threat), and again White can play hard for a win.

Leko's king move had a straightforward objective, to bring the king nearer to the centre in an endgame. He can wait to see what White does before deciding where he should move his other pieces.

25.Bc4

One senses, from Kramnik's exclamation mark, that he had considered as the main line 24...Ncd4 , and that he had to readjust his play.

25...Ncd4

Not 25...Nxb4? 26.Bxe6 Nd3+ 27.Kc2 , and White wins a piece.

26.Bxe6!

A good player is always adaptable. The bishop-pair has been useful, but it is now time to give up the bishop-pair in order to break up Black's solid knight pair.

26...Nxe6

Not many players would seriously consider taking the isolated doubled pawns with 26...fxe6?! , even though Nb5 is prevented. 27.Rd1 Rc4 28.f4 is a good line for White.

27.Nb5

The knight finds itself on a good attacking square, thanks to the removal of Black's knight from d4.

27...Ra8

The only way to cover the pawn. If 27...a6? 28.Nd6 White wins a pawn.

28.a4!

A good example of the principle that if you can't improve your pieces, you have to improve your pawns, here to add protection for the knight on b5. White's queenside pawns also start to put pressure on Black's.

Kramnik would have considered 28.Rc1 Ba6 29.Nc7 Nxc7 30.Rxc7 Bf1 , heading for a draw, or, slightly more complicated, 28.Nd6 Ba6 29.b5 Rd8 30.Nxf7 Kxf7 31.bxa6 Rd3 32.Re1 Nc7 , with a minimal edge for White, which Kramnik regards as level in Informator.

28...Ba6

Postny regards this as the source of Black's troubles, preferring 28...Bc6!? 29.Nc3 , and White has been unable to try the Nb5-a3-c4 manoeuvre. Even so. Black would have to work hard to hold the position after 29...Rd8 30.b5 Be8 31.Rc1! ( 31.Nd5? f5 is premature) 31...f6 32.Nd5 followed by Nb4, and the knight can find another route to attack.

29.Na3

White would like to smother any activity by the bishop.

Kramnik gives 29.Nc3 Bc4! as equal, presumably in view of 30.Rd1 Rd8 , and an exchange of rooks.

29...Rc8?

This is the move that Kramnik pinpoints as the real weakness. Black needs to keep the bishop in open play. Being stuck with the bishop on b7, behind White's b5- and e4-pawns, is unpleasant.

Kramnik suggests 29...Bd3! 30.Rd1 Rd8 31.b5 Ke7 32.Kc3 Be2 33.Rxd8 Nxd8 . Black's bishop is now on the far side of the board, attacking pawns on unprotected squares (a4 and g2), rather than granite-protected squares such as b5 and e4. In addition, there are so many escape squares for the bishop that White cannot round it up.

White is, of course, still better, having gained extra space on the queenside, and setting up an attack on the a7-pawn, or maybe prising open Black's pawns with a5. It is in the nature of such lines that it is almost impossible to establish clearly whether White should win, or whether Black should be able to hold the draw. 'Plus over equals' would be the standard comment when writing up a game.

Kramnik gives here 34.Nc4 (maybe 34.a5!? could be considered) 34...Bf1 35.g3 f6 36.f4 , with a slight edge for White, but 35...Bg2! looks more accurate and more active. If 36.Nxe5 Ke6 37.Kd4 ( 37.Nc6? Nxc6 38.bxc6 Kd6 favours Black) 37...f6 38.Nc6 Nxc6+ 39.bxc6 Kd6 40.f4 Kxc6 41.e5 , and there are various ways of reaching a level opposite-coloured endgame.

Of course, all this is not forced It merely happens to be the line that Kramnik suggested in his notes. Leko with his last move indicated that he was worried about White taking up the c-file, which must surely imply that 30.Rc1!? is well worth considering, avoiding the exchange of rooks. White keeps a slight edge. Whether he has a win with best play is not so clear.

It is often a feature of a well-played technical win that it is far from clear exactly when the loser made the decisive mistake. White was able to keep a slight edge from the opening, but this is still a long way from a clear win. But when did Black make the critical mistake? In most games, even at the highest level, the critical mistake can be isolated, and questioned. Not so in this Kramnik-Leko game. Postny questioned Black's 28th, while Kramnik himself questions Black's 29th instead, but in either case the alternative moves would still leave White with a slight advantage.

A possible thought might be that Kramnik was able to keep the opening edge from early on, and that he played superb chess throughout Somewhere along the line, possibly in the late opening, Leko made a very slight slip, which Kramnik exploited. The obvious thought would be whether Leko made a mistake later on. We shall see.

30.b5

Kramnik now slams the door on Black's bishop.

30...Bb7

And Black has even lost two tempi. Should we say 'tempi', borrowing from the Italian, or 'tempoes', as given by Postny for ChessBase? The pragmatic answer is that it doesn't really matter all that much, so long as the meaning is clear!

31.Rc1

In chess terms, simplification is often the best way of clarity. The player with the positional advantage would often like to exchange off good defensive pieces.

31...Rxc1

Or 31...Ke7 , but Black would not want to surrender the c-file to the rook.

32.Kxc1

White's bishop is better than Black's bishop, and is capable of picking up pawns.

32...Ke7

Postny notes that 32...f5 33.exf5 Nf4 34.g3 Ng2 35.Bf2 Bxf3 36.Nc4 e4 37.Kd2 gives no counterplay. Black's knight on g2 is stranded.

The immediate 32...Nf4 33.Bxf4 exf4 34.Nc4 gives White an advantage too. Black cannot prevent White's king from Teaching d4.

33.a5

White prises open a passed pawn.

33...bxa5

33...Nc5?! looks wrong, but proving this might sometimes require more thought. 34.a6 Bc8 35.Bxc5 bxc5 36.Nc4 wins only by a tempo. If the pawn were already on f6 rather then f7, he would draw:

(a) If Black were to try to cover the e5-pawn, with 36...f6 , White wins after 37.Na5! Kd6 38.b6! , queening on a8, an attractive finish.

(b) If instead 36...Bd7 37.b6 axb6 38.Nxb6 Bc6 39.a7 Kd6 40.a8Q Bxa8 41.Nxa8 Kc6 42.Kc2 Kb7 43.Kc3 Kxa8 44.Kc4 Kb7 45.Kxc5 Kc7 46.Kd5 , and if 46...f6? , White creeps in with 47.Ke6 . The best practical chance for Black is to give away a pawn, with 46...Kd7 47.Kxe5 Ke7 , except that Black would not have much chance of drawing, a pawn down in a king and pawn ending.

34.Bxa7

White's passed pawn is much better than Black's. White has extra cover on each square in front of the pawn, whether with the bishop (b6 and b8) or the knight (b7 after White's Nc4 and Na5), whereas Black has no realistic chance of promoting his a-pawn. Also, Black's pieces, notably the king and bishop, are not very flexible. The king cannot reach c5, and the bishop is blocked in by the opposing pawns. Leko decides it is time to sort out this particular problem.

34...f5!

Running for freedom.

Postny gives 34...a4 35.Nc4 f6 36.Kb2 Nf4 37.g3 Nd3+ 38.Ka3 f5 39.Nd2 as winning for White.

35.exf5

Not much choice here, but now Leko has chances of attacking White's king-side pawns.

35...Nf4

Now three of White's kingside pawns are under threat.

36.g3

The doubled pawns are expendable in an emergency, but White needs to keep hold of the g- and h-pawns.

36.Bb8? Nxg2 37.Bxe5 Ne3 is only a draw.

36...Nh3

Not perhaps the square that the knight would have preferred, but 36...Nd5 blocks his bishop, while 36...Ne2+ 37.Kd2 Bxf3 38.Ke3 Bh5 39.b6 will queen the pawn.

37.Nc4

Kramnik decides to aim for a direct attack, rather than cutting off the knight with 37.Be3 . In his notes, he suggests that the bishop retreat is not worse, and hints that it may well have been slightly the more accurate choice:

(a) If 37...Ng5 38.Bxg5 hxg5 39.Nc4 Bxf3 40.Nxa5 , and White should eventually win with the extra pawn. Black's problem on the queenside is that once White has a pawn on b6 and a knight on a5, it is close to impossible to attack the b-pawn with the king.

(b) On other lines, such as 37...Bxf3 38.Nc4 Kf6 39.Nd6 a4 40.b6 , Black can struggle on, but his knight on h3 is heavily constrained.

37...Ng5

Leko is alert to the smallest possibility of escape. He needs to bring the knight back into active play.

After 37...Bxf3 , Kramnik gives 38.Nxe5 Be4 39.Nc4 , and White should win.

38.Nxa5

38.f4!? certainly seems well worth considering, especially given that after the alternative it is not totally clear that White is winning. Kramnik gives this as a possibility, without further analysis, while Postny suggests that Black is able to hold.

Postny gives 38...exf4 39.gxf4 Nf3 40.Nxa5 Bd5 41.b6 Kf6 42.Nc4! ( 42.b7 Bxb7 43.Nxb7 Kxf5 , equal) 42...Ke7! 43.h3 Kd7 'with good drawish chances', but 44.Ne5+! looks highly promising. One of the usual rules of thumb in a bishop of opposite colour and pawn endgame, with two passed pawns, is that three clear files between the pawns will be a clear win, as the king breaks through without difficulty, whereas only two clear files is often insufficient. Here 44...Nxe5? 45.fxe5 followed by f6 is clearly enough to win. When the white king reaches e5, it is effectively all over. Black being unable to cover the b-pawn, the f-pawn, and the king threatening to invade on d6.

Black therefore has to try 44...Kc8 , but then 45.Ng4 h5 (otherwise f6 by White, giving a second passed pawn; 45...Ng1? and 45...Nd4? would each lose a piece after 46.b7+ ) 46.Ne3 , and White has excellent winning chances. If he has a chance to reach d6 with a knight, he will be winning material.

38...Bd5?!

But this time he should take with the bishop, 38...Bxf3! . The immediate tactical point is that after 39.b6 Kf6 40.b7 Bxb7 41.Nxb7 Kxf5 Black will be safe, White being unable, the king and knight being so far apart, to cover the two remaining pawns.

Kramnik gives 39.Kd2 Bd5 as best for both players, but then White's king is not on an especially good square. Black threatens ...Nf3, with check, and also, after 40.b6 , there is another check with gain of tempo, with 40...Ne4+ followed by ...Nd6. Kramnik suggests instead 40.Ke3 Ne4 41.Bb8 Kf6 42.Nc6 Kxf5 43.Nxe5 . White is a pawn up, and who knows, he might be winning, or maybe not. There seem to be genuine winning chances though. Black will at some stage need to bring the king to the centre or to the queenside, and that being the case, White will be able to prod the kingside pawns, to set up weaknesses. Play could, for example, continue with 43...Ke6 44.Nd3 Bb7 45.Kd4 Kd7 (otherwise Nc5+ is strong) 46.Be5 , keeping the squeezing options open.

Thus it is possible that Black was losing anyway, even with the best moves.

'Passed pawns must be pushed.'

39...Nxf3

And for the defender, pawns must also be taken. Quite often the only hope for the defender is to exchange or capture the final pawns.

The easy part for White is to win the bishop. How though is he going to keep at least one of his three pawns?

40.h3!

This was played on move 40, at the end of the time control. Normally one cannot analyse in great depth then, and it is a case of positional instinct rather that calculation. Here Kramnik considers that it is better to drop the pawn on h3 rather than on h2. He wants to make it more difficult for Black to return his knight after taking on h3.

It would take a lot of calculation to decide where the pawn will best drop, and in practical terms it would be an irrelevance to analyse what is going on. You could easily lose on time before making a confident decision!

After 40.b7?! Bxb7 41.Nxb7 Nxh2 the knight covers good squares on g4, f3, and f1, holding the balance.

40...Ng5

40...Ng1 is less accurate. After 41.b7 Bxb7 42.Bxg1! the win is straightforward. Black's bishop only covers light squares, and so cannot defend on the dark squares.

41.b7

There is no sense in delaying. White will need to bring the bishop into play.

41...Bxb7 42.Nxb7 Nxh3

After all the chopping of material, the position still looks unclear. White's king is further away from the critical pawns than Black's king, and there seems to be a significant danger of Black winning the f-pawn, and then exchanging the other pawn. White needs some remarkably delicate play from his bishop.

43.Bb6!

A quiet but highly effective move. Kramnik plans to manoeuvre his bishop to h4, guarding the g3-pawn, and covering the f6-square, if Black has not already advanced his king. Meanwhile Black cannot move his knight.

We begin to sense that Kramnik has gone beyond just winning a piece, and that he has found a clear winning plan.

43...Kd7?!

This loses a tempo, Black's king no longer being able to play to f6 next move.
