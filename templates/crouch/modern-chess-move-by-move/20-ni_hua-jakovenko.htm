Game 20
Ni Hua - D.Jakovenko
Nizhniy Novgorod 2007
Sicilian Defence B85

This is the only game in this collection in which the author knowingly selected a game in which the player eventually later won, but was earlier losing. The impact of the game is outstanding, though. How on earth did Jakovenko win this game? Wasn't he about to get checkmated? The spirit of Emanuel Lasker, the great World Champion of a century ago, survives, even though these days attackers and defenders have immensely greater technique than a century ago, and arguably even a quarter century ago.

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 e6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nc6 5.Nc3 Qc7 6.Be2 a6 7.O-O Nf6 8.Kh1

White pushes the king to a safer square, with thoughts of opening up the centre. He will want to keep the king safe.

Not, of course, 8.f4?? Nxd4 9.Qxd4 Bc5 , pinning the queen against king, but 8.Be3 is possible, and leads to Game 16, Shirov-Illescas.

8...Be7 9.f4

Clearly aiming for a pawn push with e5, and maybe a later kingside attack.

9...d6

The natural reply, keeping the Scheveningen formation.

9...d5 is also worth considering if Black doesn't mind the isolated d-pawn after 10.exd5 exd5 . If 11.Bf3 O-O , White is only equal if he tries 12.Nxd5 Nxd5 13.Bxd5 Nxd4 14.Qxd4 Qxc2 . Instead 10.e5 Nxd4 11.Qxd4 Nd7 leads to something approximating to a French Defence.

10.Be3 O-O

Natural development on both sides.

11.Qe1

The queen wants to attack. This is the most direct plan for White, with the idea of Qg3 followed by e5.

11.a4 leads to the Scheveningen main line, restraining Black's counterplay on the queenside. Black develops behind the pawns with, for example, 11...Re8 , followed later by ...Bf6, and it is difficult for White to open up the centre with his pawns, while Black is waiting for a good opportunity for ...e5 himself. There is much manoeuvring, often ending up with sharp play. In some lines, Black develops with ...Be7 and ...Bd7, before re-fianchettoing with ...Bd7-c8-b7 and ...Be7-f8-g7.

Sometimes White will have played a4 before Kh1, such as after, earlier, 8.Be3 Be7 9.f4 d6 10.a4 O-O 11.Kh1 . Ni Hua has been careful to find a slightly less common attacking idea, aiming for an innovation later.

11...Nxd4

Here Black aims to push the b-pawn. The immediate 11...b5?! is premature in view of 12.Nxc6 Qxc6 13.e5 Nd5 14.Bf3 , with pressure on the long diagonal. Black avoids this by exchanging the knights himself.

11...Bd7 is standard, here and in many slightly different positions. It is a developing move, bringing the rooks working together, usually a good start for the middlegame. Also, Black has the idea of playing ...b5, and recapturing with the bishop, rather than the queen, on c6.

11...e5 is possible, but reduces some of Black's flexibility. After 12.fxe5 dxe5 13.Qg3!? , threatening Bh6, White has a slight edge.

11...d5 12.e5! resembles a French Defence, except that Black has given a tempo with ...d7-d6-d5, instead of more directly ...d7-d5. White is probably happy.

Various other moves have also been tried.

12.Bxd4 b5

12...e5? 13.fxe5 dxe5 14.Qg3 puts Black under pressure in the centre. After 14...Bd6 15.Rxf6 exd4 16.Qxd6 Qxd6 17.Rxd6 dxc3 18.b3! White will be able to swallow up the advanced pawn on c3 in the fullness of time, and without giving away doubled isolated pawns.

13.a3

He wants to keep his knight on a good square.

13.e5 opens up the position a little too quickly, at least if aiming for an edge. After 13...dxe5 14.fxe5 Nd7 15.Ne4 Bb7 there is:

(a) White can try a little tactic with 16.Nf6+ , and if 16...gxf6? 17.Qg3+ Kh8 18.exf6 Qxg3 19.fxe7+ e5 20.exf8Q+ Rxf8 21.hxg3 exd4 , and after all the removal of pieces, White is the exchange ahead. However, Black can defend better with 16...Nxf6! 17.exf6 Bxf6 18.Bxf6 gxf6 19.Rxf6 Qxc2 20.Qg3+ Kh8 21.Qe5 Qe4 22.Rxe6+ Qxe5 23.Rxe5 , ending up as a draw, or maybe as a micro-advantage for Black after 23...Rae8 24.Rxe8 Rxe8 25.Bf1 Kg7 , and the king is slightly closer to the opponent's king in the endgame.

(b) 16.Bd3 is safer, with probable equality after 16...Rad8 . As one might suspect, there is a string of dangerous pins after 16...Nxe5? . Play continues with 17.Qg3 f6 18.Nxf6+ Bxf6 19.Rxf6 Rxf6 20.Bxe5 Qf7 21.Bxf6 Qxf6 22.Rf1 Qe7 ( 22...Qxb2? 23.Qc7 ), and after the tactics, a positional grind follows. White has the more active pieces, and the better pawns, not quite enough to win quickly, but enough to cause pressure. A top-level player would be happy to grind this down. This, however, stems from a mistake by Black. With more accurate play, with 16...Rad8 , followed, if required, by exchanging bishop for knight on e4, play is equal.

13...Bb7

The most accurate move.

The reader will recall that a move earlier, we considered 12...e5 , instead of 12...b5 13.a3 , and regarded it as unsound. What changes with the extra queenside pawn moves?

Let us try, in comparison with the earlier situation, 13...e5 14.fxe5 dxe5 15.Qg3 Bd6 16.Rxf6 exd4 17.Qxd6 Qxd6 18.Rxd6 dxc3 . Black's queenside pawn structure here is more advanced than in the earlier line, and 19.b3?! Bb7 is now comfortable for Black. White can try instead 19.bxc3 Bb7 20.c4 Bxe4 21.cxb5 axb5 22.Bxb5 Bxc2 , but his outside passed pawn will not force a win after accurate play by Black. For example, 23.a4 Rfb8 24.Rd2 Rxb5 25.Rxc2 Kf8 is a drawn rook and pawn endgame.

So is this only equal? Not quite. The tactics do not give an advantage this time, but positional play with 16.Be3! gives White a reasonable chance of a good edge. Black's knight on f6 is under pressure, and his d5-square is weak. White could, of course, have tried this in the corresponding line after 12...e5? , but there would have been less point, as there White's tactical reply was more effective.

14.Qg3

14.Bd3 or 14.Bf3 have been tried, but seem slightly less logical. The queen move is a constructive attacking move, and quiet defending of a pawn is not necessary as yet.

If 14.e5 , Black's best attempt at complete equality seems 14...Nd7! 15.exd6 Bxd6 16.Qg3 f6 , followed by ...e5. White's attack is slightly mistimed.

14...Rad8

It soon becomes clear that Black is setting up a defensive fortress, rather than aiming for any immediate counterattack. This is, of course, quite common in the Scheveningen. He manoeuvres behind the pawns, taking care that his king will not be in immediate danger, and waits, and waits further. Black also looks for possibilities of counterplay, when given the chance, later on.

The next few moves are all theory.

15.Rae1

Obvious enough, although 15.Rad1 and 15.Bd3 are also well worth considering. Black has probably not quite yet equalized in any of these lines, but the edge is slight, just a normal edge. Chess theory holds that White starts off with a small edge, with his extra move, but that this is not a decisive advantage if Black plays accurately.

15...Rd7

This has also been tried after White's previous-move alternatives. For example, 15.Bd3 Rd7 16.Rae1 would have transposed into the game. Black wants to use the rook on the d-file to defend if White opens up the position with e5. Black also wants to add protection to the knight with ...Qd8. Black is hoping not to have to give way with ...Ne8, with a continued slight edge for White.

16.Bd3

The bishops are lining up towards the black king's fortifications.

16...Re8

A quiet and unobtrusive move, but what else can he play? Look through the position, and imagine Black's moves, piece by piece, and really it is only the rook move that makes sense. The only other constructive move is with the queen, but 16...Qa5?! moves too far away from the kingside defence after, for example, 17.Qh3 , while 16...Qd8?! is too passive.

This leaves the rook moves. 16...Rc8 looks at first slightly more aggressive, but try playing 17.e5 dxe5 18.Bxe5 Qd8 (or other queen moves), and there is a problem: 19.f5! is strong. If the rook were on e8, Black could cover this second pawn break.

17.e5

White decides it is time to break open the centre. The only other way of trying to attack on the kingside with quiet moves is 17.Qh3 , but then it is Black's turn to take over the e5-square with 17...e5 :

(a) If then 18.Be3 , Black strongpoints the e5-square with 18...Bd8 .

(b) 18.fxe5?! dxe5 veers to favour Black.

(c) The sacrificial line 18.fxe5?! dxe5 19.Nd5 Bxd5 20.exd5 Rxd5 21.Rxf6 Bxf6 22.Qxh7+ Kf8 is quite simply unsound.

17...dxe5

The only good move. 17...Nh5 18.Qg4 g6 leaves White too much free space after 19.f5 .

18.Bxe5

And this is White's only move.

18.fxe5? drops the bishop after 18...Rxd4 , leaving Black a clear pawn up after 19.exf6 Qxg3 20.hxg3 Bxf6 .

18...Qd8

Black's kingside is under attack. He needs to provide extra cover to the f6-square.

19.Ne4

Putting pressure on Black's knight.

19.f5 has been tried a few times, but 19...Bd6! equalizes.

19...Nh5

The knight runs away. Clearly Black will want to protect the knight with ...g6.

19...Bxe4 20.Rxe4 Bf8 cuts down any mating attacks, but White keeps the bishop-pair, and reasonable chances of a durable edge.

19...g6!? is well worth considering. Then 20.Nxf6+ Bxf6 21.Bxf6 Qxf6 , and now there is the danger of overpressing with 22.f5?! . Various quieter lines would equalize. The pawn push sets up a few pins and discovered checks, but 22...Qxb2! seems a good response:

(a) If then 23.fxe6 fxe6 24.Bxg6 Rg7! , and it is not a good idea to win two rooks for queen with 25.Bf7+?! Kh8 26.Bxe8 Rxg3 27.hxg3 Bd5 . Black's bishop is far stronger than White's, and White's pawns are also weak. Here 25.Rxe6! is far better, and if 25...Rxe6?? 26.Qb8+ and a back-rank mate. Thus the position ends up as a draw by perpetual after either 25...Rc8 26.Rb6 Rxg6 27.Rxg6+ hxg6 28.Qxg6+ Qg7 29.Qe6+ Kh8 30.Qh3+ or 25...Ra8 26.Bxh7+ Kxh7 27.Re7! Rxe7 28.Qh4+ Kg8 29.Qxe7 Qg7 30.Qe6+ Kh8 31.Qh3+ .

(b) White has tried instead 23.f6?! , which is dangerous, with an attempt to throttle on g7, but it is unconvincing. After 23...Qxa3 24.Qh4 Red8! Black can counterattack on the d-file, and defend with ...Qf8. This should cover all attacks: for example, 25.Re3 Rd4 or 25.Rxe6 Rxd3 26.cxd3 Qa2 with a counter-threat on g2.

20.Qe3!?

An innovation. White has tried both 20.Qh3 g6 , and 20.Qg4 g6 , but the apparently promising kingside attacks have proved not to materialize, and in the end White can keep equality, but not much more. We will limit ourselves to exploring the bold 20.Qg4 g6 21.f5?! exf5 22.Rxf5 :

(a) The computer then gives a 'refutation' with 22...Ng7 23.Bxg7 gxf5 , supposedly winning for Black. Look closer, though, and it is in White's favour after 24.Qxf5 Kxg7 25.Nd6! Bxg2+ 26.Kg1 Qb6+ 27.Kxg2 Rg8 28.Qxf7+ Kh8+ 29.Kh3 .

(b) In F.Volkmann-J.Horvath, Budapest 1997, Black won quickly after 22...Rxd3! 23.Rxh5 Qd7 24.Qe2? Rd5 25.Qf3 gxh5 26.Qxh5 f6 27.Ng5 fxg5 , and White resigned. White could, though, have added to the sacrificial complexities with 24.Rxh7!? Kxh7 ( 24...Qxg4?? 25.Rh8 mate) 25.Qf4 White is a rook down, but is threatening mate, and attacking the rook, as well as having an extra pawn if the position gets simplified:

(b1) 25...Bxe4? 26.Qxf7+ Kh6 27.cxd3 Bf5 28.Qg7+ Kh5 29.Qh7+ Kg5 30.Re3! , threatening h4+ and mate, is a win for White, not surprisingly, given the exposed black king.

(b2) 25...f6 26.Bxf6 Rd5 27.Qh4+ Rh5 28.Bxe7 with enthralling play. Unfortunately, reality sets in with 28...Rxh4! 29.Nf6+ Kh6 30.Nxd7 Rg4 , and White is in trouble.

Thus if White cannot make progress with his queen on the light squares, then maybe he can try to make progress on the dark squares on the other side of the board?

20...Nf6?

Quite often an improvement can have an exaggerated impact Jakovenko is clearly suspicious of the new move, and offers a repetition with 21.Qg3 Nh5 , but of course Ni Hua does not have to make yet another queen move. He can play for an edge.

Ftacnik suggests 20...g6 , and if 21.Nc5 Bxc5 22.Qxc5 Nf6 23.h3 Nd5 24.b3 with equality, but White can try to improve with 21.Rd1!? . If Black wants to exchange bishop for the highly-centralized knight, White should have the opportunity of making Black exchange the light-squared bishop rather than the dark-squared bishop. If 21...Bxe4?! 22.Qxe4 , Black's queenside pawns are weak. 21...Nf6 is another option for an exchange, but again after 22.Nxf6+ Bxf6 23.Be2! , White is slightly better. He will eventually want to exchange the light-squared bishops.

21.Bxf6!

By deflecting Black's bishop. White can set up the exchange of Black's light-squared bishop with Nc5.

21.Nc5 Bxc5 22.Qxc5 Ng4 , as given by Ftacnik, is only equal.

21...Bxf6

21...gxf6 weakens the pawn structure, and so does not help. There are many good tries for White, the most direct being 22.Rf3!? , pressing on the g- and h-files.

22.Nc5!

So the bishop goes.

22...Bxg2+!?

Black is in 'another fine mess', borrowing from Laurel and Hardy, based on Ftacnik's comment. How is Black going to escape? There are few examples in this book of tactical escape from a losing or almost losing game, as we are concentrating mostly on classic winning chess, whether through excellent positional chess, or through deep tactical calculation. The art of defence, of changing a losing position into a winning position, is however a vital aspect of chess, even and especially at top level. A century ago. World Champion Emanuel Lasker was the pioneer of aggressive defensive play. Jakovenko continues the great tradition.

It looks as though Black's position is about to collapse very quickly after White's initial attack, but somehow it just about holds, and if there is not a quick win, then there is hope for a draw, maybe even hope for a win. The percentage chances are often more attractive in playing for a win, even in a clearly worse position.

Black's bishop sacrifice, based on the recovery of the piece with ...Bd4, forces White's king out into the open, which provides some hope for Black. His own king looks much weaker, and for a long time he will be faced by a discovered attack. If there is counterplay, though, there is life.

22...Rc7? 23.Nxb7 Rxb7 24.Bxh7+! Kf8 25.Be4 Rb6 26.c3 (Ftacnik) leaves White a clear extra pawn and active pieces. The percentages are not good. How can Black possibly win this?

Black has another option, 22...Bxb2!? chopping material on the other long diagonal. Then 23.Nxb7?! Rxb7 24.Qe4 Rc7 25.Qxh7+ Kf8 leads only to equality; Black has done some damage to White's queenside pawns. White can instead grab the exchange with 23.Nxd7 Qxd7 24.Rb1 Bd4 25.Qh3 g6 26.c4 bxc4 27.Bxc4 . With bishop and pawn for the rook, there might be hope for a draw if Black's pieces and pawns were watertight. Unfortunately this is not the case, Black's isolated a-pawn is a problem, and also neither bishop is totally secure in the centre.

23.Kxg2 Bd4

Forking the queen and knight. White in return sets up play on h7.

24.Bxh7+

An immediate punch.

24.Qh3 Bxc5 25.Qxh7+ ( 25.Bxh7+ Kf8 ) 25...Kf8 26.f5 Qg5+ 27.Kh1 looks promising, but Black has some intricate bishop play with 27...Be3 28.Rf3 Bf4 29.Re4

29...Be5!! , an excellent tactical resource. White equalizes after, for example, 30.Rfe3 f6 31.fxe6 Rxe6 , but why can't he take the bishop? After 30.Rxe5 , Black has 30...Rxd3! , with queen forks on the second row after either 31.cxd3? Qc1+ 32.Kg2 Qxb2+ or 31.Rxd3? Qc1+ 32.Kg2 Qxc2+ . In each case, a different rook drops. Finally, 31.Qh8+ Ke7 32.f6+ Qxf6 33.Qxe8+ Kxe8 34.Rxf6 Rd1+ 35.Kg2 gxf6 ends up with an extra pawn for Black in a rook ending, but it is not going to be easy to win.

24...Kh8!

A bold decision. Black is now permanently in danger of being under threat of checkmate, and lethal discovered checks. If, though. White is not actually winning, then still there is hope.

Not every player would have tried Jakovenko's move, and many would have decided that it would have been better to take a realistic approach, and concede that Black has gone wrong, and that all Black can do is try to salvage the game with 24...Kxh7 25.Qd3+ g6 26.Nxd7 Qxd7 27.c3 Qb7+ 28.Qf3 Qxf3+ 29.Rxf3 . This would still have been fairly hopeless. White would eventually be able to set up a queenside passed pawn.

In terms of defensive play, sometimes one should not be too worried about leaving the king under severe pressure, so long as the opponent does not have an immediate forced win, especially if the alternative is to end up with a 100% loss.

25.Qh3

And, one feels, this ought to get to the end.

25...Bxc5

Black recovers the knight, with level material. He doesn't even hold an extra pawn in compensation for the difficulties on his exposed h-file. The one small comfort is that White's king is also exposed, but it is difficult to see how Black can attack it. Black can, of course, slip in an occasional check, if given the opportunity, to gain a tempo, but that is about as much as he can do.

26.Re5

'The second truly critical position in a dramatic game', according to Ftacnik. There must be well over half a dozen moves, including four discovered checks from the bishop, that need to be considered, and each of these is likely to be promising, but not quite enough to clinch the issue. One could easily imagine that time trouble was going to be an issue.

There are two broad options to be considered - either to use a discovered check immediately, or to hold the check until later, improving the other pieces first. As we can see, Ni Hua took the second option.

What happens if he gives the check immediately? Thus we have:

(a) There is no immediate checkmate, so 26.Bg6+?! Kg8 27.Qh7+ Kf8 28.Qh8+ Ke7 29.Qxg7 Rd2+ 30.Kg3 Rf8 seems too crude. If White is not careful, Black can hit hard on the g-file. 31.Bf5 (threatening Rxg6+) 31...Kd6 32.Rd1 Kc6 holds the balance, but White would have liked to have done more.

(b) 26.Bf5+ Kg8 27.Qh7+ Kf8 28.Qh8+ Ke7 29.Qxg7 Kd6 similarly allows Black to escape.

(c) The simple 26.Be4+! Kg8 27.Qh7+ Kf8 28.Bc6 is the best of the discovered checks, concentrating on winning the exchange, rather than hoping for checkmate.

After 28...Rd2+ 29.Kh1 g6? , hoping for 30.Bxe8?? Qa8+ , White has a winning attack with 30.f5! . If then 30...gxf5 31.Rxf5 exf5 32.Qh8 mate.

Black therefore tries 29...Ke7 30.Bxe8 Kxe8 31.Qh8+ ( 31.Qe4 Rxh2+! with a perpetual) 31...Bf8 32.Qh3 . Then 32...Qd5+ 33.Qf3 Qxf3+ 34.Rxf3 Rxc2 35.f5! still gives White winning chances, but 32...g6! helps consolidate Black's pawn structure, with good chances of holding the draw. So this is close to a winning try for White, gaining the exchange, but Black's active pieces, and the weakness of White's kingside pawns, and the openness of his king, keep play close to a balance. Ni Hua would have wanted more.

(d) Of the other bishop checks, 26.Bd3+ Kg8 27.Qh7+ Kf8 28.f5!? Qg5+ 29.Kh1 Ke7 , given by Ftacnik, gives White some chances for attack, but is not as good as grabbing the exchange. White can win a pawn after 30.fxe6 fxe6 31.Rxe6+ Kxe6 32.Qe4+ Qe5 33.Qxe5+ Kxe5 34.Re1+ Kf4 35.Rxe8 , but it is unlikely that he would have good winning chances, Black having the more active pieces in a rook and bishops-of-opposite-colour endgame.

(e) Of the non-bishop moves, Ni Hua chooses the most aggressive rook move. Also 26.Kh1!? , sidestepping checks, is a sensible option, with a possible transposition to variation 'c' after 26...Rd2 27.Be4+ Kg8 28.Qh7+ Kf8 29.Bc6 .

By a process of elimination, Ni Hua's rook move, adding to the pressure on Black's king, would seem the best chance of a large advantage. It is now up to Jakovenko to show whether he can defend.

26...Bd4!

Black needs to use the bishop on both diagonals, defending between d4 and h8, and counterattacking towards g1.

26...Rd2+? fatally weakens the f7-square. White wins after 27.Kh1 Bd4 28.Rh5 g6 29.Bxg6+ Kg8 30.Rh8+ Bxh8 31.Qh7+ Kf8 32.Qxf7 mate.

27.Re4

After 27.Rh5 g6 28.Bxg6+ Kg8 , with the rook on d7, there is no immediate checkmate after 29.Rh8+?? Bxh8 .

27.Bg6+ Kg8 28.Qh7+ Kf8 29.Qh8+ Ke7 30.Qxg7 Rf8 also runs out of steam after, for example, 31.c3 Bxe5 32.fxe5 Ke8 .

There is, however, a third option to be considered, the quiet positional move, 27.Re2!? . White is arguing that he is not giving checkmate himself, but that he still undoubtedly has an edge, and the best way of handling this is to cut out any awkward counterplay, in particular checks on his second rank. White has several good consolidating ideas, such as c3, Rd1 and/or a bishop discovered check. It would seem sensible for Black to take the initiative with 27...Bxb2 . Play might continue with 28.Be4+ Kg8 29.Qh7+ Kf8 30.Bc6 g6 31.c4 . Here there are some complicated options for Black, maybe ending up about equal. The simple line is 31...Rd2!? 32.Rxd2 Qxd2 33.Rf2 Qd1! 34.Rxb2 ( 34.Bxe8 Qg4+ is an immediate draw) 34...Qg4+ 35.Kf1 ( 35.Kh1 Qd1+ 36.Kg2 Qg4+ repeats) 35...Qxf4+ 36.Rf2 Qxc4+ 37.Kg1 Qc1+ 38.Rf1 Qc5+ 36.Rf2 , draw. Except of course that this is 'simple' with the help of computer, but can easily seem very complicated over the board.

27...Bf6

The bishop has now reached a safe and active square, which is progress for Black.

Even so, Black could consider 27...Bxb2 , not so much to grab the pawn, although it is useful, but more to use the queen on the d8-h4 diagonal, while still keeping the bishop on the long diagonal. Ftacnik gives a perpetual check after 28.f5 Qg5+ 29.Rg4 Qh6 30.Rh4 Qd2+ 31.Rf2 Qg5+ 32.Rg4 Qh6 , but this is not the whole story.

In this line, White has 29.Kh1! , and there are no more checks. Black has to give up the queen for rook, bishop and pawn, with 29...Qh6 30.Rh4 Kxh7 31.Rxh6+ gxh6 32.fxe6 fxe6 . Material is level, but Black has weaknesses on the light squares, and White's queen, with the help of the rook, will cause considerable problems.

Advantage to White, then? We can go back a move earlier, with the easily overlooked 28...Bc1!? , a startling indication of the mobility of the bishop across the board, but naturally only on the dark squares. Then 29.Rxc1? over-presses. After 29...Qg5+ 30.Kf3 Qxc1 31.Bg6+ Kg8 32.Qh7+ Kf8 33.Qh8+ Ke7 34.Rxe6+ fxe6 35.Qxe8+ Kd6 36.Qxe6+ Kc7 Black's king escapes, and White has given up the exchange. It would seem wisest to play safely with 29.fxe6 Rxe6 30.Rxe6 fxe6 31.Bd3+ (or another bishop square), and now the next stage of the bishop excursion continues with 31...Bh6 . Black will experience some temporary discomfort after 32.Qxe6 Rd6 33.Qe4 g6 , but he should be able to hold.

Jakovenko decides instead not to take the pawn.

28.Qh5?!

Ni Hua is still trying to force the win, cutting through the ...Bh4 valve, but with this move, he overbalances. All this can only be seen in retrospect, and Jakovenko in turn makes a mistake in reply.

28.Re2 leads to a broadly balanced position after 28...Bh4 29.Be4 Kg8 30.Bc6 Rd2 31.Rxd2 Qxd2+ 32.Kh1 Rc8 33.Be4 Rc4 34.Qf3 . The tactics on both sides finally settle down. Here Black may, of course, also try 28...Bxb2!? , transposing into the line 27.Re2!? Bxb2 , probably level.

28...g6?

It turns out that Jakovenko did not quite make a perfect defence after his opening slip up, but how many of us would have seen the hidden defence?

After 28...Rd2+ 29.Kg3 Qd5 30.Qxf7? Kxh7 31.Qxe8 Bh4+!! ( 31...Qxe4 32.Qh5+ is a perpetual) 32.Kxh4 Rxh2+ 33.Kg3 Qd2 White is suddenly in serious danger of being checkmated.

He is a Took up, but his pieces do not coordinate, and he cannot give any saving check. The only way of avoiding an immediate loss is 34.Re2 Qxe2 35.Qc6 , returning the rook, but avoiding checkmate. Black keeps an extra pawn after in the endgame after 35...Qxc2 . There may be more ambitious ideas.

White can avoid taking the poisoned bishop, but after 32.Kf3 Rxc2 33.Qxe6 Qd3+ 34.Kg4 Rg2+ 35.Kxh4 Qxf1 36.Qf5+ Rg6 , Black's win is straightforward, the only slightly difficult point is finding the quiet king move on 37.Re3 Qf2+ 38.Kh5 Qxh2+ 39.Rh3 Qe2+ 40.Kh4 Kh6 . If then 41.Rg3 Qh2+ 42.Qh3 Qf2 , zugzwang. A startling resource, analysed by Ftacnik, but one cannot expect that Black should be winning. The run of play has been so much in favour of White that there must surely be drawing resources, or better, for him.

After 30.Qh3 , Ftacnik stops his analysis by suggesting that 30...Qd6 is slightly better for White. Here 30...Rxc2 is more ambitious:
