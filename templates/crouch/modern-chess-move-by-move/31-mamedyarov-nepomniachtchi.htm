Game 31
S.Mamedyarov-I.Nepomniachtchi
Russian Team Championship 2008
Semi-Slav Defence D47

1.d4 d5 2.c4 c6 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Nf3 e6 5.e3 Nbd7 6.Bd3 dxc4 7.Bxc4 b5 8.Bd3 Bb7 9.e4

For 9.O-O , see Game 1, Kasimdzhanov-Kasparov. We are still very much in main-line play, which makes it all the more noteworthy that Mamedyarov was able to find a whole unexplored variation just a few moves later.

9...b4

Black kicks the white knight away from the centre, which is good, but he is also weakening his queenside pawn structure.

The computer shows some initial interest in 9...c5!? , but there are too many pins after 10.Bxb5 Nxe4 11.Ne5 Nef6 12.Bg5 Bc8 with advantage to White.

10.Na4

10.e5? bxc3 11.exf6 cxb2 12.fxg7 Bxg7 13.Bxb2 c5 leads to an edge for Black.

10...c5

Black is now attacking both central pawns, but White's pawns can run. White often needs to attack with the pawns before Black is fully developed.

11.e5

He has to attack with the e-pawn.

11.d5? exd5 is mistimed here. In comparison with the Kasimdzhanov-Kasparov encounter, White no longer has a knight on c3.

11...Nd5

Clearly the best move. 11...Bxf3 12.Qxf3 Nd5 is just about playable, but White has the advantage of the bishop-pair in an open position.

12.O-O

The early stages of a new idea. Mamedyarov had already tried 12.Nxc5 Nxc5 13.dxc5 Bxc5 , which gave a win against Topalov, a big result, but Black can improve, and Mamedyarov has moved on from this line.

Here 14.Bb5+?! does not gain much. The king has to move to a slightly worse square, but White's bishop has given up a tempo to go to a weaker square. Black is equal.

14.O-O is best, and if 14...O-O? 15.Bxh7+ Kxh7 16.Qc2+ White wins a pawn. Therefore 14...h6 is necessary, and after 15.Nd2!? we have

(a) If 15...O-O , the standard line, White had a slight edge, and later a win, with 16.Ne4 Bd4 17.Nd6 Bc6 18.Bh7+ Kxh7 19.Qxd4 in S.Mamedyarov-V.Topalov, Essent 2006.

(b) A success, but the next month Gelfand showed he was able to equalize after 15...Nc3!? 16.Qc2 Qd5 17.Nf3 Rd8 18.Ne1 Bd4 19.Bd2 Nb5 in S.Mamedyarov-B.Gelfand, Moscow 2006:

(b1) 20.Bxb4 Bxe5 has been tried a few times, but with Black having equalized.

(b2) Mamedyarov tried 20.Nf3 , Gelfand continuing with 20...Bb6 21.Bc4 Qc5 22.Be3 Qc6 23.Qe2 Nc7 . White's position looks slightly more promising just at the moment, but it could easily fade to equality. The game finished with 24.Rac1 Bxe3 , and a draw was soon agreed after 25.fxe3 O-O 26.Bd3 Qb6 27.Nd2 Ba6 28.Bxa6 . Here Krasenkow gives 25.Qxe3 Qb6 26.Qxb6 axb6 27.Bb3 Nd5 as equal, but one cannot help thinking that White has a micro-edge after 28.Rfd1 .

There are plenty of alternatives at earlier stages, and Mamedyarov no doubt wanted to try something new against a well booked-up opponent.

12...cxd4

Black opens up White's pawn centre, but White also has his pieces. This position is still known.

13.Nxd4

But this is less common, and Mamedyarov was clearly thinking in terms of a follow-up. White gambits a pawn. 13.Re1 is the normal move, but White has given up a tempo to secure the e5-pawn. 13.Re1 g6 14.Bg5 Qa5 is about equal. Some months earlier, a draw was agreed in S.Mamedyarov-B.Gelfand, Moscow 2007, after 15.Nd2 Rc8 ( 15...Ba6 was the older way, but the exchange sacrifice is promising) 16.Nc4 Rxc4 17.Bxc4 Bg7 18.Rc1 Nxe5 19.Bf1 Nd7 20.a3 O-O 21.Bd2 Rb8 22.Qb3 Ba8 23.Rc4 Ne5 24.Rc5 Qd8 25.axb4 d3 26.Qd1 Nd7 27.Rc4 Ne5 28.Rc5 Nd7 29.Rc4 Ne5 . White has the exchange for a pawn, but Black's minor pieces, plus passed pawn, are difficult to dislodge.

If Mamedyarov is going to search for a win, he has to try something new.

13...g6

But can't Black just take the pawn? After 13...Nxe5 14.Bb5+ Nd7 White has played many times the slightly unconvincing line 15.Re1 Rc8 16.Qh5 g6 , with some degree of compensation for a pawn, but not really giving a plus.

Then a high-level quickplay game showed that White could dispense with Re1, thereby not giving the extra defensive resource ...Rc8. J.Piket-A.Shirov, Monaco (rapid) 2002, continued 15.Qh5 N5f6 16.Nxe6! Nxh5 17.Nxd8 Rxd8 18.Re1+ Be7 19.Nc5 Bc8 20.Bg5 Nhf6 21.Bxf6 gxf6 22.Rad1 , and although Black was a piece up, the multiple pins meant that he could not escape, and sooner or later would have to move the king, giving away the extra piece, and settling to aim for an uncomfortable but playable rook and minor piece endgame. Indeed, Shirov chose 22...Rg8 23.f4 Kf8 24.Nxd7+ Bxd7 25.Rxd7 Bc5+ 26.Kf1 Rxd7 27.Bxd7 Kg7 , and the game was later drawn.

White, with more time for reflection, realized that the bishop for knight exchange on f6 was unnecessary. So 21.Rad1 was tried, although after 21...a6 22.Ba4 h6 23.Bxf6 gxf6 24.Nxd7 Bxd7 25.Rxd7 Rxd7 26.Rd1 O-O 27.Rxd7 Rc8 28.g3 Black was eventually able to draw in Radjabov-Shirov, Wijk aan Zee 2003. Some entertaining tactics, followed by long endgame grinding, that is the modern way.

After 15.Qh5 , Black can also try 15...g6 16.Qe5 Qf6 17.Nf3 . However, Black still has some pin problems, one idea being 17...Rc8 18.Bg5 Qg7 19.Rac1 , and if 19...Rxc1 20.Qb8+ , with mate to follow. Black can, of course, improve.

Finally, there is a possible queen sacrifice with 15.Nc6?! Qc7 16.Qxd5 exd5 17.Re1+ Be7 18.Nxe7 Kf8 , but White has only two minor pieces for the queen, and it is not enough.

14.f4

Strangely this is a new line, yet what could be more natural than protecting the advanced pawn?

The main continuation has been 14.Qg4 , with the not too deep idea that if 14...Nxe5? 15.Bb5+ Nd7 , White can regain the piece, with a winning attack, after 16.Nxe6 fxe6 17.Qxe6+ .

There have, of course, been tactics after, for example, 14...Bg7 15.Bg5 , and if 15...Nxe5 16.Nxe6 Nxg4 17.Nxd8 :

(a) If 17...Rxd8 18.Bb5+ Rd7 19.Rae1+ Ne5 20.Nc5 Bc8 21.Bh6! O-O 22.Bxg7 Kxg7 23.Rxe5 Rd6 , and after various tactics, White keeps a slight positional edge.

(b) Or 17...h6 18.Bh4 g5 19.Nxb7 gxh4 20.Bc4 Nb6 21.Nxb6 axb6 22.Nd6+ Ke7 23.Nxf7 Rhe8 , V.Razuvaev-A.Bagirov, Jurmala 1987, and although White is probably better, the opposite-coloured bishops helped lead to a draw.

Entertaining, but has Black ever tried 14...Qa5 , the computer recommendation? 15.Nxe6 Qxa4 makes White's attack look silly, while if 15.Bb5 Ba6 Black is doing well. If White abandons his queen for the kingside, quite often a good response is for Black to take over on the queenside.

14...a6

It is understandable for Black to want to prevent Bb5, or even Nb5 after an early ...Bg7, but it is also slow, and Mamedyarov unleashes an aggressive pawn sacrifice.

We are still on the cutting edge of modern opening theory, and between the time that the game was played in 2008, and my annotation a few months later, and the book being published in 2009, developments will have been found. It is difficult to believe that Mamedyarov's line will prove to have been a truly decisive opening innovation, but for the time being, it works.

Watch this space.

14...Rc8 and 14...Qh4 are to be considered.

15.f5!!

The double exclamation mark is for shock value. Although this pawn sacrifice is hardly unexpected, dating back to the ideas of Pillsbury and others, it is a position which has not been seen before. So we have a position which Mamedyarov has seen before, but Nepomniachtchi hasn't. This can be extremely difficult for the defender, who has to play with 100% accuracy over the board, while the opponent is able to use his home analysis.

Nepomniachtchi is comfortably a 2600+ player, aiming for more, but he loses in 23 moves. Given a second chance, he would have been able to find an improvement.

15...Qh4?

A big miscalculation, which leaves him on the way to a quick loss.

There are three ways of taking the pawn, and we must consider the three captures:

(a) 15...gxf5 would be instinctively be rejected by a top player, and indeed by many others. After 16.Nxe6 fxe6 17.Qh5+ Ke7 18.Bg5+ N7f6 White has several ways of playing for a winning advantage. Probably the most clear-cut is 19.Bxf5 exf5 20.exf6+ , and Black is fatally exposed to White's queen and two rooks. Even here, though. Black's play is not wholly to be discounted. After 16...Qe7! 17.Nxf8 Rxf8 18.Bxf5 Nxe5 19.Qd4 Black equalizes with 19...Rg8! , hitting g2. There are plenty of alternatives, and maybe someone will try it.

(b) 15...exf5 is another possibility, again with wide open central play. After 16.e6 fxe6 17.Nxe6 Qf6 18.Re1 Ne5 19.Nac5 Bxc5 20.Nxc5 O-O-O! Black has escaped the problems with the king on the e- and d-files, and is a pawn up.

White opens Black's king on the queenside with 21.Bxa6 Bxa6 22.Nxa6 Qb6+! 23.Kh1 Nc7 (much clearer than 23...Ng4?! 24.Qc2+ Nc3 25.h3! with no perpetual check) 24.Qe2 Nd3 25.Nxc7 , and now Black can force a repetition with 25...Nf2+ 26.Kg1 Nh3+ , but 25...Nxe1? 26.Be3 would be unwise. A draw, unless of course Mamedyarov has found something even deeper for White.

(c) Finally, there is 15...Nxe5 16.fxe6 fxe6 17.Nxe6 Qd6 . The computer gives this as equal, through several possible lines, but the human player might well slightly prefer the two bishops after 18.Nxf8 Rxf8 19.Rxf8+ Qxf8 , and maybe something like 20.Be4 Rd8 21.Qe1 Qe7 22.Bd2 Bc6 23.Bc2 .

We can suggest, for the sake of argument, that there were four reasonable moves, two of which were equal, one a very slight advantage for White, and one was close to losing. How often it happens that when a player is suddenly under severe pressure, he plays the very worst move!

16.fxe6!

Much better than the presumably anticipated 16.Nf3 Qh5 , although even here White is slightly better after 17.fxe6 fxe6 18.Ng5 .

16...Qxd4+

He takes a knight with check!

16...fxe6 17.Nxe6 Qe7 is an attempt to repair the damage, but Black is effectively a tempo down.

17.Kh1

We are only four moves away from well-known theory, but Black is already in a desperately poor position, and we can be reasonably certain that Mamedyarov has analysed this in advance, and seen it through to the end.

17...O-O-O?!

Avoiding the critical line.

Unfortunately, if 17...fxe6 , or indeed any knight escape, White wins the queen with 18.Bxg6+ (or 18.Bb5+ after a knight move).

The computer suggests 17...Qxe5 18.exd7+ Kxd7 as the best chance, but Black's pieces are fragile, with the king out in the open, and plenty of pins, and there must be significant danger 19.Bf4 seems sharpest, speeding up his development, and more or less forcing Black to open up the d-file with 19...Nxf4 :

(a) If then White uses a double-check, with either 20.Bb5+ or 20.Bf5+ , the king can scurry away with 20...Ke7 , and then on to f6, with likely equality.

(b) 20.Be4+! is more accurate. If then 20...Nd5 21.Bxd5 Bxd5 22.Nb6+ Kd8 23.Re1 Qd6 24.Nxd5 , and White is much better, Black's king still being under pressure, and his development is slow. Thus 20...Bd6 21.Bxb7 seems critical:

(b1) If 21...Rad8 , White can coordinate with 22.Nb6+ Kc7 23.Nc4 Qe7 24.Qf3 with a positional edge.

(b2) 21...Ne2 ( 21...Nh5 transposes) 22.g3 Nxg3+ 23.hxg3 Qxg3 leads to a mating threat and possible perpetuals. The position is incredibly open, and White has chances of countering the counterattack: 24.Nc5+! Kd8 (the only safe king move) 25.Rf2! overturns Black's counterplay after, for example, 25...Qh4+ 26.Kg1 Qg5+ 27.Bg2 Qxc5 28.Bxa8 with a win for White.

So it seems that Black is in trouble, anyway.

18.exd7+

But this is clearly winning.

18...Kb8

18...Rxd7 19.Bf5 Qxd1 20.Bxd7+ wins the exchange for White.

19.Bxa6

Here he grabs a pawn, while keeping his own advanced pawn for as long as possible.

19...Qxe5?

Black can still keep the position alive, just about, with 19...Qxd1 20.Rxd1 Rxd7 . The computer may suggest that White is effectively positionally two pawns ahead, but it can sometimes be difficult to convert this into a win. White is in fact only one pawn up, and it is going to be a long time before he will be thinking about a quick promotion. Black's pieces, slightly uncoordinated at the moment, can soon work together.

The computer's main line is 21.Bxb7 Kxb7 22.a3 Be7 , and then if 23.Bg5 ( 23.axb4 Bxb4 24.Bd2 Be7 25.Bg5 is slightly less effective) 23...Rhd8 24.Bxe7 Rxe7 25.axb4 Rxe5 , and White's doubled extra pawn is only a slender plus in the equation. More important, perhaps, is that even in the endgame Black's king is isolated and under attack from two rooks and a knight.

There are many possible ideas for innovation here, perhaps starting with 22.Bh6 or 22.Be3 . For the author, this has been a long and hard exercise to write such a complicated book, and the temptation is to give an end-of-term exercise to ask the reader to find the best line for White. This is, after all, the type of exercise that top players are increasingly having to work on.

At the very least, Black's position is not collapsing yet. Instead, after Nepomniachtchi's possibly dazed reply, Mamedyarov finishes quickly.

20.Re1

Gaining time by attacking the queen.

20...Qd6

Most other moves lose to the same reply.

Of others, White has 20...Qh5 21.Bxb7 Kxb7 22.g4 , winning the knight.

21.Re8!

All sorts of pins and small tactics now come up, based on the use of the rook on the back rank. Perhaps there is nothing particularly deep at this stage, but the point is that White was able to take advantage of the isolated pawn on d7 before it drops.

This is very much in the style of Kasparov. Not even Tal would have been able to play a game like this, since he never had the technology available of extremely deep computer-based theoretical analysis. As we have seen from this collection, there are more and more players who have been able to combine both ultra-deep theoretical knowledge, and the clarity of thought over the board. The challenge of the newer super-super-grandmasters is whether they can emulate Kasparov's achievements, and whether anyone can go beyond, even if only slightly. Chess carries on, chess continues, and chess evolves.

21...Qxd7

Or 21...Bg7 22.Rxh8 Bxh8 23.Bxb7 Kxb7 24.Bf4! Qxf4 25.Qxd5+ and Black's king will soon drop.

22.Bf4+!

The first bishop move, and it wins. For such a situation to have worked, all the other developed pieces must have coordinated with maximum effectiveness.

22...Bd6

If 22...Ka7 23.Bxb7 Qxe8 ( 23...Kxb7 24.Rxf8! and Nc5+) 24.Bxd5 , and the poor king has no cover.

23.Rxh8

1-0

Black has had enough. 23...Rxh8 24.Nc5 Qe7 25.Bxd6+ Qxd6 26.Bxb7 Qxc5 27.Qxd5 wins a piece, or 23...Nxf4 24.Rxd8+ Qxd8 25.Bxb7 Kxb7 26.Nc5+ Kc8 27.Rc1 with an easy win.

Mind-blowing attacking chess.
