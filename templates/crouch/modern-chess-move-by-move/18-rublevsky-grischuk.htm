Game 18
S.Rublevsky-A.Grischuk
7th matchgame, Elista 2007
Scotch Game C45

1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.d4

The Scotch Opening, which Rublevsky tried out several times in this match. In the end, he lost more games than he won with the white pieces, a serious shortcoming in match-play.

The Scotch is a principled enough opening to be tried, but the general feeling is that opening up the centre so quickly gives rather less long-term pressure than the Ruy Lopez, examined from Game 7, Svidler-Topalov, onwards.

3...exd4

There is probably no need for Black to have to think about other lines.

4.Nxd4

The nine-times British Champion from the 1950s and 1960s, Jonathan Penrose, was an advocate of the Scotch Gambit, 4.c3 . There are certainly many tactical ideas if Black were to accept the challenge, but it is difficult for White to prove any sort of advantage after Capablanca's suggestion, 4...d5!? 5.exd5 Qxd5 6.cxd4 Bg4 7.Be2 Bb4+ 8.Nc3 Bxf3! 9.Bxf3 Qc4! . A peculiarity of the rules of chess is that a player is not allowed to castle with the king over check, so that, for example, 10.O-O is not allowed, as the queen covers the f1-square, which the king was intending to cross. If castling were allowed in this position, White would be better. Instead it is only equal.

4.Bc4 has been tried, but isn't White effectively losing a tempo after 4...Nf6 5.e5 d5 6.Bb5 Ne4 7.Nxd4 Bd7 with equality?

4...Bc5

In many ways, the simplest and most direct move, developing, and attacking the d4-square.

Black can also try attacking the pawn on e5, the main line being 4...Nf6 5.Nxc6 bxc6 6.e5 Qe7 7.Qe2 Nd5 8.c4 Ba6 9.b3 O-O-O . Certainly this breaks the symmetry, with White's pawns in danger of being overextended, and his pieces underdeveloped, while Black's pieces are on strange positions. Rublevsky found an unusual idea after 10.g3 g5!? 11.Bb2 Bg7 12.Nd2 Nb4 13.Nf3 Rhe8 14.a3 g4 :

(a) Previously White had tried 15.Nh4 Bxe5 16.O-O-O Na2+ 17.Kc2 , reaching a strange position where the stray knight on a2 is about to be trapped, and one of Black's bishops is ineffective, but where everything else is centralized and active. This is maybe equal but complicated.

(b) S.Rublevsky-A.Onischuk, Foros 2007, saw instead 15.axb4!? gxf3 16.Qe3 Qxb4+ 17.Bc3! Rxe5! (a good tactic, but not winning) 18.Bxb4 Rxe3+ 19.fxe3 Bxa1 20.Bh3 , and White was two pawns down at the start of the endgame. Black's pieces were unable to cooperate, however, and he soon had to drop pawns. Play continued 20...Be5 21.O-O Bb7 22.Rd1 c5 23.Bxc5 Bc6 24.Kf2 , and White was now clearly at least equal. The game was later drawn, and Black's pawn on f3 was unexpectedly still on the board at the end.

4...Qh4!? is something of an oddity, one possibility being 5.Nb5 Qxe4+ 6.Be2 Kd8 7.O-O . Sometimes Black even tries 7...Qe8 , exchanging the bed of the king and queen. It is the sort of opening that Steinitz would have enjoyed playing, but he suffered a World Championship loss in M.Chigorin-W.Steinitz, 19th matchgame, Havana 1892, after 6.Be3 Kd8 7.N1c3 Qe5 8.Nd5 Nf6 9.Nbxc7 Bd6 10.f4 Qe4 11.Bd3 Qxg2 12.Rg1 Qxh2 13.Qf3 Nxd5 14.Nxd5 Qh6 15.O-O-O . Then, as now, gambit play was difficult to handle for both sides. Players would not be surprised to learn that White won from this position, but the computer gives Black as an edge.

There are few recent games at high level with 4...Qh4 , indicating that the pawn grab is regarded with suspicion.

5.Nxc6

Rublevsky has tried 5.Be3 several times, but for this Candidates match, he varies his play. After 5...Qf6 6.c3 Nge7 Rublevsky has tried, with some success, 7.g3!? , with, for example, two wins against Sasikiran in 2005. The general view, though, is that this line tends to give Black good equalizing chances after 7...d5 or 7...h5 .

5...Qf6

5...bxc6 6.Bd3 leaves White slightly better. Doubled pawns are often not serious weaknesses, but the player with the doubled pawns would have liked some compensation in return.

6.Qf3

Rublevsky had recently dropped trying 6.Qd2 . In S.Rublevsky-V.Akopian, Sochi 2004, play continued 6...dxc6 7.Nc3 Bd4!? 8.Bd3 Ne7 9.O-O Ng6 10.Ne2 Bb6 11.Ng3 Ne5 12.Be2 O-O 13.Qc3 Re8 14.Be3 Ng4 15.Qxf6 Nxf6 16.Bxb6 axb6 . Not very exciting if White is trying for a win, and Rublevsky duly overpressed and lost.

6...bxc6

6...Qxf3 7.gxf3 bxc6 is, it has to be said, fairly easy equality, despite much recent interest. After 8.Be3 Bxe3 9.fxe3 Rb8 10.b3 Ne7 11.Nc3 d6 12.f4 f5 , Zhang Zhong-V.Akopian, Turin Olympiad 2006, Black was comfortable, and indeed his opponent later overpressed and lost.

7.Qg3

So at least we reach a middlegame. Black's pieces have developed quickly, but his pawn structure is slightly suspect. White has reasonable chances of playing for a slight edge. Moreover, this is one of the few lines where decisive results outnumber draws in the 4...Bc5 variation.

7...h5

7...d5 8.Bd3 Bd6 9.f4 gives a slight edge to White. Black prefers to maintain the kingside tension.

8.h4

The usual reply, but White could also ignore the pawn push with 8.Bd3!? h4 9.Qf4 .

8...Nh6

For so long as this developing move is satisfactory, there is no particular need for alternatives.

8...d5 9.Nc3 ( 9.e5 Qf5 10.Bd3 Qg4 is equal) 9...Bd6 10.f4 dxe4 11.Nxe4 Qe6 is, at the moment, unclear.

9.f3

Preserving his pawns.

9.e5 Qf5 10.Bd3 Qg4 is comfortable for Black.

9...d5

In a ChessBase annotation, Ftacnik gives 9...O-O 10.Nc3 Re8 11.Be2 as equal. Readers may decide whether 10.Qg5 Qd4 11.Qxh5 is foolhardy, or a refutation of this, or something between.

10.Nc3

10.e5? Nf5 11.exf6? ( 11.Qf4 Qg6 bails out to a slight edge for Black) 11...Nxg3 12.fxg7 Rg8 traps White's rook.

Has White gained anything from his opening? Ftacnik noted at the time that Rublevsky had tested this position five times in Elista, and had won only once, losing twice. Rublevsky really could do with widening his opening range.

10...Bd4

Grischuk varies from their previous encounter.

Shortly before the Grischuk-Rublevsky match, S.Rublevsky-V.Tkachiev, Poikovsky 2007, had gone 10...Bb4 11.Bd2 dxe4 12.O-O-O e3 13.Bxe3 Bxc3 14.Bg5!? Bxb2+ 15.Kb1 Be6 (but not 15...Qc3?? 16.Rd8 mate nor, more sophisticatedly, 15...Rb8 16.Bxf6 Be5? 17.Kc1 Bxg3?? 18.Rd8 mate) 16.Bxf6 Bxf6 17.Re1 , and sadly Black's enterprising queen sacrifice did not work, and he soon lost.

Who is going to have the bravery to repeat Tkachiev's queen sacrifice? Grischuk was reported as saying that he was prepared to take on Rublevsky in this line, but as we shall see later, Rublevsky was the first to diverge. Indeed, here 15...Rb8! would have sacrificed the queen more effectively, and if 16.Bxf6 gxf6 .

If White wants a quick and easy draw, there is 17.Qg7!? Ba3+ 18.Ka1 Bb2+ 19.Kb1 with a perpetual. Can he try for more, though? 17.c3 Bf5+ 18.Rd3 is a possible attempt, and if 18...Ba3+? 19.Kc2 Rb2+ 20.Kd1 Rb1+ 21.Ke2 and the king escapes, and Black does not have enough for the queen. Better is 18...Bxc3+! 19.Kc2 Be5 20.f4 . Ftacnik assesses this as a clear advantage for White, but this seems to overstate White's play. 20...Bd6 looks safest, allowing the pieces and pawns to work together. Two minor pieces and two pawns for the queen would be an uncommon material balance. Black will, though, win the exchange soon, resulting in a more common balance, with Black being roughly half a pawn ahead in terms of material. Much depends on whether White or Black can bring his pieces working together, and also on whether Black finds he has an overexposed king, or whether some pawns drop. There are so many possible lines to analyse in depth. Quite possibly the position is about equal, and the better player will win.

In one of his other games against Grischuk, Rublevsky decided to avoid the queen sacrifice with 14.bxc3 :

(a) This had earlier been tried, with a win for Black, following 14...Nf5?! 15.Qg5?! Nxe3 16.Qxe3+ Qe7 17.Qxe7+ Kxe7 in Ni Hua-E.Najer, Ergun 2006, and after White mishandled a rook ending.

Marin, annotating for ChessBase, suggests that White has a considerable advantage after 15.Qxc7! , and if 15...Nxe3?! 16.Rd6 Be6 17.Qxc6+ Ke7 18.Qc5! with plenty of nasty discovered checks.

(b) Another try is 14...Qxc3 , which looks risky, but maybe there are chances of equalizing after 15.Bd4 Qa3+ 16.Bb2 Qe3+ 17.Kb1 Be6 .

(c) Finally, we return to S.Rublevsky-A.Grischuk, 4th matchgame, Elista 2007, in which Black tried too hard to avoid tactics, and ended up positionally worse after 14...O-O?! 15.Qg5 Nf5 16.Qxf6 gxf6 17.Bf4 with a later win for White.

Perhaps we can see why Grischuk changed tack later on.

11.Bd2

11.Nd1 seems possible, and complicated, but without any advantage to White.

11...Rb8

Hitting b2. As we shall soon see, Black is reasonably certain of being able to draw, with the help of a perpetual on b2.

What happens, though, if White tries for a win?

12.O-O-O

12.Nd1 Qe7 allows Black to take over the initiative.

12...Be5

Black avoids the temptation of offering a queen exchange:

(a) 12...Qe5? 13.Qxe5 Bxe5 14.exd5 cxd5 15.Re1 pins and wins the bishop.

(b) 12...Qd6? 13.Qxd6 cxd6 14.Bf4 Bxc3 15.bxc3 allows White an advantage.

13.f4

In this, the first play-off game, Rublevsky avoids following their sixth-round encounter with 13.Qg5 Qd6 14.exd5 O-O 15.Bc4 cxd5 16.Bxd5 Qxd5 17.Nxd5 Bxb2+ 18.Kb1 Bc3+ 19.Kc1 Bb2+ 20.Kb1 Bc3+ , drawn by perpetual, S.Rublevsky-A.Grischuk, Elista 2007.

13...Bd4

The only move.

14.Qd3

14.e5 Nf5 15.Qf3 Qg6 16.Bd3 Qg4 leaves Black comfortable. In this line, 15.exf6 Nxg3 traps the rook, as if 16.Rh2 Bg1 , and 16.fxg7 Rg8 17.Re1+ Kd8 still does not allow White's rook to escape.

14...Bg4

This might end up as a draw, if White so wants.

14...dxe4 15.Qxe4+ Kf8 16.Be3 Bxc3 17.bxc3 Bg4 18.Bd4 Qf5 19.Re1 is close to equality, but the small gap between discomfort and safety can be difficult to close. White's two bishops make an impact on both sides of the board, given an open pawn structure, while Black's knight covers only the kingside. After, for example, 19...Qxe4 20.Rxe4 Nf5 21.Bc5+ Kg8 22.Bf2 White is better.

15.e5

15.Be2 is to be considered, but is ultimately not successful:

(a) After 15...Bxc3 16.Bxc3 Qxf4+ ( 16...Bxe2 17.Qxe2 Qxf4+ 18.Rd2 also forces Black to work hard, given the threat on g7; maybe 18...O-O , but White has an edge after 19.exd5 cxd5 20.Qxh5 Nf5 21.Qg5 ) 17.Qd2 Qxd2+ 18.Rxd2 Black's pawn advantage is only temporary, as he is under attack on g7 and e5. Meanwhile, the knight is the least effective of the minor pieces. Playing on, we might have 18...Bxe2 19.Rxe2 Kf8 20.exd5 cxd5 21.Re5 c6 22.Rhe1 , and White has definite chances of playing for a win, in spite of his pawn minus.

(b) So far, so good, for White, but it is unlikely that White should be better. Indeed, further investigation shows that Black has an edge after 15...Bxe2! 16.Qxe2 O-O! :

(b1) After 17.e5 Qe7 Black is threatening to attack with ...Qb4.

(b2) White also has problems with the dark squares after 17.Qxh5 dxe4 , and if 18.Rde1 e3 19.Bxe3 Bxc3 20.bxc3 Qxc3 .

(b3) 17.exd5 Ng4! is a useful zwischenzug. If White deals with the knight fork, then Black recaptures on d5 with advantage. Therefore 18.dxc6 Nf2 19.Qxh5 Nxh1 20.Rxh1 Qxc6 , but Black's pieces are active, and the extra exchange is more significant than the two pawns.

Rublevsky's move is therefore the best, objectively.

15...Bxc3

The only move.

16.Qxc3

White can take the queen with 16.exf6 Bxb2+ 17.Kb1 Bxf6+ 18.Kc1 Bb2+ 19.Kb1 , but Black has at least a draw. What Rublevsky might have been concerned with is not so much that he is worried about giving away another draw with the white pieces, but rather that White is still in trouble after 19...Bxd1! .

Rublevsky tries to play it safely, and with, he hopes, chances of an edge. Grischuk in reply plays smooth and effective positional chess.

16...Qe6

Now the tactical exchanges are out of the way, it is time to assess the positional balance of power.

The immediate thought is that Black has excellent control with his light-square play, with bishop, queen and knight having the opportunity to find useful central and semi-central outposts. White's central pawns are, if anything, in the way. He would like to take advantage of the diagonal from a1 to h8, but the e5-pawn is in the way. Also, White's f4 pawn is in the way. White would have liked to have exchanged bishop for knight on h6, and/or pushed out Black's bishop with f3. Thus White is not better. The practical question is whether White can hold equality, or whether he is worse.

17.Re1

It is too early to think about sacrificing the exchange.

17...O-O

Black, of course, castles, bringing both rooks into play. Grischuk waits to see which minor piece should take over on f5. It is best to keep open the option.

18.Be3?!

White wants to use the bishop on the diagonal, but there are too many hits against it, with ...d4, ...Nf5, ...Ng4, or even ...c5, threatening ...d4.

Ftacnik gives instead 18.Qa5! , which helps tie down Black's queenside pawns, and gives a new lease of attacking chances for the light-squared bishop if he were to try Ba6 against ...Rb7 or ...Rfc8. Ftacnik gives further 18...d4 19.b3 Qe7 20.Bd3 Nf5 as equal. Playing on a few moves, the computer suggests that White can play for an advantage after 21.e6 fxe6 22.Rxe6 Qxe6 23.Bc4 Qxc4 24.bxc4 Ng3 25.Re1 Ne2+ 26.Rxe2 Bxe2 . The queen and opposite-coloured bishop can easily prove to be highly effective. Imagine, for example, the chances White would have after Qxd4 and Bc3. Probably, though, Black should not open up the centre so quickly.

18...Nf5!? is a good pawn sacrifice, and if 19.Qxc7 Rfc8 20.Qa5 c5 , and Black may feel it is a positive advantage to have given up the backward c-pawn.

18...Rfd8

Preparing a possible ...d4-push. The immediate 18...d4 is already possible, but not yet effective, and after 19.Bxd4 Qxa2 20.Bc4 Qa4 21.Bb3 Qa6 a draw by repetition is likely.

If 18...Nf5 , Ftacnik gives 19.Bxa7 Ra8 20.Bc5 Rfd8 21.a3 d4 22.Qc4 Qh6 as equal.

19.Qc5

Marin notes that 19.Ba6? would unexpectedly lose to 19...d4 20.Bxd4 c5! .

It is sometimes difficult to see why such a move is so strong. Black attacks the bishop on d4, certainly, but surely he won't be able to take it? To make any move in chess, a player moves into a square, but also vacates another square. The square that disappears opens up lines through that square, and here Black is threatening ...Qe6xa6, the queen travels by express through c6.

19...a5!?

Perhaps this is not quite the 'just in time' move that Marin suggests, since 19...Nf5 still gives Black an edge. If 20.Ba6?! , the hoped for defence, then 20...Rb6 leaves Black happy.

The ...a5 move is useful in helping any attack.

20.Kb1

And 20.Qxa5? simply helps Black's attack, with 20...d4 21.Bf2 Ra8 22.Qb4 Qxa2 . White needs to add protection to the a2-square.

20...a4

Grischuk conspicuously avoids the natural knight move, 20...Nf5 , and we shall soon see why. A few moves later, he plays ...Bf5, adding pressure on the h7-b1 diagonal.

21.Bd3

Rublevsky sees that Black is planning ...Bf5, and so sets up a bishop exchange on d3 with the hope of recapturing with the c-pawn, putting pressure on Black's doubled c-pawns. It doesn't quite work, but maybe it is the next move that is the problem.

Ftacnik gives instead 21.Qa5 d4 22.Bd2 Bf5 23.Qxa4 Ra8 24.Qb3 d3 25.Qxe6 dxc2+ 26.Kc1 fxe6 27.Bc4 Ng4 , which should end up with level play after 28.Re2 . However, Black can improve with 24...Qxb3! (opening the a-file) 25.axb3 d3! . Here, with the queens off the board, it is time to start a mating attack: 26.cxd3 Rxd3 27.Bxd3 Bxd3+ 28.Kc1 Ra1 mate, or if 26.c4 Ra6 27.b4 Rda8 28.b3 Ra1+ 29.Kb2 R1a2+ 30.Kc3 Rc2+ , and Black wins a piece.

This is hardly an improvement for White, and 21.Qa3 is a losing transposition after 21...d4 22.Bd2 Bf5 23.Qxa4 .

Looking through the other alternatives, 21.Bd2 looks reasonable, but Black is again better after 21...Nf5 22.Bd3 Ng3 23.Rhg1 Ne4! 24.Bxe4 dxe4 , and if 25.Ba5 Rb5 26.Qc3 Rdb8 27.Rxe4 Rxb2+ 28.Qxb2 Rxb2+ 29.Kxb2 a3+! 30.Kxa3 Qd5 31.Ra4 Qc5+ , spearing the pawn on g1.

21...Bf5

Showing that Grischuk judged well in delaying ...Nf5.

22.Rc1?!

Both Ftacnik and Marin suggest 22.Be2 as giving equality with, of course, a possible repetition after 22...Bg4 . However, 22...Be4! is promising for Black. If then 23.Bxh5 Nf5! 24.Be2 (to prevent ...Rb5) 24...Nxe3 25.Qxe3 Rb6 , and as we have seen before, and we shall see again, White is in trouble on the b-file.

Therefore 24.Bg4 , pinning, looks critical, and there are several dead ends, but 24...a3! 25.Qxa3?! Bxc2+ 26.Kxc2 Nxe3+ 27.Rxe3 Qxg4 favours Black. If 28.g3 d4 , with attack.

Here White might try 25.b3!? Rb5 , and then take on a3 more accurately with 26.Qxa3!? Bxc2+ 27.Kxc2 Nxe3+ 28.Rxe3 Qxg4 29.g3 . Then probably Black has a slight edge after 29...c5 30.Rd1 Qg6+ . Sharper is 29...d4 , but it seems that Black has no more than a perpetual after 30.Rd1 dxe3 31.Rxd8+ Kh7 32.Qf8 Qe2+ 33.Kb1 Qe1+ 34.Kb2 .

White may also try, instead of the immediate capture on a3, the intermediate 26.Bxf5!? Bxf5 27.Qxa3 , but Black has good attacking chances, and excellent compensation for the two pawns with 27...d4 28.Rd1 c5 29.Bf2 Rdb8 . There are sacrifices coming up on b3. If, for example, 30.Ka1 Bxc2 31.Rc1 Bxb3 32.axb3 Qd5 , and the additional threat of ...Ra8 will lead White's position to fold.

In total, the onus is on White to prove he can hold. There is not a simple drawing option available.

22...Bxd3

Grischuk does not even have to give away any pawns to keep attacking against the king.

23.cxd3 Nf5

This is a dream square for the knight, provided Black keeps in mind the thought that the knight has the chance of moving to even better squares.

24.Bf2

24.Bd2 also ends up in problems. 24...Qg6 25.Qxc6 Qxg2 is good for Black, while if White tries to stir things up with 25.Rhg1 Nxh4 26.Qxc6 Qxd3+ 27.Rc2 , Black has a winning sacrifice with 27...Rxb2+! 28.Kxb2 Rb8+ 29.Kc1 Qd4 .

24...Rb5

Black gains a tempo with his attack on b2. There is no point in defending c6, as White's b-pawn is far more significant than Black's doubled c-pawn.

25.Qxc6

Not satisfactory, but what else is there? Certainly 25.Qa3 Rdb8 26.Rc2 Rb4 27.g3 d4 is miserable.

25...Rdb8!

Hitting b2.

26.Qxe6

The least-resistance option, but it turns out that Black is winning the endgame. Others:

(a) 26.Rc2?? loses immediately: 26...Rxb2+ 27.Rxb2 Rxb2+ 28.Kxb2 Qxc6 .

(b) 26.b3 axb3 27.a4 Rb4 28.Qxe6 fxe6 29.Rxc7 Rxa4 allows White to keep playing, but obviously he is in serious trouble on the queenside.

(c) 26.Qc2 is a possibility, there being no immediate win after 26...Rxb2?! 27.Qxb2 Rxb2+ 28.Kxb2 . Black instead waits until the pieces and pawns are better coordinated, and breaks the tension later with, for example, 26...Qa6!? (with thoughts of threats on d3) 27.Rcd1 c5 28.Rhg1 c4 29.dxc4 Rxb2+ 30.Qxb2 Rxb2+ 31.Kxb2 a3+ 32.Kb3 Qxc4+ 33.Kxa3 Qc3+ 34.Ka4 Qc2+ , winning.

26...Rxb2+

An in-between move, or alternatively a 'zwischenzug'.

27.Ka1 fxe6

Good knight versus bad bishop, and a rook on the seventh, with no weaknesses in Black's position.

28.Rb1

The attempt to mix up the position with 28.Ba7 R8b7 29.Rc6 a3 30.Bc5 Rc2 31.Rb1 leads to an attractive mating combination with 31...Nd4! , and if 32.Rxb7 Rc1+ 33.Rb1 Nc2 mate, or 32.Bxd4 Rxb1+ 33.Kxb1 Rxc6 .

White may still look as though he is able to hold, thanks to the pin, but this is illusory.

28...a3

The only move. After 28...Rxb1+? 29.Rxb1 Rxb1+ 30.Kxb1 d4 31.Kb2 , Black would even have been worse.

29.Bc5

29.Rxb2 Rxb2 30.Rf1 d4 31.g3 c5 leaves White move-bound.

29...Nxh4!

The knight joins the battle.

30.Rxb2

White's bishop, 'bad' a few moves ago, could set up counterplay with 30.Bxa3 Rxb1+ 31.Rxb1 Rxb1+ 32.Kxb1 Nxg2 33.Bc1 , but one would assume that Black is winning. After all, his passed pawn is the more advanced, and cannot be covered. It is still a close call after 33...h4 34.a4 h3 35.a5 h2 36.a6 h1Q 37.a7 , as analysed by Ftacnik, and unexpectedly White's pawn will be promoted. Black is still a couple of safe pawns up after 37...Nxf4 38.a8Q+ Kh7 , though, and will eventually win. However, Black must be careful not to be over-clever. If 33...Kf7? 34.a4 Ke8?? ( 34...h4 still wins) 35.a5 Kd7 36.a6 Kc6 37.f5! , and with two passed pawns coming up, it is White who queens and wins.

Perhaps the most entertaining of all the knight tours, ending up with a rook sacrifice, is, as given by Ftacnik, 30.Rbg1 Nf5 31.Rxh5 Ng3 32.Bxa3 Rd2 33.Rh2 Ne2! 34.g4 Ra8 35.Bb2 Nc3 .

30...axb2+ 31.Kb1 Nxg2

A more straightforward knight tour.

32.f5

This will not change the outcome.

32...Nf4

Or maybe 32...exf5 , but Grischuk prefers to keep the knight in play.

33.fxe6

He will not queen the pawn.

33...Nxd3

As Grischuk appreciates too, and takes another pawn.

34.Ba3 Nxe5

And another.

35.Kc2

If instead 35.Bxb2 then 35...Nc4 wins the bishop.

35...Nc4 36.Bc5 Nd2!

Quickest Black makes another good knight move to win the rook.
