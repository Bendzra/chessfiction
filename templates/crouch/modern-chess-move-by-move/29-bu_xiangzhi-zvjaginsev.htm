Game 29
Bu Xiangzhi-V.Zvjaginsev
Russian Team Championship 2008
King's Indian Defence E83

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3

The Samisch variation. There is also another Samisch system in the Nimzo-Indian, after 1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.a3!? Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 , often with the idea of setting up a big pawn centre with f3 and, if given the chance, e4.

Clearly 5.f3 here has the same general idea, but playing against a different defensive opening. In the 'hyper-modern' days, advocated by Nimzowitsch, Tartakower, Réti and others, leading players often experimented with Black on concentrating on quick piece development, but delaying pawn advances in the centre. Samisch tended in his younger years to defend as Black with the hypermodern ...Nf6 and ...e6 after White's initial 1.d4 . He most certainly was not the sort of dogmatist to have argued that the big pawn push in the centre would have refuted the modern systems. Rather, he was trying out new ideas.

These days, White usually prefers to develop his pieces in the King's Indian, such as with 5.Be2 and Nf3, as in Game 19, Van Wely-Radjabov. As Bu Xiangzhi demonstrates, however, there are still advocates for Sämisch's ideas.

5...O-O

Black develops. He knows he is going to have to castle sooner or later, and decides to do it now. He is waiting to see what White is going to do, and will then decide what to do himself. Is it, for example, going to be ...c5 or ...e5? Or even ...a6, ...c6 and ...b5?

6.Nge2

The obvious 6.Bd3 tends to be less popular, the bishop getting in the way. 6...c5!? 7.d5 Nbd7 , with a quick ...Ne5, should be equal.

6.Be3 could easily transpose into the main line. 6...c5!? is possible, and White usually offers a Dragon structure with 7.Nge2 . White can, of course, win a pawn with 7.dxc5 dxc5 8.Qxd8 Rxd8 9.Bxc5 Nc6 . He has weakened his dark squares considerably though, and experience has suggested that this is only an equalizing line after 10.Nd5 Nd7 11.Nxe7+ Nxe7 12.Bxe7 Bxb2 13.Rb1 Bc3+ 14.Kf2 Bd4+ 15.Kg3 Re8 16.Bg5 Nf6 . This position has, indeed, occasionally been agreed drawn around here, but in A.Graf-E.Inarkiev, Spanish Team Championship 2007, Graf tried to squeeze on with 17.Bxf6 Bxf6 18.Be2 Re5 19.Rb5 Rxb5 20.cxb5 Be6 21.f4 Bc3 22.Nf3 Bxa2 23.e5 . In this book, we have seen several examples of White trying to play for an edge in a 'drawn' endgame. White had chances of trying for an edge, but eventually the game was drawn.

6...a6

This strange looking move, seemingly developing nothing and attacking nothing, is based on the original idea of Oscar Panno in the mid-1950s. He wanted to try some new lines against the g3 fianchetto line of the King's Indian, and developed the idea of attacking White's c-pawn, no longer defended by the bishop, with moves such as ...a6, ...Nc6, ...Bd7, ...Rb8, and later ...b5. If White were to exchange on b5, then maybe ...Na5 or ...Ne5 followed by ...c5.

The Panno system may be applicable against the Sämisch as well. White has a big central pawn structure, and the pawn to attack is often the c-pawn, preferably from the flank.

6...c5 is perhaps the most straightforward option. Dreev has tried 7.d5 e6 8.Ng3 a few times, without great success, and has since reverted to the main line pseudo-Dragon with 7.Be3 .

7.Be3

7.Bg5 is also possible, and Black has a choice between continuing with a Panno, with 7...Nc6 , or pushing a pawn forward with 7...c5 or 7...c6 .

7...Nc6

More aggressive than 7...Nbd7 .

8.Qd2

8.d5 Ne5 is a more direct attempt for an edge in the centre, but the trouble is that the knight in the centre is secure, as if at some stage White plays f4, Black has ...Neg4 with counterplay. Several years earlier, Bu Xiangzhi tried 9.Nd4 c5 10.Nc2 e6 11.a4 exd5 12.cxd5 Nh5 13.Be2 , but Black started to take over the initiative in Bu Xiangzhi-Ye Jiangchuan, Yangon 1999. Play continued 13...f5 14.exf5 gxf5 15.O-O f4 16.Bf2 Qg5 17.Kh1 Bf5 18.Na3 Rf6 19.Ne4 Bxe4 20.fxe4 Rg6 , and Black later won.

Bu Xiangzhi now uses a much more aggressive way of handling the game. He starts by developing. Before too long, he will castle queenside, and start a pawn rush on the kingside with ideas of h4 and g4, if given the opportunity.

8...Bd7

Sticking to the Panno theme. Black has also tried 8...Rb8 , with a similar idea, and this is the most common move at top level. Then 9.Rb1 e6 10.b4 Nd7 11.g3 (abandoning the pawn rush; he now just wants to castle) 11...Nb6 12.Qd3 f5 13.Bg2 fxe4 14.fxe4 d5 15.c5 dxe4 16.Nxe4 Nd5 17.Bd2 e5 was agreed drawn in Bu Xiangzhi-P.Svidler, Internet 2004. Here 9.Rc1 Bd7 10.Nd1 is an attempt at side-stepping the Panno plan. After 10...b5?! 11.c5! , White keeps an edge, and does too after 10...e5 11.d5 Ne7 .

If 9.h4 , Black could, as in the main game, try 9...h5 . Instead 9...b5 10.h5 e5 11.d5 Na5 12.Ng3 bxc4 13.O-O-O leads to sharp play with castling on opposite sides, and attacks on the files against either king. The latest try is 13...Bd7 14.Bh6 Bxh6 15.Qxh6 Qe7 16.Be2 Rb4 17.Rd2 c6 18.Nf1 , J.Lautier-P.Svidler, Internet 2004, and now Hazai suggests 18...Rfb8 as promising. Svidler tried 18...Kh8 19.hxg6 fxg6 20.Qxg6 cxd5? , but 21.Ne3! , with an attack on d5 and pressure on f5, proved good for White, who later won.

The alternative approach is 8...Re8 . The problem for White is that it is difficult to develop his kingside minor pieces, so that, for example, after 9.Nc1 e5 10.d5 Nd4 11.Nb3 Nxb3 12.axb3 White has given away enough tempi with the knight to allow Black reasonable chances of equality.

9.h4

Does this look as though White is about to punch the Dragon? Be assured that this is no illusion. White is aiming for a quick and direct mating attack, with plenty of sacrifices. Enjoy it!

The Dragon Variation in the Sicilian has always been popular, with sharp tactical play, and the chance of quick demolition after a slight mistake by either side. Such attacking ideas are also possible in the Samisch King's Indian. White has to be aware, though, of the differences in the pawn structure between the Samisch and the Dragon. Had White tried 9.Bh6?! , for example, Black would have had comfortable counterplay with 9...e5 .

9...h5

It is not pleasant to make a pawn push in front of the king, but allowing White to push further with h5 is even more uncomfortable.

9...e5 10.d5 Ne7 11.g4 gives White excellent chances of rolling through. In this particular line, Black's ...a6 proves to have been a waste of time, and White plays extremely vigorously.

9...b5 10.cxb5 axb5 11.Nxb5 Na5 (threatening ...Nb3) 12.Na3 at least diverts attention from the kingside, but Black probably does not quite have sufficient compensation for the pawn.

Finally, 9...Rb8 is slower than the line given with 8...Rb8 (instead of 8...Bd7 ).

10.Bh6

10.O-O-O is just as aggressive as in the game. In A.Miles-M.Jadoul, Brussels 1986, play continued 10...b5 11.Bh6 Kh7 , and now White side-stepped the bishop exchange with 12.Bg5!? . The idea is, of course, to lure the king to the h-file, to open up a scorching attack with g4, and if ...hxg4, then h5. Few players would be happy to defend such a position, and Jadoul attempted to pacify the h-file with a piece sacrifice with 12...bxc4 13.g4 Nxg4 14.fxg4 Bxg4 15.Bh3 Bxh3 16.Rxh3 . Black had three pawns for the piece, but it was White's extra piece that won the day. I leave it as an open-ended exercise for the reader to decide what is going on, presumably with computer assistance, after 13...hxg4 14.h5 Nxh5 .

10...e5

Following the lead of the Miles-Jadoul game, 10...Kh7!? would perhaps be a safer option, 11.Bxg7 Kxg7 loses a tempo for Black, but he would be happy with his kingside defensive structure, with ...Rh8 after g4. Here 11.Bg5 Na5!? 12.Ng3 Nc6 13.Nge2 is a possible draw. There is no clear advantage for White after 13.d5 Ne5 14.f4 Neg4 .

11.O-O-O

11.d5 Na5 12.Bxg7 Kxg7 13.Nc1 b6 leaves Black comfortable.

11...b5

Both players are aiming for a sharp battle.

11...Nxd4 12.Nxd4 exd4 13.Bxg7 Kxg7 14.Qxd4 Re8 is a steadier approach, White frying to make use of the pinned knight. However, 15.g4?! hxg4 16.Be2 gxf3 17.Bxf3 Re5 18.Rdg1 , following a computer suggestion, turns out not to be good. 18...Qh8! 19.h5 Qh6+ 20.Kb1 g5 keeps Black's extra pawn. There are, of course, quieter lines on move 15, generally ending up about equal.

12.Nd5

Like it or not, White has to take the initiative. 12.Bxg7?! Kxg7 is already better for Black, White's central pawns being under pressure, leaving his king slightly too open.

12...Re8

12...bxc4? loses material after 13.Nxf6+ Qxf6 14.Bg5 Qe6 15.d5 .

12...Nxd5 13.Bxg7 Kxg7 14.cxd5 Nxd4 15.Nxd4 exd4 16.Qxd4+ gives White a slight positional edge. Black will have to work out what to do with his backward c-pawn. If ...c5 at some stage, White will have dxc6, and Black's pawn on d6 is isolated.

Zvjaginsev continues to play for his own attack.

13.g4

Now it is definite that tactics, attacks and sacrifices are going to take place, and that there will not be a quiet, close-to-equal middlegame. The last chance for placid play would have been 13.Nxf6+ Bxf6 14.d5 Ne7 15.Nc3 , roughly equal.

13.Bg5?! is a sensible idea, except that after 13...Nxd4 14.Nxd4 exd4 15.Qxd4 Black can jump out of the pin with 15...Nxd5! , and is slightly better.

We are now reaching as close to pure tactics as anyone is likely to find. All the pieces are on the board, there are three 'pawn-takes-pawn' captures on either side, which will suddenly change the pawn structure, maybe in unpredictable ways. Also there are knight-takes-knight and bishop-takes-bishop captures, which might help simplify the position, or alternatively might set up further tactics. Once minor pieces go, there are more chances, on either side, for attack against the king.

There is no time for delicate calculation as to whether there is a weakness in the pawn structure. All that a player can realistically do is to capture something, look for checks, get out of the opponent's checks, and if the player has the chance of a breather, find the occasional quiet developing move for a piece, which is then brought into the attack.

13...hxg4!?

If White is pushing all his pawns forforward, a natural reply by Black is to start taking them.

13...bxc4 14.Bxg7 Kxg7 15.gxh5 Nxh5 16.Qc3 is playable but unclear. However, Black is probably far more likely to be concerned about 14.Bg5!? . With the help of the computer, it is easy enough to be able so show a few entertaining wins for White:

(a) For example, 14...hxg4 15.h5 gxf3 16.Ng3 Nxd4 17.h6 c3 18.bxc3 Ne6 19.hxg7 Nxg5 20.Qxg5 Ng4 21.Nf6+! Qxf6 ( 21...Nxf6? 22.Rh8+ Kxg7 23.Qh6 mate) 22.Rh8+ Kxg7 23.Nh5+ Kxh8 24.Nxf6 , and White wins.

(b) Or 14...Nxd4 15.Nxd4 exd4 16.gxh5 c3 17.bxc3 dxc3 18.Qg2 Ba4 19.hxg6 Bxd1 20.Bc4! , and Black is in great trouble on the kingside.

(c) In either case the ...c3 pin-prick is useful, but does not quite work. Black can get his retaliation in first, though, with 14...c3!? , and any capture with the queen ( 15.Qxc3 exd4 16.Nxd4 Nxd4 17.Qxd4 Nxd5! ), or knight ( 15.Nexc3 Nxd4 ) is inconveniently timed. The straightforward 15.bxc3! seems best, covering the d4-square. White's king is now opened up, but it is difficult for Black to start an attack there:

(c1) 15...exd4 16.gxh5 dxc3 17.Nexc3 is promising for White.

(c2) 15...hxg4 16.h5 gxf3 17.Ng3 exd4 18.cxd4 leads to tense play, and with quiet moves, White will create some pressure, and Black will defend. Black can try for a tactical escape with 18...Nxd4 19.Qxd4 Nxd5!? . If now 20.Qxd5? Qb8! , and Black is clearly better. White's king now being fully exposed with Black's queen and two bishops threatening to attack. Thus White needs to simplify. A possible finish might be 20.Bxd8 Bxd4 21.Rxd4 , and Black cannot quite equalize: for example, 21...Nc3 22.Bf6 Nxa2+ 23.Kd2 Re6 24.e5 dxe5 25.Rxd7 Rxf6 26.Bc4 with a substantial edge.

Maybe Zvjaginsev's move is best, but he still has to play extremely accurately afterwards.

14.h5

Having gone this far, he must continue with all-out attack.

14...gxf3

White will quickly chew up the king-side after 14...Nxd5 15.cxd5 gxf3 16.hxg6 fxe2 17.gxf7+ Kxf7 18.Bxe2 Nxd4 19.Bh5+ Kg8 20.Bxg7 .

15.hxg6

A useful practical technique of attack is to force the opponent to have to make the critical and difficult decisions. Here the most direct method of play would be 15.Bxg7 Kxg7 16.hxg6 , but then there is 16...Ng4!? with complicated play not necessarily in favour of White. This knight move is not difficult to see, and creates problems. Bu Xiangzhi transposed the moves slightly, and after 15.hxg6 Black has several options to make, of which 15...Ng4 is but one.

15...fxg6?!

Watch out now for some heavy sacrifices.

If 15...fxe2 , then White does not bother taking back the pawn with 16.Bxe2?! , sound but not advantageous after, for example, 16...Nxd4 17.Bxg7 Nxe2+ 18.Qxe2 Kxg7 19.Rdf1 There is some complicated play to follow, and Black could easily make a wrong turn, but with care Black has chances of holding the balance with 19...Rh8 20.Rxh8 Qxh8 21.Nxf6 Qh6+ 22.Kb1 Be6 . Indeed, there could be a repetition after 23.gxf7 Bxf7 24.Qg4+ Qg6 25.Qd7 Rf8 26.Qxc7 Qg2 27.Nh5+ Kg8 28.Nf6+ Kg7 .

Instead White can continue sacrificing with 16.Bxg7!! exd1Q+ 17.Kxd1 .

It is highly unusual for a player to make four pawn captures with the same pawn on four successive moves, and the natural inclination is that White cannot give away so many pieces, or indeed pawns. White is only a rook down, though, and material may quickly be recovered. More importantly, can Black escape from mate?

(a) 17...Nh5 18.Qh6 fxg6 19.Rxh5 Bg4+ 20.Kc1 Bxh5 21.Bf6 Qxf6 (the only move) 22.Nxf6+ Kf7 23.Nxe8 Rxe8 24.Qh7+ Kf8 25.Qxc7 Nxd4 26.Qxd6+ forces Black to lose too much material to avoid checkmate.

(b) 17...Bg4+ 18.Kc1 Nh5 19.Qh6 soon transposes.

(c) This leaves 17...Ng4 , irritatingly covering the h6- and h2-squares from the queen, and also the f6- and f2-squares. There is another sacrifice coming up: 18.Bf6! Nxf6 ( 18...Qxf6 19.Nxf6+ Nxf6 20.Qh6 does not slow White down) 19.Qh6 and White wins on the h-file. If 19...Bg4+ 20.Kc1 Bh5 21.Rxh5 Nxh5 22.Qh7+ and mates.

A stunning finish, but there is more to follow. We have not seen a queen sacrifice just yet, and on cue there is 15...Nxe4 16.Bxg7 Nxd2 17.Bf6 .

Naturally Black must soon give the queen back, and after 17...Qxf6 18.Nxf6+ Kg7 19.Nxe8+ Rxe8 20.gxf7 Kxf7 21.Kxd2 fxe2 22.Bxe2 White remains a safe exchange ahead.

If instead 17...fxg6 , White has, of course, the guarantee of a perpetual check after 18.Rh8+ Kf7 19.Rh7+ , since if 19...Ke6?? 20.Bh3 mate. There are two other king retreats: 19...Kg8! forces a perpetual in 20.Rh8+ ; 19...Kf8 is also a likely draw by perpetual, but White has for now 20.Rxd2! .

However wild and aggressive the attack becomes, the number one rule of chess is that if no mistake is made on either side, the end result is a draw. In wild games, as here, it is likely to end up as a perpetual check, while in quieter games it is more likely that the defender will gradually equalize in an endgame.

But the game is not yet finished. Bu Xiangzhi won quickly, and Zvjaginsev therefore lost quickly, but maybe the mistake came later on? We shall see.

16.Nec3

The right way, even though the knight is going away from the kingside. The d5-square is an excellent square for the knight, and if White exchanges the first knight for a good defender, then the second knight will jump in.

White has a more bloodthirsty alternative with 16.Bxg7 Nxe4 17.Rh8+ Kf7 18.Rh7 Nxd2 19.Bxe5+ Kf8 ( 19...Ke6?? 20.Bh3 mate) 20.Rh8+ Kf7 , but this is only a perpetual. Moreover, Black does not have to comply with a draw: 16...Kxg7!? 17.Qh6+ Kf7 18.Nec3 would transpose into the main line after 18...Nxd4 , but Black has alternatives, the simplest being 18...Rh8!? with advantage to Black.

16...Nxd4

Black's problem is that he cannot make any serious threat so as to stew down White's attack. Take a pawn? Attack a knight? That is nothing, when compared with serious mating threats against an exposed king. If, for example, 16...b4?! 17.Bxg7 bxc3 18.Qh6 cxb2+ 19.Kb1 , and Black has run out of ideas, while White has one big idea. There is a similar idea after 16...exd4 17.Bxg7 , while various other possibilities end up with a quick win for White.

16...Nh5! is the toughest defence, adding an extra barrier on the h-file. If White were to sacrifice the exchange on h5 at some stage, an annoyance would be for Black to play ...Bg4 after Rg1. The pawn on h5, apparently feeble, helps block up the kingside highly effectively. The other black kingside pawn, on f3, makes all the difference between White winning easily, and White having problems.

The verdict of 'unclear' is never fully satisfactory, and it is better for both the analyst and the reader to be able to say that either White is better, or Black is better, or the position is equal or very close to equal. In practical terms, over the board, an 'unclear' assessment is often the best that a player can achieve. A player will try to make good moves, and wait to see whether the opponent will make a mistake.

Here 17.cxb5!? is sensible, there being no obvious kingside winning plans for White. He recovers a pawn, and possibly more importantly, he brings the light-squared bishop into play, most probably with Bc4. Then:

(a) 17...Nxd4 18.Bc4 seems good for White. If 18...Be6 19.Rxh5 gxh5 20.Rg1 axb5 21.Rxg7+ Kh8 22.Rxc7 Bxd5 23.Bg7+ Kg8 24.Nxd5 bxc4 25.Nf6+ and White wins.

(b) 17...axb5! sets up counterattacks. After 18.Bxb5 Nxd4 19.Rxh5 we have:

(b1) After 19...Rxa2!? 20.Bc4 ( 20.Nxa2? Nb3+ ) 20...Ra1+ 21.Nb1 White is ahead on material at the moment, and this makes the position easier for him. If 21...Be6 22.Bxg7 Ra4 23.b4 gxh5 24.Bf6 , and White wins. Or 21...Ne2+ 22.Bxe2 fxe2 23.Rg1 gxh5 24.Rxg7+ Kh8 25.Qxe2 , and White is clearly better, such as after 25...Bg4 26.Rxg4 hxg4 27.Qh2 .

(b2) Sometimes when defending, it is best to grab material first, and then return the extra material later. Therefore 19...gxh5! is indicated: 20.Bxd7 Qxd7 21.Rg1 Rf8! 22.Rxg7+ ( 22.Bxg7 f2! ) 22...Qxg7 23.Bxg7 f2 24.Ne3 f1Q+ 25.Nxf1 Rxf1+ 26.Nd1 Rxa2 , and after complicated play, with sacrifice and counter-sacrifice, Black is winning. So White has to reinforce, or at least speed up, his attacking ideas with 20.Rg1 :

(b21) One idea for Black is to pick up the bishop on b5, but this seems slow: 20...Nxb5 21.Rxg7+ Kh8 22.Bg5! wins for White: for example, 22...Nxc3 23.Bxd8 Nxa2+ 24.Kb1 Kxg7 25.Qg5+ , soon giving checkmate, or 22...f2 23.Qxf2 Rf8 24.Qg2! .

(b22) 20...Bxb5 21.Bxg7 Ne2+!? 22.Nxe2 f2!? 23.Bf6 fxg1+ 24.Nxg1 Qd7 is a cleverly thought-out defensive plan by Black. Visually, one would expect that White would be winning comfortably, but Black is two exchanges ahead. The help of the computer suggests that there is still a win after 25.Qg5+ Kf7 26.Qxh5+ Kg8 27.Qg6+ Kf8 28.Nf3! .

(b23) The next, and most secure, option is to set up a pawn-and-bishop defensive chain with 20...Bg4! :

(b231) If then 21.Bxe8? Rxa2! , and Black has successfully set up winning counterplay.

(b232) 21.Bg5 Bh6! sees excellent use by both sides of the two bishop-pairs, combining attack and defence of various sets of pins. After White accepts the bishop sacrifice with 22.Bxh6 , it is time for Black to show what can be done with rook and knight with 22...Rxa2! . Then the tactics continue after 23.Bc4 ( 23.Nxa2 Nb3+ or 23.Kb1 Ra1+ 24.Kxa1 Nb3+ would both pick up queens with knight forks) 23...Ra1+ 24.Nb1 Kh7 . If now 25.Bg5?! , there is a win for Black after 25...f2! : 26.Rxg4 Nb3+ 27.Bxb3 f1Q+ . and Black should win as the two queens are powerful, or 26.Rf1 Nf3 27.Bxd8 Rxb1+ 28.Kxb1 Nxd2+ followed by ...Nxf1, and Black wins on material.

White needs to sacrifice the exchange a move earlier, with 25.Rxg4! hxg4 26.Bg5 f2 27.Bxd8 Nb3+ 28.Bxb3 f1Q+ 29.Qd1 Qxd1+ 30.Bxd1 Rxd8 31.Bxg4 . After plenty of tactics, we reach an unusual endgame of two rooks, plus an extra pawn, versus three minor pieces. Black must be better, as he can create a passed pawn, while White cannot, and Black is more able to create pressure with his pieces than White. Whether Black can win is open to question.

But this is just one complicated line of a complicated variation. There are several more ideas to be considered. Instead of the capture on b5. White could simply try 17.Bg5!? . Then 17...Qc8 releases the tension somewhat, and 18.cxb5!? axb5 19.Nxb5 Rxa2 20.Bc4!? , for example, gives White reasonable attacking chances. It is possible that there might still be an improvement on this.

More critical, with chances of death or glory, would be 17...Qb8!? when White must do something quickly. Indeed. 18.Rxh5 gxh5 19.Nf6+ Bxf6 20.Bxf6 Kf7 21.Qh6 Rg8 is critical:

(a) 22.Qh7+ Kxf6 23.Nd5+ Kg5 24.Qxd7 Nxd4 25.Nxc7 Ra7 26.Qe7+ Kg4 27.Bh3+ Kg3 28.Rg1+ Kh2 is too wild. Black's king hides between White's pieces.

(b) 22.Nd5 Qf8 23.Qxh5+ Rg6 24.Qh7+ Rg7 25.Qh5+ Rg6 ends up as a repetition.

(c) 22.dxe5! seems at first a little too straightforward and obvious, when one would normally expect some heavy sacrifices, but the pawn bolsters the bishop on f6, and can also take other pawns. There is, of course, a pin on the d-file. 22...Qe8!? offers a quick repetition, but White can play for more with 23.Qh7+ Kf8 24.exd6! Rc8 (this seems the best) 25.Be7+ Nxe7 26.dxe7+ Qxe7 27.Rxd7 Qxh7 28.Rxh7 Re8! . The first impression might well be that Black even has the more promising play, as he has kept his passed f-pawn, and White could easily have to sacrifice his extra material. 29.Rxh5!? Rg1 30.Rf5+ Kg7 31.Rxf3 Rf8 32.Rxf8 Kxf8 will soon end up as a drawn endgame, though, and White can improve: 29.Kd2! Rg1 30.Ke1! ( 30.Bd3?! f2 31.cxb5 axb5 32.Rxh5 f1Q 33.Bxf1 Rxf1 only draws), and White, with bishop and knight against rook, remains in control, possibly also winning.

Maybe Black is already in trouble by now. We now return to the game and 16...Nxd4 :

17.Bxg7

Black's kingside fortress is now knocked down into the open. A result, even if three pawns have fallen.

17...Kxg7 18.Nxf6

18.Qh6+? allows too many chances of offering a queen exchange after 18...Kf7 19.Nxf6 Qxf6 20.Nd5 Qg7 .

18...Qxf6 19.Nd5

After the complications of the previous gambit play, it is within range to calculate that White is clearly better. He has not sacrificed any pieces, as opposed to pawns, and has by far the more active pieces, with Black's king being seriously open to attack.

19...Qf8

The only move. 19...Qf7? 20.Qh6+ is hopeless.

20.Qh6+

20.Nxc7 Rac8 21.Nxe8+ Bxe8 is about equal, but White can do better.

20...Kf7 21.Qg5

In this extremely fierce attack, this is the first time that White has retreated one of his pieces. Even this is only to get away from the rook, which wants to run through to h7 with a winning check.

21...Qg7

Black has covered the threat. It even looks as though he might have guarded against the attack.

22.Rxd4!

White sacrifices, just in time.

22...exd4

22...c6 would by now be a desperate try. 23.Rd3 cxd5 24.Rxf3+ Kg8 24.Rh6 Re6 25.cxd5 wins.

23.Qf4+

Now there is no doubt that White is winning.

23...Bf5

23...Kg8 24.Nf6+ Kf8 25.Rh7 Qxh7 26.Nxh7+ Kg7 27.Ng5 Rf8 28.Qh2 should end up as a win for White, queen and piece outweighing two rooks and a few pawns, especially if the pawns have no clear promoting prospects.

Of course, 23...Ke6? 24.Bh3 mate wins immediately.

24.exf5

Now White has an extra piece -bishop and knight versus rook - and he has every reason to think that he has won the battle.

24...g5

Black cannot allow the f-file to be opened.

25.Qg4

25.Qxf3? g4! allows Black counter-play.
