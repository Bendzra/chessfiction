Game 2
A.Dreev-L.Dominguez
Poikovsky 2005
Semi-Slav Defence D45

1.d4 d5 2.c4 c6 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.e3 e6 5.Nf3 Nbd7 6.Qc2

For 6.Bd3 , see both Game 1, Kasimdzhanov-Kasparov, and Game 30, Mamedyarov-Nepomniachtchi.

6...Bd6

6...dxc4 7.Bxc4 is a loss of tempo in comparison with the 6.Bd3 dxc4 7.Bxc4 , and White should have an edge.

7.g4!?

Shabalov's move, and a relentlessly aggressive gambit idea, a traditional thought in a new setting. White is going to attack with the g-pawn, pushing the knight to a worse square, but Black can, of course, take the pawn. White then gains some time for an attack, and this sets up genuine pressure. Gambit play on both sides tends to be difficult to handle, requiring good judgement, good calculating ability, and cool nerves.

One is reminded of a much earlier gambit, giving up the b-pawn. This was the Evans Gambit, 1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bc4 Bc5 4.b4!? Bxb4 5.c3 followed by d4, which dates from the 1820s, and has still occasionally been tried by Kasparov, including a win against Anand.

What plans might follow over the next few moves in the Shabalov Gambit? White is unlikely to castle on the kingside, and so will castle queenside with ideas of attacking on the kingside if Black castles on that side. But, of course, Black has not castled yet, which makes the g-pawn thrust all the more surprising. Does it work? This remains to be seen.

There are several non-sacrificial alternatives. 7.b3 or 7.Bd2 are possibilities, as are 7.Be2 and 7.Bd3 , met by 7...dxc4 . Finally, 7.e4 dxe4 8.Nxe4 Nxe4 9.Qxe4 e5!? 10.dxe5 O-O 11.exd6 Re8 12.Qxe8+ Qxe8+ 13.Be3 , with rook, bishop and passed pawn for the queen, is unclear. This was explored a lot in the early 1980s, but interest died down somewhat when it was apparent that White could not establish a clear edge.

7...dxc4

Black could consider snatching the g-pawn with 7...Nxg4 8.Rg1 . After 8...Nxh2 9.Nxh2 Bxh2 10.Rxg7 experience favours White. If instead 8...h5 9.h3 Nh6 , White treats this as a gambit line: 10.e4 (rather than 10.Rxg7?! ) 10...dxe4 11.Nxe4 Bb4+ 12.Bd2 Bxd2+ 13.Qxd2 with pressure, such as after 13...Nf6 14.Nc3 Nf5 15.O-O-O Kf8 16.Bd3 Bd7 17.Ne5 , White later winning the game in A.Shirov-V.Akopian, Oakham 1992. This was one of the very early examples of this line.

After the earlier attempts at refuting the pawn sacrifice by snatching it, many have played more quietly, arguing that opening up a kingside pawn creates weaknesses for White, rather than Black, on the kingside. 7...h6 and 7...Bb4 have therefore been tried.

8.Bxc4

8.g5 Nd5 9.Bxc4 is also to be considered, but White is not required to push the pawn, and he can keep more options without pushing it immediately.

8...e5

8...Nxg4 9.Rg1 is even more dangerous now.

9.Bd2

A recent innovation. Older games concentrating on 9.g5 Nd5 , and now 10.Ne4 or 10.Bd2 . White has not tried 10.Bxd5 cxd5 11.Nxd5 , snatching a pawn. It is worth considering, at least in analysis, but too many of White's pieces seem too open.

9...O-O!?

It is very brave to castle in front of White's pawns. Still, we should not hastily give a question mark just yet Dominguez has clearly examined this position in advance, and decided that it is interesting and playable. Now Dreev has to take up the challenge.

Dominguez has also tried 9...exd4 10.Nxd4 Ne5 11.Be2 Nfxg4 12.Ne4 Be7 13.O-O-O O-O 14.Bc3 (Lutz prefers 14.f4 in his analysis) 14...Qc7 15.Rdg1 f5 16.h3 with complicated play, favouring Black, in B.Gelfand-L.Dominguez, Calvia Olympiad 2004. It ended up in perpetual check, although Black was at one stage winning.

Evidently Dominguez must have decided that this was not fully satisfactory for Black. White has a few promising attacking ideas after 14.f4 Ng6 15.f5 N6e5 , such as starting off with Lutz's suggestion of 16.Bxg4 Nxg4 17.Rhg1 c5 18.Nc6 bxc6 19.Bc3 , with unclear play.

There are other possibilities to be considered too, and it is noticeable that since 2004 after 9.Bd2 , Shirov has won both as White and as Black against strong opposition. This variation is complicated; there are few options of a quick draw.

10.O-O-O!?

Dreev has played this line on both sides, and is therefore fully aware of what the most dangerous lines are, and also what the safest lines are. As White here he aims for a wild kingside attack, castling queenside first.

In V.Dobrov-A.Dreev, Internet 2004, White played more quietly with 10.g5 Nd5 (actually reached by transposition after 9.g5 Nd5 10.Bd2 O-O ), and after 11.O-O-O ( 11.Ne4 or 11.Nxd5 are to be considered) 11...exd4 12.exd4 Nb4 13.Qe4 Nb6 14.Bb3 Re8 Black was comfortable, and later won.

10...exd4

10...Nb6!? is a possibility, White replying with 11.dxe5 :

(a) After 11...Nxc4?! 12.exf6 Qxf6 13.Ne4 Qxf3 14.Ng5 Bf5 (the only move) 15.gxf5 White is clearly better.

(b) 11...Bxg4! is better, with unclear play after 12.exf6 Bxf3 13.fxg7 Re8 14.Qf5 Nxc4 15.Qxf3 Qb6 16.b3 Rad8 17.Ne4 Be5 .

11.Nxd4

Much more active than 11.exd4 .

11...Ne5

11...Nb6 seems dangerously decentralizing. Maybe then 12.Bd3 Bxg4 13.f3 .

12.Be2

Offering a pawn.

12.g5 Nxc4 13.gxf6 Qxf6 14.Ne4 Qe7 15.Nxc6 bxc6 16.Qxc4 Ba6 17.Qxa6 Qxe4 18.Bc3 Rfd8 19.Rhg1 Bf8 ends up level.

12...Nexg4

Otherwise, 12...Nfxg4?! 13.Ne4 gives White a strong attack, and 12...Bxg4 13.f4! Bxe2 14.Ncxe2 Ng6 15.e4 , as given by Dreev, offers excellent compensation for the sacrificed pawn. White has the better pawn structure, and the abandoned g-pawn allows him to attack on the g-file. Here Dreev also gives 13...Ng6 14.Bxg4 Nxg4 15.Nf5 without comment, but implying that White is doing well. After 15...Nf2 16.Ne4 Nxd1 17.Rxd1 Be7 18.Bc3 Qc8 19.Nxe7+ (not 19.Nxg7? Qh3 ) 19...Nxe7 20.Rg1 Ng6 21.f5 White wins the knight. The computer gives this as equal, but queen, rook, bishop and knight versus queen and two rooks gives the attacker a good variety of attack: 21...Qc7 22.fxg6 fxg6 23.Ng5 Qe7 24.Kb1 is promising for White.

13.Rhg1

By now he is obliged to give up both pawns, otherwise the initiative will fade.

13...Nxf2

A difficult decision to make. Black takes the second pawn, but his opponent will have a dangerous attack. Is the attack sound, or not? Or should Black quietly defend, relying on a single extra pawn? Ultimately, no player over the board will be able to calculate to the end which plan is better, and it will end up as a matter of judgement. A player who has previously analysed the position at home will have a significant advantage, not necessarily because he has a better position, but rather because his opponent has to make a critical decision without assistance.

The move that Dominguez played, taking the f-pawn, is quite clearly better than taking the h-pawn, which gives White a clear attack on the g- and h-files. In practical terms, there is no need to analyse further.

What is more interesting is whether Black should develop quietly with 13...Re8!? :

(a) The natural line is 14.Rg2 Ne5 15.f4 Ng6 16.Rdg1 , but then Black has 16...Bh3! with some tactics.

If then 17.Rf2 , Black has an unexpected bishop pin with 17...Nxf4! 18.exf4 Bc5 . Thus play might continue instead with 17.Rg3 Qd7 18.f5 Bxg3 19.Rxg3 , apparently trapping the bishop, but Black again has 19...Nf4! 20.exf4 ( 20.Bc4 Bg4 ) 20...Qxd4 21.Rxh3 Rad8 with advantage. He has set up considerable pressure on the d- and e-files, and White's minor pieces are ineffective.

(b) This leaves 14.Nf5!? Bxf5 15.Qxf5 with standard gambit play. It is always difficult to establish whether the active pieces have more impact than the extra pawn. Here White might have the edge. He has pressure on the g-file, and if he can set up advancing pawns on the e- and f-files, his bishops become dangerous. A computer suggestion is 15...Re5 16.Qf3 Rg5 17.e4 Nxh2 18.Qh3 Rxg1 19.Rxg1 , but Black is under pressure.

Overall, Black has to work hard to equalize, and this is the purpose of the opponent's gambit play.

14.Rdf1

The only move, but good. Two rooks on half-open files against the king will provide excellent attacking chances.

14...Nh3

Black could go the full monty with 14...Bxh2 15.Rg2 N2g4 16.Rh1 . Again, the computer prefers the three pawns, but human players will tend to want the pieces. Black's pawns are only of decisive significance if he can force the exchange of queens, or if he is able to try to promote.

It may well be that Black's position is playable after 16...h5 17.Rgxh2 Nxh2 18.Rxh2 Qd6 19.Rh1 , but who is better? This is the usual difficult question of gambit play. Possibly at some stage someone will analyse this over the board.

15.Rg2

The only move. White has no other safe moves on the g-file.

This would seem to be a highly acceptable gambit line. White's pieces are more active, and he has particular pressure on the f- and g-files, and on the various diagonals leading to the king's fortress. There is also the problem for Black that his knight on h3 is out on a limb. Certainly it stops White from doubling rooks on the f- or g-files, but otherwise it is a weakness, especially if the light-squared bishop is going to be exchanged.

15...Be5

Regarded by Dreev as a mistake at the time, although the question mark was dropped by the time he wrote up this game in Informator. There Dreev gave as his main line 15...g6 16.Bc4 Kh8 17.Ne4!? Nxe4 18.Qxe4 Qe7 19.Qxe7 Bxe7 20.Bc3 Kg8 21.Nf5 Bxf5 22.Rxf5 b5 23.Bb3 Rae8 24.Kd2 c5 25.a3! c4 26.Bc2 Rb8 27.Rh5 Nf2 28.Re5 ( 28.Rxf2? Rfd8+ , and then ...gxh5) 28...Bh4 29.Rh5 Be7 with a draw by perpetual.

This is, of course, just one line in a whole string of variations. Black forces the exchange of queens, not really what White wants, but White's rooks and bishops are so powerful that Black only just finds a way for equality. One cannot help feeling that the trend of play favours White, in the sense that any slight improvements are more likely from White, rather than from Black. One possibility is 16.Bd3!? , with ideas of a sacrifice on g6, and also adding pressure with Nf5:

(a) If then 16...Be5 17.Nf3 Qe7 , and we have transposed into Dreev-Dominguez, which is fully satisfactory for White.

(b) 16...Re8 17.Nf5!? Bf8 18.Rf3 Kh8 19.Rxh3 gxf5 20.Bxf5 gives White ample pressure for the pawn; Black's king is in danger.

(c) 16...Nd5 17.Nxd5 cxd5

looks as though the position is ripe for a sacrifice, but this does not quite seem to work. Slower play with 18.Bc3 Qe7 19.Kb1 keeps Black under pressure, facing problems over attacks on the king, and finding a way to keep the knight safe. If, for example, 19...a6 (preventing Nb5) 20.Nf5 Bxf5 21.Rxf5 Qxe3 22.Rxd5 , and White is ready to sacrifice on g6. Black has to react quickly with 22...Nf4 23.Rxd6 Nxg2 24.Qxg2 Rfd8 25.Rxd8+ Rxd8 26.Bc2 . It is difficult to assess what is going on with two bishops versus rook and two pawns, but here White's unopposed bishop-pair appears to have the more impact.

16.Nf3

There is no obvious follow-up after 16.Nf5 g6 17.Rf3 Kh8! , and if 18.e4 gxf5 19.exf5 Rg8 . Dreev prefers to gain a tempo.

16...Qe7

Or simply 16...Bc7 . There are so many different possibilities for White to try to prove an edge, but everything seems inconclusive:

(a) Dreev himself gives 17.Bd3 Kh8 18.Bf5 h6 as 'unclear'. The computer suggests that Black is winning, but usually the human player will be sceptical about such pronouncements. Nevertheless, there is an onus for White to prove he can show equality at least

(b) The author prefers 17.Nh4!? , which at least gives no winning for Black score on the computer. The program gives Black two extra pawns, while White has at least a pawns-worth of piece activity, and the human might feel rather more. It will take many pages to analyse in detail, and there are many complicated lines that remain to be given in this game, so we leave it to the reader to decide what is going on. A starting point for analysis might well be 17...Kh8 :

(b1) Then 18.e4 Qe7 19.Nf5 Bxf5 20.exf5 (now Black's knight is in trouble) 20...h6 21.Rf3 Ng5 22.Bxg5 hxg5 23.Rxg5 Qb4! keeps sufficient defensive possibilities for Black.

(b2) Possibly 18.Nf5 , and if 18...g6 19.Nh6 when the unusual symmetry of the knights prevents Black's knight on h3 escaping into active play. Here 18...h6 , giving an escape square, would be provocative. White would seem to have good prospects for the exchange sacrifice after 19.Rxg7!? Bxf5 20.Qxf5 Kxg7 21.Qxh3 followed by e4, for example: 21...Nh7 22.e4 Ng5 23.Qh5 Qd6 (threatening ...Qxh2) 24.h4 Nh7 25.Rg1+ Kh8 26.Bxh6 Rg8 27.Rf1 Rg6 28.Bg5 . Here Black can hold out with a draw by perpetual after 28...Qe6! 29.Bg4 Qc4 30.Be2 Qe6 .

17.Bd3

White delays taking the bishop. There is no need to hurry, as the bishop won't run away. If 17.Nxe5 Qxe5 18.Bd3 , Dreev gives 18...Rd8! 19.Bf5 h6 (adding an escape square on g5 for the knight) 20.e4 Kf8 , and Black is consolidating.

17...g6

If 17...Bc7 Dreev's plan is to swallow up Black's exposed knight on h3 with 18.Bf5 followed by exchanging the bishops, and then Qf5.

18.Bc4?!

White makes a change of approach, and perhaps most importantly sets up threats of Rxg6+.

Dreev was clearly already tempted by 18.Nxe5 Qxe5 19.Bxg6 fxg6 20.Rxg6+ , but it is only a draw after 20...hxg6 21.Qxg6+ Kh8 21.Qxg6+ Kh8 22.Qh6+ Kg8 . Black could even play more ambitiously with 20...Kh8 , but Dreev notes another draw after 21.Rh6 Ng5 ( 21...Qe7 22.Nd1! followed by Bc3 is painful) 22.Qg6 Bg4 23.Rxf6 Rxf6 24.Qxf6+ .

This could in fact be the correct finish, with best play on both sides. Dreev is starting to overpress, despite his subsequent quick win.

18...Ng4?

Black underestimates how quickly the f6-square will collapse. There may be three pieces covering that square, but in three moves time it is White who controls this critical square.

Dreev suggests that Black should play 18...Bc7 when there are several options for White. One possibility is the rook sacrifice with 19.Rxg6+ hxg6 20.Qxg6+ Kh8 21.Qh6+ Nh7 22.Bd3 f6 23.Nh4 ( 23.Bxh7 Qg7! ) 23...Qg7 24.Ng6+ Kg8 25.Bc4+ Rf7 26.Ne7+ Kf8 27.Ng6+ Ke8!? ( 27...Kg8 leads to perpetual) 28.Qh5 N3g5 29.Nf4 ,

which is given as unclear by Dreev. White is a Took down, but he can regain the exchange without problem, and attacking with h4 will threaten the remaining piece. But is White's position encouraging? Probably not. Black can, for example, try 29...b5 , and wait to see whether White can show sufficient compensation.

White does not have to sacrifice the rook, but quieter moves would not be as threatening as he would like. If 19.e4?! (instead of the violent sacrifice on g6), then 19...Be6 , and if 19.Ne2 , then 19...Kh8 . White is not making any progress. Instead White could consider 19.Nd4!? , and if 19...Be5 , then 20.Nf3 , repeating. Quite often this is a fair enough response after sharp play.

Could Black try for more, though? 19...Kh8 is possible, and if 20.Rf3 Ng4! 21.Rxh3 Ne5 with advantage to Black. Here 20.e4 gives reasonable, if slightly vague compensation for the extra sacrificed pawns, but one must beware counterplay. Black can, for example, try 20...Rd8 21.Be3 Bf4! with advantage.

The general impression is that White is struggling after 18...Bc7! , and that objectively White's 18.Bc4 was an error. Sometimes, though, a slight error, followed by a bigger error from the opponent, turns out much better than totally accurate play often ending up in a draw.

19.Nxe5

The bishop must not escape this time.

19...Nxe5

Dominguez must have relied a lot on this centralizing move, but White too can take up the middle squares.

20.Ne4!

The knight is about to arrive on f6 without Black having the chance of playing ...f5.

20...Kh8

There are other moves, but in each of these cases White will reply with Nf6+, and Black replies with ...Kh8 anyway.

21.Nf6!

The impossible square for the knight! Compare this diagram with that after 17...g6 . and one can see how much progress Dreev has made over the last few moves.

Both players have entrenched their knights on the sixth rank, but what a difference of prospects they have! White's knight is on an extremely dangerous attacking square, with ideas of checkmate, while Black's knight does little. Indeed, the worry for Black is how to recover the horse into play. Covering the squares on g1 and f2 from the knight is useful for Black, but in the broader context of play, it doesn't do all that much.

21...b5

If 21...Qc5 , White even allows the exchange of queens with 22.Be2 Qxc2+ 23.Kxc2 Nd7 24.Ne4 , which Dreev gives as a win for White. There is no immediate checkmating attack when the queens are off, but Black's h3-knight is likely to fold: for example, 24...f6 25.Bc3 Kg7 26.Nd6 Ne5 27.Bxe5 fxe5 28.Rxf8+ Kxf8 29.Nxc8 Rxc8 30.Bg4 , and the horse end up in the knackers' yard.

22.Be2

Thinking about hitting the knight, rather than trying to keep up much less effective pressure on f7.

22...Nd7

On quiet moves, such as 22...b4 , White continues with 23.Qe4 followed by Qh4.

23.Bc3

A decisive scissors attack.

23...Qxe3+

Gaining a pawn with tempo, but not altering the basics. Black is about to lose.

24.Kb1

To a safe square, and keeping the queen and bishop on strong attacking squares.

24...Nxf6

After this White sacrifices and checkmates, but if 24...Ne5 25.Qe4 Qxe4 26.Nxe4 Re8 27.Nd6 Re7 28.Bxe5+ then White wins too.

25.Rxf6

With an enormous threat of discovered attack.

25...Kg8

Which Black avoids.

26.Rgxg6+

The usual reason for a sacrifice in front of the opponent's king - to destroy the pawn cover, and thereby to bring the king into the open.

Here 26.Rfxg6+ also works.

26...fxg6

26...hxg6 loses immediately after 27.Rxg6+ Kh7 (or 27...fxg6 28.Qxg6 mate) 28.Rg7+ .

27.Rxg6+

27.Qb3+? Be6! 28.Rxe6 Qg1+ 29.Kc2 Rf7! would be careless.

27...Kf7

Obviously, 27...hxg6?? 28.Qxg6 is mate.

28.Rg7+

Forcing the king further out

28...Ke6

28...Ke8 29.Bh5+ wins.

29.Bg4+

Another piece to help the mating attack, with gain of tempo. Indeed, extra pieces work!

29.Qb3+? allows Black to slip out with 29...Kf5 30.Bg4+ Kf4 31.Be5+ Ke4! 32.Qb4+ Kd3 , and while it is clear that White can force a perpetual easily enough, it is much more difficult to find a win.

29...Kd5

29...Kd6 leads to the same result

30.Qd1+

Back to square one.

30...Ke4

30...Kc5 31.b4+ isn't quite checkmating for White, but he picks up the queen with a winning position after 31...Kb6 32.Bd4+ Qxd4 33.Qxd4+ , or 31...Kc4 32.Qb3+ Kd3 33.Qc2+ Kc4 34.Bd2+ .

31.Re7+

This too wins the queen.

31...Kf4 32.Rxe3 Bxg4

Since if 32...Kxe3 33.Qd4 mate. No further comment is needed now. It is just a case of check, check, check, then checkmate, with queen and rook slicing open the king. There must have been a massive time scramble here, otherwise Black would have resigned.
