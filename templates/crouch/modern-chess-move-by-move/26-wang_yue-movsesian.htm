Game 26
Wang Yue - S.Movsesian
Pamplona 2007
Queen's Gambit Declined D38

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Nf3

For alternative lines against the Nimzo-Indian, see 4.Qc2 in Game 12, Bareev-Efimenko, and 4.e3 in Game 15, Sokolov-Polgar.

4...d5

The Ragozin Variation. It can be reached via either a Queen's Gambit or, as here, the Nimzo-Indian. Naturally there is a mixture of both sets of ideas.

More standard Nimzo-Indian plans would include 4...c5 , 4...O-O , or perhaps 4...Ne4 .

5.cxd5

White treats it as a version of the Queen's Gambit Exchange Variation, normally played with ...Be7 rather than ...Bb4 The Nimzo bishop pins the White knight on c3, but also allows Black's knight to be pinned on f6 by Bg5. This allows sharper play for Black, but in return is less solid.

5...exd5

5...Nxd5 and 5...Qxd5 would be playable, but slightly less trustworthy.

6.Bg5

Continuing the main line.

6.Qa4+ Nc6 7.Ne5 may be a temptation, but after 7...Bd7 Black is comfortably equal. If 8.Nxd7 Qxd7 , Black is ahead in development, making White's bishop-pair of little significance. If instead 8.Nxc6 Bxc3+ 9.bxc3 Bxc6 10.Qa3 Ne4 , Black is equal.

6...O-O

Black wants to play ...c5 without the preparatory ...Nbd7.

The standard main line is 6...Nbd7 7.e3 c5 8.Bd3 Qa5 9.Qc2 c4 10.Bf5 O-O 11.O-O , maybe slightly favouring White. The bishops often drop back to h4 and g3.

6...h6 7.Bh4 ( 7.Bxf6 is level) 7...g5 8.Bg3 Ne4 is the most uncompromising line for Black, quickly gaining space with his pieces, though before the remaining pieces have developed, and leaving the king open.

After 9.Nd2 Nxc3 10.bxc3 Bxc3 11.Rc1 Bb2 12.Bxc7 Qe7 13.Bd6 Qe6 14.Rb1 Bc3 , the general impression might well be that White keeps an edge, but it has been difficult for him to prove this.

The endgame seems better for White after 15.Ba3 Nc6 16.e3 Bxd4 17.Bb5 Be5 18.O-O Bd6 19.Bxd6 Qxd6 20.Ne4 Qe5 21.Qxd5 Qxd5 22.Nf6+ Kf8 23.Nxd5 Ne7 24.Nc7 Rb8 . Following 25.f4 Bf5 26.e4 Bxe4 27.Rbe1 Nd5 28.Rxe4 Nxc7 29.Bc4 Ne6 , I.Sokolov-A.Onischuk, Poikovsky 2006, White kept a very slight endgame edge, but Onischuk carefully held the draw. Less than a fortnight later, Sargissian demonstrated a much clearer endgame advantage with 25.e4 Kg7 26.Rfd1 a6 27.Bf1 Bg4 28.f3 Rhc8 29.Rbc1 Bh5 30.Kf2 , and duly won in G.Sargissian-K.Landa, German League 2006. White's advantage in this was to create an attacking force with the kingside pawns, rather than, as in the Sokolov game, concentrating on his pieces.

7.e3

White develops his second bishop.

7.Qb3!? looks promising, but seems almost unexplored.

7...c5

Movsesian aims for immediate central counterplay.

Mimicking the Queen's Gambit Exchange with, for example 7...c6 8.Bd3 Nbd7 9.O-O , seems promising for White. The bishop on b4 is not adding to the defence, when compared with the bishop on e7 in the QGD.

8.dxc5

White forces Black to decide how to recover the pawn. Other moves allow Black to equalize.

8...Nbd7

8...Bxc5? 9.Bxf6 Qxf6 10.Qxd5 allows White to win a pawn for inadequate compensation.

9.Rc1

White has tried 9.Be2 Bxc3+ 10.bxc3 Nxc5 11.Bh4 Nce4 12.Qd4 Qa5 13.Qb4 Qc7 , drawn in V.Loginov-G.Kuzmin, Tashkent 1987, without much progress. Wang Yue plays some thing far more ambitious, and this, indeed, is the current main line.

9...Qa5

Black needs to keep up pressure on the diagonal, otherwise he is worse. 9...Nxc5 10.Qd4 Bxc3+ 11.Qxc3 leaves White happy.

10.a3!?

This is not the only try, but it is certainly the most enterprising. White sacrifices the exchange, but keeps the extra pawn, and sets up a dangerous queenside pawn majority.

Alternatively:

(a) 10.Bxf6 Nxf6 11.Qd4 Bxc5 12.Qd2 Be6 is about equal. White can gain a pawn with 13.Nxd5 Qxd2+ 14.Nxd2 Bxd5 15.Rxc5 Rfc8 , but it is unlikely that he will keep it permanently.

(b) 10.Nd2 leads to a few minor tactics after 10...Ne4 11.Ncxe4 dxe4 12.a3 Bxc5 13.b4 (White can perhaps try something else here) 13...Bxb4 14.axb4 Qxg5 15.Nxe4 Qe7 16.Qd4 , but once Black has brought the knight into play with, for example, 16...Nb6 , he is equal.

10...b6 , tried a couple of times, is inventive. Play continued with 11.c6 d4 12.cxd7 dxc3 13.bxc3 Bxc3 14.Bxf6 gxf6 15.Rxc3 Qxc3 16.dxc8Q Raxc8 , which is the end of Black's attack. After 17.Ba6 Rcd8 18.Ke2 , A.Moiseenko-G.Giorgadze, Spanish Team Championship 2005, Black should not have convincing compensation for rook versus two bishops, but White's play later crumbled, possibly the result of time pressure.

10...Bxc3+ 11.Rxc3

11.bxc3 is possible, and will give reasonable compensation for a pawn sacrifice, but Wang Yue is aiming for much more with an exchange sacrifice. He is following a line devised by Topalov, quite often a guarantee of sharp and intricate play.

11...Ne4

The only sensible move.

12.b4

12.Qa1 Nxc3 13.b4 transposes, but Black may have additional possibilities with 12...f6!? 13.b4 Qd8 14.Bf4 Nxc3 15.Qxc3 Re8!? , giving chances of equality. It is often best to cut down the additional possibilities.

12...Nxc3

A queen move is absurd here. He must accept the sacrifice, otherwise he is a pawn down.

13.Qa1

Or 13.Qb3 , transposing.

13...Qa4

Black is aiming for counterplay. 13...Qc7 14.Bf4 Qc6 15.Qxc3 allows White easy play.

14.Qxc3

14...a5!?

Movsesian is aiming for a quick sacrificial counterattack before White has developed his kingside. It is an interesting idea, but not totally convincing.

Another possibility is 14...f6 15.Bf4 Re8 :

(a) If 16.Nd4 a6 , White has to avoid 17.Bb5? axb4!. 17.b5 is possible, but after 17...Ne5 18.Be2 Bg4 White cannot trap the queen, and Black probably keeps a slight edge.

(b) 16.Bd3 seems to give White a slight edge after 16...Nf8 ( 16...a5? 17.Bc2 axb4 18.Qd3 ) 17.Nd4 Bd7 18.Bc2 Qa6 19.Bb3 (or, of course, 19.Bd3 , repeating) 19...Bc6 20.a4 Bxa4 21.Bxd5+ Kh8 22.Bc4 b5 . Black is the exchange for a pawn up, but White has the more impressive minor pieces, and a strong passed pawn. Moreover, Black's bishop on a4 is just about on the worst possible square.

Movsesian has every reason to avoid this line. The problem is on his next move.

15.b5

He wants to keep his pawns strong and active.

15...Nxc5?!

This had been played many months earlier by Carlsen (see the notes to Black's 16th). Movsesian clearly overestimated the improvement he had analysed.

It is always difficult for the defender whether to make a return sacrifice after a gambit, or whether to try to defend. Here it seems that defence would have been the better option.

Black would probably have done better in the style of Steinitz, keeping the extra material, and manoeuvring with pieces rather than pawns, giving nothing away. 15...Re8 , followed by ...Nf8, and if required ...Ne6, puts the onus on White:

(a) After, for example, 16.Nd4 Nf8 17.Bd3 Ne6 18.Bc2 Qc4 19.Qxc4 dxc4 20.Nxe6 Bxe6 21.Kd2 Rac8 Black seems to be slightly better.

(b) Or 16.Bd3 Nf8 17.O-O Bd7 18.Ne5 Rac8 19.Rb1 Rxe5 20.Qxe5 Qxa3 with advantage to Black.

16.Qxc5 Bd7

It is essential for Black to develop with the bishop, taking control of the d-file with the rook. It is also useful to try to attack the b5 pawn. Black needs to set up a queenside pawn majority as soon as possible in case of any piece simplification.

16...Be6? 17.Qc1 Rfc8 18.Qa1 Qc2 19.Be2 Qc1+ 20.Qxc1 Rxc1+ 21.Bd1 Ra1 22.a4 was a quick win for White in V.Topalov-M.Carlsen, Wijk aan Zee 2007. Movsesian demonstrates an improvement. but Wang Yue still shows that Biack s piay is inadequate.

17.Be2

White must develop quickly, before his king is in trouble.

The computer suggests grabbing another pawn, with a likely winning advantage after 17.Qxd5 , but the experienced player will be likely to regard this as much too greedy. 17...Be6 18.Qd3 Rac8 19.e4 is White's defensive idea, but after 19...h6! the bishop cannot cover both the d8- and c1-squares. If 20.Bd2 Rfd8 21.Qb1 Qxa3 , even the computer admits Black is now winning.

17...Rfc8

Black has a choice of moving rooks to c8. He is being careful, though, to keep the queen's rook on a8 to help the pawn being pushed through.

18.Qxd5 Rc1+

18...Bxb5 19.Bd1 Rc1 transposes to the notes to Black's 19th move. Here 19...Qc4 20.Qxc4 Bxc4 leaves Black's rook less active than in the main line.

19.Bd1

The only move.

19...Qxb5

Hoping to salvage something in the endgame. Black has a rook and pawn for two minor pieces, which would mean that Black is about half a pawn-point down in terms of material balance. If, though, Black is able to exchange queens, and start his queenside pawns moving, the play might become unclear.

19...Bxb5 20.Kd2 Qxa3 21.Qxb5 Qa2+ 22.Kd3 ( 22.Kxc1?? Rc8+ leads to checkmate) 22...Rac8 continues with wild and sacrificial play, but in the end Black runs out of attack. A rook versus three minor pieces is a large deficit. Here 23.Nd4 R1c5 24.Qxb7 Qc4+ 25.Ke4 Rxg5 26.Bb3 Qc5 27.f4! allows Black to recover a piece, but leaves White firmly in control.

20.Qxb5

The simplest and most direct way of playing.

Lines such as 20.e4 Ra1 21.Be3 are possible, but do not necessarily gain ground.

20...Bxb5 21.Nd4

The dream centralization of the knight, which ensures that White's position is, at the very least, secure, and now White can play for a win.

Is it absolutely certain that he is indeed winning by force? It is difficult to say. Black will have chances of creating counterplay with his extra queenside pawn, and this could easily create problems if he is accurate. White has done the best he can so far, and if it can be shown that he is not winning, then so be it. The onus is on Black to play extremely precisely. The fact that he has to resign on move 32 would appear to indicate that he is not defending so well.

21...Bd7

A choice to be made here. The inaccurate way of playing the defence would be 21...Ba4? 22.Kd2 Rxd1+ 23.Rxd1 Bxd1 24.Kxd1 , and after simplification. Black has no counterplay. White's overall plan is to centralize the king, maybe to d3, and put pressure on Black's queenside pawns. White has two minor pieces versus only one rook, and if a black pawn is under attack from both sides, it is difficult to set up a good defence.

21...Ba6 is more sensible. A computer suggestion is 22.Kd2 Rac8 23.Bg4 Rxh1 24.Bxc8 Rxh2 25.Bh3 , but on the whole human players do not want to sacrifice a pawn just for simplification unless, of course, there is a clear win. They would prefer 22.a4 Rac8 23.Be7 Rb1 24.Kd2 b5 25.axb5 Bxb5 26.Nxb5 Rxb5 27.Bc2 . This position is difficult to assess, although clearly Black is not better. Can White win? Or can Black hold?

The extra pawn on the kingside would be a win for White if Black's a-pawn drops. Black's defensive hopes are to keep the a-pawn alive, and preferably dangerous. White will then find it difficult to bring the king to the a- or b-files. Also, while the bishops would help defend any push with the a-pawn, it is hard to attack the pawn directly.

Ultimately, though, it would be unlikely for Black to defend. Try, for example, 27...Rd5+ 28.Bd3 Rd7 29.Bh4 f6 30.Ra1 Rcd8 31.Ra3 Rd5 when Black will be unable to improve his pieces, not least because if the king centralizes with, for example 31...Kf8 , White's bishops can start to attack the kingside pawns, such as after 32.Kc2 . White meanwhile will be able at some stage to bring the dark-squared bishop into play, and maybe start pushing some kingside pawns.

Movsesian prefers to keep control with the bishop on the e8-a4 diagonal.

22.O-O

This is played not so much to bring the king to safety, but rather to allow the bishop to move, without using up any more time.

After 22.Kd2 Ra1 , the bishop is still pinned. 23.Be7 Ba4 , followed perhaps by ...Ra2+ or ...Rc8, gives chances to defend for Black.

22...Rac8

The rooks need to help.

22...b5 23.Bc2 Rxf1+ 24.Kxf1 b4 25.axb4 axb4 26.Ke2 Ra2 27.Kd3 would allow White to swallow up Black's passed pawn with little difficulty.

23.Bf3?!

He would like to bring his pieces into play, naturally, but this provides weaknesses, in particular making it easier for Black to set up a queenside passed pawn.

23.Be7! is tighter, covering the a3-pawn. There is no easy way for Black to push the bishop off the diagonal. If 23...b5? 24.Nb3 , White gains a pawn with a knight fork.

23...Kf8!

Movsesian keeps the bishop away from e7, and does not mind so much giving up a pawn on b7. Naturally, he will want to take White's a-pawn.

23...b5 24.Be7 is a critical alternative. Black cannot safely breakthrough with 24...b4 25.axb4 a4 26.b5 , as now the bishop covers a3, and White also keeps his passed pawn. Here 24...R1c3 looks complicated at first, but 25.Be2! successfully attacks Black's queenside pawns.

24.Bxb7

A pawn gone, but this is not the whole story.

24...R8c3

White's pawn will drop too.

Much will depend on Black's remaining a-pawn. If this pawn can be held indefinitely, then he has good chances of holding. If not, then with two extra minor pieces and an extra pawn in return for the rook, White will win.

25.a4!?

A clever response? Or a good bluff?

If the a-pawns are exchanged, White should still have a winning material advantage. If, however, White has to give up the a-pawn, and Black still keeps his own a-pawn, the position could quite likely end up as a draw. To put this the other way round, it doesn't really matter whether White keeps his a-pawn or not. All that is important is getting rid of Black's pawn.

This helps explain why White strangely gives up his a-pawn. White wants to stop Black playing ...a4 himself, when the bishop protects the pawn.

25...Bxa4

Naturally, but now the bishop is blocking the a-pawn.

26.Bd8

All part of his plan.

26...Bb3

One gets the impression that Movsesian changes his mind halfway through, and decides next move that he feels forced to make a piece sacrifice in desperation.

26...Rc5 27.Bb6 Rxf1+ 28.Kxf1 Rc1+ 29.Ke2 Rb1 30.Bc5+ Ke8 31.Bd5 Bd7 gives much better chances for Black to hold. Even so, the likelihood is that White should win with careful play.

27.Nxb3

Or was Movsesian merely setting a desperate trap? 27.Bxa5?? Bc4 wins a rook, as if 28.Bxc3? Rxf1 mate.

Taking the bishop by White is more natural anyway.

27...Rxf1+ 28.Kxf1

And when Black has taken the knight, a few tactics mean that his a-pawn win not be lost, and so the struggle remains.

28...a4??

A strangely panicky response.

Black could still force White to work hard with 28...Rxb3 29.Bc6 , and then 29...Rc3 . Compared with positions discussed on Black's 21st, White's pieces are much more fluid. Play would still be delicate, though, after 30.Be4 a4 31.Ba5! , making use of checks and pins on b4:

(a) If then 31...Rb3 32.Bc2! Rb2 ( 32...Ra3?? 33.Bb4+ ) 33.Bxa4 Ra2 34.Bb4+ , and the bishops escape when White wins.

(b) Black lasts longer with 31...Rc1+ 32.Ke2 Kg8 ( 32...a3 33.Bb4+ ) 33.Bd2 Rh1! , and it is still a genuine fight. After 34.h3 a3 35.Bd5 Black's advanced passed pawn looks dangerous, but White's king and two bishops will pester Black's rook, and cover the advance of the a-pawn: for example, 35...Ra1 36.Bc3 Rc1 37.Kd3 Rd1+ 38.Bd2 Ra1 39.f4 a2 40.g4 h6 41.Bb3 Rh1 42.Bxa2 Rxh3 , and while the pawns are level, and on the same side of the board, the two bishops will eventually outplay the rook.

It is always difficult to interpret the psychology of a player making obvious mistakes. A possible interpretation might well have been that Movsesian had appreciated that his opening preparation was seriously flawed, and that he felt depressed at his play, and so tried a wild stab.

29.Nd4

This will do nicely. The three pieces should be able to block Black's outside passed pawn. Even if by some chance the pawn will advance and force the sacrifice of a minor piece in return, the other two pieces will win.

29...Rc5

If 29...a3 , then simply 30.Bd5 .

30.Bb6

All three minor pieces are working together.

30...Re5

30...Rc1+ 31.Ke2 a3 32.Bd5 is also simple enough for White.

31.Ba6 a3

As far as it gets. Black's bishop sacrifice was nowhere close to working.

32.Bc4

1-0 The end.
