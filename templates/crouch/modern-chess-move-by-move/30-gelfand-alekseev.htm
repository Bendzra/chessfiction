Game 30
B.Gelfand-E.Alekseev
Russian Team Championship 2008
Semi-Slav Defence D43

1.d4 d5 2.c4 c6 3.Nf3 Nf6 4.Nc3 e6 5.Bg5 h6

6.Bh4

Only to be played if you are ready for sharp and complicated play.

6.Bxf6 Qxf6 is quieter. There were a couple of quiet and steady draws, with Gelfand as Black, in this very event. Also, a few months earlier, a game E.Alekseev-B.Gelfand, Moscow 2007, in which Alekseev tried 7.Qc2 dxc4 8.e3 b5 9.a4 Bb7 10.axb5 cxb5 11.Nxb5 Bb4+ 12.Nc3 , and play was level, drawn at move 24. Yes, the colours were reversed!

7.e3 Nd7 8.Bd3 dxc4 9.Bxc4 g6 10.O-O Bg7 is the main line after the bishop-for-knight exchange. White is ahead in development, and has extra space with his pawns, but it is not so easy to break open the centre. If the pawn structure is opened, Black's pieces soon become active.

6...dxc4 7.e4 g5 8.Bg3 b5 9.Ne5!? Bb7

There is a wide choice here.

For 9...Bb4 , see Game 27, Cheparinov-Nepomniachtchi.

9...h5!? 10.h4 g4 11.Be2 Bg7 12.O-O Nbd7 leads to a popular line at top level, and one which can also come about via a 9.Be2 move order.

The statistics show that over a large number of 2600+ games, White keeps an insignificant plus score, over the relevant time period, and more than half the games have been decisive. This clearly is attractive for Black if he wants to play for a win, but White too has excellent chances.

10.h4

Naturally he wants to prod the pawns.

10...g4

In a couple of games in Georgia in the late 1990s, Jobava showed a significant innovation for White, with a piece sacrifice, after 10...Bg7 11.a4 a6 12.hxg5 hxg5 13.Rxh8+ Bxh8 14.Bxc4! . The bishop is too dangerous to touch. After 14...bxc4? 15.Nxc4 there is a big knight threat on d6, and if 15...Bc8 16.e5 Nd5 17.Qh5 Ke7 18.Qxg5+ f6 19.Qg6 , Black's king is in serious trouble.

Black can, however, decline the piece sacrifice, giving back his extra pawn. It is by no means obvious why giving up the extra doubled c-pawn should be any worse than Alekseev's return of the g-pawn in the illustrative game. Black was not yet fully equal, even so, after either 14...b4 15.Na2 a5 16.Qd3 Qe7 17.O-O-O , B.Jobava-S.Nikoladze, Tbilisi 1999, or 14...Nbd7 15.Qb3 Qe7 16.Be2 Nxe5 17.Bxe5 , B.Jobava-V.Raceanu, Artek 1999. Jobava won both games.

Let us get up to date. Gelfand-Alekseev was played on the 5th of April 2008. A fortnight or so later, on the 22nd, Karjakin tried something new with 10...Rg8!? 11.hxg5 hxg5 .

Now Black was a pawn up, so White had to do something quickly. A.Grischuk-S.Karjakin, Baku 2008, finished up as a perpetual after 12.Nxf7 Kxf7 13.e5 Nd5 14.Rh7+ Bg7 15.Qh5+ Kf8 16.Qf3+ Ke8 17.Qh5+ Kf8 18.Qf3+ Ke8 19.Qh5+ . Quite possibly both players had worked out in advance that this could end up as a draw, but that 10...g4 would have been discouraging for Black.

11.Nxg4

The obvious move, but 11.Be2 h5 transposes into well-known main lines.

11...Nbd7

11...Nxg4 12.Qxg4 Qxd4 13.Rd1 Qg7 14.Qxg7 Bxg7 15.e5 Bf8 16.Ne4 Bb4+ 17.Ke2 Nd7 18.h5! gives good compensation for the pawn. Instead Black had an edge, and later won, in P.Eljanov-A.Dreev, Sochi 2005, after 14.Qf4?! Na6 15.Be2 Be7 16.e5 (Lutz has suggested 16.O-O!? ) 16...Nb4 17.Ne4 Nd5 .

12.Nxf6+

There are various alternatives, but none seem to keep an edge. Gelfand forces his opponent to decide which way he will recapture.

12...Nxf6

12...Qxf6 is also possible: for example, 13.Qd2 O-O-O 14.h5 Bg7 15.O-O-O ( 15.Bh4?! Qxd4 favours Black) 15...Qe7 16.Qe3 with maybe a slight edge for White, and later drawn in B.Gelfand-E.Najer, Odessa (rapid) 2008.

13.Qf3

White has good reason to be happy with his position. He has kept his central pawn structure, and his pieces are well developed. He has not even needed to worry about the sacrificed pawn.

13...Rg8

Black must avoid 13...Qxd4?? 14.Rd1 , winning a piece.

13...b4 loosens up the queenside pawns. After 14.Ne2 c5 15.Be5 Bg7 16.Ng3 cxd4 17.Bxc4 White keeps an edge. It seems too early to open up the queenside.

13...Bg7 does not quite seem satisfactory either. After 14.Be5 it is White who takes control of the long diagonal, and the knight is awkwardly pinned.

14.Be2?!

Quiet development, indeed too quiet. Gelfand aims to castle kingside, keeping the king safe, and once this has been done, he will aim, perhaps, to attack in the centre.

Unfortunately this seems mistimed. 14.Rd1! is better, keeping the d4-pawn safe, and more generally bringing another piece in play. If 14...b4?! 15.Na4 , and there is no clear follow-up, as if 15...c5? 16.Nxc5 Bxc5 17.dxc5 , and it is White who is attacking. Or 15...c3 16.bxc3 bxc3 17.Bd3 , and if 17...Qxd4?? 18.Bc2 , winning a piece.

14...a6?

A waste of a tempo, and from now on, Gelfand plays superbly.

After 14...b4! 15.Na4 c5 16.Be5? there is no pin on the e5-h8 diagonal, and Black simply plays 16...Nxe4

16.Bxc4 would in effect admit that White has lost a tempo with his previous Be2. After 16...Bxe4 17.Bb5+ Ke7 18.dxc5 Bxf3 19.Bd6+ Qxd6 20.cxd6+ Kxd6 21.gxf3 Rc8 Black has the much better pawn structure.

White can play for equality with 16.Nxc5 Bxc5 17.dxc5 Nxe4 , but Black still keeps an edge after 18.Qe3 Nxg3 19.fxg3 Qc7 or 18.Qf4 Qd4! .

15.Rd1

Now White is again in control. White's 14.Be2 and Black's 14...a6 are both inaccurate, but the end result of the slips on either side has been to gain an extra tempo for development.

15...Nd7

It is also unfortunate that he has to retreat after playing ...Nd7xf6 earlier.

One can sense that it will soon be time for White to start a direct attack.

16.O-O

But first he must complete his development 16.Bf4!? is also worth considering.

16...Qf6

Some more backtracking when one compares with Black's 12th-move alternative. A reminder that in the Gelfand-Najer game, Black played ...Qd8xf6 in one move.

16...Bg7 is a possible alternative.

17.Qe3

Naturally he does not want to exchange queens. He is looking for an attack.

17...O-O-O

And Black has castled his king into some sort of safety, in draughts formation on the light squares. There might be a few question marks on the dark squares, particularly on the h2-b8 and g1-a7 diagonals, but so long as Black is safe on the light squares, he should be able to hold the dark squares.

18.b3!

So Gelfand quickly sets up pawn clashes on the light squares. Black's c4-pawn looks impressive, but as soon as it is exchanged, White can open lines on the c-file and the f1-a6 diagonal. Suddenly Black is on a breezy fortress.

18...cxb3

This is unpleasant for Black, but after 18...b4? 19.Na4 c3 20.a3 a5 21.axb4 axb4 22.d5! White has standard mating ideas:

(a) If then 22...cxd5 23.Qa7 (threatening Qb8+) 23...e5 24.Rxd5! with a winning attack. The most precise line after 24...Bxd5 is 25.exd5 (avoiding the queen sacrifice after 25.Ba6+ Qxa6 ) 25...Rg6 26.h5! , and Black can do nothing.

(b) 22...exd5 23.Qa7 Bd6 24.Ba6 Bxa6 25.Qxa6+ Kc7 provides more resistance, but after 26.exd5 Bxg3 27.fxg3 Qd6 28.Qa7+ Kc8 29.dxc6 Qxc6 30.Rxd7! White wins. Black's position folds on 30...Rxd7 31.Nb6+ Kd8 32.Qb8+ Ke7 33.Re1+! .

Black's best chance is an immediate counterattack with 18...c5! . He needs to keep the bishops in full play. White too has an excellent diagonal, on the h2-b8. Tactics will come thick and fast, e.g. 19.bxc4 cxd4 20.Rxd4 ( 20.Qxd4? Qxd4 21.Rxd4 Rxg3! 22.fxg3 Bc5 favours Black) 20...Bc5 21.Rxd7 Bxe3 22.Rc7+ Kb8 .

This would have been a nightmare position to analyse over the board. After 23.Rxf7+ Rxg3 24.Rxf6 Black's rook and bishop have somehow been forked by an unmoved pawn. After further tactics with 24...Bd4! 25.Rxe6 Rxc3 26.cxb5 axb5 27.Bxb5 Rc2 28.Rxh6 Bxe4 Black has an extra bishop in return for a few pawns. Black has no pawns, but his two rooks and two bishops are active. The computer gives this as equal, but White's pawns do not do not work very well. White could try 29.Re6 Bd5 30.Re2 Rg8 31.Re8+ Rxe8 32.Bxe8 Rxa2 , which is entertaining, but White is clearly not better, and so it is necessary to find an improvement.

So we return to considering White's options earlier. 19.e5! cxd4 20.Rxd4 Nxe5 21.Rxd8+ Kxd8 22.Rd1+ Kc8 23.Bxe5 Rxg2+ 24.Kf1 Qxh4 25.Bf3! is almost disappointingly quiet after the previous tactics, but the point is that White has kept his extra piece, is defending and can attack himself.

The elaborate line is interesting, but sometimes the simplest and more direct line is to be preferred.

19.axb3

Black has two extra pawns on the queenside, covering the a-, b- and c-files, but what can they do that is active? If the pawns advance, the king will be opened up. Meanwhile, White has an extra pawn in the centre, the d-pawn, and this constricts Black's hopes for ...c5 or ...e5.

Black's pieces, and in particular the light-squared bishop, will find themselves constrained in trying to defend the weak pawns in front of the king, and this in the end makes it difficult for him to stay in the game.

19...Be7

19...e5!? , opening up the dark squares for both sides, is close to the edge. After 20.dxe5 Nxe5 21.Rxd8+ Kxd8 22.Qa7 Kc8 23.Nd5 Qe6 24.Nb6+ Kc7 25.Nd7! Qxd7 ( 25...Bd6 26.Bxe5 Qxd7 transposes) 26.Bxe5+ Bd6 27.Rd1 Rg6 , it looks at first as though it is all over with an attractive double pin with 28.h5 Re6 29.Bg4 . Black can, however, swiftly equalize with 29...Rxe5! 30.Bxd7 Bc5 , trapping the queen.

With care White would still have an edge through gradual positional play with, for example, 28.Qc5 Qe7 29.Rxd6 Rxd6 30.b4 f6 31.Bg3 Bc8 32.h5 , and White slowly improves his position, fixing perhaps on the h6 weakness, and eventually breaking through. There are too many zugzwangs to allow Black to survive.

20.Rc1

Eyeballing the c-file.

20...Qh8

If 20...e5?! , White has the advantage, and can even win a pawn with 21.Nd5 Qe6 22.Nxe7+ Qxe7 23.d5 c5 24.Qxh6 . Black thus decides to move the queen out of the way before playing ...e5.

20...Qg7 at first seems more natural, but Alekseev wants to keep open the possible exchange sacrifice with ...Rxg3. Here 21.Bf3 e5 22.Nd5 Bd6 23.Rfd1 keeps an edge for White.

21.Bh2!

Gelfand decides he does not want to allow the ...Rxg3 sacrifice.

21.Bf3 is to be considered, although 21...e5 22.dxe5! ( 22.Nd5 Bd6 23.Rfd1 Kb8 is equal) 22...Rxg3! ( 22...Nxe5 23.Nd5 Ba3 24.Qa7 , and White has some advantage) 23.Nd5! ( 23.fxg3?? Bc5 ) 23...Ba3 24.fxg3 Bxc1 25.Rxc1 Qxe5 26.Qxh6 Kb8 27.Ne3 Qxg3 28.Nf5 Qc7 winds up as about equal after fast-moving play.

21...Qg7

And Alekseev decides he might as well take the extra tempo, with the threat of mate on g2.

22.Bf3

22.Qg3!? , forcing an exchange of queens, gives an edge, but he can try for more. White intends to keep the queen on the board, within the view of mate threats.

22...e5

The b8-h2 diagonal just has to be covered.

23.Nd5

Naturally, Black's c-pawn is pinned. Also his pawn on e5 is under enormous pressure.

23...Bd6

23...Bxh4 is to be considered. Then 24.Qc3 Rde8 25.Qa5 Bd8 26.Rxc6+! Bxc6? 27.Qxa6+ soon wins for White, the other rook joining the attack. 26...Kb8! provides a better defence, when Black is attacking queen and rook, but after 27.Qc3 Re6 ( 27...Ka7 28.Nc7 keeps the attack going) 28.Rxe6 fxe6 29.Nb4 Bb6 30.Nc6+ Bxc6 31.Qxc6 Ka7 32.Ra1 Black is still under great pressure.

24.Qc3

The creaking pressure continues.

24.Rfd1 also adds to the pressure, and if 24...exd4?? 25.Bxd6 dxe3 26.Ne7 mate.

24...Rde8?

Allowing a clean and attractive sacrificial attack.

Black needs to open his defences on the second rank with 24...f5! . After 25.exf5 exd4! 26.Bxd6 dxc3 there is no checkmate. Of course, White continues with 27.Ne7+ (if 27.f6 Qxf6! ) 27...Qxe7 28.Bxe7 Ne5 29.Rxc3 Nxf3+ 30.Rxf3 c5 31.Rg3 Rxg3 32.fxg3 Rd2 33.Rf2 Rd1+ 34.Kh2 (he is not interested in a draw) 34...Bd5 35.Bxc5 Bxb3 . After a breather to work out what is going on once the tactics have subsided, it soon becomes clear that there is no draw with the opposite-coloured bishops, and that White's kingside pawns are more numerous and better placed than Black's queenside pawns.

There may be other possibilities for White, but this seems the clearest. 25.Qa5 , for example, gives an edge for White, but probably not a decisive one after 25...fxe4 26.Bxe4 Kb8 27.Rxc6 Bxc6 28.Qxa6 Nf6! 29.Qb6+ Qb7 30.Qxb7+ Bxb7 31.Nxf6 .

25.Qa5

Again with ideas of a rook sacrifice on c6.

25...Re6

Black wants to add an extra defence to the bishop on d6 and the pawn on c6. This is sensible, but it doesn't quite work.

White still has the same winning idea after 25...Qg6 26.Rxc6+ Bxc6 27.Rc1! (not 27.Qxa6+? Bb7 , and both bishops are defended) 27...Nb8 28.Rxc6+! , etc, with a winning attack.

25...Kb8 26.Rxc6 Bxc6 27.Qxa6 Rc8 28.Rc1 should win for White comfortably, even though he is currently a rook down. Black is about to drop a minor piece, and White still has good attackers and a few extra pawns. If, for example, 28...Qg6 29.Rxc6 Rxc6 30.Qxc6 Rc8 31.Qxb5+ Ka8 32.Nb6+ Nxb6 33.Qa5+ Kb8 34.Qxb6+ Ka8 35.Qa5+ Kb7 36.Bxe5 , and White will eventually win.

It is now time for Gelfand to think about a sacrificial attack, pushing away Black's remaining queenside pawns.

26.Rxc6+

It works!

The computer shows interest in 26.dxe5 Nxe5 27.Nb6+ Kb8 28.Bxe5 Bxe5 29.Nd7+ , but at best it is only a slow technical grind. If tactics work, use them.

26...Bxc6 27.Rc1

Clearly Gelfand was not interested in 27.Qxa6+? Bb7 .

27...Nb8

The most consistent reply. He might as well force White to make a second sacrifice.

27...Kb7 28.Rxc6 Kxc6 29.Qxa6+ Nb6 30.Qxb6+ Kd7 31.Qb7+ Kd8 32.Nb6 wins for White.

28.Rxc6+

Of course, White must carry on the demolition.

28...Nxc6

After this Black has two rooks for a bishop, yet his position is hopeless. His queen is out of action, while White's queen and knight chew up Black's king and a few random pawns or pieces.

29.Qxa6+

Now Black cannot protect his b5-square.

29...Kd8

If 29...Kb8 30.Qxb5+ Ka7 31.Qb6+ Ka8 32.Qxc6+ Kb8 33.Qb5+ Ka8 34.Nb6+ Ka7 35.Nd7 , and White wins.

30.Qb6+

Not 30.Qxc6?! , as with a preliminary check he can aim to take the knight with check.

30...Kd7 31.Qb7+ Kd8

With his last two moves, though, Black has avoided the fatal capture with check. What now?

32.Nb6!

With mating threats.

32...f5

32...Nb8 33.Qc8+ Ke7 34.Nd5 mate would have been an attractive finish.

Or 32...Re7 33.Qc8 mate.

33.Qc8+

It is time for hoovering up.

33...Ke7 34.Qd7+

34.Nd5+ Kf7 35.Bh5+ is also good, indeed slightly more accurate.

34...Kf8

34...Kf6!? might well have caused a flutter in time scramble. After 35.Nd5+ Kg6 36.Qxe6+ Kh7 37.Nf6+ Kh8 38.Nxg8 Nxd4 39.Qxd6 Nxf3+ 40.Kh1 Nxh2 41.Nf6 White would have safely achieved the time control, keeping a material advantage.

35.Qxe6

He has recovered the rook, and still has a big attack.

35...Qe7

If 35...Qg6 36.Nd7+ Kg7 37.Qxg6+ Kxg6 38.exf5+ Kxf5 39.Bxc6 , and a win on material.

36.Qxh6+

36.Qxf5+?! Kg7 37.Nd5 Qf7 allows complications.

36...Rg7

Similarly, 36...Ke8 37.Nc8 wins.

37.Nc8

1-0

Now the bishop goes, and other pieces will soon fall.
