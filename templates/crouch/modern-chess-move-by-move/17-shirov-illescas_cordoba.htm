Game 17
A.Shirov-M.Illescas Cordoba
Pamplona 2006
Sicilian Defence B85

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 e6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nc6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2

We are heading for a Scheveningen. White makes a quiet developing move with the bishop.

For 6.Nxc6 , see Game , Bacrot-Rublevsky.

6...Qc7

Or Black can simply play 6...d6 , delaying what to do with the queen, which sometimes stays at home.

7.O-O

Having played Be2 quickly, he might just as well castle quickly on the king-side.

7...Nf6

Black aims to catch up his kingside development.

7...b5 is possible, but after 8.Nxc6 dxc6 (or 8...Qxc6 9.Bf3 ) 9.a4!? White keeps a slight edge. If 9...b4 10.Nb1 followed by Nd2 and Nc4, and Black's position is playable, but slightly worse. He has lost his queenside pawn dynamism.

8.Be3

For 8.Kh1 , see Game 20, Ni Hua-Jakovenko.

8...d6

This, believe it or not, is a slightly unusual position, even though it seems clearly mainstream. If Black is aiming for a Scheveningen, he generally plays 8...Be7 first, then later ...d6. There is no real objection to Black's play, it seems just a question of habit. If Black plays ...d6 early on, then quite often what happens is that he usually plays ...Be7 before ...Qc7.

There was another possibility for Black in trying 8...Bb4 . Then 9.Na4 Be7 (or perhaps 9...Bd6 10.g3 b5 ; not though 9...Nxe4? 10.Nxc6 Qxc6 11.Qd4 Bf8 12.c4 , with advantage to White) 10.Nxc6 bxc6 11.Nb6 , and White has the normal slight edge.

9.f4

The standard attacking plan. White can make a pawn charge with e5, usually as a pawn sacrifice, or f5, or even g4-g5.

9...Bd7

A small piece of originality in the move order, but Shirov, a noted attacking player, has always been confident in finding new ideas in less-explored variations. Illescas is taking something of a risk, and indeed he ultimately loses.

As happens so often in experimental play, the innovator as Black loses, but it is not totally clear that the line is bad. With some extra homework, Black's play could have been improved.

9...Be7 leads to well established main line theory:

(a) 10.Kh1 transposes into Game 20, Ni Hua-Jakovenko.

(b) 10.a4 , restraining Black's queenside, is also popular.

(c) 10.g4?! is not so effective, though. After 10...Nxd4 11.Bxd4 e5 12.Be3 exf4 13.Rxf4 Be6 Black is equal.

10.g4!

Shirov jumps quickly at any unusual play from his opponent. Probably we shall never find out what Illescas would have been planning after mainline ideas such as 10.a4 or 10.Kh1 .

10...h5

This would have been prepared analysis.

10...Nxd4?! 11.Bxd4 e5 is less effective than in the ...Be7 line, since after 12.Be3 exf4 13.Bxf4 , Black would have to lose a tempo if he plays 13...Be6 . The computer suggests 12...Qc6 13.Bf3 h5 as a possibility, but after 14.g5 exf4 15.e5 Qc8 16.Bxf4 Qc5+ ( 16...dxe5!? ) 17.Kh1 dxe5 18.gxf6 exf4 19.Qe2+ Kd8 20.Rad1! Black is in trouble with his king.

11.g5

11.gxh5 is possible, but unthematic. If he wants to push forward with the pawns in front of his king, he needs to attack, rather than make unnecessary pawn exchanges. Here 11...Nxd4 12.Bxd4 e5 is equal.

11...Ng4

With counterattack.

12.Bxg4

This is positionally forced, Black's two knights being troublesome.

12...hxg4 13.g6!

Not 13.Qxg4? Nxd4 14.Bxd4 e5 , and Black wins a piece.

An old game, G.Fiensch-W.Pietzsch, East German Championship, Schkopau 1958, continued instead with 13.Nxc6 Bxc6 14.Qxg4 O-O-O 15.Nd5 with advantage to White, although after the knight was eventually captured, Black kept some chances. The game was later drawn. A good line for White, one might think, and quite possibly earlier theoretical books might have given Black a question mark at move 9.

One of the most significant influences of modern opening theory is that with the help of a modest computer engine, it is possible to re-establish previously rejected lines. The computer engine suggests that 13...bxc6! is equal, and also that later 13...Qxc6 14.Qxg4 b5 might give chances of equality. By quietly drinking a coffee, and glancing at the computer, it is easy to find a respectable innovation without effort The computer then gives, without much effort on my part, 13...bxc6 14.Qxg4 Rb8 15.Rab1 d5 16.g6 Bc8 . This leads to an interesting and experimental position in which White has an extra pawn, but his kingside pawns are uncomfortably open, and Black has a useful extra passed pawn. Black should probably be reasonably happy with this line, given that the main theoretical problem for Black is to avoid a clear advantage to White.

13...O-O-O

White's pawn stab is dangerous, and Illescas rushes his king to the queenside, in safety, allowing his opponent an extra passed pawn on the seventh, but relying on Black having developed his pieces, and being able to make use of Shirov's advanced and exposed pawns.

13...Rh3!? is to be considered, again with wild open play:

(a) The 'positional' approach is 14.gxf7+ Kxf7 15.f5 Nxd4! ( 15...Kg8 16.Nxe6 Bxe6 17.fxe6 Ne5 18.Nd5 with advantage to White) 16.Bxd4 ( 16.fxe6+ Kg8 17.Bxd4 Bxe6 18.Nd5 Bxd5 19.exd5 g3 20.hxg3 Rxg3+ gives White only a slight edge) 16...exf5 17.Qxg4 Kg8 18.exf5 , and White has a workable edge.

(b) 14.f5 could transpose into the game after 14...O-O-O 15.gxf7 . Alternatively, Black could try 14...Rxe3 15.gxf7+ Kd8 16.fxe6 Nxd4 17.e7+! , and this must surely deserve a diagram.

Sometimes diagrams of Shirov's games can, indeed, look like misprints. Here play would continue with 17...Bxe7 18.f8Q+ Bxf8 19.Rxf8+ Ke7 20.Rxa8 Rxc3 21.Qxd4 , and White is winning.

Black could try to quieten the position down with 13...Nxd4 14.gxf7+ Kxf7 15.Bxd4 Kg8 , but his position does not look fully secure. 16.Rf2 Rc8 17.Rg2 is good for White.

14.gxf7?!

What else? White snatches a pawn, sets up an annoying pawn on the seventh, and starts to undermine the whole of Black's defensive pawn structure. For an attacking player, this is almost an ideal early scenario. The problem is that Black can also attack on the kingside, against White's exposed king, and Illescas soon sets up counterplay. Black's pawn on g4 can easily create problems, and a simpler way for White is to take the pawn. Not though 14.Qxg4? Nxd4 15.Bxd4 e5 , and Black wins a piece.

Therefore 14.Nxc6! Bxc6 15.Qxg4 , and White wins a pawn, and also creates pressure on the light squares with gxf7, f5, or Nd5. 15...d5 keeps play active, but after 16.exd5 Bxd5 17.Nxd5 Rxd5 18.gxf7 Qxf7 19.Rad1 White retains an edge. Simple play in a complicated line.

14...Rh3

Black now has serious counterplay.

The computer suggests 14...Nxd4 15.Bxd4 g3 as an alternative, but this soon fizzles out, with advantage to White, after 16.hxg3 Bc6 17.Qg4 Qxf7 18.Rad1 .

15.f5?

A question mark, even though Shi-rev's move is imaginative and deeply thought through, and leads to spectacular winning play. This may seem a harsh judgement from the critic, but what other assessment could reasonably have been made? If Illescas Cordoba had played completely accurately, he would have been better. If a player with the white pieces ends up in a worse position, he must have made a mistake.

Shirov is a renowned maximalist, an attacker, although naturally when needed, like all great players, he will switch to steady positional play. Here he senses it is time for all-out attack. If over the board he cannot find a way for his opponent to find a refutation, then he has every moral right to play for an attack. If he outplays his opponent, he will win. If his opponent outplays him, he will lose. If both players make absolutely the best moves over a long string of moves, then who knows, he might be winning, drawing or losing.

In this particular game, the likelihood would be that Shirov would have assumed that his attack was good. It is only later that doubts may start to be expressed.

Many of us less ambitious players would be more than content to defend and develop quietly with 15.Qd2! , protecting the bishop on e3, giving away nothing on the b8-h2 diagonal, and waiting to see how Black will deal with the f7-passed pawn:

(a) The computer's initial suggestion is 15...Nxd4 16.Bxd4 g3 17.hxg3 Rxg3+ 18.Kf2 , but this cannot be good for Black, and indeed the computer revises this as a win for White, who still has the extra pawn on f7, and his pieces are active.

(b) Black could also try 15...g3 16.hxg3 Rxg3+ 17.Kf2 Rg4 in which case the simplest for White is to untangle the pieces with 18.Nb3!? . Again, it would be extremely difficult for Black to demonstrate any chances of equality.

15...d5

Black goes on the basis that that one bishop cannot simultaneously cover both the c7-h2 diagonal and the a7-g1 diagonal.

15...Rxe3? eliminates the bishop, but 16.fxe6 is strong, destroying Black's light squares. As well as the advanced passed pawns, Nd5 is a threat.

15...Nxd4!? is sensible, if again sharp. After 16.Qxd4 d5 17.Bf4 Bc5 18.Bxc7 Bxd4+ 19.Kg2 Kxc7 20.fxe6 Bxe6 21.f8Q Rxf8 22.Rxf8 dxe4 White has an extra exchange, but his knight is not so active, and Black has a good bishop-pair. This would have been an interesting battle after, for example, 23.Re8 Kd7 24.Rb8 Be5 25.Rd1+ Kc6 26.Re8 Rxh2+ 27.Kg1 Rh6 with White perhaps being slightly better.

Black's move in the game improves on this, provided he plays with great accuracy.

16.Bf4

At least the bishop is safe, and on a good and active square.

16...Qb6

But now White's knight is pinned, and threatened.

17.fxe6

A radical way of breaking up the opposing pawn structure. Shirov simply captures the opposition pawn chain, and now has White pawns, rather than Black, on f7 and e6.

17.Na4? would win quickly if Black were to fall for the trap with 17...Qxd4+?? 18.Qxd4 Nxd4 19.Nb6 mate. The trap is easily avoided, though, with 17...Qa7 18.c3 e5 , and advantage to Black.

17...Bxe6

A sensible reply, not least because the knight on d4 is pinned.

17...Nxd4 18.exd7+ Rxd7 19.Nxd5 Nf3+ 20.Kg2 Qc6 is to be considered, but Black does not quite break open White's kingside, and White can build up his central play. After 21.Bg3 Rxf7 22.Qd3! (centralizing), White keeps an advantage:

(a) For example, if 22...Bd6 (the only realistic chance to attack the h2-square), White has 23.Nb6+ Qxb6 24.Qxd6 with a much better pawn structure for the endgame.

(b) 22...Qh6 does not improve on this. White has 23.Qc3+ Kd8 24.Qa5+ Ke8 25.Nc7+ Kd7 26.Qd5+ Kc8 27.Qe6+ and although Black has wriggled well, White still exchanges the queens with an advantage.

(c) If 22...Bc5!? , the tactics favour Black after 23.Rad1 Nh4+ 24.Kh1 Rxf1+ 25.Qxf1 Nf3 26.Qc4 Rxg3! 27.Nb4 ( 27.Ne7+ Bxe7 28.Qxc6+ bxc6 29.hxg3 with an endgame edge to Black) 27...Qd6 28.Qg8+ Qf8 29.Qxf8+ Bxf8 30.hxg3 , and now Black picks up the knight with 30...Bxb4 . However, 23.Qc4! gives White an advantage, not just with the obvious threats of 24.b4 or 24.Nb6+ , but also with the sideways threat of e5, with thoughts of Qxg4+.

18.Na4

18.Nxd5 is the alternative. Then 18...Rxd5 ( 18...Qxd4+?? 19.Qxd4 ) 19.exd5 Nxd4 20.dxe6 Ne2+ 21.Kg2 results in complications. The trouble is that Black is winning after 21...Qxe6 22.Rf2 Qe4+ 23.Kf1 g3!! ( 23...Nxf4? 24.Qxg4+ ) 24.Qd2 (what else?) 24...gxh2! ( 24...gxf2? 25.Rd1 Qh1+ 26.Kxe2 Qf3+ only draws) 25.Rxh2 Rf3+ 26.Rf2 Nxf4 27.Rxf3 Qxf3+ , and then a few well-placed checks.

18...Qa7

Not 18...Qxd4+? 19.Qxd4 Nxd4 20.Nb6 mate.

19.c3

Just a quiet defensive pawn push. Not quite such an innocuous move as it looks, though, as the pawn later turns into a queen.

19...dxe4!

This is analytically the best move, even though Illescas later loses. His mistake comes later, and only as a result of not pushing his passed pawn a second time, a move later. Sometimes the best way of defending is vigorous counterplay.

19...Rd7 is to be considered with the idea of 20.exd5 Bxd5 21.Qxg4 , and there is no check on the diagonal. Play is unclear, maybe favouring White, after 21...Nxd4 22.cxd4 Qxd4 23.Rf2 Rd3 24.Rc1+ Bc6 . Moreover, White can continue with 20.Kg2! , as in Shirov's main line, and if 20...Bxf7 21.Qxg4 Rh8 22.e5 White keeps a strong positional edge without complications.

20.Kg2!

White unpins the d4-knight from the king, but it is still unpinned against the queen. This is, however, very much a minor pin, and in most lines White is more than happy to give up the queen in return for rook and bishop, and so the elimination of two pieces which are covering the pawn on the seventh.

20...Rxd4?

This must surely be one of the most difficult positions in this book for the player to analyse over the board. Almost all of the lines for Black lose quickly. There is just one extremely obscure line for Black which would have given him, unexpectedly, an advantage. We need to strip out the more obvious but less effective tries, to leave, finally, the only good move

(a) Black decides in the game to get rid of the knight as soon as possible. The alternative way of removing the knight immediately, avoiding dropping the bishop on e6, is 20...Nxd4? , but this opens up the c-file too early. Play continues 21.cxd4 Rxd4 22.Rc1+ Kd8 :

(a1) Then 23.Bb8?! is an attractive tactic, except that Black has a counter-tactic with 23...Rxh2+! 24.Bxh2 (the only move, but now the queen is no longer attacked) 24...Rxd1 25.Rcxd1+ Bd7 26.Bd6 , and Black just has time to set up a perpetual with 26...Qe3! 27.Bxf8 Qh3+ .

(a2) White has an even more attractive, and better idea with 23.Nc5! , attacking the bishop with the knight and, by breaking up the a7-g1 diagonal, attacking the rook. If 23...Bxc5 , then a diversion sacrifice with 24.Qxd4+ Bxd4 25.f8Q+ and mate next move. Or 23...Rxd1 24.Rcxd1+ Rd3 ( 24...Ke7 25.Bd6 mate) 25.Nxe6+ Ke7 26.Nxf8 Rxd1 (or 26...Kxf8 27.Rxd3 exd3 28.Bd6 mate) 27.Bg5+ Kxf8 28.Be7+ Kxe7 29.f8Q+ and checkmate follows. White again makes full use of the passed pawn on the seventh.

(b) Black would certainly have considered 20...Rd3 , but this allows 21.Nxe6 Rxd1 22.Raxd1 . Black's queen is not very effective, whereas White's pieces and passed pawn create dangerous threats. Then 22...b5 23.Nxf8 Rxf8 24.Rd6 is uncomfortable for Black. If, for example, 24...bxa4 25.Rxc6+ Kd7 26.Rc7+ Qxc7 27.Bxc7 Kxc7 28.Kg3 , and White will end up with an extra pawn in a rook and pawn endgame.

(c) Black could consider developing the back row with 20...Rh8 21.Nxe6 Rxd1 22.Raxd1 , but White still keeps a strong attack:

(c1) If 22...e3 23.Bc7 e2 24.Nb6+ Qxb6 25.Bxb6 exf1Q+ 26.Kxf1 , and Black is almost in zugzwang.

(c2) Or 22...b5 23.Nxf8 Rxf8 24.Bd6 Rxf7 25.Nb6+! Qxb6 ( 25...Kd8 26.Bb8+ ) 26.Rxf7 , and White's pieces dominate.

(d) 20...Rf3 21.Nxe6 Rxd1 22.Raxd1 ends up with a slightly better position for Black than in the previous line, but is not quite adequate:

(d1) If, for example, 22...Rxf1 23.Rxf1 b5 24.Nb6+ Qxb6 25.Nxf8 , and Black will be unable to defend against queening on f8: 25...Nd8 26.Nd7 wins.

(d2) 22...b5 23.Nxf8 Qxf7 24.Nb6+ Kb7 25.Rd7+ , and White wins material.

(d3) 22...e3 23.Nxf8 e2 24.Nd7 exf1Q+ 25.Rxf1 Kxd7 26.f8Q is winning for White. If you lose a queen, setting up a new queen is often more than full compensation.

Finally, there are a few fiddly pawn moves with 20...e3 , 20...g3 , and 20...g5 , which might or might not challenge the balance of play, but which need to be examined.

(e) 20...g3 is easy enough to eliminate when considering Black's best move. After 21.hxg3 Rh8 22.Nxe6 White is simply a pawn upon variation 'c'.

(f) 20...g5 21.Bxg5 forces White to a different diagonal with the bishop, but it is by no means an inferior one. After 21...Rd5 22.Nxe6 Rxd1 23.Raxd1 White is still winning.

(g) Now for the final pawn push, 20...e3!! .

The idea is not so much to try to queen the e-pawn, but rather to open up the long diagonal for the bishop:

(g1) Black is a key tempo up after the thematic queen sacrifice with 21.Nxe6? Rxd1 22.Rfxd1 e2 23.Re1 b5 , and if 24.Nxf8? Qxf7 .

(g2) The critical line is 21.Qe2 Nxd4 22.cxd4 Rxd4 .

This is about as wild an open position as you can get. In contrast with the previous lines here, Black is attacking sharply, as well as defending. Indeed, Black's attack is at least more dangerous than White's, and therefore play should be at least equal:

(g21) 23.Bxe3? loses quickly after 23...Bd5+ 24.Kg1 g3! , and if 25.hxg3 (or an inserted check on the c-file) 25...Rh1+ 26.Kf2 Rh2+ .

(g22) That leaves 23.Rac1+ Kd7 and then:

(g221) White would suffer the same problem on 24.Bxe3? Bd5+ 25.Kg1 g3 .

(g222) White might try instead 24.Rcd1 Bd5+ 25.Kg1 when he has set up a useful semi-pin on the d-file. Indeed, after 25...Rd2?! there is an unusual perpetual with 26.Qxg4+ Be6 27.Qe2 Bd5! 28.Qg4+ Be6 ; an attractive finish, but Black can play for more. 25...Rf3! is good for Black. If 26.Bxe3 Rxd1 27.Bxa7 Rdxf1+ 28.Qxf1 Rxf1+ 29.Kxf1 Bxf7 , Teaching a minor piece endgame in which Black is a pawn up and has the bishop-pair against bishop and knight. It is quite likely that Black will win. This cannot be what White would want.

(g223) Unfortunately for White, 24.Rc7+ Kd8 25.Nc5 Bxc5 26.Rxc5 Qxc5 is no improvement. After 27.f8Q+ Qxf8 28.Bg5+ Qe7 29.Bxe7+ Kxe7 Black has rook, bishop and two pawns for the queen. The queen has no active play, though, and Black should win comfortably. Here even two queens would not hold the position together after 27.Bg5+ Qxg5 28.f8Q+ Kd7 . Black should again win.

There is an old question of chess philosophy of whether there is much point for the analyst trying to examine the position so deeply, when neither of the players would have had the chance of calculating any but a few sample lines of a genuinely complicated position. Underlying this point is the recognition that the annotator would also, before the help of the computer, be guessing just as much as the players.

These days, someone wanting to analyse a game in depth would be able to give a reasonable indication of whether in an unbalanced position the attacker is better, equal or worse. I would feel reasonably certain that without the help of computer analysis, I would have been unable to find the critical line, and would have regarded Shirov's attacking play in this game as brilliant and totally sound. Instead Shirov is shown as having overpressed his attack, always of course assuming that I have not made my own errors in interpretation. Indeed, the computer is of considerable help in interpreting what is happening in a game of chess, going beyond the familiar question of finding innovations in the opening.
