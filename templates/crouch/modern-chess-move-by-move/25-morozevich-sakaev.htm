Game 25
A.Morozevich-K.Sakaev
Russian Championship, Moscow 2007
Slav Defence D10

1.d4 d5 2.c4 c6 3.Nc3 Nf6

4.cxd5

For 4.e3 , see Game 1, Kasimdzhanov-Kasparov, and many other games. Here 4...a6!? 5.Nf3 b5 6.c5 , as in Game 32, Inarkiev-Ni Hua, gives the closest structural similarities with the Morozevich game, in that the pawns in the centre are blocked, and cannot be further attacked by the c-pawns. In both cases, the players have to work hard to make the position interesting.

On 4.Nf3 , the traditional main line of the Slav is 4...dxc4 5.a4 , but these days Black tends to prefer at top levels the Semi-Slav with 4...e6 .

In this game, though, we reach the Exchange Variation of the Slav. Strong players, and of course Morozevich is an extremely strong player, may use this as part of a 'plus over equals' philosophy. As the game develops, White is unlikely to lose, but if he finds that he has even the slightest chance of an edge, he can grind away indefinitely.

A lower-graded player might get anxious about the strength of his opponent, and use the Exchange Variation as an excellent chance to keep the play dull, equal, and, all being well, as a good drawing option. Among stronger players, the player with the white pieces might simply want to take the percentages. The Slav is difficult to play for an attack with White, and it will take a lot of effort to try to find an edge against good opposition, and this would in any case lead to some degree of risk. Why not play a simple and symmetrical line, with little chances of a loss, aiming for a standard 55% score with White without taking up much effort? Morozevich, as we shall soon see, tends to score rather more than 55% in this line.

4...cxd5

Black cannot avoid the symmetry: 4...Nxd5?! 5.e4 keeps White ahead in development.

Quite often, Black players may want to avoid the Exchange Variation, either avoiding the Slav completely, or, if aiming for a Semi-Slav, kicking off with 1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Nf3 c6 . The danger for this approach is that the Orthodox Exchange Variation, with 4.cxd5 exd5 , is if anything more pleasant for White than the Slav Exchange.

5.Bf4

A useful developing square. Morozevich is being careful not to commit the knight with 5.Nf3 , although that move is, of course, possible.

5.Bg5 is worth considering, though less popular than Bf4. White is aiming for a slight edge.

5...Nc6

Natural development.

6.e3

6.Nb5? Qa5+ is a waste of time. Now, though, the knight threat at least forces the opponent to consider this possibility.

6...a6

A few days later, Grischuk tried 6...Bf5 , again against Morozevich. After 7.Bb5 e6 8.Qa4 Qb6 9.Nf3 Be7 10.Ne5 O-O 11.Bxc6 Rfc8 12.O-O bxc6 13.Rfc1 c5 14.dxc5 Rxc5 15.b4 Rcc8 16.a3 , Black should probably be equal. In A.Morozevich-A.Grischuk, Russian Championship, Moscow 2007, Grischuk seriously overplayed his position with 16...d4? 17.exd4 Qxd4 18.Nc6 Qd7 19.b5 , and Morozevich quickly won. However, other lines, such as 16...Ng4!? 17.Qd7 Nxe5 18.Qxe7 Nd3 , would have led to comfortable play for Black.

The quiet pawn push, with 6...a6, is aiming for great solidity, and perhaps later some pawn counterplay with ...b5. First of all, White cannot start an attack with either Nb5 or Bb5.

7.Rc1

A favourite with Morozevich.

7.Nf3 has been tried several times in recent years, although often only as a prelude to a quick draw. Black can squash out any thoughts of his opponent of setting up pressure with Ne5 by playing 7...Bg4 , and exchanging on f3. With such a fixed and stable pawn structure, there is not much chance of taking any real advantage of the bishop-pair.

7...Bg4

Black wants to keep play interesting.

7...Bf5 is possible, with no realistic chances of an advantage for Black, but every chance of holding the draw with careful play. For example, 8.Nf3 Rc8 9.Be2 e6 10.O-O Bd6 11.Bxd6 Qxd6 12.Na4 O-O 13.Nc5 Rc7 14.Qa4 , draw agreed, A.Morozevich-V.Malakhov, Sochi 2005.

8.f3

Morozevich gives his first indication that he is not going to accept a quick draw. Indeed, soon play gets lively.

8.Qb3 Na5 9.Qc2 e6 gives good chances of equality.

8...Bd7

The bishop retreats to a good defensive square, happy that White has no chance of creating pressure with Ng1-f3-e5.

9.g4

Are those kingside pawns going to become strong or weak? Naturally there are arguments for both sides.

Morozevich some time earlier had tried 9.Nge2 e6 10.Bg3 Rc8 11.Nf4 Na5 12.Be2 Nc4 13.Bxc4 Rxc4 14.Nd3 Be7 15.Qb3 b5 16.O-O O-O 17.Ne5 Rc8 in A.Morozevich-P.Leko, Wijk aan Zee 2005, but was unable to demonstrate a lasting edge. The game was eventually drawn. Morozevich tried 18.Ne2 Qb6 19.Bh4 Qd6 (and if 20.Bg3 Qb6 , repeating), but possibly 18.Bh4!? immediately might improve.

9...e6

9...Rc8 is possible, but Black would probably want, after 10.h4 , to play 10...e6 anyway.

10.h4

There is no real point in playing 10.g5 just yet, in view of 10...Nh5 . To justify White's kingside pawn push, he needs to keep going with h4 and h5 first, giving Black no time to respond with ...Nh5 after g5.

10...Be7

To complete his development.

11.Bd3

11.Qb3 is possible, but after 11...b5 White's queen is arguably on the wrong side of the board.

11...O-O?!

Could this be premature castling? Morozevich would like to argue so. Sometimes it is best to think about castling, but only to make the final decision later. Here if 11...Rc8 12.h5 h6! , Black has defended successfully, keeping the rook on the h-file and making it difficult for White to play g5.

White can instead try quiet development with 12.a3 Qb6 13.Rh2 , keeping the guesswork open on both sides as to whether White is ready to play h5, or whether Black has decided it is now time to castle, with no other constructive moves being available:

(a) Black could try a tactical break with 13...e5!? 14.dxe5 Nxe5 15.Rhc2 Nc4 , leading to likely equality.

(b) Black can try 13...Na5?! , but this seems less effective. White sets up some knight galloping with 14.Na4 Rxc1 15.Nxb6 Rxd1+ 16.Kxd1 Bb5 17.Bxb5+ axb5 18.Nc8! Nc4 19.Rc2 Kd7 (otherwise 19...Bf8 20.Na7! is unpleasant) 20.Nxe7 Kxe7 21.Ne2 , leading to an edge.

12.Bb1

With obvious intentions of setting up a queen and bishop battery on the b1-h7 diagonal.

White's g1-knight is undeveloped, but with the help of a couple of moves to bring it into play, it could become effective in a kingside attack. Indeed, 12.Nge2! looks more accurate, with thoughts of helping a later kingside pawn push with Ng3. If 12...Ne8 13.Bg3 Rc8 14.a3 . White does not have a drastic edge, given that the pawn structure is symmetrical, and Black's pieces are developed, but he should still have some chances of a nibble. White's Bd3 move could have been delayed until later.

12...Qb6

In a later game, I.Khenkin-E.Postny, Maalot-Tarshiha 2008, Black was tempted to force the White king to move with 12...Ne8 13.h5 Bh4+?! 14.Kf1 , but this was totally unnecessary. The king is as safe on f1 as on e1. After 14...Bg5 15.Bxg5 Qxg5 16.f4 Qe7 17.Qc2 White had a strong attack, and later won.

Sakaev's move seems better.

13.Rh2

Defending on the rank, while attacking on the file, a useful combination.

13...Rfc8

Black is still holding the balance, but he is defending, not attacking. If White cannot force a kingside breakthrough, then in the longer term Black will have useful chances on the queenside.

In other words, Black is close to equal, and White must be careful not to drift.

14.h5

There are many sensible but quiet moves, none of which creates an edge. If, for example, 14.Qd3 , with thoughts of g5, Black can simply push the queen away with 14...Nb4 , nothing gained, nothing lost, for either side.

White could consider covering the b4-square with 14.a3 , but this weakens the light squares, and 14...Na5 allows Black to keep the balance.

14.g5 Nh5 15.Qd3 g6 is again possible for White, but does not create any real initiative.

14...Be8

14...h6!? is possible. The tactical point is that White cannot break through on the kingside with 15.g5? hxg5 16.Bxg5 in view of 16...Ng4! :

(a) If now 17.Bxe7 Nxh2 18.Bd6 Nxd4! 19.Bxh2 Qxb2 20.Qd2 Qxd2+ 21.Kxd2 Nb5 , and Black is ahead on material.

(b) Black also keeps a slight positional edge after 17.fxg4 Bxg5 , with pressure on the e3- and d4-pawns. If, for example, 18.Re2 Nxd4 19.exd4 Bxc1 20.Qxc1 Qxd4 , and Black is better. Thus 18.Qd3 Nxd4! is critical (White could also transpose with 17.Qd3 Bxg5 18.fxg4 Nxd4! ):

(b1) Then 19.Qh7+ Kf8 20.h6 (there are no other effective attacking moves) 20...Nf3+! wins by force for Black.

(b2) Therefore he has to play 19.h6 immediately, but Black still wins after 19...Bxe3 20.Qh7+ ( 20.hxg7 f5! ) 20...Kf8 21.hxg7+ Ke7 , the king escaping and White's passed pawn being ineffective.

(b3) If White tries to side-track, avoiding checks by 19.Kd1 , then 19...Nb5! is good for Black.

White must not play so hastily. Indeed, 15.Nh3! keeps reasonable attacking chances. If 15...Bf8 16.Rg2 Kh8 , then maybe 17.a3!? , aiming to play Qd3 or Qc2 without any threats of ...Nb4.

Perhaps it is this quiet plan that Sakaev would have been concerned about. Pushing a pawn in front of the king opens up potential weaknesses, and the pawn push is ideally to be avoided for as long as possible.

15.Qd3

The computer's initial suggestion is 15.g5 Nd7 16.h6 g6 , but then the only way to attack would be to try to give mate on g7, and unfortunately the bishop can easily cover with ...Bf8. Often it is better in defence to allow one weak square in front of the king, but without a pawn exchange, rather than try to defend against open lines, such as the h-file or the b1-h7 diagonal. This theme recurs throughout the rest of the game.

The text move is natural enough, but 15.a3 would probably have been more accurate.

15...Nb4

To gain time for the defence with ...Nd7 and ...Nf8.

16.Qd2

He is not yet ready to repeat with 16.Qd1 Nc6 .

16...Nd7

Black continues his plan. A knight on f8, next to the king, is extremely difficult to dislodge, and with knight and a couple of bishops to defend the king and pawns, he can cover White's queen attack. The only ways of trying to attack the king would be a piece sacrifice or putting pressure on the g7-square.

17.Nh3?!

Morozevich seems stumped by Sakaev's accurate defence, and for the time being he drifts around. It is easy enough to demonstrate, in retrospect, that this knight move must have been a positional error, since before too long Morozevich switched around the attack with Ng1 and Nf3 (after f4 with the pawn), but without gaining any positional advantage.

17.g5!? , gaining space with the king-side pawns, looks better. Then after 17...Nf8 18.Nge2 White has improved his coordination. White could start to coordinate his rooks with the help of Kf2, and maybe think either of a central pawn push with e4 or put pressure on the g- and h-files.

17...Nf8

With extra cover on the h7-square. White will have to do more than simply attack on the b1-h7 diagonal.

18.Bg5

18.h6 is again to be considered, but again 18...g6 , followed later by ...f6, blocks out most attacks.

By contrast, an exchange of dark-squared bishops will be of more concern to Black, though it is still not so clear that White is then better.

18...Qd8

18...Bd6 19.f4 f6 20.Bh4 Bf7 gives White more space on the kingside. Whether this is likely to convert into any real initiative is open to question.

19.Bxe7

The exchange of the dark-squared bishop will happen sooner or later. White has additional freedom with his kingside pawns, but this will not lead to any significant advantage.

19...Qxe7

Black has defended carefully, and is close to equal. All White has is some extra space with his pawns on the king-side, while Black can set up some pressure on the queenside when the rooks have been doubled on the c-file.

20.f4!?

The pawn push starts.

20...f6

And Sakaev sets up his kingside barriers.

20...Qh4+?! looks tempting, but after 21.Qf2 Qxg4 22.Ng5! White threatens to trap the queen with Rh4. Black can survive with 22...e5 23.Rh4 Qd7 24.fxe5 , but this is not what he really wants. Here Black could instead exchange queens with 21...Qxf2+ 22.Nxf2 , but White keeps a slight edge. With the possibilities of g5 and Ng4, followed with h6, White if anything has better chances of kingside pressure without the queens.

21.Kf2

It is very difficult to play with complete accuracy a string of strategic moves, without making an unnecessary move with loss of tempo. A perfectionist might well be wondering whether this king move was necessary, especially with the king getting in the way of the second rank (queen to f2 or g2).

21.Ng1!? might be better, and after quiet play by Black, White has avoided the king move. White played this knight move next time round, so the question is one of timing.

If Black wanted to try to open up the game, before White has the chance of playing Nf3, then he could try 21...e5 22.dxe5 fxe5 23.fxe5 ( 23.Nge2 is to be considered) 23...Qxe5 24.Nf3 Qg3+ 25.Qf2 , and Black's isolated d-pawn is under pressure, whether the queens are on the board or off. If, for example, 25...Qd6 , White has 26.a3 Nc6 27.Ba2 with an edge.

Psychologically, it is of course difficult for White to retreat to his earlier knight move, Ng1-h3, and return with Nh3-g1 and Ng1-f3. Very few players would want to consider it, and fewer would actually play this line. Here too it is doubtful whether White would have been more than level after 21...Rc7 22.Nf3 Rac8 anyway.

21...Qd6

Possibly Sakaev felt he wanted to prevent f5.

21...Rc7 is again about equal. See too the note to Black's 26th, below.

22.Ng1

Redeveloping.

22.e4?! is visually aggressive, but leaves far too many weaknesses on the kingside and in the centre after 22...dxe4 23.Nxe4 Qb6 .

22...Rc7 23.Nf3 Rac8

Natural moves by both sides.

24.h6

White wants to weaken Black's dark-squared pawns on g7, f6 and e5. He is quite probably not expecting that Black would allow the opening of the h-file, but the ...g6 reply will weaken the f6-pawn.

24.g5 f5 is at best equal for White, since if he were to try to take advantage of the e5-square with 25.Ne5 , Black can offer the exchange of knights with 25...Nd7 or 25...Nc6 . White would need to be careful about Black's pressure on the c-file, helped perhaps by ...b5.

24...g6

Of course, Black wants to keep the files closed.

25.a3

If 25.e4?! dxe4 26.Nxe4 Qe7 27.Rxc7 Rxc7 28.g5 Nd5 , and Black's knight gains a critical defensive square. After 29.gxf6 Nxf6 30.Nxf6+ Qxf6 , Black has if anything slightly the better of equality, White's three pawns in the centre and on the kingside each being isolated.

25...Nc6

26.e4

He plays to continue the attack, necessarily so.

26.g5?! f5 might look at first to be equal, but Black now even has a slight edge. White's a-pawn push having weakened his queenside. Black could consider at various stages ...Na5, or possibly ...b5-b4.

26...Qd8

Bringing the queen back to a safer square, in case of e5, or, after ...dxe4 then Nxe4. Maybe there is a slight question mark over Black's 21...Qd6 . Was this the most accurate?

26...dxe4 27.Nxe4 Qd8 28.g5 makes some progress for White on the dark squares.

27.Ba2

White adds a little pressure first before pushing with e5: 27.e5 b5 is premature.

27...Ne7

Black decides to be suspicious of opening up the centre with 27...dxe4 28.Nxe4 Kh8 29.g5 f5 30.Nf6 . There are too many dangerous open lines after, for example, 30...Nd7 31.Nxd7 Qxd7 32.d5! . Here the computer gives an interesting tactical idea with 29...Ne5?! 30.Rxc7 Ng4+ 31.Kg3 Rxc7 (or 31...Qxc7 32.Re2 ) 32.Re2 , but Black's knight is exposed and does not attack effectively. The strong human player will always be careful about such desperado tactics, and sometimes they work, but not here.

28.e5

White decides it is time to close up the kingside pawns, and hopes that he can make some advantage of his extra squares.

28.exd5?! Nxd5 29.Bxd5 exd5 would not be so appropriate. This would be fine if he had castled, and his kingside pawns had not pressed forward, but just here, there are too many hits on the kingside pawns: for example, ...Qd6, ...Bd7, ...Ne6, and Black can, of course, use his rooks on the e-file.

28...f5

Black is happy to block the kingside. Indeed, he might even have been thinking of trying to play for a queenside edge, French-style.

The pawn structure is no longer symmetrical in the centre, once White has played e4 and e5, as White's d4-pawn cannot be protected by any pawns, while the corresponding black pawn on d5 is protected by the e6-pawn. Thus White has to be careful.

29.Rg1

29.g5? would be a positional blunder. White would have no chance of an advantage on either side, while Black could gradually make a few useful pinpricks on the queenside.

Morozevich instead wants to open up lines with gxf5.

29...Rc6?!

It is difficult to work out what Black is planning with this move. Maybe he is just deciding that it is equal, and that all he needs to do is to hold the balance for a draw.

A more constructive approach, perhaps, would be to advance his queenside pawns with 29...b5 , followed by ...a5 and even ...Rb7, by now a better square for the rook than c6.

30.Bb1

Morozevich by contrast is playing rather more directly, putting pressure on f5. Even so, any possible edge for White would seem microscopic.

30...Kh8

Moving away from the g-file.

31.Rhg2

A bit more pressure on f5. 31.gxf5 Nxf5 32.Bxf5 exf5 would have been ineffective in terms of winning. Black can plug the gap with ...Ne6, with comfortable play.

31...fxg4

Do not forget that Sakaev has his own ideas of active play. This is a convenient time to exchange pawns, then cement his kingside, and gradually switch the focus to the queenside.

32.Rxg4 Bf7

The bishop is now going to be a 'big pawn' on f7 or g8. If this is required to hold up the defensive pawn structure, then so be it The bishop plays an important role.

33.Ne2

The knight gradually shifts over to the kingside attack.

33...Nd7

While Black thinks of starting counter-pressure on the queenside. It is only because the bishop can defend h7 that Black can release the knight from f8.

34.Ng5

More pressure, but Black is holding.

34...Bg8

Sakaev has built up a strong fortress, and it is extremely difficult to see a clear breakthrough. Indeed, if anything, Black could even be better. If, for example, Black has the chance of playing ...Nb6, the initiative could start to swing round to him.

35.R4g3

The rook needs to cover both sides of the board.

35...Nb6

The counterplay starts.

36.b3

So White covers both squares, but loses control of the a3-square.

36...Qf8

With pressure on the a3, f4 and h6 pawns. It is easy enough to cover two pawns, but three starts to create problems.

37.a4

A slightly reluctant pawn push.

37...Nd7

The knight manoeuvre to b6 has done its job, provoking White into playing b3 and a4, and now Sakaev finds a fresh manoeuvre, bringing the knight eventually to b4, via d7, b8 and c6.

He is not interested as yet in taking the h6-pawn, which is dangerous, opening up the h-file.

38.a5

Morozevich would like to play b4, but ...Nb6 would be a good reply. Hence an extra pawn move, although in structural terms it is not particularly desirable. The run of play is in favour of Sakaev.

38...R6c7

Preparing his alternative knight manoeuvre.

39.Rf3

He decides he needs to overprotect the f4-pawn. The h6-pawn is not too great a concern.

39...Nb8

Continuing his plan.

40.b4

Probably more to keep the pawns guarded, rather than to try to take the initiative.

40...Nbc6?!

The last move before the time control, and not the most accurate. Another knight move, 40...Nf5! , keeps White under pressure, with the rook-pair making it difficult for White to cover weaknesses on the b- and c-files.

41.Rc1!

With the chance of Rc5, blocking most of Black's queenside play.

41...Qxh6?!

This is very committal, almost surprisingly so just after the time control. It is often difficult to establish what is going on psychologically when a player suddenly does something unusual or controversial. Black has disdained the pawn grab for so many moves, but now he plays it, giving his opponent the first chance he has had of a genuine attack.

Probably Sakaev is making something of a reflex reaction after his previous slight error, allowing White to bring his rook to the c-file. If he can do nothing more on the queenside, the only possible chance he has of winning is on the kingside, and so he takes his risks.

41...Nf5 42.Rc5 is safe for Black, but he is not necessarily playing for a win here.

42.Rh3

He must use the h-file, and not 42.f5? Qh2+ .

42...Qf8

Back to the f-file.

43.f5!

A second sacrifice to vaporize a second pawn. White needs to advance the knight to f4 in order to keep the momentum of his attack, but he cannot do this if the pawn is blocked there.

43.Rch1 Nf5 leaves Black ahead.

43...Nxf5

If 43...exf5 , there will be more sacrifices coming up. The pawn sacrifices so far by White have been to open up lines for his pieces, so that he can start to think of a piece attack. The second phase would be to destroy Black's pawn defences, with piece sacrifices, and then aim for checkmate:

(a) 44.Nxh7?! is enterprising, but there is no need to hurry. If Black's pawn can be forced to reach h5, there will be even better sacrifices there.

(b) After 44.Rch1 h5 45.Nf4 Qe8 46.Rxh5+! gxh5 47.Nxh5 , White will win either king or queen. Or 45...Nd8 46.Nxh5 gxh5 47.Rxh5+ Kg7 48.Nh7 with another breakthrough.

It is more natural for Black to keep open the f-file for pieces. After all, White's king is still vulnerable on the f-file.

44.Nf4

From a blocked pawn structure, and following a few clearance sacrifices, all of White's pieces are attacking. All except for the king, which would rather be well hidden.

This position is now critical. If there is any weakness in Morozevich's sacrificial play, either Sakaev will find a refutation, or at least hold the balance, or alternatively his position will fold.

44...Qe8

Black crawls back to passive defence once he has grabbed the pawn, but could he have played more actively?

(a) After 44...Qxb4 45.Qxb4 Nxb4 46.Nxg6+ Kg7 47.Rxc7+ Rxc7 48.Nf4 Black is two pawns up in the early endgame, but it is clear that White's pieces are far the more active, and that Black's bishop on g8 is by now highly ineffective

(a1) If now 48...Nxd4 , White can win a rook with a few tactics after 49.Ngxe6+ Nxe6 50.Nxe6+ Bxe6 51.Rxh7+ .

(a2) To cover the knight check on e6, Black can try 48...Re7 , but White still plays 49.Nfxe6+ when Black has a choice of recapture:

(a21) 49...Bxe6 50.Rxh7+ Kg8 51.Bxf5 Rxh7 52.Bxh7+ Kh8 53.Nxe6 Kxh7 54.Nd8 Kg8 (but not 54...Nc6? 55.Nxc6 bxc6 56.Ke3 , and White will win the king and pawn ending) 55.Ke3! ( 55.Nxb7? Nc6! , and Black eventually draws) 55...Kf8 56.Nxb7 Ke7 , and White is now a safe pawn up.

(a22) 49...Rxe6 50.Bxf5 Rh6 51.Rb3 , and again White is better, although as before it is not absolutely clear that Black will lose.

In practical terms, 44...Qxb4 is unsatisfactory, except only for use in emergency.

(b) 44...Nfxd4! is far more combative, grabbing a second pawn again, but also attacking, and breaking open White's central pawn structure. White still has heavy attacking forces, and Black is relying on the pin on f4:

(b1) 45.Nf3 is a way of breaking the pin, with great complications after 45...Kg7 , a computer suggestion. The human player might well feel more at ease as Black after 45...Rg7!? 46.Nxd4 g5 , breaking White's attack quickly, and giving Black an advantage.

(b2) 45.Kg2! is more secure, ending up as a probable draw after 45...Qxb4 46.Nxg6+ Kg7 47.Qf4 Nf5 48.Bxf5 . If then 48...Qxf4 49.Nxf4 exf5 50.Nge6+ Bxe6 51.Nxe6+ Kg6 52.Rch1 , and White holds with reasonable care. Naturally he cannot force a win in this endgame after so many pawn sacrifices. Alternatively, 48...exf5 49.Qxf5 , and it ends up in a perpetual check after the continuation 49...Qd2+ 50.Kg3 Qe3+ 51.Kh2 Qd2+ ( however, if 51.Kh4 , White wins).

One gets the feeling through many of Morozevich's games that while he is in his element over the board in such complicated tactical play, few others can stand the pace.

45.Bxf5

White is more than happy to block the f-file.

45...exf5

Black elects to keep as many pawns as possible on the kingside.

45...gxf5 weakens the g-file and the h5-square.

46.Rc5

White has in compensation the strong passed e-pawn, and later in the game this decides the result First he consolidates his c-file.

46.e6? Ne5! 47.Rxc7 Ng4+ is premature, though.

46...Rd8?!

A more active try is 46...Ne7 with difficult play. Black's idea is to open the valve with the knight, allowing an exchange of rooks, and then counterplay with ...Qa4. Later, Black will be able to close the valve with ...Nc6. The critical line is 47.Nfe6! Rxc5 48.bxc5 :

(a) There are then various sacrificial threats on h7. If Black tries 48...h5 , White keeps the initiative flowing with 49.Ne4! f4 50.Nf6 ( 50.Qxf4? Nf5 favours Black, while 50.Nd6 Qd7 is less clear than the main line) 50...Qf7 (or 50...Bxe6 51.Nxe8 Bxh3 52.Nf6 with advantage to White) 51.Ng5 Qf8 52.Qxf4 , and White is pressing hard.
