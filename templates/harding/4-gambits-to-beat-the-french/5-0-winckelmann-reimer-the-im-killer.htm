WINCKELMANN-REIMER: THE IM-KILLER

Regular readers of CC magazines and periodicals such as the German Gambit Revue will have noticed that many of the theoretical articles in them concern sidelines not often seen in over-the-board (OTB) play. One obvious case in point is the Winckelmann-Reimer Gambit against the French Winawer:

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 dxe4 and now 6.f3!?

Clearly this gambit is a member of what one might call the “Extended Blackmar Diemer Gambit Family”, which also includes hybrids like 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Be3 dxe4 4.f3 which we considered in an earlier chapter and, against the Caro-Kann, 1.e4 c6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 dxe4 4.f3 . White offers a pawn in order to obtain an initiative based on open lines and accelerated development; Black can be caught in two minds between attempted refutation by acceptance and a policy of seeking to return material for a counter attack. However, transposition from the BDG itself is improbable in this case.

The Winckelmann-Reimer Gambit is not mentioned in ECO (even the 1997 3rd edition, which devotes several rows to the traditional 6.Qg4 which nobody plays any more!). This gambit was invented in Germany and it is largely there that players are aware of it although the circle of its exponents is growing, especially in correspondence play. Not for nothing is it called “The IM-Killer”.

You will also look in vain for 6.f3 in such books as BC02, The Complete French (Psakhis), Winning With The French (Uhlmann), Mastering The French (McDonald & Harley), New Ideas in the French (Short), Franzdsische Verteidigung (Slotnik) or even French Defense (Minev) — a book which is usually a good source for other new, old and offbeat ideas. Not surprisingly it is also not mentioned in the older French Winawer: Modern and Auxiliary Lines (Moles and Wicker). Grandmaster Suetin's book French Defence at least mentions it, without analysis, saying: “The gambit continuation 6.f3!? has hardly been seriously tested.” In fact this is really a quote from Bobby Fischer's notes to the game he lost against V.Kovacevic (Rovinj-Zagreb 1970), a game in which Fischer played the traditional 6.Qg4 and regretted it.

Only the admirable John Watson passes the test. On page 117 of the 2nd (1996) edition of his Play The French he does recommend a line for Black. Later in this chapter we shall look in some detail at the line he suggests.

Readers who want to look in detail at the early published material on the Winckelmann Riemer Gambit should consult the theory articles by Konikowski in Fernschach 11/1993 and 12/1994 and the longer article in the Ukrainian-published CC lnformator 3. Since then Winckelmann wrote a book about the gambit (basically a database dump) which is now in its second edition, and almost every issue of Gambit Revue also has articles and games about it. Grandmaster Gutman has also taken it seriously with analysis of it in the respected German theory periodical Schach-Archiv, including a very detailed study of all the main lines given the state of knowledge in 1997. You will find a lot of references to this and you can find all Gutman's articles on the line if you buy the Schach-Archiv CD.

There are far too many published games to review in detail, or even to give more than a percentage in the Study Material section, so I have taken a more analytical approach here and those who want more examples can look in databases or hunt out the references given above.

First, however, I shall begin with a game which I played in the 1995 Irish Championship (OTB) against an opponent who had been “difficult” for me in the past, especially as we had sometimes analysed the French Defence together. So in the past I had avoided 1.e4 against him but on this occasion, having been the last to finish the previous round the night before, I knew the pairings. Already on the way home, I remembered those articles in the CC publications and hatched a plot to surprise him with a line that was very little known indeed. One of my main objectives was to bring him into time trouble, which — together with a strong preference for the initiative (which the gambit would deny him) — is his chief weakness.

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 dxe4 6.f3 Nc6

Black, as I expected, spent a lot of time on his reply — probably trying to find an answer that I would not have prepared deeply. He certainly succeeded on that count and my answer is perhaps not best.

7.Bb5!?

Not in Winckelmann's book or other analysis of the gambit that I have seen. 6...Nc6 is rarely played and I later found out that Winckelmann had replied 7.a4 in one game back in 1988.

7...Bd7 8.Qe2

Gutman evidently saw the present game in Gambit Revue 4/1997 and he recommends that White play 8.Nh3! .

8...Qh4+ 9.g3 exf3 10.Nxf3 Qh5 11.O-O a6?!

If 11...O-O-O? 12.Ng5! (threatening f7) gives a big advantage, but 11...Nf6!? is probably better.

12.Bd3 Nf6 13.Rb1

I found it hard to decide whether this move or 13.c4!? should be preferred, and thought too long myself.

13...Rb8 14.c4 O-O 15.Bf4!? e5!?

15...Qa5 would allow me a kingside attack by unpinning the f3-knight. Also 15...Rfc8 is obviously too passive, but 15...Ne8 came into consideration.

16.dxe5 Rfe8 17.Qd2 Bg4!?

After 17...Ng4!? 18.Ng5! h6 19.Bh7+ the complications seem to favor White.

18.Qc3?

A misjudgment. I should have continued 18.exf6! Bxf3 19.Qf2 with the dual idea to recapture on f3 and also fxg7.

18...Nd7!

Now White is probably lost. I had spent too much time examining 18...Bxf3 when I intended 19.exf6 unclear, since 19.Rxf3 Ng4! (not 19...Qxf3?? 20.Bxh7+ ) gives Black an attack.

19.Ng5 Nf8 20.Rbe1 h6 21.Nf3 Ng6 22.e6

The only move to keep White in the game.

22...Nxf4 23.exf7+ Kxf7?!

Black tries to be too clever. Simply 23...Qxf7 24.gxf4 Qxf4 is very good for Black.

24.Nh4!!

This threatens to win the queen by Bg6+ and is an awkward move for Black to meet as he was already getting short of time. Not 24.gxf4 Bxf3 25.Rxe8 Qg4+ when Black would win easily.

24...Qc5+ 25.Kh1 Rxe1 26.Qxe1 g5

27.Qe4!

The move Tom had missed; I manage to save my piece after all and Black's reply is probably not the best.

27...Ne5?! 28.gxf4 Nxd3 29.Qg6+ Ke7 30.Qh7+ Kd6?

In mutual time trouble (we had to reach move 40), Black goes wrong. If 30...Kf8 31.fxg5+ wins in view of 31...Nf2+ 32.Rxf2+ Qxf2 33.Ng6+ Ke8 34.Qe7 mate, but maybe 30...Ke6!? is playable.

31.Qxd3+ Kc6 32.fxg5 Re8 33.Rf6+ Re6 34.Qe4+ Kd6 35.Rxe6+ Bxe6 36.gxh6 Bxc4 37.Nf5+ Kd7 38.Qd4+ Bd5+ 39.Kg1 Qxd4+ 40.Nxd4 Be4 41.Nf3!

There is time to think again and Black has a lost endgame.

41...Bxc2 42.Ng5 b5 43.Kf2 c5 44.Ke3 Ke7 45.Kd2 Bf5 46.h7 Bxh7 47.Nxh7 a5 48.Ng5 b4?

The win takes a bit longer after 48...a4 49.Ne4 b4 50.Nxc5 bxa3 51.Kc2 Kf6 52.Kb1 Kf5 53.Nd3 Kg4 54.Nf2+ Kf3 55.h3

49.a4! 1-0

Of course that game has little meaning for theory (after move 13) but it does show the practical problems that White can create and how hard it is for Black to neutralise the pressure even when he appears to be getting on top.

b) 11...Qe7 doesn't equalise either: 12.a4!? (Hergert gave 12.Qxc7 O-O 13.Bg5 Rfe8 = but White is better after 14.Qg3 ) 12...O-O-O 13.Ba3 Qe8 14.c4 with initiative to White — Gutman.

12.Qf2 Ndf6

A new move but White will later exploit the offside position of this knight. Alternatives were analysed in Schach-Archiv.

a) 12...Qe7 13.a4 O-O-O 14.Ba3 Qe8 ( 14...Qf6? 15.Qe1 ) 15.Qxf7 ±, and

b) 12...O-O 13.a4 Nhf6 14.Bg5 h6 15.Qh4 hxg5 16.Nxg5 Re8 17.Bh7+ Kf8 18.Bg6 Ke7 (Winckelmann-Zitzewitz, corr., 1997) 19.Nxf7!? Qc8 20.Rae1 Nf8 21.Ne5 Nxg6 22.Nxg6+ Kd7 23.c4 with a dangerous attack.

13.Be2 O-O

This seems to allow White an automatic attack so the unclear 13...Qd7 needs to be examined. To prevent Black castling queenside safely, some such continuation as 14.Qh4 Qd5 15.Bf3 Qa5 16.Bxc6+ was probably in White's notebook.

14.Qh4 Qd5 15.Bf3 Qa5 16.Bd2 Bxf3 17.Rxf3 Qa4 18.g4 Qxc2

Black apparently counted on this active queen invasion for counterplay but he has misjudged the whole line.

19...Qxd2 20.h6

20...Qxh6!?

Apparently defeatist but after 20...Ne8! 21.hxg7 Nxg7 22.Kh1 seems to be the critical continuation. After 22...f6 23.Rg1 the concentration of heavy White pieces on the kingside give him a winning attack. The impression in the diagram that the White king is more exposed than its opposite number is an optical illusion which Kh1 rectifies: rooks are very strong pieces, especially when attacking, and Black's do not get a chance to shine.

21.Qxh6 gxh6 22.Rxf6

Black's three pawns are broken and do not compensate for the missing knight.

22...Kg7 23.Rf3 c5

In order to get counterplay by creating a passed pawn duo.

33.c7 Rc8 34.Ra6+ Kf5

Hoping to infiltrate the king, but White does not panic. 34...Kg7 35.Kf1 and White can use his king in the blockade.

35.Rxh6 e3 36.Rh5+ Ke6 37.Nd3!

Also worth a brief mention:

a) There has been relatively little experience with 6...Qd5!? and White has tried several replies. The most encouraging example is: 7.Rb1 a6 8.c4 Qf5 9.Rb3 Nf6 10.g4 Qg6 11.h4 exf3 12.Nxf3 Nxg4 13.Bd3 f5 14.Bf4 c6 15.Bd6 h5 16.Qe2 Qf6 17.O-O g6 18.Re1 a5 19.d5 a4 20.Rbb1 Na6 21.Qd2 Rg8 22.dxe6 Rg7 23.c5 Nc7 24.Bc4 Nd5 25.Bxd5 cxd5 26.Rb6 Re7 27.Bxe7 Qxe7 28.Rd6 1-0, Oller-Streichenbach, thematic corr. event, 1993.

b) 6...c6 7.Nh3 Nf6 ( 7...e5 8.Ng5 exd4 9.Bc4 Nh6 10.cxd4 exf3 11.O-O O-O 12.Nxf3 ) 8.fxe4 Nxe4 9.Qg4 Qa5 10.Qxg7 Qxc3+ ( 10...Rf8 11.Bd3 Qxc3+ 12.Ke2 Qxa1 13.Qxf8+ Kxf8 14.Bh6+ 11.Kd1 Rf8 12.Rb1 Nd7 13.Bd3 with initiative to White (Gutman).

Before proceeding with the analysis it is worth quoting the conclusion (in Gambit Revue 1/98) of one of the gambit's most persistent critics, Volker Hergert. He believes that 6...Bd7 gives Black equality without problems while 6...c5 could be the refutation. To this we can add that the situation is also not clear after 6...e5 which may turn out to be strong.
