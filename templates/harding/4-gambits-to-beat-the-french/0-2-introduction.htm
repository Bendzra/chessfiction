Why Gambits?

The French Defence, 1.e4 e6 , is one of the most solid and resilient defences that Black can play and it is effective at all levels of chess skill. So White is constantly looking for new ways of meeting it.

One way to throw Black off his path of a slow strategic buildup is to offer an early gambit. This book discusses several — some sounder than others! It is largely based on my experiences over 20 years of playing the French with Black both over-the-board and in correspondence events, but I have also ventured a few of these gambits with White in my time. This introduction will feature several examples of my own games.

My experience of feedback from readers during my career as a writer and editor has also convinced me that there is great interest in gambits among the general chess reading and playing public, which is not always reflected in the writings of mainstream theoreticians who chiefly play in grandmaster tournaments and other master events such as the professional leagues in Europe. Some magazines deal with unusual openings on a regular basis, e.g., Tom Purser's BDG World (on the Blackmar-Diemer Gambit and its offshoots), Gambit Revue, Kaissiber and Randspringer (all in German), Myers Opening Bulletin, Orang Utan Magazine, etc., so there is clearly an interest in the byways of theory. Many of the gambits discussed in these magazines get tested in unofficial thematic tournaments contested by correspondence enthusiasts.

This book is not intended for grandmasters seeking a new weapon to play at Linares or in world championship qualifying events — but even they might find something here that will help them garner some easy points in the early rounds of a weekend tournament!

Master players tend to pour scorn on gambits; if they think the line is refuted, naturally they won't play it. However, few take the time and trouble to research the gambits in question and see if the “refutation” is really valid.

That is part of the reason why correspondence games tend to feature strongly in this and other books on gambits. Apart from the thematic tournaments devoted to particular openings (in which games with White and Black are played simultaneously from a set position against each opponent) many postal and email players venture gambits like the ones in this book in open competitive events too. On the basis of their own analysis and of games not often to be found in standard theoretical works and the publications read by over-the-board (OTB) players, the CC specialists find moves which are sometimes improvements, but are often at least as good as the moves in the books, with added surprise value.

Some of these improvements even turn out to be major theoretical discoveries. The final section of this book deals with the Winckelmann-Reimer Gambit which has literally “come from nowhere” to be a major variation that can really upset regular players of the Winawer Variation. Especially when you find that the blinkered folks who compile “ECO” left it out of the 3rd (1997) edition of volume C altogether! After 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 dxe4 they devote several lines of play to 6.Qg4 , which nobody plays any more, and there is not even a footnote on 6.f3!? .

Gambits in the French

There are of course more gambits against the French Defence than can be fitted in a book of this size. Some are such a part of mainstream theory that I decided to leave them out altogether as they do not fit in with the theme of this book.

The most obvious example is the Albin-Chatard-Alekhine Attack which arises in the Classical French after 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Bg5 Be7 5.e5 Nfd7 6.h4 . Black, 95% of the time, does not accept this gambit by 6...Bxg5 , so any extensive treatment would take us way off the point. However you will find an example in the first chapter, on Lesser Gambits.

Another line that I decided not to include is the gambit in the Tarrasch Variation that arises after 3.Nd2 Nf6 4.e5 Nfd7 5.Bd3 (or first 5.Ngf3 ) 5...c5 6.c3 Nc6 7.Ngf3 , when Black has the option of capturing on d4. Again, Black has so many ways of declining or avoiding that gambit that it simply doesn't fit. However, one example of it is given later in this introduction.

In this book I wanted to concentrate on gambits which are almost impossible for Black to avoid if he plays the French Defence at all.

I made a slight exception for the Milner-Barry Gambit, because it is historically one of the most important and most controversial of all the French Gambits; I simply couldn't resist taking a new look to see how it really stands today. It is true that after 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3 Black can avoid the Milner-Barry by not adopting the set-up with 4...Nc6 and 5...Qb6 but below master level the alternatives are not often seen, and anyway the Milner-Barry can arise by transposition via 5...Bd7 as we shall see later on.

The title of this book “Four Gambits to Beat the French” refers to the four gambits which are hardest to avoid and which, also being quite sound, therefore present the greatest threat to the French Defence player, that is: the 3.Be3 Alapin Gambit, the Wing Gambit, the Milner-Barry and the Winckelmann-Reimer.

Other gambits are also included in this book in the first chapter but with much less detail. In most cases you will find supplementary games for study in the back of the book. Such gambits include ones that are either very rare, definitely unsound or which are easily avoided by declining them.

Gambits For Fun

It must be remembered that chess is a game which many people play for fun or the love of it rather than for victory at all costs or rating points. They also like to read about and analyse openings which are fun, and which are within the ability of the non-GM to find something new. It's not likely that a new move discovered by an average player in a strategic main line of the Ruy Lopez (Spanish Opening) or Queen's Gambit Declined is an improvement on what the professionals have found in 75 years or more of top-level investigation. In an opening line where tactics play a large role, it's more likely that you or I can make a discovery — with or without the aid of a computer sidekick.

Even players who mostly play master class or elite events can benefit by playing some more relaxed gambit games, maybe by email for a quicker finish. Online blitz play with Internet chess servers is also very popular these days. Gambits can be very effective in that context, especially with the fast rate of play and the fact that you can (though perhaps it is cheating, and bad for training the memory) have a reference work to hand when playing the early moves.

Gambits To Win

There is a practical side to it, too: well-chosen gambits can be a winning weapon in CC, even at high levels. This is especially true where they can be used to enforce a difficult series of material-versus-position judgment calls on opponents, even (perhaps especially!) when they are computer-aided. Most programs don't evaluate positional gambits accurately, reckoning most variations favorable to the material-taker.

A typical scenario (with or without computer) is as follows.

Move 5: I can refute this gambit in half a dozen ways!

Move 10: Maybe my opponent has slight compensation but I'll soon neutralise it.

Move 15: Maybe one of the other refutations was clearer.

Move 20: X is the best move, but if I play it my opponent can force perpetual check/a level endgame.

Move 25: I definitely underestimated his attack!

Move 30: I resign.

Here is an example from my own experience.

1.e4 e6 2.Nf3 d5 3.e5 c5 4.b4 cxb4 5.a3 Nc6 6.axb4 Bxb4 7.c3 Ba5

7...Be7 may be safer.

8.d4 f6!

In this case, directly challenging the apex of the pawn chain seems justified. Otherwise Black will be castling into danger later. White must keep control of e5 and in some cases the exchange sacrifice ...Rf8xNf3 will be a weapon to undermine that strongpoint.

9.Bf4 Bc7

Another possibility is 9...fxe5 10.Nxe5 Nxe5 ( 10...Nge7 11.Bd3 Nxd4? 12.Qh5+ ) 11.Bxe5 Kf8 but it is probably better to hold ...fxe5 in reserve.

10.Bd3

Alternatives are 10.Na3 Nge7 ( 10...fxe5!? ) 11.Nb5 Ng6 12.Bg3 fxe5 and 10.Bb5 , but White usually wants this bishop on the b1-h7 diagonal.

10...fxe5

10...Nge7 may be better but Black wanted to open the f-file for counterplay.

11.dxe5 Nge7

One idea now is ...Nf5-h4, which obviously must not be allowed.

12.Qc2

In view of the continuation, this may seem to waste a tempo but a weakness is induced.

I also considered 12.Na3 O-O 13.Bxh7+?! ( 13.Qd2 is necessary) but after 13...Kxh7 ( 13...Kh8!? ) 14.Ng5+ Kg8 15.Qh5 Rxf4 16.Qh7+ Kf8 17.Qh8+ Ng8 White gets nowhere.

12...g6

12...Nf5 13.h4!? with the idea 14.g4 , e.g., 13...h6 14.g4 Nfe7 needs more analysis. If 13.g4 Nh4 but 13.O-O!? may be playable.

13.O-O

I could see no progress in 13.Na3 O-O 14.Qd2 d4 or 13.h4!? O-O .

13...Nf5

Here if 13...O-O 14.Bh6 Rf7 15.Re1 White is in control, but he would have to be careful if Black played the exchange sacrifice 14...Rxf3 15.gxf3 Nxe5 . However, giving up material is usually not Black's idea after accepting the gambit pawn, unless there is a clear-cut return.

14.Na3

Thus White has more or less completed his development and he has induced a weakness in the Black king position. The main drawback is that he is a pawn down, but this is the a-pawn and its counterpart has a long way to go to reach a1! Meanwhile White can use the a-file to develop his knight and rook. Potentially the biggest drawback in White's position is that a later ...d4 could be serious if he loses the initiative, or that Black could build up play against the c-pawn. For now, however, the c-pawn plays a useful role in covering b4 and d4 while leaving d4 as a square that a knight can exploit later.

14...O-O

White will start to probe on the queenside after 14...a6 15.Nb5 or 14...Nh4 15.Nxh4 Qxh4 16.Nb5 .

15.Qd2!

This is more obscure than alternatives; part of the reason why I chose it is that his best reply is not obvious. If 15.Nb5 Nh4 16.Nxh4 Rxf4 or 15.Bg5 Qd7 and it's White who has tricky decisions to make.

15...b6 16.Nb5 Rf7 17.Rfe1

White plays a Nimzowitschian overprotection of e5 — and Black cracks immediately!

17...Qf8?!

This allows White a favorable transformation of the Pawn structure.

18.Bxf5 gxf5

The rook must defend c7 and the e-pawn must defend d5. So the king position is further weakened and control over the center is enhanced.

19.Bh6

19.Nfd4 is a good positional move but it is natural to see where Black will put his queen.

19...Qc5?

Remember what I wrote above? “Move 20: X is the best move, but if I play it my opponent can force perpetual check/a level endgame.”

Here Black probably ought to try 19...Qd8 20.Bg5 repeating moves to see how White will avoid the draw. Then 20.Ra4!? is an interesting attacking idea to bring a heavy piece to the battleground long before his a8-rook can play a role. The other possibility was 19...Qe8 20.Nd6 Bxd6 21.exd6 and now the rook looks good on e1! Also possible is 20.Ra4!? , bringing up the reserves.

20.Qg5+

20.Nxc7 Rxc7 21.Qg5+ Kh8 looks drawish.

20...Kh8 21.Qh5! Qe7

It's all over. 21...Nd8 22.Ng5 is not much better:

a) 22...Kg8 23.Nxh7 Bxe5 ( 23...Rxh7 24.Qe8+ ) 24.Qg6+ ;

b) 22...Qe7 23.Qh4 Kg8 24.Nxc7 Qxc7 25.Nxf7 Kxf7 26.Qf6+ Ke8 27.Qh8+ Kd7 28.Qxh7+ .

22.Ng5 Black resigns. 1-0

On the whole, I have had pretty good success with the French Defence, especially in correspondence play, but I have had some pretty embarrassing losses against gambits in OTB contests where my memory failed me or I mis-analysed complications. You will find a couple of examples in this book.

The final chapter of this book deals with a gambit that has become a cult in Europe during the 1990s, thanks chiefly to the example of player Thomas Winckelmann of Bad Schwartau, Germany. In 1988 the German correspondence chess federation BdF organised a thematic tournament for its members on the theme of the 4 a3 variation against the Winawer French. Winckelmann and another player from his local club, Wolf Reimer, decided to try out the idea 6 f3 which had been mentioned casually by Fischer and Suetin but never properly investigated. The 1997 Encyclopaedia of Chess Games: French Defence (ed. Adam Kuligowski & Harry Wienigk) calls this the “Fridlizius-Winckelmann Gambit” on the basis of a nine-move fragment from 1900 played by one Fridlizius (a Swede?) but I think the name WRG has become established by now.

In 1995 Winckelmann published the first edition of his book about the gambit and is currently running his second CC tournament to test the variation.
