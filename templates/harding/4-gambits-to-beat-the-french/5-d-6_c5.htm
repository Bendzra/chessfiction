6...c5

This is one of the most plausible ways of declining the gambit — an immediate strike against White's center to avoid the danger of being cramped later. However, ...cxd4 is not always a threat as it might undouble White's c pawn. It seems that the first player can still create difficulties if he is resourceful.

White has tried a wide variety of moves in this position, of which the most important are:

D1: 7.Bb5+

D2: 7.Qd2!?

D3: 7.Rb1

D4: 7.Bf4

A summary of others:

a) 7.fxe4 Qh4+ and Black's counterplay is worth at least a draw.

b) 7.Be2 see Winckelmann-Kroggel in the Introduction.

( 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 dxe4 6.f3 c5 )

7.Bb5+ Bd7!?

The critical line 7...Nc6 8.Rb1 is considered under D3 as that is the normal route to the position nowadays.

8.Qe2

Konikowski suggested 8.a4!? , e.g., 8...Qa5 9.Ra3 Bxb5 10.axb5 Qxb5 11.fxe4 Nf6 12.e5 Nd5 13.Qg4 Qb1 14.Ne2 O-O 15.O-O Qxc2 16.c4 Ne7 17.Rg3 Ng6 18.h4 h5 19.Qxh5 cxd4 20.Nxd4 Qxc4 21.Nf3 Nc6 22.Rg4 Qc5+ 23.Kh1 Qb5 24.Re1 Qd3 25.Ng5 Rfc8 26.Qh7+ Kf8 27.Nxe6+! Ke7 ( 27...fxe6 28.Rxg6 +-) 28.Nc5 Qc3 29.Bg5+ Ke8 30.Rc1 1-0, Gaard-Winckelmann, corr., 1994.

8...cxd4

Or 8...Qa5 9.Rb1 Qxc3+ 10.Bd2 Qxc2 11.Rc1 Qb2 12.Rxc5 Nc6 13.fxe4 Qxd4 14.Rc4 ( 14.Be3 Qa1+ see Baer-Leisebein in the Study Material) 14...Qb2 15.a4 a6 16.Bxc6 Bxc6 17.Nf3 Qa2 18.O-O Bxa4 19.Ne5 Bb5 20.Nxf7 Bxc4 21.Nd6+ Ke7 22.Nxc4 Qb3 23.Qg4! Qxc4 24.Qxg7+ Kd6 25.Bf4+ e5 26.Qxe5+ 1-0, Koser-Oeller, corr., 1995.

9.fxe4 Qa5 10.Rb1 Qxc3+

If 10...Bxb5?! 11.Rxb5 Qxc3+ 12.Bd2 Qxc2 13.Nf3 with the idea of 0-0 and Rc1.

11.Bd2 Qc7

White is doing very well against alternatives.

a) 11...Qc5 12.Bd3 (as in Reimer-Bischof, corr., 1993);

b) 11...Qxa3 12.Nf3 Nc6 13.O-O Rd8 14.Ng5 (Winckelmann-Vogel, in the Study Material).

12.Nf3

This is the critical position for the line:

a) 12...e5 13.O-O f6 14.Nh4 Ne7 15.Qh5+ g6 16.Nxg6! Nxg6 17.Rxf6 Qxc2 18.Re6+ (Or 18.Bxd7+!? Nxd7 19.Re6+ Kd8 20.Bg5+ Kc7 21.Rc1 d3 22.Rxc2+ dxc2 23.Qe2 ± Winckelmann) 18...Kd8 19.Qg5+ Kc7? (better 19...Kc8 ) 20.Ba5+ b6 21.Rc1 Qxc1+ 22.Qxc1+ Kd8 23.Qg5+ Kc7 24.Rxg6 1-0, Bangiev-Prang, Rinteln, 1995.

b) 12...Bxb5 13.Qxb5+ Qd7 (Why not 13...Nc6 , e.g., 14.Qxb7 Qxb7 15.Rxb7 Nf6 or 14.Nxd4 a6 ?) 14.Nxd4 Nf6 15.e5 O-O?! ( 15...Ne4!? ) 16.exf6 Qxd4 17.fxg7 Rd8 18.Qd3 Nc6 19.Qxd4 Rxd4 ( 19...Nxd4 ) 20.Bc3 Re4+ 21.Kf2 Rc4 22.Rb3 1/2-1/2, Krits-Skorna, Budapest First Saturday IM, May 1998. This shows that the WRG is sometimes appearing in OTB master tournaments nowadays, but as White was a bit lucky to draw I think it also shows why WRG experts now prefer other 7th moves.

D2:

7.Qd2!?

In Gutman's opinion, this relatively untried move (the Gauss Attack) is the only possibility to justify the gambit! White plans to play Bb2 and then castle queenside in most cases. My view is that this is an implausible move.

7...Nf6

Alternatives analysed by Gutman:

a) 7...exf3 8.Nxf3 Nf6 9.Bd3 Qc7 ( 9...O-O 10.O-O b6 11.Qf2 ) 10.O-O c4 11.Be2 Nbd7 12.Qg5 O-O 13.Bf4 and White has more than sufficient compensation for the pawn;

b) 7...Nc6 8.Bb2 cxd4 9.cxd4 Nf6 ( 9...Qb6?! 10.O-O-O Nf6 11.Qg5 O-O 12.d5 h6 13.Qg3 ) 10.O-O-O O-O (or 10...Bd7 11.Qe3 exf3 12.Nxf3 Qe7 13.Bd3 ) 11.Qe3!? exf3 12.Nxf3 Nd5 13.Qe1 Bd7 14.h4!? with the initiative to White at the cost of a pawn — Gutman.

c) 7...e5?! 8.Bb2 cxd4 9.cxd4 Nc6 10.d5 e3!? 11.Qd3 Nd4 12.O-O-O!? Qg5 13.f4! Qxf4 14.Ne2 Nxe2+ 15.Bxe2 Bd7 16.Rdf1! Qg5 17.h4 Qe7 18.d6!? ( 18.Qxe3 f6 unclear) 18...Qe6 19.Rh3! f6 20.Rg3!? g6 21.Bg4 Qf7 22.Bxe5 Bxg4 23.Rxg4 Qd7 24.Rc4! 1-0, Winckelmann-Jedinger, BDG fax, 1998.

8.Bb2 O-O

a) If 8...Qa5 then 9.fxe4! and now:

a1) 9...cxd4 10.Bd3! Qb6 11.O-O-O dxc3 12.Bxc3 Nbd7 13.Nf3 O-O ( 13...Nc5 14.Qf4 ) 14.Qg5 h6 15.Qg3 Qc6 16.Bb2 b6 17.Rhf1 Qa4 18.Ne5!? Nh5 19.Qh4 Nxe5 20.Bxe5 Qxa3+ 21.Bb2 Qc5 22.e5 with the initiative;

a2) 9...Nxe4!? 10.Qe3 f5 ( 10...Nf6 11.O-O-O O-O 12.Bd3 Bd7 13.Nf3 Nc6 14.Rhf1 Rad8 15.Ng5 ) 11.O-O-O O-O 12.g4! Nc6 13.Bg2 Nf6 14.gxf5 exf5 15.Nf3 Bd7 16.Ne5 Rae8 17.Qg5 and the two bishops are strong.

b) Or if 8...Bd7 9.fxe4!? Nxe4 10.Qe3 Bc6 11.Nf3 cxd4 12.cxd4 Qa5+ 13.c3 Nd7 14.Bd3 Ndf6 15.O-O with the initiative.

c) 8...Nc6 9.O-O-O ( 9.Qg5 cxd4 10.cxd4 O-O 11.Rd1 Qb6 ) 9...O-O was recommended by Hergert in Gambit Revue 1/98: it transposes to D22.

a) 10...f5 11.Nf3 Qa5 12.O-O-O Nd7 13.g4!? b5!? ( 13...fxg4!? 14.Qxe4 Nf6 unclear — Leisebein) 14.gxf5 exf5 15.dxc5 Ndxc5 16.Rg1 Na4!? 17.Qd4! Qc7!? 18.Bxb5! Nxb2 19.Rxg7+! Qxg7 20.Rg1! Qxg1+ 21.Qxg1+ Kh8 22.Qd4+ Nf6 23.Ng5 h6 24.Bc6 Rb8 25.Qxa7 Bd7 26.Nf7+! Kg8 27.Nxh6+ Kh7 28.Nxf5 1-0, Winckelmann-Leisebein, corr. (test-game), 1998.

b) 10...Nd6! (threatens 11...Qh4+ ) 11.Nf3 c4 12.Be2 Nc6 13.O-O Ne7 14.Qf2 Nd5 with clear advantage to Black — Hergert.

The latest way of playing this line for Black. Older examples followed 8...Nf6 when:

a) Winckelmann has tried 9.Bg5 h6 10.Bxf6 Qxf6 11.Qe2 exf3 12.Nxf3 Bd7 13.O-O when if 13...cxd4 Konikowski suggests 14.cxd4 O-O-O 15.Ne5 Qe7 16.Nxd7 Qxd7 17.c3 .

b) 9.Qe2 and now:

b1) Winckelmann-Preuss, corr., 1994, went 9...O-O 10.fxe4 cxd4 11.Nf3 Qb6 12.Rb3 dxc3 13.e5 Nd5 14.Bd3 Qc7 15.O-O g6 and now 16.Bh6 should have been played.

b2) 9...cxd4 10.fxe4 O-O ( 10...Bd7!? was an editorial recommendation by Melts in CC Informator 3.) 11.Nf3 (CC-IM Michael Melts also suggested 11.Bg5!? here.) 11...Qb6 12.Rb3 Na5 (Now White's attack flows easily; Black should try Konikowski's 12...e5!? ) 13.Rb4 dxc3 14.Bd3 Qc7 15.e5 Nd5 16.Rh4 g6 17.Bh6 Re8 18.O-O Nc6 19.Ng5 Nxe5 ( 19...Qxe5 20.Rxf7! ) 20.Bg7 Kxg7 21.Rxh7+ Kg8 22.Qe4 Qc5+ 23.Kh1 Nf4 24.Qxf4 f5 25.Qh4 1-0, Koser-Knorr, corr., 1992.

( 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 dxe4 6.f3 c5 )

7.Bf4

Winckelmann in GR 1/97 wrote: “A strong move which in former years was overlooked in favor of 7.Bb5+ and 7.Rb1 . The great advantage of this move is that White is able to play 8.fxe4 whether Black plays 7...Nf6 , 7...Nc6 or 7...Qa5 .” However, his more recent preference for line C suggests he may have lost confidence in this move.

7...Nf6?!

The reason for Winckelmann's disenchantment probably has something to do with the following game: 7...Nc6 8.fxe4 cxd4 9.Nf3 Qa5 ( 9...e5 10.Nxe5 Nxe5 11.Bxe5 Qh4+ 12.g3 Qxe4+ 13.Qe2 = Gambit Revue) 10.Rb1 Nf6 11.Bd3 e5 12.Rb5 Qxc3+ 13.Bd2 Qxa3 14.Nxe5 a6 15.Rb3 Qc5 16.Nxc6 Qxc6 17.Rb4 O-O 18.O-O Nxe4 19.Bxe4 Qxe4 20.Rf4 Qc6 21.Rbxd4 Be6 22.Bb4 Rfc8 23.Rd6 Qc7 24.Qd3 b5 25.Bc3 Qc5+ 26.Rfd4 Bf5 27.Qg3 Bg6 28.R6d5 Qa3 0-1, Leisebein-Hergert, corr. (test-game), 1997.

b) 10...Qxd4 11.Nf3 Qc3+ 12.Bd2 Qc5 13.Rb1! a5 14.Rb5 Qxa3 15.O-O Qe7 16.Qe1! a4 17.e5! Nd5 18.Ng5! f5 19.exf6 gxf6 ( 19...Nxf6 20.Qh4! ) 20.Ne4 f5 21.Bg5 Qc7 22.Rxf5! e5 ( 22...exf5 23.Nd6+ ) 23.Rf2 Nce7 24.c4 1-0, Winckelmann-Herbold, corr. test-game, 1995.
