( 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3 Qb6 6.Bd3 cxd4 7.cxd4 Bd7 )

8.Nc3 Nxd4

There is no consistent alternative.

9.Nxd4 Qxd4 10.O-O

This position is the real starting point of the traditional Milner-Barry Gambit.

It is rare for White to follow 8.Nc3 by 10.Qe2 but there is nothing obviously wrong with delaying castling so this move order, avoiding 8.O-O Nb4 , should perhaps be investigated; it was employed in the French league game Bellet-Apicella, 1995. After 10...a6 11.O-O main line B arose. Kosten remarks “Perhaps 10...Bb4!? ”. Presumably the critical reply is 11.O-O offering a pawn at c3 to obtain the bishop pair and a huge development lead.

Black now has three distinct paths:

A1: Take the second pawn: 10...Qxe5

A2: Retreat the queen: 10...Qb6

A3: Secure the b5 square: 10...a6

The latter normally transposes to line B1 near the end of this chapter.

In the early days of the Milner-Barry Gambit, Black sometimes tried various other moves and there are several classic examples of how these inferior tries are demolished:

a) 10...Bb4? 11.Nb5! Qxe5 12.Qg4! Ba5 13.Bf4 Nh6 ( 13...Qf6 14.Qg3 Ne7 15.Bg5 Nf5 16.Bxf5 Qxb2 17.Rab1 Qe2 18.Nd6+ Kf8 19.Rxb7 1-0, Lapin-Ksenofontov, corr., 1955) 14.Qg3 Qh5 15.Qxg7 Rg8 16.Qxh6 Qg4 17.Bg3 a6 18.Nd6+ Ke7 19.Qe3 Bb4 20.Nf5+ 1-0, Kottnauer-Palmer, England, 1961.

b) 10...Nh6 11.Nb5 Qb6 12.Be3 Qa5 13.Bd2 Qb6 14.a4 a6 15.Be3 Qa5 16.Nd4 Be7 17.Qh5 O-O-O 18.b4 Bxb4 19.Rfc1+ Kb8 20.Nb3 Qxa4 21.Rxa4 Bxa4 22.Qh4 a5 23.Nxa5 d4 24.Nxb7 1-0, Kappler-Matthey, Kiekrz, 1995.

c) 10...Rc8 11.Nb5 Qb6 12.Be3 Qa5 13.Rc1 Bc6 14.Nxa7 Rc7 15.Nxc6 Rxc6 16.Qb3 Rxc1 17.Rxc1 Ne7 18.Qxb7 f6 19.exf6 gxf6 20.Bb5+ Kf7 21.Qd7 Bg7 22.Bc5 Qd8 23.Qb7 Bf8 24.Bb6 Qd6 25.Bc7 wins the queen ( 25...Qb4 26.Be8+ ) 1-0, Kampen-Holzapfel, Nuremberg, 1988.

d) 10...Ne7 11.Nb5 Qxe5 12.Re1 Qb8 13.Qf3 and now:

d1) 13...e5? 14.Rxe5! Qxe5 15.Bf4 Qxb2 16.Nd6+ Kd8 17.Nxf7+ Ke8 18.Be5 d4 19.Nd6+ Kd8 20.Nxb7+ Ke8 21.Nd6+ Kd8 22.Qxa8+ Nc8 23.Rb1 Qxa2 24.Nxc8 Bxc8 25.Rc1 Qe6 26.Bc7+ Ke7 27.Ba5 +- Messere-Buchan, England 1959;

d2) 13...Ng6? 14.Bxg6 hxg6 15.Bf4 Qd8 16.Nc7+ Ke7 17.Nxd5+ Ke8 18.Nc7+ Ke7 19.Qa3+ Kf6 20.Qc3+ 1-0, Herter-Nievergelt, 1957;

d3) 13...Bxb5! 14.Bxb5+ Nc6 15.Qxd5 Qd6 16.Qg5 h6 17.Qg4 g5 18.Be3 Be7? 19.Bd4 O-O 20.Bc3 Rfd8 21.Qh5 Bf8 22.h4 Qc7? 23.Re4 Bg7 24.hxg5 Bxc3 25.bxc3 hxg5 26.Qxg5+ Kf8 27.Qf6 Ke8 28.Rh4 Qe7 29.Bxc6+ 1-0, Messere-Pethybridge, corr., England, 1958.

This is Black's best move, so long as it is followed up correctly!

The double-pawn deficit means that White has fewer options than in Main Line B, which could also suit those defenders who don't want to have to study and memorise too many variations. This is why the 8.O-O and 10.Qe2 move order appeals to many MBG players.

White has terrific compensation for the two pawns sacrificed:

A121:

( 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3 Qb6 6.Bd3 cxd4 7.cxd4 Bd7 8.Nc3 Nxd4 9.Nxd4 Qxd4 10.O-O Qxe5! 11.Re1 Qb8! 12.Nxd5 Bd6 )

13.Qg4

This is the critical move. Unless White can make something of it, he must abandon the double pawn sacrifice version of the MBG and concentrate on the lines with an early Qe2.

13...Kf8

This secures both the king and g-pawn although it locks in the rook for a time, to offer White some hope. Not 13...Bxh2+? 14.Kh1 Be5 15.Bf4! Bxf4 16.Qxg7 and White wins the exchange.

14.Bd2

Unclear says ECO. Most other moves allow either 14...f5 or 14...Bxh2+ and 15...Be5 — Watson. Black also stood better after 14.Nf4 Nf6 in Glek-Zlotnik, Moscow 1980.

14...h5!

Not 14...f5? 15.Qf3 exd5?? 16.Bxf5 Bxf5 17.Qxf5+ Nf6 18.Bc3 and White has a dangerous attack.

Also 14...Bc6 15.Bc3! e5 16.Ne3 was good for White in Soylu-Zueger, Haifa 1989. With his king on f8, Black is unready for adventures like ...e5-e4.

15.Qh3 Bc6

Now Black has an extra pawn with a defensible position, although a few minor points remain unclear. Other moves give White more hope:

a) 15...exd5?! (works against other moves) 16.Qxd7 Bxh2+ 17.Kh1 Nf6 18.Qf5 Bd6 19.Rac1! Qd8 20.Bg5 and White was somewhat better in Borg-P.Nikolic, Kavala 1985. Black might have been able to reach a tenable ending by 20...Qd7 21.Bxf6 Qxf5 22.Bxf5 gxf6 whereas in the game after 20...Be7?! 21.Re5! all that saved him was his much higher rating.

b) 15...Be8 led to a draw in Sarink-Rittner, Perfors Memorial corr. 1978; this retreat is better a move later when the White knight is decentralised.

c) 15...Nh6 16.Ne3 ( 16.Bc3 Ng4! — Watson) 16...Kg8 and now:

c1) After 17.Nc4 Bc7 18.Qxh5 Nf5 19.Qg5 Bxh2+ 20.Kf1 Bb5 Black survived comfortably: in Pyhala-McDonald, Gausdal 1990.

c2) Therefore White should try either 17.g3!? (Borg) or 17.Bc3! Ng4 18.Nxg4 hxg4 19.Qxg4 Bxh2+ 20.Kf1 Qf8 21.g3 Rd8 22.Re5! see Finkel-Slutzkin in the Study Material.

d) 15...Nf6!? is worth more consideration. See Vezzosi-Godena in the Study Material.

16.Nb4

16.Ne3!? is a major alternative.

16...Nf6 17.Nc4 ( 17.Bc3 unclear — Borg) and now:

a) 17...Bc7 18.Bc3 and now:

a1) 18...Nd5 19.Ne5 Nxc3 20.Nxf7!? Bxh2+? (Black should settle for a draw by 20...Kxf7 21.Qxe6+ Kf8 22.Qf5+ Kg8 23.Bc4+ Bd5 24.Bxd5+ Nxd5 25.Qxd5+ Kh7 26.Qf5+ =) 21.Kh1 (Not 21.Qxh2? Qxh2+ 22.Kxh2 Kxf7 ) 21...Ne4? 22.Bxe4 Kxf7 23.Bxc6 Rh6 24.Bd7 Be5 25.f4 Bxf4 26.Bxe6+ Kf8 27.Rad1? ( 27.Qf5+! Rf6 28.Qh7 is more exact) 1-0, Salnikov-Prudnikova, USSR, 1991. Kosten points out that after White's actual 27th move, 27...Qe8! was an improvement on resignation!

a2) Kosten cites Watson's suggestion 18...Ng4 with approval, but Black needs an improvement on 19.g3 b5 20.Na5 Qb6 21.Nxc6 Qxf2+ 22.Kh1 Qb6 23.Bb4+ Ke8 24.Qh4 Nf6 25.Be4 Rg8 26.Bg2 g5 27.Qd4 Ng4 28.Bc5 Qa6 29.Nb4 1-0, Novak-Johannessen, European Under 15 Ch, Rimavska Sobota, 1996.

b) 17...Nd5 — I.Kuznetsov, Informant 55;

c) 17...Be7 18.Ne5 Bd5 19.Rad1 b5 20.Bg5 Qb7 21.a3 Rd8 22.Bc2 Qb6 23.Bb3 Bc5 24.Re2 Bd4 25.Bxd5 Rxd5 26.Be3 Bxe3 27.Rxd5 1-0, Bennett-Wastney, 62nd New Zealand Corr. Ch, 1995-96;

d) 17...Bf4 18.Bb4+ Kg8 19.Rad1 Ng4 20.Bd6 Bxd6 21.Nxd6 Qc7 22.Bc4 Rh6 23.Qh4 Rf6 24.Qxh5 Nxf2 25.Rd4 Qb6 26.Qh4 Qxb2 27.Nf5 Nh3+ 28.Qxh3 Rxf5 29.Red1 Qf2+ 30.Kh1 Bd5 31.R4d2 Qf4 32.Bd3 Rc8 33.Bxf5 Qxd2 34.Bh7+ Kh8 35.Bc2+ Qh6 36.Qd3 Kg8 37.h3 g6 38.a4 Qg7 39.Kg1 Qb2 40.Rd2 Rc3 41.Qd4 Qc1+ 42.Bd1 Rc4 43.Qf2 Rxa4 44.Kh2 Qc7+ 45.Kh1 Ra3 0-1, Stranjakovitch-Gentilleau, Cannes, 1990.

So 16.Ne3 is not exhausted yet.

Now we return to 16.Nb4 , which is normally given as a drawing line — but maybe Black can do better?

16...Bd7!?

Or 16...Nf6 17.Nxc6 bxc6 18.Bc3 Ng4 19.g3 Qb6 20.Re2 Rd8 21.Be4 with some complications but drawn (Messere-Endzelins, Potter Mem corr., 1975-77).

Black can of course play 16...Be8 (see below after move 18) but there is no harm in testing White's intentions first.

17.Bc3 Ne7 gets the knight out.

17...Bc6 18.Nb4

White has to be content with a draw by repetition and several games have ended that way.

If Black wants to play for a win, it's the moment of truth...

18...Be8!

Instead of 18...Bd7 1/2-1/2 in such games as Motwani-Brunner, Clichy open, 1991.

Black threatened 19...Nf6 . If 19.Bc3 Ne7 gives Black a comfortable advantage; the threat is ...a5 and ...Nd5 (eyeing c3 and f4) with queenside advance or centralization (...Be5, ...Qd6) in store.

A3: Securing the b5-square

( 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3 Qb6 6.Bd3 cxd4 7.cxd4 Bd7 8.Nc3 Nxd4 9.Nxd4 Qxd4 10.O-O )

10...a6

For many years this was regarded as the most rational way of defending the Milner-Barry, an impression gained partly from the ease in which Black won some early games with it. However, as White is in effect given a tempo to play with, it was possible for more dangerous continuations to be developed. Playing the Black side of this line does require a good memory and alert defence, as can be seen from the cautionary tale in the Introduction, viz. my first loss to Peart!

11.Qe2

This is the most critical move, although others have been tried.

a) 11.Kh1 Qxe5! (better than the normal 11...Ne7 ) and White's gambit looks shaky, e.g., 12.Re1 (Also 12.f4 Qd6 13.Be3 Be7 14.Bd4 Bf6 was good for Black in Wade-Menvielle, Palma de Mallorca 1966) 12...Qd6 13.Qf3 (Brinck-Claussen v B.Sørensen, Copenhagen 1989) 13...Bc6 14.Bf4 Qd7 with a solid position for Black (Messere).

b) 11.Re1!? has been tried in several games, none being convincing for either player. The main line goes 11...Ne7 12.Ne2 ( 12.Be3!? also has failed to impress.) 12...Qxe5 13.Bf4 Qxb2 but Zlotnik cites Estrin's suggestion 13...Qf6 14.Qb3 Bc6! with approval. Also, my old suggestion 11...Qb6 deserves attention, as it threatens ...Bc5 to free Black's development.

c) 11.Be3!? Qxe5 12.Re1 Qd6! Horton;

d) 11.Qf3!? (Pytel) 11...Ne7 (Better than capturing on e5 at once; Black wants to make White lose time with his rook.) 12.Rd1 Qxe5! ( 12...Nc6 13.Bg6 forces an unclear queen sac) 13.Bf4 Qf6 14.Re1 Bc6 15.Qg3 Ng6 16.Bg5 Bd6! and Black stood better, but eventually there was a draw in C.Brady-Harding, Dublin 1981.

Now we return to 11.Qe2 .

The move usually played is 11...Ne7 transposing to line B1 below.

Tony Kosten's 1998 book The French Advance calls this “the most natural developing move” and does not mention any alternatives at all! However, Black has played a variety of alternatives here. Lane's Beating The French (1994) does cover some of them.

a) 11...Qb6 12.a4! Ne7 13.Be3 Qc7 14.f4 (Stoica-Voiculescu, Romania Ch 1975) and Black gets kicked around.

b) 11...Qa7 12.Kh1 Bc5 13.f4 with an edge to White in Lane-Sims, Cardiff 1981.

c) 11...Qh4 is as yet unrefuted, e.g., 12.f4 Nh6 13.Be3 Rc8 14.Rf3 Bc5 15.Bxc5 Rxc5 16.Raf1 Qe7 17.Rh3 Nf5 18.Qf2 Bc6 19.g4 d4 20.Nd1 Ne3 (unclear) 21.Nxe3 dxe3 22.Qxe3 Ra5 23.a3 g6 1/2-1/2, Rozentalis-Epishin, USSR Ch, 1990. The amusing Botoc-Borchers, in the Study Material, is another unclear line that shows how things can go horribly wrong for White if Black gets his d-pawn moving.

f) 11...Rc8!? (Keres) is a possibly viable alternative:

f1) It's well known that 12.Rd1 Bc5 13.Bc2 Qh4 14.g3 Qe7 (Cuartas-Mecking, Bogota 1977) is good for Black, but White can do better.
