F:

( 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 dxe4 6.f3 )

6...Nf6

A different approach. Black shows he intends to retain the gambit pawn while getting on with development.

7.fxe4!

It is doubtful if there is anything better:

a) 7.Be3?! O-O 8.f4?! Nd5 9.Qd2 b6 10.Be2 Ba6 11.c4 Ne7 12.Nh3 Nf5 13.O-O Nd7 14.Bf2 Nf6 favored Black in Guttenberg-Huebner, Cologne 1958.

b) 7.a4 when:

b1) 7...e5!? transposes to line H1 below.

b2) 7...Bd7 8.Bg5 Bc6 9.Bb5 Bxb5 10.axb5 Qd5 11.Bxf6 gxf6 12.Qe2 c6 ( 12...f5 favors Black — Konikowski) 13.b6 a6 14.fxe4 Qb5 15.Qf3 Nd7 16.Ne2 Qxb6 17.O-O Ke7 18.c4 Rab8 19.c5 Qc7 20.Nf4 Rhg8 21.Nh5 Rg6 22.e5 f5 23.Nf4 Rg7 24.Qe3 Rh8 25.c4 Qb8 26.Nd3 Rhg8 27.g3 Rg4 28.d5 cxd5 29.cxd5 Rc4 30.c6 bxc6 31.d6+ Kf8 32.Rxa6 Kg7 33.Rfa1 h6 34.Ra7 Qb6 35.Qxb6 Nxb6 36.Nf4 Re8 37.Nh5+ Kg6 38.Re7 Rf8 39.Nf4+ Kg5 40.Rxe6 1-0, Winckelmann-Horstmann, corr, 1993

b3) 7...O-O!? 8.Bg5 h6 9.Bh4 Nc6!? 10.fxe4 ( 10.Bb5 e5! ) 10...g5 11.e5 gxh4 12.exf6 Qxf6 with advantage to Black — Gutman.

c) 7.Bg5!? Qd5! ( 7...exf3 8.Nxf3 see A) 8.Qd2 c5 9.Bxf6 gxf6 10.Rd1 Bd7 11.fxe4 Qxe4+ 12.Kf2 Bc6 13.Nf3 (Triposky-lvanov, Dnepropetrovsk 1993) 13...Rg8!? with advantage to Black.

7...Nxe4

The recapture looks obvious but Kaissiber editor Stefan Buecker has proposed 7...e5!? 8.Bg5! h6 when:

a) 9.Bh4?! g5 10.Bg3 exd4 11.cxd4 Nxe4 ( 11...O-O 12.Qd3 ) 12.Be5 O-O ( 12...f6? 13.Qh5+ Kf8 14.Bc4 Qe7 15.Nf3 Nd6 16.Nh4! gxh4 17.O-O Nf5 18.Rae1 1-0, Winckelmann-Zitzewitz, corr., 1995) 13.Qf3 ( 13.Qh5 Nc6 14.Nf3 Qd5 15.Bd3 Nxe5 16.dxe5 Kg7 ) 13...Qd5 14.O-O-O Nc6 favors Black.

b) After 9.Bxf6!? Qxf6 10.Nf3 Bg4 11.Bb5+! ( 11.dxe5?! Qf4 ) 11...c6 12.Bc4 Bxf3 ( 12...O-O 13.O-O ) 13.Qxf3 Qxf3 14.gxf3 Nd7 (Buecker) White reaches a favorable ending by 15.a4 Ke7 16.Ke2 Rhd8 17.Rad1 Rab8 18.h4 according to Gutman.

8.Qg4 Nf6

Black has also tried:

a) 8...f5 9.Qxg7 Qf6 10.Qxc7 Nc6 11.Nf3 O-O 12.Bd3 Rf7 13.Qf4 Nxc3 ( 13...Rg7 14.Bxe4 Rg4 15.Qxg4+! fxg4 16.Ng5 ) 14.Bd2 Nd5 15.Qg5+ Qxg5 16.Bxg5 favors White — Gutman.

b) 8...Nxc3 9.Bd3! (Not 9.Qxg7?! Qh4+ 10.Kd2 Ne4+ 11.Ke3 Qf6 which favored Black in the 1994-5 correspondence game Steinert-Neubauer) and now:

b1) 9...g6 10.Nf3 e5 11.Qg3 when:

b11) 11...exd4 12.O-O O-O 13.Bh6 Nd7 ( 13...Re8 14.Ng5 f5 15.Bc4+ Nd5 16.Rae1 c6 17.Qd6! — T.Winckelmann) 14.Ng5 c5 15.Bc4 b5 16.Bxf7+ Gaard-Gestel, corr., 1996;

b12) 11...O-O 12.dxe5 Qd5 ( 12...Re8 13.Bd2 Nd5 14.O-O Qe7 15.c4 Qc5+ 16.Kh1 Ne3? 17.Qf2 +- Steinert-Neubauer, corr., 1994-5) 13.Qh4 Nc6 14.O-O Nxe5 15.Nxe5 Qxe5 16.Bh6 with initiative to White — Gutman;

b2) 9...Kf8 10.Nf3 and the king position means White's chances are to be preferred according to Gutman's analysis:

b21) 10...e5 11.Qh5 e4 12.Bc4 Qe8 13.Ng5 f6 14.Nf7 Rg8 15.a4! ;

b22) 10...Nc6 11.Qh5!? Kg8 12.Bd2 Nd5 13.O-O Nf6 14.Qh4 ;

b23) 10...Nd7 11.O-O Nf6 12.Qh4 h6 13.Ne5 Kg8 14.Kh1!? with a dangerous initiative.

b3) 9...Nd7 10.Nf3! (After 10.Qxg7 Qf6 11.Qg3 of Winckelmann-Zitzewitz, 1995, Gutman suggests 11...e5!? 12.Bg5 Qb6 13.Nf3 e4 14.Kd2 Qa5 15.Rhe1 f6 ) 10...Rg8 11.O-O Nd5 12.c4 N5f6 13.Qg3 with the initiative.

9.Qg3!

The original recommendation 9.Qxg7 is risky because of 9...Rg8 10.Qh6 Rg6 11.Qe3 c5 12.Bb2 Qa5!? ( 12...cxd4 13.cxd4 Nbd7?! 14.Nf3 Qa5+ 15.c3 favored White in the correspondence game Pillhock-Streichenbach, 1994.) 13.O-O-O Nc6 and Black has good counterplay according to Gutman.

9...c5

Instead 9...Ne4 10.Qxg7 Qf6 11.Qxf6 Nxf6 12.Nf3 leads to a favorable ending for White (Gutman).

10.Nf3

Now:

a) 10...O-O 11.Bd3 Nc6 12.O-O cxd4 13.Bg5!? ( 13.Qh4 dxc3 14.Bg5 h6 15.Bxh6 Ne7! ) 13...dxc3 14.Kh1 h6 15.Bxh6 Ne8 16.Bxg7 Nxg7 17.Ng5 f5 18.Qh4 Rf7 19.Qh7+ Kf8 20.Rxf5! with a decisive attack according to Winckelmann.

b) 10...Nc6 11.Bd3 cxd4 12.O-O dxc3 13.Qxg7 (If 13.Kh1 Black replies 13...Nh5! 14.Qg4 Qa5 15.Bg5 g6 .) 13...Rg8 14.Qh6 and there is no secure place for the Black king so White has the initiative (Gutman).
