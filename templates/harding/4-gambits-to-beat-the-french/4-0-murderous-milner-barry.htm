THE MURDEROUS MILNER-BARRY

This gambit was invented in England shortly after the Second World War and for many years it was not widely known beyond those shores — as we saw in the introduction, where it caught out Zagorovsky!

The original game (or at least the first to be published) is not at all well known, so we present it now with updated notes based on those first written by the gambit's pioneer, the late Sir Stuart Milner-Barry.

British master Sir Stuart Milner-Barry (1906-95) probably devised his new way of handling the Advance Variation of the French Defence some time in the 1940s, and got the first opportunity to play it in this game.

[...]

P.S.Milner-Barry-Isles
British Ch, Swansea, 1951

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3 Qb6 6.Bd3

Milner-Barry's note here states: “I have long sought the opportunity of trying the gambit which follows”. This move had long been abandoned in favor of 6.Be2 (and later 6.a3 ) because of the problem of defending the White d-pawn.

6...cxd4

The correct move order! In the famous game Nimzowitsch-Salwe, 6...Bd7 was punished by 7.dxc5!

Here is another lively game that is part of the MBG legend although it is not strictly with the gambit: 6...Nge7? 7.dxc5! Qc7 8.Na3! Nxe5 9.Nb5 Nxd3+ 10.Qxd3 Qxc5 11.Be3 d4 12.Bxd4 Qc6 13.Ne5 Qxg2 14.Nd6+ Kd8 15.Bb6+ 1-0, Milner-Barry v. Trott, llford, 1951.

7.cxd4 Bd7

Now White used to lose a tempo by 8.Be2 as in Paulsen-Tarrasch, 1888 (to be found in My System). Also 8.Bc2 (as in a game Steinitz-Burn, among others) is unattractive.

Milner-Barry realised that White could sacrifice the d-pawn for an initiative, forcing Black to waste time with his exposed queen. Meanwhile White would complete his development and launch an attack on the uncastled Black king. 8.Nc3 was Milner-Barry's move order, but 8.O-O (as played by Messere in the game featured in the Introduction) is usually preferred nowadays. These move order questions will be examined at the start of the analytical section of this chapter.

8.Nc3

Milner-Barry wrote: “It is obviously better to develop the bishop at d3, on its natural diagonal, if possible. It can retreat if necessary to b1, and the queen's knight can support the queen's pawn from e2. Naturally, therefore, Black accepts the gambit.”

8...Nxd4 9.Nxd4 Qxd4 10.O-O Qxe5

“In a later round Hallmark — against A.R.B.Thomas — more cautiously retired his queen to b6, but after 10...Qb6 11.Qg4 followed by Be3 White had ample compensation for the pawn and won decisively.”

11.Re1 Qd6

“I should prefer 11...Qb8 giving back one pawn 12.Nxd5 Bd6 but White still has chances. Probably Black can take both pawns, but he has considerable difficulties of development to contend against.”

Modern theory recognises 11...Qb8 as the critical continuation so Sir Stuart's instincts were correct.

12.Nb5 Bxb5

“No longer 12...Qb8 because of 13.Qf3 threatening Bf4”

However, 12...Qb8 has since become a main line — albeit one reckoned good for White — see the analytical section below.

13.Bxb5+ Kd8 14.Be3

“White's strategy must be to make play with his bishop and his rook on the c-file, and hope to force some weakening pawn moves, e.g., ...a6 or ...b6 which would allow him to force an entry.”

Other moves mentioned for White by ECO here are 14.g3 and 14.Qf3!? (both analysis by Sveshnikov) but not the recently discovered 14.Qh5! which is stronger.

14.Qh5! g6 15.Qf3 f6 16.Bf4 e5 17.Rxe5! , e.g., 17...fxe5 18.Bxe5 Qc5 (or 18...Qxe5 19.Qxf8+ Kc7 20.Rc1+ 1-0, Weigel-Schmidt, corr., Germany, 1976) 19.Ba4 Ne7 20.Bxh8 Kc7 21.Qf4+ 1-0, Sella-Nicolini, corr., Argentina, 1991.

14...Qb4

Black has since tried 14...Ne7 15.Rc1 and now:

a) 15...Qb4 16.Qd3 Nc6 17.a3! Qxb2? 18.Bg5+! Be7 19.Bxc6 bxc6 20.Rb1 Qa2 21.Bxe7+! 1-0, Bohak-Houska, corr., 1967.

b) 15...Nc6 unclear — ECO.

c) 15...Nf5 16.Bc5 (Messere recommended 16.Qf3!? with the idea 16...Nxe3 17.Qxf7 Qe7 18.Qxe7+ and 19.Rxe3 giving White good play for the pawn.) 16...Qb8 ( 16...Qf4? 17.g3 Qg5 18.Qxd5+ exd5 19.Bb6+ axb6 20.Re8 mate, Faulds-Lang, New Zealand 1956); 17.Qxd5+ Bd6 18.Qxf5 1-0, K.Pytel-Lenk, corr., 1963. However, a possible improvement here is 16...Qxc5 17.Rxc5 Bxc5 (W.Heidenfeld, later suggested by Kosten).

15.Qd3 Nf6

16.a3

“ 16.Rac1 is correct. This and the next move are based on a misunderstanding.”

16...Qh4 17.g3 Qh3 18.Qc3

“White was fortunate to discover the resource to keep his attack alive. The intended 18.Bf4 is completely met by 18...Bc5 . Now White threatens Qa5+, and also gives himself the option of turning out the Black queen by Bf1.”

18...Bd6 19.Rac1

“19 Bf1 first is safer. White encouraged ...Ng4 deliberately.”

19...Ng4

“He has hardly a choice because if 19...Ke7 there is no good answer to 20.Bc5 .”

20.Qxg7 Qxh2+ 21.Kf1 Nxe3+ 22.Rxe3 Qh3+?

“This loses. He must play 22...Qh1+ 23.Ke2 Qxc1 24.Rxe6 (Not 24.Qxh8+ Ke7 25.Qxa8 when comes 25...Qxb2+ 26.Kf3 Qxb5 and Black has the better chances, e.g., 27.Qxa7? Bc5 ) and readers may like to adjudicate the position for themselves:

24...Rc8 (seems to be best) 25.Qxf7 and hope to mate Black before he is mated himself. If 25.Qxh8+ Kc7 and 25.Rxd6+ Kc7 26.Qe5 Qc2+ 27.Kf1 Qb1+! 28.Kg2 Qe4+ is also not adequate for White.”

23.Kg1 Ke7

“No better is 23...Rf8 24.Qf6+ Be7 25.Qe5 Rc8 26.Rxc8+ Kxc8 27.Rc3+ and mate follows.”

24.Qg5+ Kf8 25.Rec3 1-0

If 25...e5 either 26.Qf6 or 26.g4 is decisive.

“An unsound but stirring game, possibly of some theoretical interest in the opening,” was Milner-Barry's final comment.
