THE DEVIOUS ALAPIN-DIEMER GAMBIT

This is a gambit I have met several times and though I have not played it as White, I do have a lot of respect for it. In the Introduction, you saw what it did to Zagorovsky!

Moreover, its almost total absence from standard theory works (barely seven lines of text hidden on page 16 of the new ECO) means your opponents won't find it easy to “book up” against it — unless they have the one book that covers it in some detail.

[...]

AJ.Deisz-Harding
EU/M/GT/360 corr. 1991-92

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Be3!?

The main game for this chapter is a very interesting, and previously unpublished, encounter I had a few years ago against a Dutchman in ICCF Master Class. Other lines that can arise are introduced in relation to it. I don't intend to go into huge detail on this gambit because if you intend to take it up in a big way you will certainly want to obtain the monograph Alapin French: Tactics For White by Tim Sawyer (Thinkers Press, 1995).

My object here is to:

a) present my own experience with the gambit (all with Black, and quite mixed);

b) give enough detail to let you decide whether this line is for you;

c) provide some updates where necessary.

This is the Alapin-Diemer Gambit. Alapin was a strong Russian master and theoretician from the late 19th century, a rival to Chigorin who was notorious for having sent some of his Evans Gambit analysis to Steinitz before one of their matches. Diemer is best known for his championing of the Blackmar Gambit 1.d4 d5 2.e4 which is rightly known now by their joint names.

If White plays the BDG (Blackmar-Diemer Gambit), and Black replies 2...e6 it is understandable that he would like some gambit options.

By the way, 3.Be3 is not the only move three gambit available. Diemer himself tried both 3.Nf3 and 3.c4 (Diemer Duhm Gambit), and the latter has experienced some Internet resurgence lately as we saw in the chapter on Lesser Gambits. However 3.Be3 is almost certainly the only one of these three moves to be feared.

3...dxe4

The saying that “The only way to refute a gambit is to accept it” is not always true, but certainly applies here. If White is not obliged to move or defend his e-pawn after 2...d5 in the French, then 95% of the theory on the opening goes out of the window!

For example, if Black plays (as he often does) 3...Nf6 , then White can obtain a position similar to the Tarrasch 3.Nd2 Nf6 4.e5 but without getting his minor pieces in a twist. There is a lot of material in Sawyer's book that I don't want to repeat, especially as White is not giving a pawn in those lines.

Here is an example from the praxis of my friend, the late Irish master Wolfgang Heidenfeld who played 3.Be3 on several occasions:

[...]

Heidenfeld-Pietzsch
Madrid zonal 1960

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Be3 Nf6 4.e5 Nfd7 5.f4

As played by Alapin against von Gottschall in Dresden 1892, though he chose the strange 5.c3 c5 6.a3 against Zinkl, Vienna 1899.

5...c5 6.c3 Nc6 7.Nf3

Heidenfeld pointed out that if now 7...Qb6 8.Qd2 White has b2 and d4 nicely protected, without having to keep his dark-squared bishop at home, as in the Tarrasch. The threat f4-f5 is already in the air. Black did not find a good answer in this game:

7...cxd4 8.cxd4 Nb6 9.Nc3 Bd7 10.Bd3 Be7 11.O-O Nb4 12.Bb1 a5 13.a3 Nc6 14.Bd3 a4 15.Ng5 g6 16.Qf3 h5 17.Qg3 Bxg5 18.fxg5 Ne7 19.Rf6 O-O 20.Raf1 Bc6 21.Qf2 Nc4 22.Rxf7 Nf5 23.Bxf5 exf5 24.Rf6 Rxf6 25.gxf6 Kf7 26.Bg5 1-0.

When I came to live in Ireland in 1976 I found that quite a few Dublin players, no doubt influenced by Heidenfeld, employed 3.Be3 as an occasional weapon and I got some practice against it. When Wolfgang and I wrote our book French: Classical Lines for Batsford, which included recommendations against some minor lines, the section on 3.Be3 was naturally given to him!

If Black really does want to decline the gambit and take play into quite different paths, then he usually goes for 3...f5 but that can come unstuck too:

[...]

Heidenfeld-P.N.Lee
Oxford, 1964

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Be3 f5!?

“Perfectly playable” said Heidenfeld.

4.exf5

The best plan, in view of 4.e5 b6 followed by ...Ba6.

4...exf5 5.Nf3 Nf6 6.Bd3 Bd6 7.Ne5 O-O 8.f4

Each player tries to establish a central outpost but in such symmetrical situations it is usually better to be the first player. Black, a British international player at that time, must have been regretting now that he didn't play his usual Sicilian. Instead of continuing with the symmetry, he chose:

8...c5?

Sawyer gives this move an exclamation mark but Heidenfeld, in his ironically-titled Lacking The Master Touch, gave it a ? and called it “somewhat premature”. Now White took advantage by:

9.dxc5! Bxe5 10.fxe5 Ng4 11.Qd2 Nc6 12.O-O Ncxe5 13.h3! Nxe3 14.Qxe3 Nxd3 15.Qxd3 ±

The Black bishop is helpless.

15...b6 16.Nc3 d4 17.Rad1 Be6 18.Qxd4 Qxd4+ 19.Rxd4 bxc5 20.Rd6! Rfe8 21.Nd5 Rad8? 22.Ne7+ Kf7 23.Nxf5 Bxa2 24.Ra6! 1-0

Other ways to decline the gambit should not give White trouble.

One possibility for Black, by analogy with the Tarrasch Variation, is 3...c5?! but this, too, seems inferior.

Here Sawyer recommends 4.dxc5 , e.g., 4...dxe4 5.Qxd8+ Kxd8 6.Nc3 Nf6 7.O-O-O+ Bd7 8.f3!? as in Mavraedis-Jestadt, San Mateo 1992. White is clearly better if he just plays 8.h3 to rule out ...Ng4.

Another possibility for White from the diagram is 4.exd5! as recommended by Chuck Cadman in a newsgroup thread in 1998 where somebody asked if the Alapin Gambit was playable. He said that a lower rated opponent in the US Junior Open then chose 4...cxd4 and after 5.dxe6! “I was wondering how I should execute the knight underpromotion after 5...dxe3 6.exf7+ Ke7 7.fxg8N+ , since my two knights were still on the board. How about that, a knight underpromotion on move 7 which is actually the best move. He saw this and played 5...Bxe6 to keep me at a one pawn advantage.”

4.exd5 has also been played by Dr Ted Bullockus (see Study Material) and it may well be the best move, e.g., 4...exd5 5.dxc5 Qa5+ 6.Nc3 Nf6 7.a3 (Bullockus-Roberts, US National Open 1976). Dr Bullockus has made a deep study of this gambit, as he told me during the 1994 ICCF Congress, and he hopes to produce his own book on the line someday; however, Tim Sawyer beat him to it.

A more sensible reply for Black than 4...cxd4 is 4...exd5 but this whole set-up makes less sense for Black when White has played Be3 instead of Nd2 at move 3; the Black isolated d-pawn is obviously much weaker here.

In my opinion, acceptance has to be tried, so for better or worse Black's choice must be 3...dxe4 .

Now, however, White has a major decision!

My opponent chose the move which I think is best, 4.Nd2 , and which we already saw in the Introduction (Daniel v Zagorovsky). This was the move played by Alapin and Mieses in the early days of the gambit and it was also the choice of Paul Keres in a famous game from his youth.

Before continuing with the game, you may ask why the knight goes to the d2 square. This is the reasoning:

a) White wants to attack the e4-pawn and then react according to how Black chooses to defend (or not defend) the gambit pawn;

b) In the Tarrasch Variation, 3.Nd2 has the drawback of blocking in the bishop but in the Alapin-Diemer Gambit, the bishop is already developed so that is no drawback here;

c) 4.Nc3 allows Black to pin the knight, if he wishes, by 4...Bb4 . (He can also play 4...Nf6 with a position that can also arise via 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Be3 dxe4 , the Rasa-Studier gambit.) Then 5.f3 Nf6 transposes to the line 4.f3 Nf6 5.Nc3 Bb4 seen below. On the whole, I think 4.Nc3 is irrelevant and inferior. Either 4.Nd2 or 4.f3 , or both, must be superior to it.

4.Nd2

After 4.Nd2 I decided to follow a line recommended by Heidenfeld in our book.

4...f5!?

This move actually gets an “!” in ECO but I will not play it again. White has found improvements, as we shall see in a minute.

Wolfgang Heidenfeld also thought Black is OK after 4...Nf6 5.c3 (After Diemer's move 5.c4 , Sawyer gives 5...Nc6 6.Ne2 e5 7.Qa4 Bd7 with advantage to Black) and now:

a) 5...b6! 6.g3 Bb7 7.Bg2 Be7 8.Qc2 O-O! (“ 8...Qd5 9.Ne2 followed by c4, Nc3 would give White a fine game,” he wrote.) 9.Nxe4 Nxe4 (or 9...Nd5 10.Nf3 Nd7 =, Mieses-Janowski, Vienna 1896) 10.Bxe4 Bxe4 11.Qxe4 Na6 “and, whether or not the White queen remains on the diagonal”, 12...Qd5 . Sawyer does not seem to have an answer to this; it's an untested suggestion and remains critical.

b) 5...Bd7 (Alapin-Showalter, Vienna 1898) “is also playable” wrote Heidenfeld. Alapin chose 6.Qc2 but American master Norman Whitaker preferred 6.Ne2 in a game cited by Sawyer.

c) It's interesting that Sawyer gives a game Whitaker-Heidenfeld, Lugano ol 1968, won by White after 5...Nbd7 6.Qc2 b6 7.Nxe4 . No doubt this is why Heidenfeld came to prefer different answers to 4.Nd2 but he still thought 4.f3 was more dangerous! I am not sure he was right about that.

Instead of following 4...Nf6 with 5.c3 , White can also play 5.f3 with the idea that after 5...exf3 6.Nxf3 he has the typical BDG recipe: two half-open files and fast development, in exchange for a pawn. This line is discussed in the Introduction, in connection with the game Daniel-Zagorovsky. However, the logical question for White to ask himself is: if I am going to play f2-f3 anyway, then why not at move 4? Unless White really wants to recapture on f3 with his queen's knight (unlikely) or is really excited by his chances in the 4.Nd2 f5 line, it is better to play f2-f3 at once if that is your intention. See later in this chapter.

There is also a problem about the 4.Nd2 Nf6 5.f3 move-order. In practice Black normally meets 5.f3 by 5...Nd5! (This is, after all, the main point behind 4...Nf6 ) and most games then continue 6.Qe2 Nc6 7.c3 exf3 8.Ngxf3 Be7 to reach the next diagram position.

Preserving the bishop is now too slow, e.g., 9.Bf2 O-O 10.O-O-O a5 11.Qe4 a4 12.Qc2 a3 13.b3 Bb4! 14.cxb4 Ncxb4 15.Qc4 Nxa2+ 16.Kc2 Ra6 0-1, Leisebein-Hergert, corr., Germany, 1995.

The best move from the diagram seems to be 9.Qf2 which scores 3-0 for White in my database. However, there has been no critical test of this line yet. Black must try to solve the problem of his queenside development, so castling is not right here, and nor is 9...Nf6 to which White replies 10.Ne4 , nor 9...Nxe3 as the bishop cannot run away. Also 9...b6 is probably asking for trouble since White has just cleared the way for 10.Bb5 , to be followed by Ne5.

I think Black wants to achieve queenside castling, capture on e3 while White is still obliged to recapture with the queen and then advance ...e5 at the right moment. All this points to 9...Qd6! as the move to try.

5.f3 Nc6!

This is the critical line for 4...f5 . The well-known game Keres-E.Verbac, corr. 1933, went 5...exf3? 6.Ngxf3 Nf6 7.Bd3 c5? 8.O-O ( 8.Qe2!? With the idea 0-0-0 — Minev) 8...cxd4 9.Nxd4 .

The Keres game ended 9...f4? 10.Rxf4! e5 11.Bb5+ +- 11...Kf7 12.Qh5+ g6 13.Bc4+ Kg7 14.Qh6+! 1-0.

In Rittenhouse-Slater, corr. (ASPCC finals) 1995, Black “improved” by 9...Be7 but it still did not take long to end: 10.Bb5+ Kf7 11.Qe2 Nd5 12.N2f3 Qd6 13.Rad1 e5 14.Nxf5 Bxf5 15.Rxd5 (with the lovely pin Bc4 to follow) 15...Qe6 16.Rxe5 1-0.

The whole line with 5...exf3 is bad for Black. After 6.Ngxf3 Nf6 7.Bd3 (Janowski and others used to play 7.Bc4 here.) 7...Be7 8.Qe2 O-O 9.O-O-O White has more than enough compensation for the pawn according to Minev, while 7...Bd6 8.Qe2 O-O 9.O-O-O Nc6 10.c3 e5 ± was a game Alapin-Janowski.

6.fxe4

Wolfgang, in our book, cited Schwarz analysis that went 6.c3 e5! 7.dxe5 Nxe5 8.fxe4 Nd3+! 9.Bxd3 Qxd3 10.Qe2 fxe4 as at least slightly better for Black. R.Schwarz, primarily a collator of opening lines, is not the most reliable source but we could find nothing wrong with this line. However, Sawyer indicates 10.Qh5+! g6 11.Qe2 because now 11...fxe4 12.Bd4 would regain the pawn with a good game, so instead Black must play 11...Qxe2+ 12.Nxe2 Bg7 with a strategic endgame and chances for both sides.

The only possibility for White mentioned by ECO is 6.Bb5 but after 6...Nf6 ( 6...Bd7!? , threatening ...Nxd4, may transpose after 7.c3 Nf6 8.Qe2 ) the Encyclopaedia's assessment of advantage to Black is correct, e.g., 7.Qe2 (If 7.Bxc6+ bxc6 Black's bishop pair makes up for damaged pawns.) Hucks-Peek, corr. 1985, went 7...Be7 8.O-O-O O-O 9.c3 Bd7 10.Nh3 a6 11.Bc4 Na5 12.Nf4 Nxc4 13.Qxc4 Qc8 14.fxe4 fxe4 15.Rde1 b5 16.Qb3 a5 17.g3 a4 18.Qc2 b4 19.Nxe4 Nxe4 20.Qxe4 bxc3 21.bxc3 Rb8 22.d5 Ba3+ 0-1.

Black's plan in this game may be better than 7...Bd7 (Threatening ...Nxd4) as seen in Roth-Bartal, corr., 1975: 8.c3 Be7 9.fxe4 fxe4 10.Bf4 O-O and Black is better since he has an extra Pawn. White can clearly improve on 11.Nxe4?! Nxd4! as played in the game, but it shouldn't be enough: 11.O-O-O!? Qe8! . If Black doesn't keep initiative, the e4-pawn will fall, leaving e6 weak and good prospects for White. But now the ...Nxd4 threat is renewed, e.g., 12.Kb1 ( 12.Nxe4 Nxd4 13.Bxd7?? Nxe2+ or 12.Bxc7 Nxd4 ) 12...Nxd4! 13.Bxd7 Nxe2 14.Bxe8 Nxf4 -+. Or if 11.Rd1!? then 11...Qe8! with the idea 12.Nxe4 Nxd4 13.Bxd7 Nxe2 14.Bxe8 Nxf4 . Or if 11.Be5 then 11...Qe8 12.a4 a6 .

After 6.Bb5 Nf6 , Roth had tried 7.fxe4 (mentioned by Sawyer) in an earlier game: 7.fxe4 fxe4 8.c3 Be7 9.Ngf3 O-O 10.O-O e5 11.Ng5 exd4 12.Bc4+ Kh8 13.cxd4 Qe8 14.Ngxe4 h6 15.Rf4 Nxe4 16.Nxe4 Bf5 17.Qf3 Bxe4 18.Qxe4 Rxf4 19.Bxf4 Bd6 20.Qxe8+ Rxe8 21.Bxd6 cxd6 22.Bb5 Rc8 23.Rc1 Ne7 24.Re1 Rc7 25.Bd3 Kg8 26.Re6 d5 27.Re5 Kf7 28.Kf2 Rc6 29.Ke3 Kf6 30.h4 Rc1 31.Kd2 Rg1 32.Re2 Nc6 33.Ke3 Rh1 34.Rf2+ Ke6 35.Rf4 Re1+ 36.Kd2 Ra1 37.a3 g5 38.hxg5 hxg5 1/2-1/2, Roth-Krizsan, Hungarian Corr. Ch, 1969.

6...Nxd4 7.Ngf3

A calmer alternative is 7.c3 , e.g., 7...Nc6 8.Qc2 Bd6 9.exf5 (N.Niedermann-W.Knorich, Hungary corr. 1987) 9...Qh4+ 10.Bf2 Qf4 11.fxe6 Bxe6 = Sawyer.

7...Bc5!

This was another Schwarz recommendation cited with approval by Heidenfeld. It improves on a 1956 postal game Seidel-Hoffmann that went 7...Nxf3+ 8.Qxf3 g6? (pathetic; 8...Nf6 9.O-O-O Qe7 10.exf5 exf5 11.Bc4 Bd7 is critical here.) 9.O-O-O Bg7 10.exf5 exf5 11.Bc4 (Minev gives 11.Bb5+!? c6 12.Rhe1 .) 11...Qf6 12.c3 Ne7 13.Rhe1 Qc6 14.Bg5 Bf8 15.Qxc6+ bxc6 16.Bf6 1-0.

8.Nxd4

8.c3 was tried in Colucci-Giusti, corr. Italy 1975. Black was too materialistic and time-wasting with 8...Nc2+ 9.Qxc2 Bxe3 10.Nc4 fxe4 11.Nfe5 Bf4 12.Rd1 Qh4+ 13.g3 Bxg3+ 14.hxg3 Qxg3+ 15.Ke2 Nf6 16.Rh3 Qg5 17.Rd8+ 1-0. Instead 8...Nxf3+ 9.Qxf3 Bxe3 10.Qxe3 seems logical and now, with an extra pawn, Black could simply play 10...Qe7 but he may also be more adventurous by 10...Nf6!? 11.exf5 O-O 12.Bc4 ( 12.fxe6 Ng4 ) 12...Nd5 .

Sawyer has played 8.Bc4!? which gives White superior development in the event of 8...Nxc2+ (although that does pick up a pawn, I don't recommend it) but does not force the knight to move so that 8...Nxf3+ is not required. I suggest continuing the rapid development by 8...Nf6! and if White plays 9.exf5 exf5! then the square e6 becomes available for Black's light-squared bishop. White can keep a temporary initiative by 10.c3 Nxf3+ 11.Qxf3 Bxe3 12.Qxe3+ Qe7 13.Qxe7+ Kxe7 14.O-O-O but after 14...Re8! , ensuring a safe square for the king at f8, the extra pawn should tell in the end. It is hard to see a viable way for White to keep pieces on the board without getting into early difficulties after 8...Nf6 . Moreover, after 9.exf5 it is certainly an option for Black to just castle but I think it gives no advantage since after 9...O-O 10.fxe6 there is a threat of e7+ which forces 10...Kh8 after which 11.O-O will lead to wholesale equalising exchanges.

8...Bxd4 9.Qe2

In a, probably later, game Deisz-Van de Wynkele, my opponent tried to improve by 9.Qh5+ g6 10.Qe2 Nf6 11.exf5 Bxe3 12.Qxe3 gxf5 but in view of the fact that ...g6 was useful for Black, the text is probably better.

9...Bxe3

I rejected both 9...Bxb2?! and 9...Qf6!? 10.Bxd4 Qxd4 11.O-O-O .

10.Qxe3 Qh4+ 11.g3 Qh6 12.Qc5

Not 12.Qe2 Ne7 and ...0-0; nor 12.Qc3 Bd7 .

12...Bd7

A critical position. I compared notes with my opponent after the game.

13.exf5

a) 13.O-O-O Bc6 14.Bg2 ( 14.Kb1 O-O-O ) and now maybe at least some of 14...O-O-O!? or 14...fxe4!? or 14...Ne7!? or 14...Nf6!? ought to favor Black;

b) 13.Qxc7? Rc8 14.Qxb7 Rxc2 15.Rd1 Qe3+ 16.Be2 Nf6 17.Qb8+ Kf7 18.Qxh8 Nxe4 19.Rf1 Rxd2 -+;

c) 13.Nc4!? with an attack says Deisz; he said he couldn't remember afterwards why he did not play this move, which I don't seem to have considered. But is it really strong?

13...Bc6

The alternative 13...b6!? (not 13...exf5?! 14.O-O-O ± with the threat of Reich) leads to a maze of variations:

a) 14.Qxc7!? Qe3+ 15.Kd1! Rc8 16.Qxa7!? ( 16.Qf4 Qxf4 17.gxf4 exf5 =) 16...Nf6 with the idea ...0-0 with an attack; I also analysed the unclear 16...Bc6 17.Qxg7 ( 17.Qa6? Rd8 ) 17...Bf3+!? 18.Nxf3 Rd8+ 19.Bd3 Qxf3+ 20.Kd2 .

b) 14.Qe5 is also unclear. Then 14...Qf6 looks solid but not clearly equalising and no prospect of advantage for Black after the queen exchange, so I concentrated on 14...O-O-O!? , e.g., 15.Bg2 Bc6!! 16.Ne4 exf5 17.Qxf5+ Kb8 and White is exposed to crossfire but in the end I didn't trust these lines.

14.Rg1 O-O-O

Deciding against 14...Nf6 and 14...Rd8!? took a lot of time here.

15.Bd3

15.O-O-O (with the threat Qxa7) was the move I spent most time on analysing:

a) 15...Bf3!? 16.Bc4 ( 16.Qxa7 Bxd1 ) 16...Kb8! 17.fxe6 Nf6! 18.e7 Rxd2! 19.Rxd2 Ne4 20.Qb4 c5 21.Qa5 b6 -+;

b) 15...Kb8 is safest but perhaps only adequate for a draw: 16.fxe6 ( 16.Kb1? Nf6! 17.Bg2 Bxg2 18.Rxg2 Ne4 19.Qb4 exf5 -+ or 16.Bd3!? ) 16...Qxe6 17.Bc4 ( 17.Kb1!? or 17.Qc4!? ) 17...Qh6 . As Black has some threats (perhaps 18.Qf2 Nf6 19.Rge1 Rhf8!? ) he is surely at least not worse.

15...Kb8

15...exf5!? was the alternative, when 16.O-O-O Kb8 could transpose to the game. However, after 16.Qxa7 Re8+ 17.Kd1 Qh5+ 18.Kc1 Nf6 19.Rd1 (discouraging ...Ne4) the Black king is more insecure than White's.

16.O-O-O

My opponent wrote afterwards; “With the endgame still to come and no pawn down I am content”. I reckoned I was probably better here, however.

16...exf5

It is not clear that White has equalised since both recaptures have drawbacks.

However White managed to steer the game to a draw. The remaining moves were:

17.Qxf5 Ne7

He set the trap 17...Qxh2? 18.Qg5 Rd7 19.Bf5! Re7 ( 19...Rxd2? 20.Qd8+! +- or 19...h6 20.Qg6! Rxd2 21.Qxg7 a6 22.Qxh8 Bd5 23.Qc3 Rxd1+ 24.Rxd1 +-) 20.Nf3 +- but I never even considered the capture on h2, as I wanted a play for initiative trying to exploit my pin on the knight.

18.Qc5 Nd5!? 19.h4!?

At the time I thought this ?! but the move proved useful. He said the position was now about =. He said moves 19-24 of White are best.

19...b6 20.Qa3 a5?!

Still chasing that illusory win.

21.Kb1 Nb4 22.Nc4 Bf3 23.Rde1!

Obtaining counterplay through control of e5.

23...Rhe8 24.Qc3 Bd5 25.b3 Nxd3 1/2-1/2.

Now it is time to examine 4.f3!? which was the usual choice of Diemer and Heidenfeld.

The main lines here are considered to be 4...exf3 and 4...Nf6 ; we shall look at both of these in a moment. It is not hard to see that both these moves give White the BDG-type position he is looking for, so when I was Black in this position I looked for other ways to handle it.

The first point to note is that fxe4 is not yet a threat for White since Black (so long as he keeps the d8-h4 diagonal open) would reply ...Qh4+ and ...Qxe4. So, find a constructive move that keeps this possibility alive.

My first attempt in this direction was not too successful:

Evidently my opponent, although he conducted the latter part of the game well, did not get real compensation for his pawn in the early stages.

The next diagram shows the position that arose after my 7...Be7 .

The position is very like the Rubinstein Variation of the French Defence except that:

a) Black has an extra pawn;

b) White has an open f-file and an extra piece developed (the e3-bishop).

The differences, objectively, ought to favor Black whose position appears very solid but he has to withstand an attack — not easy to do, especially at rapid time limits. Even in correspondence play, Black can have problems.

The critical line after 4...c5 is 5.dxc5 Qxd1+ 6.Kxd1 .

This position may be uncomfortable for a gambiteer, who is perhaps unwilling to exchange queens so early and give up castling rights, but on the other hand White is not a pawn down. The 4-2 queenside pawn majority could be useful and the better endgame player will win. There just isn't any meaningful experience with this line yet.

Another double-edged attempt, mentioned without a continuation in ECO, is 4...f5 , to hold the pawn on e4 but at the risk of harming the pawn structure.

The main line now is 5.Nd2 , transposing to lines already considered. Of course White can also play 5.Nc3 but of course trying to double Black's pawns by 5.fxe4? does not work, on account of 5...Qh4+ etc.

An even more radical reply for Black is 4...e5!?

This could be the best reason of all for White to prefer 4.Nd2 . That's unless Sawyer's untried suggestion 5.d5 is any good.
