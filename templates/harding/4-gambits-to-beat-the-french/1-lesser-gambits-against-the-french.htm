LESSER GAMBITS AGAINST THE FRENCH

This chapter makes no attempt at systematic treatment of its material. It should be viewed as a bonus chapter giving brief coverage of some other gambits in the French Defence which are not covered in the four main chapters.

[...]

First comes a gambit which does not have any name so far as I am aware: 1.e4 e6 2.b3 d5 3.Bb2 dxe4 . I did not include this as a main gambit for several reasons:

a) Black does not have to capture on e4 in the first place;

b) If he does, then after 4.Nc3 ( 4.f3?! has been tried but is unimpressive) Black usually plays 4...Nf6 and after 5.Qe2 ( 5.g4!? ) makes little attempt to hold on to the pawn. Even if he does, it rarely succeeds:

1.e4 e6 2.b3 d5 3.Bb2 dxe4 4.Nc3

If Black really wanted to hold the pawn he would have to play 4...f5 which is rarely seen and highly unclear.

5.d3!?

Also 5.f3 has been seen. There follows 5...Bd6 and now:

a) 6.g3? h5 7.Bg2 h4 8.fxe4 hxg3 9.h3 Nc6 10.Qe2 Nd4 11.Qd3 e5 12.exf5 Bxf5 13.Ne4 Nf6 14.Bxd4 Nxe4 and 0-1, 32 moves, in Habershon-R.Watson British Corr. Ch Candidates, 1983-84.

b) Somewhat better is 6.Nb5 , e.g., 6...Nf6 7.fxe4 Nxe4 8.Qh5+ g6 9.Qh6 Rg8 10.Nf3 Nf6 11.O-O-O Qe7 12.Re1 Ng4 13.Nxd6+ cxd6 14.Qf4 e5 15.Nxe5 dxe5 16.Bxe5 Nc6 17.Bc4 with some initiative to White: 17...g5 18.Qg3 Ngxe5 19.Bxg8 Kf8 20.Bd5 Qa3+ 21.Kb1 Nb4 22.Qc3 Qxa2+ 23.Kc1 Qxc2+ 24.Qxc2 Ned3+ 25.Qxd3 Nxd3+ 26.Kd1 Nxe1 27.Rxe1 Rb8 (Black is temporarily a pawn ahead but his lack of development means that White will gain material.) 28.g3 Bd7 29.Re3 b6 30.h4 h6 31.Be6 Rd8 32.Re5 1-0 (rather premature) Kretschmer-Poetzschmann, Prelims 11th East German Corr. Ch, 1981.

5...exd3 6.Bxd3 Nf6 7.Qe2 Qe7

8.O-O-O g6 9.Nf3 Bh6+ 10.Kb1 O-O 11.Bc4 Re8 12.h4 a6 13.Rhe1 b5 14.Nd5! Nxd5 15.Bxd5 c6 and now White launched an instructive combination:

16.Bxe6+!! (This leads to a combination of an e-file pin and deadly threats on the dark squares.) 16...Bxe6 17.Ng5 Bxg5 18.hxg5 Ra7 19.Qe5 Qg7 20.Qc5 Qf7 21.Rxe6 Rd7 22.Rxd7 Qxd7 23.Rxe8+ Qxe8 24.Qc3 1-0, Schneider-Schlag, East Germany corr., 1982. This game shows that the fianchettoed dark-squared bishop can do real damage if it is not watched.

Moving on now to gambits following 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 , I should make brief mention of the Diemer-Duhm Gambit, 3.c4 .

This can obviously arise in various ways, e.g., if Black declines the Blackmar-Diemer Gambit ( 1.d4 d5 2.e4 ) with 2...e6 or even if White answers the Queen's Gambit Orthodox ( 1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6 ) with 3.e4!? .

Here are two games by the gambit's originators:

After 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 we already looked in the Introduction at the main gambit that can arise in the Tarrasch Variation. However, there is another gambit possibility.

After 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 the principal gambit in the Advance Variation (the Milner-Barry) arises following 4.c3 and in that chapter we shall also deal briefly with the related Nun and Srensen Gambits.

However, White need not defend the d-pawn. It is possible to play 4.Nf3 and leave it for Black to capture.

The British player David Rumens had a lot of success with this gambit in the 1970s and it is quite hard for Black to play even when he achieves objective equality from the opening, as the following game shows.

[...]

Now we consider the positions arising after 3.Nc3 . If Black plays 3...dxe4 he avoids gambits but tends to get a lifeless game in which a draw is all he can hope for.

So let us look briefly at 3...Nf6 and then move on to 3...Bb4 .

The Raser-Studier Gambit, 1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Be3 , has to be mentioned briefly. To my mind, it is an inferior version of the 3.Be3 gambit considered in Chapter 3. For a fuller coverage, see The Blackmar-Diemer Gambit Keybook by Tim Sawyer.

Here is a rather messy example of the gambit succeeding; note the early move order: 1.d4 Nf6 2.Nc3 d5 3.e4 e6

4.Be3 dxe4 5.f3

5...Nd5 6.Qd2 Bb4 7.a3 Bxc3 8.bxc3 Nc6 9.Bf2 Nf6 10.Bh4 O-O 11.Bc4 e5 12.d5 Na5 13.Ba2 Qd6 14.Ne2 exf3 15.Ng3 e4 16.O-O e3 17.Qxe3 Ng4 18.Qxf3 Qb6+ 19.Kh1 Qh6 20.Rae1 Bd7 21.Qf4 Qxh4 22.h3 f5 23.Re7 Bb5 24.Qd4 Nf2+ 25.Rxf2 Qxg3 26.Rf3 Qg6 27.Qc5 Qb6 28.d6+ Kh8 29.Qe5 Rf6 30.Rg3 Qxd6 31.Rgxg7 Nc4 32.Rxh7+ 1-0, A.Tonelli-Sgherri, corr. Italy, 1993.

Another possibility for White is to prevent the pin by 5.a3 , but this is probably too slow: 5...Nc6 6.f3 Nd5 7.Nxd5 exd5 8.Qd2 Bd6 ( 8...Bf5 Sawyer) 9.O-O-O O-O 10.fxe4 dxe4 11.Bc4 Kh8 12.Ne2 Ne7 13.Rdf1 Nf5 14.Nf4 c6 15.Rf2 Qe7 16.c3 Bd7 17.Rhf1 Rae8 18.Nh5 Nxe3 19.Qxe3 f5 20.Nf4 g5 21.g3 gxf4 22.gxf4 Be6 23.Bxe6 Qxe6 24.Rg2 Rg8 25.Rfg1 Rxg2 26.Rxg2 Rg8 0-1, Rasmussen-E.Donaldson, Tacoma, WA, 1990.

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Bg5 Be7 5.e5 Nfd7 6.h4

The Albin-Chatard-Alekhine Attack in the Classical French, a dangerous line which Black normally prefers these days to avoid by 4...dxe4 .

I shall just give one totally unconvincing but amusing example of what can happen when Black accepts the gambit, which he rarely does in practice. I did so in a couple of games and scored 50% but I don't think I shall be repeating this line.

6...Bxg5 7.hxg5 Qxg5 8.Nh3 Qe7 9.Nf4 Nc6 10.Qg4 Nxd4!? 11.O-O-O!

11.Qxg7 Qf8 should be fine for Black, although I managed to lose a game from this position: 12.Qxf8+ Kxf8 ( 12...Nxf8!? ) 13.O-O-O Nc6?! (This plan to trap his invading knight misfires.) 14.Nb5 a6 15.Nxc7 Ra7 16.Ncxe6+ fxe6 17.Ng6+ Kg7 18.Nxh8 Ndxe5?! . My opponent said afterwards he considered moves 18-21 were the turning point; this was Black's last chance. 19.f4! I badly underestimated this, overlooking his next. 19.Be2 Kxh8 is OK for Black with two pieces versus rook.) 19...Ng4 20.Nf7! Nf2?! (Still chasing a mirage) 21.Nd6 Nxh1 22.Nxc8 Ra8 23.Nd6 Nd8? 24.f5! +- 24...exf5 25.Nxf5+ Kf6 26.Ne3 Nc6 27.Nxd5+ Ke5 28.Nb6 Rd8 29.Re1+ Kf4 30.Bxa6 Nf2 (Preferring a quick death) 31.Bxb7 Nb4 32.Kb1 Rb8 33.Re7 Rd8 34.Nd7 1-0, Lynn-Harding, Irish Corr. Ch, 1989.

11...Nf5 12.Nfxd5!

With compensation (Kasparov/Keene in BC02). This is critical. Not 12.Ncxd5? when 12...Nxe5! does work.

12...Nxe5??

Forgetting my own analysis! 12...exd5!? is decidedly risky but very hard to analyse out, e.g., 13.Nxd5! Nxe5!? 14.Qe2!? Qd6 was just one of the lines I had examined while writing my book The Classical French and the variations got muddled in my mind while playing the present game over-the-board. Later I examined 12...Qc5 when Fritz5 thinks White wins by 13.b4 but I am not so sure.

13.Qa4+ Bd7 14.Nxc7+?!

This should still win but permits complications. Both my opponent and I missed the simple kill 14.Nxe7! Bxa4 15.Nxf5 +-.

14...Kf8 15.Qe4

After 15.Rxd7!? Qxd7 ( 15...Nxd7 16.Nxa8 a6 17.Nc7 ( 17.Bxa6!? ) 17...Nc5 18.Qa5 +-) 16.Qxd7 Nxd7 17.Nxa8 Ke7 18.Nc7 a6 Black will recover some material but probably not enough to save himself.

The text move is apparently strong but it temporarily loses the initiative. Now, unluckily for my opponent (who was one of a British junior squad at this international open) I hit upon a brilliant move which maintains the strong central knight and creates a safe place for the king.

15...f6!! 16.Nxa8 Bc6 17.Qe1 Kf7

And Black will have some compensation for the exchange.

18.Rg1

A miserable move but it prepares his next.

18...Rxa8 19.Bb5

To eliminate my good bishop.

19...Rc8!

Spotting the right plan again. Black is on a roll...

20.Bxc6 Rxc6 21.Qd2 Rb6!

The move he overlooked. If 22.Qd8? , then 22...Qb4 , or if 22.b3?? , then 22...Nc4! . Despite not having a dark-squared bishop, the attacking concept carried out by Black has some similarity to a Dragon Sicilian.

22.f4 Qb4 23.Na4 Qxa4 24.fxe5 Qxa2 25.Qc3 Qa1+ 26.Kd2 Qa4 27.Rgf1 Rc6 28.Qb3 Qa5+ 29.c3?

After 29.Kc1 Black may not have more than a draw without risk.

29...Rb6 30.Qa3 Qxe5 31.Rfe1 Qf4+ 32.Kc2 ( 32.Ke2? Qe3+ 33.Kf1 Ng3 mate) 32...Ne3+ 33.Rxe3 Qxe3 34.Rd7+ Kg6 35.Qe7 Qe2+ 36.Kc1 ( 36.Rd2 Rxb2+ 37.Kxb2 Qxd2+ 38.Kb1 Qd5 -+) 36...Qxb2+ 37.Kd1 Qb1+ 38.Kd2 Rb2+ 39.Ke3 Qe1+ 40.Kd4 Rd2+ 41.Kc4 Qe4+ 0-1, Garner-Harding, Dublin Telecom, 1991.

Finally, there is the Winawer.

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.a3

A gambit line can arise sometimes via 4.Nge2 . Here is quite a good example of what can then happen: 4...dxe4 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.Nxc3 Nc6 7.Bb5 Nge7 8.Bg5 f6 9.Be3 O-O 10.Qd2 f5!? 11.O-O-O a6! 12.Bxc6 Nxc6 , really putting White to the test by holding the pawn as long as possible.

Maybe White can now get some advantage after 13.f3 , e.g., 13...e5 14.d5 Na5 15.Qe2 b5 16.fxe4 f4 17.Bc5 Rf7 18.d6 in Wiener-Bischoff, German Ch 1984.

A game I played continued: 13.Bg5 Qe8 14.f3 e5! 15.dxe5 Nxe5 16.fxe4 fxe4 17.Nd5 Rf7 18.Qc3 Bg4 19.Rde1 Nc6 20.Qg3 Qd7 ( 20...Qe5 21.Qxe5 Nxe5 22.Rxe4 ) 21.Ne3 Bh5 22.Qh4 Bg6 23.Rd1 Qe6 24.Kb1 Ne5!? 25.Rd4 (Totally unexpected; I had prepared for 25.Nd5 Nc4 or 25.h3!? ) 25...Nc6 26.Rd5 Raf8 27.Rhd1 Ne7 28.Rd8 Nf5 29.Qg4 Rxd8 30.Rxd8+ Rf8 31.Rxf8+ Kxf8 32.Qf4 Qf7 33.Qxe4 Nxe3 34.Qxe3 Qf1+ 35.Ka2 Qc4+ 36.Ka1 ( 36...h6 37.Qf4+ ) 1/2-1/2, Mark Thomas-Harding, BPCF corr., 1985.

4...Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 Ne7

If Black accepts the gambit by 5...dxe4 the old move was 6.Qg4 but in this book we concentrate on the Winckelmann-Reimer, 6.f3!?

With 5...Ne7 Black avoids the WRG and offers White the chance to return to the Main Line Winawer by 6.e5 , while ECO only considers 6.Bd3 . (EC02, C15, Note 83 Ed.)

However, it is possible to offer a new gambit:

6.Bg5! dxe4 7.f3! exf3 8.Nxf3 h6 9.Bc1!? Nbc6 10.Bd3 O-O 11.O-O a6 12.Qe1 Re8 13.Qg3! Kh8 14.Bxh6!! Nf5 ( 14...gxh6 15.Ng5! Rf8 had to be tried; not 15...hxg5 16.Qh3+ Kg7 17.Qh7+ Kf8 18.Rxf7 mate) 15.Bxg7+! Nxg7 16.Ng5 1-0, Koser-Winkler, corr. German Gambit Open, 1996.
