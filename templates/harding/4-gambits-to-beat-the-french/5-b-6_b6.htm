6...b6

John Watson's recommendation, given an ! as the safest reply in the 2nd edition of his Play the French. However, I note that the Germans who are analysing the WRG most deeply do not pay so much attention to 6...b6 now, preferring other lines that follow later in this chapter.

Watson mostly concentrates on the move 7.Bb5+!? ( 7.Be3 Bb7 8.Bb5+ , as in two early games by Winckelmann-Flugrath, corr., 1988, also looks unclear at best.) followed by:

a) 7...c6?! when:

a1) 8.Bc4 b5 9.Bb3 Qa5 10.Bb2 Nf6 11.fxe4 O-O = (intending ...Bb7, ...c5 says Watson). Leisebein,P-Brinke, thematic corr., 1991, concluded: 12.e5 Nd5 13.a4 Nxc3 14.Qd2 b4 15.Nf3 Ba6 16.Bxc3 bxc3 17.Qf4 Nd7 18.Qe4 Rfc8 19.Ng5 Nf8 20.Qf3 Rc7 21.Ne4 Rd8 22.Qe3 c5 23.Nxc5 Rxc5 24.dxc5 Rd2 0-1.

a2) 8.Be2 Bb7 ( 8...Nf6 transposes after 9.Bg5 h6 10.Bh4 exf3 11.Nxf3 Nbd7 12.O-O Bb7 13.Bd3 into a line considered above.) 9.Nh3! Nf6 10.O-O is an improvement given by Gutman.

b) Watson recommends 7...Bd7 when:

b1) He only considers 8.Qe2 in reply, e.g., 8...Bxb5 9.Qxb5+ and now:

b11) 9...Qd7!? 10.Qxd7+ ( 10.Qe2 exf3 11.Nxf3 Nc6 12.O-O Nf6 13.Bg5 h6 14.Bh4 Nd5! clearly favors Black, or if here 13.Ng5 Qd5! ) 10...Nxd7 “with good play”, e.g., 11.fxe4 Ngf6 12.e5 Ne4 13.Ne2 c5 14.O-O cxd4 ( 14...Rc8 is also promising.) 15.Nxd4 Rc8 16.Ra2 f5 (or 16...f6 ) with an edge to Black.

b12) 9...c6 10.Qe2 exf3 11.Nxf3 Ne7 “also leaves White struggling to find compensation” wrote Watson, but Gutman claims that 12.O-O Nd7 13.Bg5 h6 14.Bh4 O-O 15.Rad1 gives White sufficient compensation for the pawn.

b2) As an alternative, White can consider 8.Bxd7+ , e.g., Koser-Sohnchen, corr., 1993, which went 8...Nxd7 9.Qe2 exf3 10.Nxf3 Ngf6 11.O-O O-O 12.Bg5 Qe8 13.Nd2 Nd5 14.c4 Ne7 15.Rae1 Ng6 16.Rf3 e5 17.Qf2 f6 18.Bh4 Qe6 19.Bg3 Qc6 20.c3 Rae8 21.h4 1-0.

Or if here 12...h6 then Gutman gives 13.Bh4 c5 14.Ne5 Qc7 15.Rad1!? Nxe5 16.dxe5 Nd5 17.Rd3 c4 18.Rg3 with good attacking chances. Black can improve by 8...Qxd7!? 9.fxe4 Qc6 10.Qd3 Nf6 , he says.

7.Nh3!

This is the typical treatment of the gambit by WRG specialists in recent times. The knight move was the main line in analysis by Gutman, published in both Schach-Archiv and Gambit Revue in 1997.

7...Bb7

Others analysed by Gutman:

a) 7...exf3?! 8.Qxf3 c6 ( 8...Qd5 9.Qg3 ) 9.Bd3 Nf6 10.O-O h6 11.Qg3 Rg8 12.a4 Nbd7 13.Ba3 Bb7 14.Bd6 c5 15.Nf4 g5 16.Nxe6 fxe6 17.Qh3 with an attack for White in Herbold-Theis, corr., 1996.

b) 7...Nf6 8.fxe4 Nxe4 9.Qg4 Nf6 10.Qg3!? (Not 10.Qxg7?! Rg8 11.Qh6 Rg6 12.Qh4 Rg4 nor 10.Qh4?! Qd5! 11.Be2 Ba6 ) 10...Bb7 11.Bd3 Nh5 ( 11...O-O?! 12.Bg5 Nbd7 13.O-O c5 14.Qh4 h6 15.Bxh6 ) 12.Qg4 Nf6 ( 12...g6?! 13.O-O O-O 14.Bh6 Re8 15.Ng5 f5 16.Qe2 Qe7 17.Rae1 , Winckelmann-Prahl, corr., 1994) 13.Qe2!? Nbd7 14.O-O Qe7 15.a4 with the initiative for White.

White has tried 8.fxe4 Bxe4 9.Ng5 when:

a) 9...Bb7?! 10.Bb5+!? and now:

a1) 10...Nd7 11.O-O Ngf6 12.Qe1!? O-O 13.Bd3 h6 14.Nf3 with the initiative to White, Gutman;

a2) 10...c6 11.O-O! Nf6 when A.Bangiev recommends 12.Bc4!? , e.g., 12...O-O ( 12...Nbd7 13.Bxe6 fxe6 14.Nxe6 — Bangiev) 13.Qd3 Nbd7 ( 13...h6 14.Rxf6 hxg5 15.Bxg5 gxf6 16.Bh6 Qd6 17.Rf1 Re8 18.Rf4 was Bangiev's line.) 14.Rf4!? h6 15.Rh4 Re8 ( 15...hxg5 16.Bxg5 Re8 17.Qh3 Kf8 18.Re1 ) 16.Nf3 with good attacking chances — Gutman.

b) 9...Bc6 was suggested by Konikowski and approved by Watson: the idea is ...Nf6, ...0-0. White's best try is 10.Nf3!? , e.g.,:

b1) 10...Nf6 11.Bd3 O-O ( 11...Qd6 12.O-O Nbd7 13.Qe1 h6 14.a4! with the initiative to White, Vogel-Bischof, corr., 1996) 12.O-O Nbd7 ( 12...Bb7 13.Qe1 c5 14.Qh4 Nbd7 15.Bg5 h6 16.Bxh6 Vogel-Theis, corr., 1996) 13.Qe1 b5!? 14.Bf4!? (trying to provoke 14...h6 , e.g., 15.Qh4 Nd5 16.Bg5 N5f6 17.Bxh6! gxh6 18.Qxh6 Ne4 19.Bxe4 Bxe4 20.Ng5 Bxc2 21.Rf4 Qe7 22.Raf1 ) 14...Nb6 15.Ne5 Qd5 16.Qg3 Bb7 17.Bg5 Nh5 18.Qh3 f6 19.Nf3 g6 20.Bh6 with the initiative to White — Gutman.

b2) 10...Nd7 11.Bd3 h6 12.O-O Ngf6 13.Qe1 O-O 14.Bxh6!!

14...gxh6 15.Qg3+ Kh8 16.Ne5 Be4 17.Nxd7 Rg8 18.Qe5! Rxg2+ 19.Kh1 Rg5+ 20.Bxe4! (queen sac!) 20...Rxe5 21.Nxe5 Kg7 22.Rg1+ Kf8 23.Raf1!? Ke8 24.Bc6+ Kf8 25.Rxf6! ( 25...Qxf6 26.Nd7+ ) 1-0, Winckelmann-Kohlbieter, BDG Fax Tournament, 1997.

b3) 10...h6 11.Bd3 Nf6 12.O-O Nbd7 13.Qe1 Bxf3 14.Rxf3 Qc8 15.a4 c5 16.dxc5 bxc5 17.c4 Rb8 18.Bf4 Rb7 19.Qh4 Rg8 (Black might seem to be safe but appearances are deceptive as he has no positive plan.) 20.Bg3 Rh8 21.Qf4 g5 22.Qd2 Nh5 23.Qc3 Ke7 24.Be4 Nxg3 25.hxg3 Rb8? (Costs a tempo; 25...Rb4 or 25...Rb6 might hold the balance.) 26.Raf1 f5 27.Qg7+ Kd6 28.Rd1+ Kc7

29.Bxf5 exf5 30.Rxf5 Rb6 31.a5 Rc6 32.Rfd5 Rd8 33.Qe7 a6 34.g4 Kb8 35.Rxd7 Rxd7 36.Rxd7 Rc7 37.Rxc7 1-0, Gibney-Mogstad, NATT4 corr., 1996-8.

c) 9...Bg6! gives White great problems says Gutman, but this remains to be proved in practice:

c1) 10.Be2 h6!? 11.Nf3 Ne7 12.Ne5 ( 12.O-O Nd7 ) 12...Be4 13.O-O ( 13.Bb5+ c6 14.O-O O-O 15.Bd3 Bxd3 16.Qxd3 b5 17.Bf4 Qd5 ) 13...O-O and he prefers Black.

c2) 10.Bd3 Nf6 11.O-O Qd5! 12.Bf4 Nbd7 also favors Black, says Gutman.

c3) 10.Bb5+ c6 11.Bd3 Nf6 12.O-O when:

c31) Gutman recommends the untested 12...Qd5!? 13.Bf4 ( 13.Be3 Nbd7 14.c4 Qd6 ) 13...Nbd7 14.Qe1 Bxd3 15.cxd3 O-O .

c32) Gambit Revue gives 12...Bxd3 13.Qxd3 h6 14.Nf3 Nbd7 ( 14...O-O 15.Ne5 Nfd7 16.Bf4 Nxe5 17.Bxe5 Nd7 18.Bd6 Re8 19.Qf3 ±) 15.Bf4 O-O 16.c4 Rc8 17.Rad1 with compensation.

c33) Not 12...Bh5? 13.Qe1 Qe7 14.a4 Nbd7 15.a5 O-O 16.Qh4 h6 ( 16...g6 17.h3 and g2-g4; 16...Bg6 17.Nxh7! ±) 17.Nh7! Rfe8 18.Nxf6+ Nxf6 19.Bxh6! Bg6 20.Bg5 1-0, Kroggel-Schaller, corr., 1994.

c4) 10.Bc4 also comes into consideration, e.g., Welling-Bosman, Netherlands Team Corr. Ch, 1995: 10...Nf6 11.Qf3 c6 12.O-O O-O 13.Bf4 h6 14.Nh3 b5 15.Bb3 Nd5 16.a4 a6 17.axb5 cxb5 18.Qg3 Nc6 (for entire game score, see Study Material)

8.Bb5+! Nd7

Or 8...c6 9.Be2!? Nf6 10.O-O c5 transposes to van der Kooij-Everett, corr., 1993: 11.Bg5 exf3 12.Bxf3 Bxf3 13.Qxf3 Nbd7 14.Nf2 O-O 15.Ne4 with the initiative.

9.fxe4 Bxe4 10.O-O Ngf6 11.Ng5 Bg6 12.Bc6

The critical position where Black can try:

a) 12...Rc8 13.Nf3! ( 13.d5 exd5 14.Re1+ Kf8 ) 13...O-O 14.Bg5 and it is hard for Black to free himself, e.g., 14...Qe7 15.Bxd7 Qxd7 16.Ne5 Qa4 17.Rxf6! gxf6 18.Bxf6 h5 19.Qd2 Kh7 20.Rc1 c5 21.Ng4 with a mating attack (Gutman).

b) 12...O-O!? when:

b1) 13.c4?! Rc8 14.Re1 e5! 15.d5 ( 15.dxe5? Nxe5 16.Qxd8 Rfxd8 17.Rxe5 Rd1+ 18.Kf2 Ng4+ ) 15...h6 16.Nh3 Ne4 17.Bb2 Re8 18.a4 Re7 19.a5 Ndc5 20.Qf3 Qd6 was unclear in David-Legky, Paris 1996.

b2) 13.Bxa8!? Qxa8 14.c4!? when in Gutman's opinion Black doesn't have sufficient compensation for the exchange.
