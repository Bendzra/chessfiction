6...e5!?

This was a recommendation of GM Robert Huebner, and it is highlighted in the recent book by Graham Burgess, 101 Chess Opening Surprises. The question after this is who has the more serious weaknesses. The idea definitely needs more attention, especially as I found a surprising lack of new examples when looking through Gambit Revue 1997, which is where one expects to find new ideas in the WRG appearing first.

Clearly the immediate counter-thrust in the center prevents White's principal plan and leads to quite different positions from those discussed earlier in this chapter. If White captures on e5, he may lose the right to castle and his queenside pawn structure is destroyed, while 7.fxe4 is a blunder, answered by ...Qh4+.

7.a4

This was tried in some early Ukrainian games with the gambit. Burgess calls this a logical move, freeing a3 for the bishop. One danger for Black, he says, is that his lack of dark-square control will give White attacking chances. On the other hand, Gutman thinks 7.a4 is too slow.

7...exd4

a) If 7...Nf6 8.Ba3 has been recommended in CC sources. Gutman recommended 7...Nf6!? in Schach-Archiv 1997 but his analysis only considered 8.fxe4 O-O 9.Bg5 ( 9.Nf3 exd4 10.cxd4 Nxe4 11.Bd3 Bg4! 12.Bxe4 Re8 13.Qe2 Bxf3 14.gxf3 f5 15.Bd5+ Kh8 16.Be6 Qxd4 17.Ra3 f4! ) 9...exd4 10.cxd4 Re8 11.e5 Nc6 12.Ne2 ( 12.c3 Qd5 13.Qf3 Nxe5! 14.Qxd5 Nxd5 15.Kd2 Ng4 with clear advantage to Black in Mai-A.Huebner, Hanover 1994) 12...Qd5 13.c4 Qxc4 14.exf6 Nb4 15.Kf2 Nd3+ 16.Kg3 ( 16.Kg1 Bg4 ) 16...h6 with an attack for Black.

b) Gutman also gives 7...Nc6 8.Ba3!? ( 8.d5 Nce7 9.c4 Nf6 10.Ba3 O-O ) with these variations:

b1) 8...exd4 9.Bb5 Bd7 ( 9...dxc3?! 10.Qxd8+ Kxd8 11.Bf8 ) 10.cxd4 a6 11.Bc4 Qf6 12.c3 Nge7 13.Qe2 and the White bishop pair is powerful;

b2) 8...Be6 9.Bb5 a6 10.Bxc6+ bxc6 11.Qe2 exd4 12.Qxe4 Qd5 13.cxd4 Qxe4+ 14.fxe4 with the better endgame for White (Karpenko-Petrovsky, Dnepropetrovsk 1993);

b3) 8...Nf6 9.Bc4!? exd4 10.Ne2 exf3 11.O-O! fxg2 ( 11...fxe2 12.Qxe2+ Kd7 13.Bxf7 ) 12.Re1 with a romantic-style attack;

b4) 8...Nge7!? 9.Bb5 ( 9.fxe4 exd4 ) 9...exd4 is critical.

8.cxd4 Nh6!?

Threatening ...Nf5 and maintaining the possibility of ...Qh4+. This move has not yet been analysed in the usual sources dealing with the gambit so far as I can see.

Instead:

a) 8...Nf6 is also good. Another Winckelmann-Tonjes game went 9.fxe4 Nxe4 10.Nf3 Qe7 11.Qc1 c6? 12.Bd3 Bf5 13.O-O g6 14.Re1 O-O 15.Bh6 Re8 16.g4 Qd7 17.Qf4 1-0, but Gutman recommends 11...Nc3!? with advantage to Black.

b) Gutman gives 8...Qe7?! 9.fxe4 Qh4+ ( 9...Qxe4 10.Qd2 Nf6 11.Bd3 Qe7 12.Ne2 Ne4 13.Qc1 O-O 14.O-O ) 10.Kd2!? Qxe4 ( 10...Nf6 11.e5 Ng4 12.Qe1 ) 11.Bd3 Qxg2+ 12.Ne2 with the initiative in White's hands.

8...Nge7

White is better after 8...Bf5 9.Bb2!? exd4 10.cxd4 says Gutman.

9.Bb2!?

9.fxe4 exd4 10.Nf3 dxc3 11.O-O O-O 12.Bg5 Bg4 is better for Black.

9...O-O 10.O-O-O

Gutman's opinion is that White has more than sufficient compensation for the sacrificed pawn but of course this has to be tested.

Perhaps inevitably, I have not come to any definite conclusions about the WRG. The latest material I have included from 1998 is a corrective to the probably over-optimistic championing of the gambit by grandmaster Gutman in Schach-Archiv last year, but a GM's opinion has to be respected. More practical tests of the critical lines are needed. It would be good to see him play some games with the gambit himself!

I think the WRG is perhaps objectively unsound, but certainly it is a better practical chance than most of White's sharp alternatives to 4.e5 in the Winawer variation. Moreover, since the vast majority of the analytical material has hitherto only been published in German, this chapter is the first serious survey of the line in English so far as I am aware. Except perhaps against German-speaking opponents, the reader should be able to score a lot of points with this gambit!

This concludes the survey of the Winckelmann-Reimer Gambit and (except for the Study Material) the whole book.
