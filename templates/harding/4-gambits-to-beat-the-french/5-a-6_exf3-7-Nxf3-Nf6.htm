6...exf3

The capture must be one of the critical moves but it is nevertheless probably unwise. Since White does not yet threaten fxe4, Black should be thinking about furthering his own development, not that of his opponent.

Winckelmann has said: “Wer das WRG annimmt, verliert garantiert!” (“When the WRG is accepted, defeat is guaranteed!” and I tend to agree with him. The capture at f3 was played a lot by disbelievers in the early days of the WRG but nowadays, forewarned, Black tends to look for subtler replies.

7.Nxf3

This position will seem familiar to Blackmar-Diemer gambit players. It resembles a position arising from the Euwe Defence ( 1.d4 d5 2.e4 dxe4 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.f3 exf3 5.Nxf3 e6 ) and the differences seem to be in White's favor.

A1:

8.Bd3

This is the first position White would like to get. The pawn at c3 means the d-pawn is more secure than in the normal BDG. Both bishops point to the kingside and after mutual kingside castling White will be ready to launch a searing sacrificial attack. The absence of a dark-squared bishop in the defending camp spells trouble at f6, and if f6 falls there is a domino effect: h7 is next.

However the immediate 8.Bg5 is also dangerous, see line A2.

8...c5

Or 8...O-O 9.O-O c5 transposing to A11 below.

Others:

b) 8...Nbd7 has been little played. Nxf6 (If 16...hxg6 17.Rxg6+ wins, but not 17.Rf8+? Kd6 ) 17.Bxf6+ Kxf6 18.Rf1+ 1-0, Ernstmeier- Jensen, CIS Chessforum, 1996.

9.O-O

9...O-O

As this tends to be suicidal, Black has lately examined other moves:

a) 9...Qa5 10.Bd2 Nc6 11.Qe1 O-O? 12.Qh4 Re8 13.Bg5 and White won quickly in Steinert-Neubauer, corr., 1996;

b) 9...Nc6 10.Kh1!? Bd7 ( 10...Qd6 11.a4 ) 11.Qe1 Qc7 12.Qh4 O-O-O 13.Bf4 Qa5 14.Bd6 with initiative to White in Leeners-Reuvekamp, corr., 1991;

c) 9...Nbd7 10.Qe1 and now:

c1) 10...O-O 11.Bg5 Qa5 12.Qh4 cxd4 13.cxd4 h6 14.Bd2 ( 14.Bxh6?! gxh6 15.Qxh6 Qh5 ) 14...Qh5 15.Qg3 b6 16.Rae1 Bb7 17.Ne5 Nxe5 18.dxe5 Nd7 19.Rf4 g5 20.h4 with an attack for White in the rapid game (15-min) Bangiev-Seger, llsede 1995.

c2) 10...Qc7 11.Ng5 c4 12.Bf4 Qc6 13.Be2 with the initiative to White. In Wegener-Betzelt, corr., 1994, there followed 13...O-O 14.Qg3 Ne8 15.Bf3 Qa4 16.Rf2 Ndf6 17.Be5 Nd5 18.Bh5 f6 19.Raf1 h6 20.Ne4 with a decisive attack according to Schach-Archiv, 1997

10.Bg5

10.Qe1 Qc7 11.Qh4 Nbd7 12.Ng5! g6 13.Bf4 Qd8 14.Qh6 +- was given by Schach-Archiv in 1991, following an early Winckelmann game, but in a later article Gutman suggested that Black could reply 10...Qd5!? 11.c4 Qh5 12.c3 Nbd7 13.Bf4 b6 .

11.Qe1!

Gutman says that 11.Bh4!? Nbd7 12.Qe1 is “less attractive but also not bad”.

The move 11.Qe1 is a characteristic Blackmar Diemer piece offer directed against Black's castled king. An early opponent of Winckelmann's wrote to him: “It looks quite interesting, but it will not work.” A few moves later Black had to resign.

11...hxg5

a) Another Winckelmann game went 11...c4 (hoping for 12.Bxc4? hxg5 13.Nxg5 Nh7 ) 12.Qh4 cxd3 13.cxd3 hxg5 14.Nxg5 Re8 15.Rf3 e5 16.Raf1 Qd5 17.Rxf6 with a strong attack. Gutman prefers 12.Bxf6 Qxf6 13.Ne5 Qe7 14.Nxc4 with advantage to White.

b) Delaying acceptance by 11...Nbd7!? 12.Qh4 c4 13.Bxc4 hxg5 14.Nxg5 also is problematic for Black, according to Gutman's analysis:

b1) 14...Re8 15.Bd3 ( 15.Rf3 Nf8 ) 15...Qa5 ( 15...Nf8 16.Rae1 Qa5 17.Re5 Qxc3 18.Rf3 ) 16.Bh7+ Kf8 17.Qh5!? Ke7 18.Qxf7+ Kd8 19.Nxe6+ Rxe6 20.Qxe6 favoring White;

b2) 14...Qa5 15.Rf3! Re8 16.Rh3 with a decisive attack;

b3) 14...Qc7!? 15.Bd3 ( 15.Rf3 Re8 16.Rh3 Kf8 ) 15...Rd8!? 16.Bh7+ Kf8 17.Bg6 Ke7 18.Nxf7!? ( 18.Bxf7 Nf8 19.Rf3 Rd5 unclear) 18...Nb6 19.Nxd8 Qxd8 20.Bd3 Bd7 21.c4 Qh8 22.Qg3 Rc8 23.c5 Nbd5 24.Rab1 and White has threats.

12.Nxg5 Qc7?

a) Against 12...Nbd7 13.Qh4 Re8 , recommended by Konikowski in CC Informator 3, Winckelmann suggests 14.Bh7+ Kf8 15.Bg6! with a strong attack.

b) Or if 12...Ng4 13.Bh7+ Kh8 14.Rxf7! , e.g., 14...cxd4 15.Rxf8+ Qxf8 16.Qh4 Nh6 17.Rf1 Qxa3 18.Bd3 Winckelmann-Clauss, corr., 1992.

c) If 12...g6 13.Qh4 Nh5 ( 13...Nbd7 14.Qh6 ) 14.g4 Schach-Archiv.

d) 12...Re8 13.Qh4 Nbd7 14.Bh7+ Kf8 15.Bg6 Ke7 16.Nxf7 Qb6 17.Ng5 Rf8 18.Rae1 , Winckelmann-Lueddeke, corr., 1992;

13.Qh4 Re8

Schach-Archiv wrongly gives 13...Rd8 as played here; Winckelmann's book confirms the rook went to e8.

14.Rxf6! gxf6 15.Nh7 Nd7

Instead 15...f5 16.Rf1 f6 17.Nxf6+ 1-0 was Winckelmann-Braack, corr., 1993.

8...h6

It is important to hit back before Bd3 and 0-0 can be played.

An alternative for Black is 8...c5 9.Bd3 Nc6 10.Rb1!? (Not 10.O-O?! cxd4 11.c4 h6 Winckelmann-Meister, corr., 1995) 10...h6 ( 10...Qa5 11.Bd2 ) 11.Bh4 ( 11.Bxf6 gxf6 ) 11...Qa5 12.O-O Nd5 (White gets the initiative after 12...Qxc3 13.Bxf6 gxf6 14.Rb3 Qa5 15.Bb5 Bd7 16.d5 exd5 17.Qxd5 O-O-O 18.Qxf7 Rhf8 19.Qc4 .) 13.Ne5!? Nxe5 ( 13...Nxc3 14.Qf3 Nxe5 15.dxe5 O-O 16.Be7 ) 14.dxe5 O-O 15.c4! Ne3 16.Qe1 Qxe1 17.Rfxe1 Nf5 18.Bf2 Nd4!? 19.Be4 Rd8 20.c3 Nc6 21.Red1 Re8 22.Bxc6 bxc6 23.Bxc5 leads to a favorable ending for White since his adversary lacks a dark-squared bishop (analysis by Gutman).

Now White has to choose between:

a) 9.Bxf6 (Bangiev) 9...gxf6!? 10.Bd3 Bd7 11.O-O Bc6 gives White nothing (Gutman).

b) 9.Bc1 c5!? 10.Bd3 Nc6 11.Rb1 O-O with advantage to Black according to Gutman.

c) 9.Bh4! when:

c1) 9...Nbd7 10.Bd3 c5 11.O-O O-O?! 12.Ne5 Qc7 13.Bxf6 Nxf6 14.Rxf6 gxf6 15.Qg4+ Kh8 16.Qf4 Kg7 17.Qg3+ Kh8 18.Ng6+ Winckelmann-Baranowski, corr., 1990;

c2) 9...Bd7 10.Bd3 Bc6 ( 10...g5? 11.Bg3! ) 11.O-O Nbd7 12.c4 Bxf3 13.Qxf3 c6 14.Kh1!? and it's hard to give Black good advice here, writes Gutman.

c3) 9...c5 10.Bd3 Nc6 11.Rb1!? Qa5 12.O-O transposes to 8...c5 above. Instead if ( 11.O-O?! cxd4 12.Qe1 Schueler-Seifert, corr., 1992) Black can reply 12...Qd5!? says Gutman.

So now you can appreciate Winckelmann's little poem about this line which, roughly translated, goes:

Take the pawn!

It has been tried —

But conies near

To suicide!

Having completed our analysis of the WRG Accepted, we shall next look at those sixth moves for Black which leave the d8-h4 diagonal open to deter White's f3xe4.
