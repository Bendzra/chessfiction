Analytical Section

Now let us look at the gambit from a more analytical point of view.

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 4.c3 Nc6

In view of the Kupreichik Variation, 4...Nc6 5.Be3!? , Black may prefer the sequence 4...Qb6 but this doesn't matter to the Milner-Barry player who just continues 5.Nf3 Nc6 6.Bd3 , etc. Also 4...Qb6 could be played to essay the Wade Variation, 5.Nf3 Bd7 6.Be2 Bb5 in which Black seeks a rapid exchange of the “bad” bishop. Then White can play in gambit style by 7.c4! Bxc4 ( 7...dxc4 8.Nc3 or 8.d5 ) 8.Bxc4 Qb4+ ( 8...dxc4 9.d5! gives White some advantage) 9.Nbd2 dxc4 10.a3 or 10.O-O , which you can find in many regular books on the French.

5.Nf3 Qb6

This was almost invariably played until Korchnoi introduced 5...Bd7 in his Candidates Final match against Spassky. There is a lot of theory on this now but all the gambiteer needs to know is that 6.Bd3 cxd4 7.cxd4 Qb6 transposes back to the main line below.

6.Bd3

However, this line is rarely seen because Black has a variety of other 6th moves that avoid gambits, notably 6...c4 . Of course, if White is not interested in offering a gambit he may well play 6.Be2 .

Now we return to 6.Bd3 .

6...cxd4 7.cxd4 Bd7

Of course, 7...Nxd4?? 8.Nxd4 Qxd4?? 9.Bb5+ would leave Black with a red face and a pain in his resigning hand, so acceptance of the gambit has to be prefaced by this move.

At this point the big question arises, which move order should White adopt? Many people who play the MBG possibly do not think here but just bash out whatever move they have been accustomed to but in fact this is a major decision point between:

A: 8.Nc3

B: 8.O-O

As discussed in the notes to Milner-Barry v Isles, and seen in the second Peart-Harding game in the introduction, the move 8.Nc3 is essential if White wants to be sure of offering the gambit. Then Black has no reasonable alternative to acceptance, since 8...Nb4 would be pointless when the bishop can retreat to b1.

The big snag about 8.Nc3 is that it gives Black the choice between the two principal defensive methods at move 10: capturing on e5 or playing ...a6. The theoretical status of the line where Black grabs both pawns (main line A) is currently very dodgy for White and therefore if White is au fait with the subtleties of the gambit, he is likely to play 8.O-O as seen in the Peart and Messere games from the Introduction.

This move order, followed up by 10.Qe2, enables White to rule out the defence that is hardest to bust. The drawback of 8.O-O is that Black can avoid the gambit with 8...Nb4 as we saw there, but this disadvantage is not so serious as the difficulties White has in the 10...Qxe5 line after 8.Nc3 .

There are some lesser offshoots after both 8.O-O (followed by 10.Qe2) and 8.Nc3 but this is the primary dilemma. When Black intends to play the defensive line with ...a6 and ...Ne7 (see B1) then it does not really matter which move order White adopts as that line can only be avoided by playing probably inferior moves such as an early Re1. Of course if White wishes to deviate from the true MBG and play the Nun or Sørensen gambits (see lines B2 and B3) then he has to play the 8.O-O move order.

It should be noted that many books do not make this important move order distinction clear. For example, in Beating the French (Batsford, 1994), Gary Lane takes 8.O-O as the main move but states “The move order involving 8.Nc3 is also perfectly playable”. As he does not mention 8...Nb4 this is not much help. The French Advance by Tony Kosten (Chess Press Opening Guides, Cadogan 1998) does not really discuss the issue either. This is one of those books that handles opening lines through the medium of illustrative games and in the case of one of them, which started 8.Nc3 Nxd4 9.Nxd4 Qxd4 10.Qe2 the author does pass a remark but the 8.O-O / 8.Nc3 issue is not raised at all.

The third edition, and the previous second edition, of the encyclopaedia ECO (C02) takes as its principal move order 8.O-O ( 8.Nc3 is not mentioned) 8...Nxd4 9.Nxd4 Qxd4 10.Nc3 , with a note on 10.Re1 and no reference to the critical 10.Qe2 which would normally transpose to its row 22 on page 20. This move order makes no sense at all for White! First, he allows 8...Nb4 if Black wants to play that, and then he makes no attempt to prevent 10...Qxe5 either!

Because most books do not recognise the specific move order issues, they tend to focus on positions around moves 15-20 and fail to give enough attention to the questions that may really be critical around moves 8-12, for example:

a) After 8.Nc3 Nxd4 9.Nxd4 Qxd4 10.Qe2 can Black punish White's delay with castling? Specifically, is 10...Bb4 good or not?

b) After 8.O-O Nb4 is there any way for White to play for an advantage?

c) After 8.O-O Nxd4 9.Nxd4 Qxd4 10.Qe2 is the Sturm-Schmid line ( 10...Ne7 11.Nc3 Nc6 ) really good for Black, and what should be done about 10...f6 as suggested by Keres?

If you are going to play this line as White, you really need to examine the critical sections later in this chapter and then decide which variations you are willing to allow and which you want to avoid. Only then can you decide between Milner-Barry's traditional 8.Nc3 with 10.0-0 and the modern 8.O-O with 10.Qe2, or other variant move orders.

I'll deal first with the traditional line where the e-pawn is also offered.
