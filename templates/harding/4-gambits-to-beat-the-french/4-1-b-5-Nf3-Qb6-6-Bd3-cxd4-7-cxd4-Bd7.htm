9.Nxd4 Qxd4

Now 10.Nc3 transposes at once to line A above, though it may transpose back to lines considered below.

White has also tried 10.Re1 Ne7 ( 10...Bb4!? with the idea ...Ne7) 11.Nc3 a6 when there might be a good plan for White here but as yet nobody has found one:

a) 12.Ne2?! Qxe5 13.Bf4 Qf6 14.Qb3 (unclear says ECO, citing Natapov-Karlsson, USSR 1972, but...) 14...Bc6 — Estrin, Watson.

b) 12.Qf3?! ( 12.Be3?! Qxe5 13.Qf3 Bc6 — Messere) 12...Nc6 13.Qg3 O-O-O 14.Bf1 Be7! With a Black plus (Lundquist-Dijkstra, corres 1962).

10.Qe2!

This is another critical position. Since the present move-order is the one almost invariably preferred nowadays by Milner-Barry specialists as their route to the main line (avoiding line A, 10.Nc3 Qxe5 ) it is worth taking a minute to examine why older sources reject this sequence. The answer lies in the game Sturm-Schmid (with 10...Ne7 11.Nc3 Nc6 ) where White made a bad mistake at move 14. With a top grandmaster like Lothar Schmid handling Black against an amateur, it is hardly surprising that the gambit failed. Nevertheless, Schmid had a very good feel for the French and this line needs to be re-examined, as does the Keres suggestion at move 10 which seems not to have been tried in practice.

When Black allows play to transpose to the 10.Nc3 a6 main line, matters are not at all as clear as generally supposed!

10...Ne7

Or:

a) 10...f6 (! — Keres) 11.exf6 Nxf6 12.Nc3 ( 12.Rd1 Qh4 ) 12...Bd6!? (or here 12...a6 ) 13.Nb5 Qe5 , etc. — Watson. It seems to me that 10...f6 must be a critical test of the 10.Qe2 move order.

b) 10...Rc8 11.Nc3 a6 12.Be3? Qxe5 13.f4? Qxe3+! has been suggested but of course this is just a trap and White can, for instance, play 12.Kh1 preparing f2-f4.

11.Nc3

White has nothing better than to offer a regular MBG, but of course by defending his e-pawn he has ruled out the main line where Black grabs two pawns.

Not 11.Kh1 Nc6 ( 11...Qh4!? ) 12.f4 Nb4 13.Bb5 O-O-O! with an edge to Black — Watson.

However 11.Nd2 , preparing Nf3, may also come into consideration.

11...a6

Now we have the main line that can also arise by transposition from main line A3 above, via 8.Nc3 Nxd4 9.Nxd4 Qxd4 10.O-O a6 11.Qe2 Ne7 . Before we look at this, there are two alternatives to consider.

A relatively unknown independent line is 11...Ng6 12.Be3 Qb4 13.a3 Qa5 14.b4 Qd8 15.f4 Be7 16.Bd4 O-O 17.Qe3 f6 Pinal-Noguieras, Capablanca Memorial, Havana 1984.

Instead of 11...a6 , 11...Nc6!? (see the next diagram) is the Schmid line recommended by old theory. This requires a detailed look because if the move is good, then that is an argument against playing the early Qe2.

12.Be3

(Not 12.Nb5 Qxe5! with a big advantage to Black, e.g., 13.Qxe5 Nxe5 14.Nc7+ Kd8 15.Nxa8 Nxd3 — Lothar Schmid).

12...Qxe5 13.f4 Qd6 14.f5

This is the critical try for White in this line. Does White have enough?

(Most books give either nothing on this 11...Nc6 line at all, or just 14.Nb5 Qb8 15.f5 e5 when Black had a big advantage in Sturm-Schmid, Dyckhoff Memorial, corr., 1954-56. That game ended 16.Qh5 a6! 17.f6 g6 18.Bxg6 Be6! 19.Nd4 fxg6 20.f7+ Kd7 21.Nxc6 gxh5 0-1.)

12.Rd1?!

This move can no longer be recommended to White and is seldom seen. Players have given up on it.

12...Nc6

Improving on 12...Qb6 as seen in the Introduction (Messere-Zagorovsky).

Alternatives include 12...Qh4!? and 12...Qa7 , but the main line is so good for Black that he does not need to look any further. Certainly it is enough to put White off.

13.Bxa6

If 13.Bb5 Qb6! (not 13...Qxe5? 14.Bxc6 ) breaks White's attack.

13...Qxe5 14.Qxe5?!

Is there an alternative? 14.Bxb7 Qxe2 15.Nxe2 Rb8 (or 15...Ra7 16.Bxc6 Bxc6 = ECO, citing Baumbach & Thormann) 16.Bxc6 Bxc6 is at least equal for Black.

14...Nxe5 15.Bxb7 Ra7!

This move, improving on the formerly preferred 15...Rb8 , was really what sent White hunting for some other way to play the Advance Variation in general, or the Milner-Barry in particular. In fact 15...Ra7 was played as long ago as Ulvestad-Rothman, USA Ch 1946, and this line was considered equal by GM Schmid in the book of the great Dyckhoff Memorial correspondence tournament.

16.Bxd5 exd5

Keres once preferred White here but in fact Black is probably winning this ending with his extra piece, although he only has kingside pawns. The reason for putting the rook on a 7 instead of b8 is to cover c7 and especially to protect the d7-bishop and the whole second rank. Once Black's development is complete, his bishop pair would ensure he has enough firepower to target White's weaknesses. So White must try to regain the piece without delay — the problem is that it doesn't work!

17.Re1

Jonasson-Flodmark, corr., 1976, went instead 17.Rxd5 f6 18.b3 Bb4 19.Bb2 O-O 20.Ne2 Rfa8 21.a4 Ra5 22.Rad1 Rxd5 23.Rxd5 Be6 24.Rb5 Rd8 25.h3 Rd1+ 26.Kh2 Bd6 27.Bxe5 Bxe5+ 28.g3 Bd5 0-1.

17...f6 18.f4

Obviously it's now or never, because the Black king would escape the pin after ...Kf7 as the next move.

18...Bc5+ 19.Kh1 d4

This is the point, although 19...O-O!? 20.fxe5 fxe5 21.Nxd5 Be6 (with the idea ...Bxd5 or ...Rf2) was tried in Hodgson-Webb, London 1975.

20.Nb1

White is still trying to regain his piece. Others:

a) 20.Nd5? when instead of 20...Bd6?! = (Ulvestad-Rothman) Black has 20...Bc6 winning material after 21.b4 (or 21.fxe5 Bxd5 22.exf6+ Kf7 -+) 21...Bf8! 22.Nb6 Bxb4 23.Nc8 ( 23.Rxe5+ fxe5 ) 23...Rc7 -+. I am unsure who first pointed this out but I remember the trick being shown to me by George Botterill (twice British Champion) back in the early 1970s, explaining that this is why the rook should go to a7 and not b8.

b) 20.Ne4? Nd3 and 20.Nd1 Bb4 also favor Black;

c) The game cited by ECO, Mnatsakanian-Monin, USSR 1979, saw White settling for a piece down and trying to liquidate pawns: 20.fxe5 dxc3 21.exf6+ Kf7 22.fxg7 Kxg7 23.bxc3 Rf8 24.h3 Rf2 25.Be3 Bxe3 26.Rxe3 Bc6 27.Rg3+ Kf8 28.Kg1 Raxa2 29.Rxa2 Rxa2 but despite the greatly reduced material, Black managed to win in 67 moves. He has the correct colored bishop for his h-pawn.

20...O-O 21.fxe5 fxe5

White has won back the piece but is left with grave weaknesses that the Black rooks can exploit.

22.Nd2 Bc6! 23.Rxe5 Rf2!!

This was originally analysis by the Dutch CC master, the late Kor D.Mulder van Leens Dijkstra.

The classic example now is: 24.Rxc5 Re7! 25.Kg1 Rxg2+ 26.Kf1 Rf7+ 27.Ke1 Re7+ 28.Kd1? d3! 29.Rf5 ( 29.Rxc6 Rg1+ 30.Nf1 Re2 ) 29...Bd7 30.Rf4 g5 31.Rf1 Bg4+ 32.Nf3 Ree2 ( 33.Rg1 Bxf3 34.Rxg2 Rxg2+ 35.Ke1 g4 ) 0-1, Meyer-Plesse, W.German Teams Ch, 1978.

In a subsequent postal game, Sarink-Despotovic, White managed to get a draw but it doesn't make the line much more attractive for White: 28.Kf1 Rf7+ 29.Ke1 d3 30.Rxc6 Rg1+ 31.Nf1 Rfxf1+ 32.Kd2 Rd1+ 33.Ke3 d2 34.Bxd2 Rxa1 35.Rc8+ Kf7 36.Ra8 g5 37.a4 Rh1 38.a5 Rxh2 39.Bc3 Ra4 40.a6 Rh3+ 41.Ke2 Rh6 42.Ra7+ Ke6 43.Kf3 Kd5 44.Rd7+ Kc6 45.Rg7 Rxa6 46.Rxg5 Ra7 47.Rf5 Rg6 48.Bd4 Rb7 49.Rh5 Rf7+ 50.Ke3 Rg3+ 51.Ke4 Rg4+ 52.Ke3 Re7+ 1/2-1/2. Black may well be able to improve somewhere.

12.Kh1!

This is definitely the line that still has life in it for White.

It is because of this move that I now favor the 10...Qxe5 lines for Black. As I have said elsewhere, White can deny his opponent the opportunity of heading for those lines by using the 8.O-O , 10.Qe2 move order but at the price that Black may then employ 8...Nb4 . It's a trade-off: what is right against one opponent may not be the best move order against another.

The well-known continuation that is in all the books.

13...Nb4 14.Rd1

Until Peart-Harding, I didn't fear 12.Kh1 because White usually followed up with rubbish like 14.Bb1 Qc4 15.Qf3 d4 16.a3 dxc3 17.axb4 Bc6 18.Qd3 Qxd3 0-1, Denckens-Harding ICCF Cup semifinal, 1977.

14...Nxd3

Black also has another interesting move here that was only discovered in the last few years, namely 14...Bc5!? (see diagram). Black allows a choice of bishop discoveries, but this is only to return the pawn in order to exchange queens and break the attack.

In view of 15.Bb5? Qf2 16.Bxd7+ Kxd7 17.Qg4? Nd3 (Kosten), there are two main lines from the diagram:

a) 15.Bxh7 Qf2 16.Qxf2 Bxf2 17.Bb1 and now:

a1) 17...Ke7 (threatening ...Rxh2+! and ...Rh8 mating) 18.h3 Rac8 19.f5?! ( 19.Bd2 Nikolenko) 19...exf5 20.Rf1 Bh4?! (Blasek-Kishnev, Gelsenkirchen 1991) 20...Bd4! 21.Bg5+ Ke8 22.Bxf5 Be6 Kishnev.

a2) 17...O-O-O! 18.h3 f6! is an improved method for Black, e.g., 19.exf6 gxf6 20.Rf1 Ba7 21.Bd2 Bc6 22.a3 d4 23.Ne4 d3! 24.Bxb4 Bxe4 (threat ...Rxh3 mate) 25.Kh2 f5 26.Ba2 Kd7 27.Rae1 Rh6 28.Bc3 Ke7 29.g4? d2 30.Bb4+ Ke8 31.Rxe4 fxe4 32.g5 Rh5 33.Bb3 Rd3 0-1, Bellet-Apicella, French League, 1995.

b) 15.Bxa6 Qf2 16.Qxf2 Bxf2 17.Be2 ( 17.Bb5!? =) 17...O-O ( 17...Ke7 18.f5! ) 18.Bd2 (threat Ne4) 18...Nc2 ( 18...Rfc8 19.Rdc1 ) 19.Rab1 Bc6 (or 19...Ne3 20.Rdc1 Rfc8 Organdzhiev-Drasko, Skopje 1992) and now:

b1) 20.Bf3 f6 21.exf6 gxf6 22.Ne2 e5 23.fxe5 fxe5 and Black stands well: 24.Rdc1 Nd4 25.Nxd4 Bxd4 26.a3 e4 27.Bg4 Rf2 28.Be6+ Kg7 29.Rd1 e3 30.Bxe3 Bxe3 31.Bxd5 Rd8 32.Bxc6 Rxd1+ 33.Rxd1 bxc6 34.Rb1 Rc2 35.a4 Rc1+ 36.Rxc1 Bxc1 37.b4 Bd2 38.b5 c5 39.b6 c4 40.g3 Be3 0-1, Szczepanski-Voigt, Cologne open, 1993.

b2) 20.a3 Ne3 21.Bxe3 Bxe3 22.f5 d4 23.Nb5 Bxb5 24.Bxb5 exf5 25.Bd7 g6 26.e6 Rfd8! with advantage to Black (0-1, 52) Mololkin-Tsin, Volgodonsk semifinal Russian Ch, 1993.

However, now that 14...Bc5 has lost its surprise value, it is probably time to suggest that 14...Nxd3 really is the right move after all.

c) The main line goes 16.b3 Qc7 17.Bb2 when:

c1) 17...Bc6 ( 17...Rc8 18.f5 Qa5 19.Rad1 Ba3 20.Bxa3 Qxa3 21.f6 was very good for White in Mohring-Kasper, East German Ch 1977) 18.Rc1 ( 18.f5!? ) 18...Rd8 ( 18...Be7 19.f5! Glek-Zlotnik, Moscow 1979 or 18...f6!? — Zlotnik) 19.Qf2 ( 19.f5? d4! 20.Rcd1 exf5 ) 19...Be7 20.Ne2 O-O 21.Nd4 Qd7 22.f5 exf5 23.Rg3! with evident compensation for White: 23...g6 24.Qf4 Rfe8 25.Nxf5 Bf8 26.Bd4 Re6 ( 26...f6!? 27.exf6 Re4 is unclear says Kosten.) 27.Nh6+ Bxh6 28.Qxh6 Rde8 29.Rf1 Qe7?? ( 29...Bb5! was necessary but White has a promising attack.) 30.Rh3 f5 31.exf6 Qf7 32.Qxh7+! 1-0, Sveshnikov-Razuvaev, Belgrade GMA, 1988.

c2) 17...b5!? is often stated to be good for Black, but there are two interesting improvements for the first player:

c21) The move to avoid is the only one in ECO, 18.Rc1? Qb7 19.Qd2 (or 19.f5 Rc8! Smirnov-Beliakov, Alushta 1994; there's a lot more detail in ECO but it's all irrelevant now.) 19...Rc8! 20.Rd1 Be7 21.f5!? ( 21.Ne2 O-O 22.Nd4 f6! R.Byrne & Mednis) 21...b4 22.Nxd5 exd5 23.f6!? Be6! 24.fxe7 Qxe7 25.Rg3 g6 which gave Black the edge in Wallyn-Mednis, Cannes open 1992.

c22) Lane considers 18.f5!? b4 19.fxe6 ( 19.Nxd5!? Chashchihkin-Walther, see Study material) 19...fxe6 20.Nxd5 exd5 21.e6 in great depth on pp59-60 of his Beating The French. Black's best reply is 21...Bc6 but it doesn't equalise according to the analysis by Lane and Ady given in that book. This needs home analysis and tests.

c23) A less dramatic improvement for White was demonstrated in A.Kazoks-T.Lindestrem, 56th European Corr. Championship, 1995-7: 18.Nd1!? Rc8 19.Ne3 Bc5 20.Qg4 g6 21.Qh4 with compensation for White. The game continued:

21...Bc6 22.Ng4 Be7 23.Nf6+ Bxf6 24.exf6! Bb7 25.Rc3 Qa5 26.Rxc8+ Bxc8 27.Qh6 Bb7 28.Qg7 Rf8 29.Bd4 Qb4 30.Rd1 h5 31.h3 h4 32.Kh2 Kd7 33.Be3 Qd6 34.Kh1 d4!? 35.Bxd4 Bd5 36.Bf2 Ke8 37.Rc1 Kd7 38.Rc5! ( 38.Bc5? Qxf4 =) 38...Bb7 39.Rg5 (Threatening Rxg6) 39...Be4 40.Bc5 Qd1+ 41.Kh2 Qc1

Up to now, Black seemed to have “hung in there” quite well but now he slips as White manages to prevent the intended perpetual check.

42.Bd6 e5!? 43.Bxe5 Qc8 44.Qh7 Qc2 45.Qxh4 Qd2 ( 45...Qxa2 46.Qe1 +-) 46.b4 Bf5 ( 46...Qxb4 47.Qf2 +-) 47.Qg3 Rc8 48.Qf3 Rc6 49.a4! bxa4 50.Qa3 Be6 ( 50...Qd1 51.b5 +-) 51.Qxa4 Bc4 52.Rg3 Bb5 53.Qb3 Bc4 54.Qa3 Bb5 55.Qf3 Bc4 56.f5 g5 57.b5 Bxb5 58.Qa3 Rc1 59.Qe7+ Kc8 60.Qxf7 Qe1 61.Qg8+ Kd7 62.Qe6+ Kd8 63.Bc7+ 1-0 ( 63...Kxc7 64.Qxe1 Rxe1 65.f7 Re8 66.fxe8Q Bxe8 67.Rxg5 +-).

Therefore 15...Qb6 is the critical move after all:

Having exchanged the light-squared bishop, Black aims to swap the dark-squared ones also to break any potential White attack. In a normal French, this policy could be very dangerous, leaving Black with the “bad” bishop against the knight, but in this case where the d5-pawn is both extra and passed, that consideration may not apply. Nevertheless, White has recently come up with a new idea to justify his gambit.

16.Be3

There is nothing better:

a) 16.f5 (Roeder-Riefner, Bundesliga, 1980) 16...d4! (or Zlotnik's suggestion 16...Bb5!? ), e.g., 17.fxe6 Bxe6 ( 17...Qxe6!? ) 18.Ne4 Be7 19.Rg3 O-O-O!? ; or 17.Qf2 Qc7 18.Rxd4 (If 18.Qxd4 Bc5 or 18.fxe6 Bxe6 then 19.Ne4 Be7 ) 18...Be7 ( 18...Bc5 19.Rxd7 unclear) 19.Ne4 ( 19.f6 gxf6 20.exf6 Bc5 ) 19...Qxe5 20.Bf4 Qxf5 threatening ...e5 with advantage to Black (Tsin & Pechenkin, New In Chess Yearbook 29).

b) 16.a4 g6 17.f5 gxf5 18.Rxd5 Bc6 19.a5 Qc7 20.Rd3 Rg8 21.Rg3 Rxg3 22.hxg3 O-O-O with clear Black advantage in Dyke-Henley, New York 1989.

16...Bc5

ECO just cites this as a Sveshnikov suggestion, with no continuation. It also gives 16...Qc7 17.Rc1 Bc6 18.Qf2 with compensation for White (Sveshnikov-Vi.lvanov, USSR, 1988).

17.Bxc5 Qxc5 18.Qd2!

This is the latest attempt to salvage the line for White. White will prevent ...d4, transfer his knight to that square, to keep the Black bishop fairly bad. Then he has a launching pad for a later attack with f4-f5! This is a very consistent and logical plan rather than the kind of wild hack you so often see in the Milner-Barry.

Older moves are looking rather sick:

a) 18.Rad1?! Bc6 19.f5 O-O-O! 20.Qg4?! ( 20.fxe6 fxe6 21.Qg4 Qe7 is still a bit in Black's favor.) 20...d4! 21.fxe6 ( 21.Rxd4 Qxe5 ) 21...h5! 22.Qh3 ( 22.Qf4 g5 23.Qf2 fxe6 — Anton) 22...fxe6 23.Qxe6+ Kb8 24.Ne2 Rhe8 25.Qh3 Qxe5 -+ Bohak-Volker-Michael Anton, 10th CC Olympiad Prelims 1986.

b) 18.f5 (the move that gets a “!” in most recent books) when:

b1) 18...d4?! (first played in Messere-Dijkstra, Potter Memorial corr. in the 1970s) no longer looks like the answer, as Messere's subsequent suggestion 19.b4 Qc7 ( 19...Qxb4 20.Ne4! with an attack) 20.Rad1 and if 20...dxc3 21.Rxd7 has been doing rather well. However, Bauld-O'Neale (in the Study Material) with 20...Rd8 is a line where White needs an improvement.

b2) White has never been able to find anything convincing against 18...Bc6 , e.g., 19.Qg4 O-O-O! 20.fxe6 fxe6 21.Rc1?! Kb8 22.Ne2 Qf2 and Black eventually won in Pirrot-Knaak, German Bundesliga 1991. In an earlier game Pirrot got equality with 21.Ne2 and Lane also suggests 21.Na4!? though White is unlikely to find anything better than “chances for both sides”.

b3) Note that the Psakhis book The Complete French has the misprint 18...Qc6 instead of 18...Bc6 . Maybe it's a playable move!

b4) Even 18...O-O-O has been successful for Black in two recent games that you can find in the Study Material.

18...Bc6!

Not 18...O-O? 19.Nxd5! Bb5 20.b4 Qa7 21.Ne7+ Kh8 22.Rh3 b6 23.f5 Qxe7 24.f6 Rfd8 25.Qc2 1-0, Rogovskoi-Kolomoitsev, Volgograd, 1989.

This is the Bohak Attack.

This ends the discussion of the MBG proper. My strong impression is that in a top-level correspondence game, Black has the better chances if he does his research (uses this book!) but over-the-board the practical winning chances are all with White, who can surprise his opponent with many little-known ideas and move-order tricks.

The two side-gambits that end this chapter are also not easy to write off.

9.Nbd2 (Nun Gambit)

This gambit is the invention of Czech master Jiri Nun — not to be confused with either Josef Nun (a world championship finalist at correspondence chess in the 1980s) or English grandmaster John Nunn. The evident point is to bring this knight to f3 after the exchange of its brother. However, I find the line hard to believe and I won't give it much space.

9...Nc6

The simplest answer according to Psakhis in The Complete French who says that if 9...Ne7 10.Nxd4 Qxd4 11.Nf3 Qa4 12.b3 Qa5 13.Bd2 (Nun-Razuvaev, Sochi-A 1989) gives White compensation. That game and many others in the Nun Gambit can be found in the Study material for you to make up your own mind.

Exchanging knights by 9...Nxf3+ (as in Brady-Clarke in the Study Material) clearly falls in with White's plan. Some other moves, e.g., 9...h6 are also available for study in the back of the book.

After the Black knight retreats, the one on d2 looks silly.

10.Nb3 Nge7

Instead 10...Qc7 11.Bf4 f5 was tried unsuccessfully in Pribik-Krecmer, Czechoslovakian Corr. Ch, 1989 (see Study Material).

11.Be3

Nun tried a different move, 11 Bf4, in his 1991 game with Bashkov (see the Study Material) but then returned to the text in a later game.

11...Qc7 12.Rc1 Ng6

Nun-Schmittdiel, Prague 1990. Black is “not far off picking up another pawn” says Psakhis. 9.Ng5 (Sørensen Gambit)

In my 1979 Batsford book French MacCutcheon and Advance Lines (in which White's 9th was published for the first time) I credited White's 9th to Irish master Eamonn Keogh. Although Eamonn discovered this ingenious gambit independently, its true progenitor is Bent Sørensen, a Dane resident in Malmo, Sweden, who first played it as early as 1963. I learned this from Swedish master Kjell Krantz and I wrote an article about it in British Chess Magazine (May 1992) which I now bring up to date.

The main lines are:

B31: 9...g6

B32: 9...Nc6

Black has a wide choice of other moves, too. However, most of these possibilities are either outright bad or at least allow White to regain his pawn without risk.

a) 9...h6?! 10.Qh5 ( 10.Nxf7!? Kxf7 11.Qh5+ Ke7 12.Nc3 comes into consideration.) 10...O-O-O 11.Nxf7 Be8 12.Nc3! ( 12.Bg6? Bxf7 13.Bxf7 Nc2 ) and if 12...Rd7 13.Bg6 threatening 14.Be3 (Krantz).

b) 9...f6? 10.Qh5+ g6 11.Bxg6+ (Krantz).

c) 9...Ne7?! 10.Nxh7 .

The latest idea for White.

9...Nc6

The principal objection to the Sørensen Gambit appears to be this move, which has the advantage of putting pressure on e5. It means that the Krantz/Sørensen idea of playing Be3 with the knight still “home” on b1 will not work.

10.Re1

This was my recommendation in the 1992 article but I doubt if White has a good continuation from the diagram position.

If White regains his pawn he faces strong counterplay: 10.Bxh7 Nh6 followed by ...Be7, with play against e5 and h2, or 10.Nxh7 Be7 threatening ...Nxe5. And if 10.Qh5 Nh6 or 10.Qf3 Nxe5 11.Qf4 Bd6 White has nothing. Sørensen has analysed 10.Nc3!? with the idea 10...Nxe5 11.Nxd5! exd5 12.Re1 , but after 12...f6 White play looks unsound to me. The only hope now is 13.Be3 which needs more analysis. Even 12...Bd6!? 13.Be3 is not clearly good for White.

Therefore White must protect the e-pawn for the time being and Sørensen himself played 10.Qe2 when maybe 10...Be7 (threatening 11...Nxe5!? 12.Qxe5 f6 ) is good. Another possibility is 10...Nb4! (Krantz, to meet 11.Bxh7 by 11...Bb5 ) while 10...Nd4 11.Qd1 is a repetition.

Instead Sørensen-Divjak, Malmo 1978, went 10...g6?! 11.Be3 Qc7 12.f4 h6 13.Nf3 Nge7 14.Nc3 Nf5 15.Bf2 Qa5 16.g4 and White got compensation for his pawn and won a messy game: 16...Ng7 17.Kh1 h5 18.g5 Nf5 19.a3 Be7 20.b4 Qd8 21.Bxf5 gxf5 22.Rad1 Nxb4? 23.axb4 Bxb4 24.Nxd5!! exd5 25.e6! Bc6 ( 25...Bxe6?? or 25...fxe6?? 26.Qb2 ) 26.exf7+ ( 26.Qb2 d4 ) 26...Kxf7 27.Ne5+ Kg8 28.Nxc6 bxc6 29.Qe6+ Kf8 30.Qxf5+ Kg8 31.Qg6+ Kf8 32.Bd4! +- 32...Rg8 33.Qf5+ Ke8 34.Rfe1+ Be7 35.Bf6 Rf8 36.Qg6+ 1-0.

10...Bc5

Krantz suggested this without further analysis.

Also 10...g6 looks solid if White just develops, so again 11.Qf3 Nh6 12.Qf6 with interesting complications.

The only game I could find with 10.Re1 back in 1992 when I wrote the article was Hatzl-Huss, San Bernardino open 1984, when Black tried 10...Be7 and won after 11.Nc3 Bxg5 12.Bxg5 Nge7 13.Qh5 a6 14.Rac1 Rc8 15.Na4 Qb4 16.Nc5 Nxe5 17.Nxe6 Rxc1 18.Nxg7+ Kf8 19.Rxc1 Nxd3 20.Qh6 Nxc1 21.h3 Ne2+ 22.Kf1 Bb5 23.Bd2 Nf4+ 0-1. So White needs an improvement after this and it would be a good exercise to see whether 11.Nxh7 is playable.
