Chapter Five. Classical Variation: 4 Qc2 0-0

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Qc2

4.Qc2 , the Classical Variation, is currently the most popular response to the Nimzo-Indian at all levels of chess, just ahead of 4.e3 . In the first two chapters of this book we saw the amount of counterplay Black can generate after inflicting White with doubled c-pawns, so it's hardly surprising that many players choose to eliminate this possibility straightaway. With 4.Qc2 White wants to have his cake and to eat it. He aims to secure the advantage of the bishop pair, by playing a2-a3, without the cost of pawn weaknesses.

QUESTION: That sounds like a good deal for White. Are there any drawbacks?

ANSWER: There is a cost that White has to accept: time. Moving the queen, followed by the a-pawn and then the queen again is certainly not the quickest way to further White's development! So Black, at least temporarily, enjoys a lead in development - an advantage to counterbalance White's of the bishop pair. What Black does to try and utilize this lead is crucial, as we'll see in this chapter.

QUESTION: What should Black play against 4.Qc2 ?

ANSWER: There are quite a number of reasonable lines for Black, and in this book I'm covering two quite distinct options:

The first is 4...O-O 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.Qxc3 d5 , which we'll study in this chapter.

The second is a completely different approach, with 4...c5 , which is covered in the next chapter.

QUESTION: What about other ways to protect the knight, such as 4.Qb3 or 4.Bd2 ?

ANSWER: Both of these moves are playable but for specific reasons they are considered relatively harmless in comparison to 4.Qc2 . They will be considered in the next chapter too.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Qc2 O-O 5.a3

4...O-O is Black's most popular response to 4.Qc2 (the other main choices are 4...c5 and 4...d5 ). Likewise 5.a3 is the consistent follow-up to 4.Qc2 . White does have other options, though, and 5.e4 will be considered in Bhat-Kaidanov and Cardon-Gustafsson, while 5.Nf3 and 5.Bg5 are covered in the next chapter.

5...Bxc3+

Black doesn't have a choice here. 5...Be7? gives White a completely free hand in the centre with 6.e4 , and there are no points for guessing what's wrong with 5...Ba5!

6.Qxc3 d5

Over the last few years this move has catapulted itself from virtually nowhere to being arguably the main line of the Classical Variation, at least at the highest level - and the trickle down effect will be inevitable.

QUESTION: What's the idea of 6...d5 ?

ANSWER: 6...d5 , probably more than any other move in this position, captures the essence of Black's philosophy in this line - to exploit his lead in development. White has just secured the bishop pair but has spent some time doing so. Ideally White would now like a period of slow play, so that he could catch up with development, consolidate and look forward to trying to exploit his small bishop-pair advantage in the long term, especially when the position opens up.

Black, in no uncertain terms, is determined to avoid this scenario and wants to give White as hard a time as possible over the next few moves while he is trying to complete his development. In this way 6...d5 differs somewhat from the traditional main line with 6...b6 (and the related 6...d6 ) where Black employs a system type of development. He still tries to exploit his development lead, but not to the same extent.

In fact 6...d5 has more in common with the sharp 4...d5 lines, and indeed there are some transpositions.

In the next few games we will study White's four main options after 6...d5 : 7.cxd5 , 7.Nf3 , 7.Bg5 and 7.e3 .

Carlsen decides to clarify the position in the centre before choosing how to develop.

QUESTION: Which way do you think Kramnik recaptured?

ANSWER:

7...Ne4!

Apologies for the trick question! Kramnik wants to recapture with the e-pawn and free up his light-squared bishop, but first of all he throws in this clever zwischenzug attack.

White's idea, quite naturally, was to follow up with 7...exd5 8.Bg5 , but 7...Ne4 prevents this while activating the knight. We can see already that Black is trying to utilize any chance to exploit his development lead and gain an early initiative. Black's clever knight move was an idea by Vishy Anand, and was possibly a crucial factor in the viability of the whole 6...d5 line. 7...Nxd5 would also gain time on the white queen, but after say 8.Qc2 Nc6 9.Nf3 Black has less chance of utilizing his development lead with the bishop stuck behind a pawn.

9.Bf4

Carlsen develops a piece and tries to gain a bit of breathing space for further development by attacking the c7-pawn.

ANSWER: On 9.Nf3 Black can continue to develop his initiative with 9...Bf5! gaining more time because of a threatened discovered attack on the queen. If then 10.Qb3 Black ignores the attack on the b7-pawn and continues to develop: 10...Nc6! . Black is intending ...Na5 and he would be very happy to seize the initiative further if White grabs on b7, with 11.Qxb7? Na5 . In fact, White is already in some trouble here and 12.Qb5 c5! 13.dxc5 Rb8 14.Qd3 Nb3 15.Rb1 Qa5+ would be a nice way to punish White's queen for her gluttony.

In the game where Anand introduced his novelty with 7...Ne4! , against Kramnik, White played 9.e3 which at least has the merit of being able to answer 9...Bf5 with 10.Bd3! . Even so, after Anand's energetic response with 10...c5 ( playing to swap off White's good bishop with 10...Bg6 11.Ne2 Nd6 is also a good idea ) 11.dxc5 Nd7! 12.Ne2 Ndxc5 13.Bxe4 Nxe4 14.Nd4 Bg6 15.Qd1 Black was fine, V Kramnik-V.Anand, Nice (rapid) 2009.

9...Nc6!

QUESTION: Why the exclamation mark? Isn't 9...c6 a more natural way to safeguard the queenside pawns?

ANSWER: I realize that developing a knight in front of a c-pawn in queen's pawn openings is often seen as a sin, but here it is fully justified. Kramnik is unwaveringly sticking to the plan he began with 6...d5 – to develop as quickly as possible in order to put White under pressure.

9...c6 is the more "positionally solid" move and it's by no means bad. However, because of its lack of threat White can develop more smoothly, and 10.e3 Bf5 11.Bd3 followed by Ne2 or Nf3 will offer White some chances of obtaining a small advantage in a quiet position.

Black can even go further than 9...Nc6 and offer a gambit with 9...Bf5!? , hoping to exploit his significant development advantage and the open c-file in return. For example, 10.Qxc7 Qxc7 11.Bxc7 Rc8 12.Bf4! ( this looks better than 12.Bxb8?! Raxb8 13.f3 Nd6 with ...Rc2 to follow ) 12...Nc6 13.Rd1 ( 13.Nf3!? ) 13...Na5 14.g4 Bd7 15.f3 Nf6 with reasonable compensation for Black, T.Wiley-C.Balogh, Arinsal 2009.

10.e3

The right move, preparing to answer ...Bf5 with Bd3. 10.Nf3 Bf5! would force White to move his queen again and fall further behind in development.

10...Re8

Another active move, cranking up the pressure. 10...Re8 sets up tactical ideas based on the e-file and ...Nxd4.

EXERCISE: Find a way for Black to keep the initiative.

ANSWER:

14...c5!

Kramnik meets the threat to his c-pawn as aggressively as possible. 14...c5 puts pressure on the centre and introduces ideas such as ...Qa5+, opening the c-file and ...c4 (if White's bishop goes to d3, which it likely will).

In contrast 14...c6? is a mistake, mixing plans. Black has chosen initiative over positional play and it's too late to get cold feet! After 15.Bd3 Bf5 White can play 16.h3! opening up the kingside to his advantage. This is a good example of how the initiative can quickly change hands in sharp positions if one side doesn't play energetically enough.

Finally, 14...Bf5!? 15.Bd3 c5! transposes to the game but this move order would allow White the possibility – good or bad – of taking on c7.

Moscow, 2011

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Qc2 O-O

5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.Qxc3 d5 7.Bg5

This is a very natural move. White pins the knight before exchanging in the centre and so eliminates the possibility of a quick ...Ne4.

7...c5!?

EXERCISE: Before moving on, try to work out Black's best responses to 8.dxc5 and 8.cxd5 .

QUESTION: 7...c5 looks incredibly sharp. Is Black employing an initiative-at-all-costs approach, just as in the previous game?

ANSWER: Yes, that's right. Zakharov is opening up the position to try and exploit his development advantage and he isn't afraid to sacrifice a pawn to do so.

In contrast, if Black settles for solid development, with say 7...Nbd7 8.Nf3 c6 9.e3 , White will consolidate and probably enjoy a small but stable advantage.

QUESTION: Why an advantage?

ANSWER: The position is very much like a standard QGD, except that White has swapped off Black's dark-squared bishop for a knight, and in a slow position Black's slight lead in development doesn't quite counterbalance this positional advantage.

QUESTION: Does Black have another active option, but one that is slightly safer, if he doesn't fancy the complications of 7...c5 ?

ANSWER: "Active" and "safe" sounds like a good mix! Yes, he does. 7...dxc4 8.Qxc4 b6 is safer but also quite thematic. 9.Nf3 transposes to the next three games (Games 22-24), and White can also play 9.Rd1 – see the notes to our second Carlsen-Kramnik encounter (Game 24).

8.dxc5

Against 8.cxd5 Black has a tactic which gains a crucial tempo on the white queen: 8...cxd4 9.Qxd4 and now 9...Nc6! .

This type of tactic is seen in more than one opening and is well worth remembering. Black is pretty active here. For example:

(a) If 10.Bxf6?! then 10...Nxd4 11.Bxd8 Rxd8 is okay, but even better is 11...Nc2+! 12.Kd2 Nxa1 13.Be7 Re8 14.d6 Bd7 followed by ...Rac8, which looks good for Black.

(b) After 10.Qd2 exd5 Black's lead in development is a major factor. 11.Nf3 h6 12.Bxf6 Qxf6 13.e3 d4!? 14.Nxd4 Rd8 15.Be2 Nxd4 16.exd4 Rxd4 17.Qc3 Bd7 18.O-O Rc8 left Black with an edge in L.Gonda-A.Horvath, Balatonlelle 2009.

(c) 10.Qh4! is wiser, because at least White puts pressure on the pinned knight:

(c1) 10...exd5 11.Bxf6 gxf6 12.e3 d4 13.Bd3 f5 14.Qxd8 Rxd8 15.exd4 Nxd4 16.Ne2 Nf3+! 17.gxf3 Rxd3 18.Rd1 Rxd1+ 19.Kxd1 Be6 with a level ending, F.Lopez Colon-J.Hernandez Carmenates, Gran Canaria 2010.

(c2) 10...Qa5+!? 11.b4 Qxd5 is an interesting alternative. Black sacrifices a pawn and allows White's queen into f6, but counts on a development lead and White's airy queenside to provide compensation. After 12.Bxf6 gxf6 13.Qxf6 Rd8 14.Nf3 e5 Black had good play for the pawn in N.Dzagnidze-N.Pogonina, Khanty-Mansiysk 2010, and "good" turned to "overwhelming" after 15.e3? ( White had to play 15.Rc1! or 15.Qg5+ followed by Rc1 ) 15...Qb3 16.Qg5+ Kf8 17.Qh6+ Ke8! 18.Nd2? Rxd2! .

8...d4!

This is the main idea behind 7...c5 .

QUESTION: What is Black's compensation for the pawn?

ANSWER: With 8...d4 Black creates a strong pawn in the centre which can be backed up by ...e5. This offers Black a space advantage and creates a slight cramping effect on White's position. Throw in a lead in development and the possibility of picking up White's extra pawn on c5, and Black's compensation for the pawn adds up to a significant amount.

9.Qg3

It's worth noting that 4...O-O 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.Qxc3 d5 is closely related to another main line, 4...d5 , and indeed there some transpositions between them. The position reached here could also be reached via 4...d5 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.Qxc3 c5!? (the Romanishin Gambit) 7.dxc5 d4 8.Qg3 O-O 9.Bg5 , although with that move order White would more likely play 9.Bh6 .

The main alternative after 8...d4 is 9.Qc2 , when Black usually plays 9...e5 .

EXERCISE: Try to devise an effective plan for Black if White fixes the centre with 10.e4 .

ANSWER: 10.e4 blocks the centre and allows White to develop his kingside fairly easily with Bd3 and Nf3 or Ne2. On the other hand, it prevents White from challenging and trying to weaken Black's centre by e2-e3 and gives Black a protected centre passed pawn.

With the position blocked Black should focus on trying to regain the c5-pawn, and here the best way to do so is with 10...a5! preventing b2-b4 and intending to follow up with ...N(b8)-d7xc5 or ...Na6xc5. If White were to allow this, his position would soon become quite miserable; for example, 11.Nf3 Nbd7 12.Bd3 Re8 13.O-O a4! (to prevent b2-b4) 14.Rfe1 Nxc5 and Black is dominating. In the game A.Dreev-R.Wojtaszek, Budva 2009, White recognized this and counterattacked with 11.f4!? but the complications after 11...h6 12.Bh4 exf4 13.O-O-O Nc6 14.Nf3 Re8 15.e5 Nxe5 16.Rxd4 Qe7 favoured Black.

In view of this, it seems wiser for White to challenge Black's centre, as he did in A.Morozevich-V.Anand Nice (rapid) 2009: 10.Nf3 Re8 ( 10...Nc6!? ) 11.e3! .

There's a crucial difference here. In contrast to the above note with 10.e4 , 11...a5 now becomes a luxury Black cannot afford, since after 12.O-O-O! Black will struggle to both keep his d4-pawn and win the c5-pawn. Instead Anand chose 11...h6 and after 12.Bxf6 Qxf6 13.Bd3 Nd7 14.O-O ( 14.b4? loses to 14...e4! 15.Bxe4 d3 ) 14...Nxc5 15.exd4 exd4 16.b4 Nxd3 17.Qxd3 Rd8 the position was equal. I feel that White would have a better chance of getting an edge by heading for the more complicated 12.Bh4! , e.g. 12...Nbd7 13.O-O-O! dxe3 14.fxe3 Qa5 , but Black does regain his pawn.

Could Turov's capture on h5 be a mistake? It's difficult to believe, but just look at what happens next!

14...g6!!

Full marks for imagination if you chose this amazing move – it's almost the very last move one might consider in such a position.

16...Bxe4 17.Rd2 f6!

EXERCISE: Try to work out what Black's threat is in this position.

Zakharov's calm 17...f6! prevents White's big threat of Qf6 ( 17...Nb3+? 18.Kd1 Nxd2 19.Qf6! ) and shows the depth of his idea with 14...g6 . Material-wise, Black has only a pawn for the piece, but:

10.Qa4

EXERCISE: Try to assess the line 10...Qd7 11.Qc2 Qc6 . Is there a significant difference between this and the similar line with 7.Bg5 above?

Carlsen's choice here is the main alternative to 10.Qc2 . It could be argued that 10.Qa4 is more challenging, since with the bishop on a6 attacked Black no longer has the solid option of an immediate ...Nbd7 followed by ...c5 we saw in the notes to the previous game.

ANSWER: The seemingly small difference of Nf3 instead of Rd1 actually makes all the difference here, making the idea of 10...Qd7 11.Qc2 Qc6?! much less appealing. After 12.Qxc6! Nxc6 White exploits the fact that he hasn't "wasted" a tempo on Rd1 and plays 13.Rc1! when Black's position is not much fun.

There are two things to note about this:

1. It often only takes a very slight change in the position for an otherwise effective plan to become useless, or vice-versa.

2. Comparing similar positions is very useful when studying openings. However, in the heat of battle in over-the-board play, it's sometimes a confusing process and shouldn't be used as a substitute for hard analysis. For example, it shouldn't take long to confirm 13.Rc1 as a good move, with or without the prior knowledge of the 7.Bg5 line.

With 7.a3 White is able – temporarily at least – to maintain his pawn centre. The cost is having do make another non-developing move. 7.a3 leads to extremely sharp lines, with both sides needing to prepare and calculate well.

In this position we can use our knowledge from the previous exercise to help us with Black's best move: 14...f5! .

I first became aware of this strong move when Chris Ward showed it to me (he and Timothy Woodward had found it while analysing this variation). After that there was a high-profile encounter with 14...f5 and the theory was supplemented by some published analysis by Kasparov and Leko. They concluded that best play led to a draw, and nothing since has altered this assessment. Here's a summary of what Black really needs to know:

(a) 15.fxe4? fxe4 16.Bb5 Bg4+ 17.Ke3 Qd8! (threatening ...Qg5 mate) 18.h4 Qb6! (now it's ...Qh6) 19.e6 Qd6! (and now, with the h-pawn moved, it's ...Qg3!) 20.Ne2 d4+ and here 21.Kd2 d3 is terminal, while 21.Nxd4 allows mate in one with 21...Qf4# .

(b) 15.Be3 Nc6! 16.Nxc6 bxc6 17.Rhc1 Rb8 18.Kf1 ( 18.Kd1 Rd8 was good for Black in F.Vallejo Pons-P.Leko, Morelia/Linares 2006 ) 18...f4! 19.Bxe4 fxe3 20.Bxh7+ Kh8 21.Bd3 with an edge for Black according to Leko.

(c) 15.e6! is best, preventing ...Bd7 and introducing tactics involving e6-e7: 15...Nc6! 16.Nxc6 bxc6 17.e7 Re8 18.Qxc6

Key Notes

1. A principle Black should remember in the 4...0-0 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.Qxc3 d5 line is "initiative at all costs". This attitude forms the basis of Black's choices in many of the games of this chapter.

2. After 4...0-0 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.Qxc3 d5 White has the two bishops, Black a lead in development. In an open position, a lead in development often trumps the advantage of the two bishops. For this reason Black aims to open up the position as quickly as possible, for example after 7.Nf3 dxc4!, 7.Bg5 c5! or 7.Bg5 dxc4! (see the first four games of the chapter).

3. Black should definitely be prepared to offer pawn sacrifices in return for activity in some lines (see Turov-Zakharov, Eames-Buckley and Zhu Chen-Pelletier).

4. White can avoid complications by playing 7.e3 but only at a cost of blocking in his dark-squared bishop. In this case Black should just accept this concession by White rather than trying to blow 7.e3 off the board (see Bareev-Ponomariov).

5. The 5.e4 line (Bhat-Kaidanov and Cardon-Gustafsson) is extremely sharp and has become quite theoretical in the past 10 years. There are no shortcuts for either player here - you need to do your homework to play this line successfully.
