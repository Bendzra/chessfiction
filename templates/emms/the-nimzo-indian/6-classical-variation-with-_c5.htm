Chapter Six. Classical Variation: 4 Qc2 with ...c5

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Qc2 c5

In this chapter we study some ...c5 lines: 4...c5 , which is another main option against 4.Qc2 ; and also 5...c5 in reply to 4.Qc2 O-O 5.Nf3 or 4.Qc2 O-O 5.Bg5 . We also take a look at two other ways White can avoid the doubled pawns, 4.Qb3 and 4.Bd2 .

In at least one way 4...c5 could be seen as the most logical answer to 4.Qc2 . White's previous move left the d-pawn undefended, so Black attacks it! Black also exploits the fact that d4-d5 is no longer available as a reply to ...c5.

The lines covered in this chapter are less forcing and, I feel, more system-like than those in the previous chapter. 4...c5 offers Black players something a bit different to 4...O-O (for a start, Black gets to keep his bishop!) and might appeal to more positional players, although there's no reason why both lines couldn't be played together, depending on mood, opponent, tournament situation etc.

QUESTION: Should White a) keep a pawn on d4 by playing 5.e3 , b) attack the bishop with 5.a3 , or c) exchange pawns on c5?

5.dxc5!

ANSWER: 5.e3 isn't a bad move but it does block in the c1-bishop. True, White does this straightaway if he plays 4.e3 , but in that case White wouldn't be rushing to commit his queen to c2. Basically, with 5.e3 White would be mixing systems.

5.a3 to force the exchange of Black's bishop is of course White's consistent follow-up to 4.Qc2 . But after 5...Bxc3+ 6.Qxc3 Black can utilize the ...c5 advance by gaining crucial time with 6...cxd4! 7.Qxd4 Nc6! .

5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 is also mixing systems - 4.Qc2 and the Saemisch. On the other hand, the queen on c2 does at least have the merit of helping White achieve e2-e4. Black has a few good options here, but for simplicity's sake I'll suggest 6...O-O 7.e4 d5! 8.e5 Ne4 transposing to Cardon-Gustafsson (Game 27) in the previous chapter.

The attraction of 5.dxc5! is that Black's most obvious recapture forces the bishop to move again, and not necessarily to a better square. If Black recaptures with the bishop, White's basic plan is to develop with Nf3, Bg5 (or Bf4), e2-e3 (or possibly e2-e4), Be2 (or Bd3) and then perhaps 0-0 and Rd1 or sometimes even 0-0-0. In that case White's position could be easy to play: he gets more space and might gain pressure down the half-open d-file.

Black, however, can prevent this plan, as we'll see in this chapter.

5...Bxc5

The immediate recapture of the c5-pawn is the move I've chosen to focus on here, though I briefly touch on the related move 5...O-O and its transpositional possibilities in Braun-Papp. Black's other main option is the combative 5...Na6!? , when the idea is to recapture on c5 with the knight rather than the bishop. This leads to sharp play, especially in the piece sacrifice line 6.a3 Bxc3+ 7.Qxc3 Nxc5 8.b4 (or 8.f3 ) 8...Nce4 9.Qd4 (threatening f2-f3 winning the knight; or 9.Qb2 ) 9...d5 10.c5 b6! 11.f3 bxc5 12.bxc5 Qa5+ 13.Qb4 Qc7 .

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Qc2 c5 5.dxc5 Bxc5 6.Nf3

As mentioned above, White's fairly straightforward plan of action is Bg5 (or possibly Bf4), e2-e3, Be2 (or Bd3), Rd1 and 0-0, or even 0-0-0.

6...Qb6!?

QUESTION: Given that the threat to f2 is pretty easy for White to deal with, why would Black want to play this move? Is the queen well placed on b6?

ANSWER: Black's queen is actually misplaced on b6, for two reasons:

1. The queen and bishop are now vulnerable to a possible knight fork on a4.

2. Black probably wants to fianchetto his queen's bishop but currently the queen is blocking the b-pawn.

However, the queen has no intention of staying on b6. She will soon retreat to the far more natural c7-square, after which Black will adopt a system-like "Hedgehog" development. The clever idea behind 6...Qb6 is to force White to play e2-e3 shutting in his queen's bishop. Black reckons it's worth giving White a "free" move to achieve this aim. This is why 6...Qb6 , while certainly not the only move for Black, has been a popular choice among experts who play 5...Bxc5 .

EXERCISE: Work out what is wrong with 6.Bg5 .

ANSWER: 6...Bxf2+! 7.Kxf2 Ng4+! is a standard trick which has caught out quite a few players.

7.e3

QUESTION: Is it too late for Black to play classically, with 7...d5 ?

ANSWER: Probably it is, even though 7...d5 certainly isn't disastrous for Black. On the surface Black's position actually looks okay, even quite active, but the unfortunate positioning of his queen and bishop allows White to gain vital time. For example, 8.a3!? dxc4 ( 8...Nc6?? loses the bishop to 9.Na4 ) 9.Na4! Qc7 10.Nxc5 Qxc5 11.Bxc4 and White will continue with b2-b4, Bb2 etc, with the bishop pair and a pleasant edge. This edge immediately became something much more substantial in the game G.Kaganskiy-G.Liss, Israeli League 2002, after 11...b5?? 12.Bxb5+! .

7...Qc7

Black sticks to the original plan. The queen has done her job on b6 and now Black prepares ...b6 and ...Bb7.

8.b3 b6 9.Bb2

9...a6

QUESTION: Does Black really need to play this move? Why not 9...Bb7 here, and perhaps ...a6 a bit later if it's really required?

ANSWER: This question arises in many related positions, and also in other openings (for example the Open Sicilian which has a similar pawn structure). Normally it's wise for Black to play ...a6 at some moment, but there are some exceptions. In this particular instance, here is probably the right time.

There's certainly no outright refutation of 9...Bb7 , and indeed it has been played many times. But continuing to allow Nb5 does invite danger. In a slightly later game, Carlsen himself faced 9...Bb7 . He took up the challenge with 10.Nb5! , and after 10...Qd8 ( 10...Qc6 might be better ) 11.O-O-O! (so that ...a6 can be met by Nd6) 11...O-O 12.Ng5! Black was already facing a fierce attack in M.Carlsen-N.Ibraev, Calvia Olympiad 2004.

QUESTION: Returning to the game and the position after 9...a6 , should White play 10.Be2 or 10.Bd3 , or are both okay?

10.Be2

ANSWER: Both moves are fine, and we'll cover Bd3 ideas in Game 30.

10.Be2 is seemingly less active, but it does have the advantage of keeping the d-file unblocked to help White create pressure with Rd1. And maybe there's another idea...

10...Bb7

EXERCISE: Take a good look at this position and list White's options here. Can you guess what Carlsen played? (Hint: it might not be the first move that comes to mind.)

White could castle on either side here, and there is also the useful move 11.Rd1 waiting to see what Black does. But Carlsen chooses something completely different:

ANSWER:

11.g4!?

Did you see that coming? Full marks for creativity if so! This move could easily shock, if nothing else because it seems so out of context with White's preceding play which, perhaps unkindly, could even be described as pedestrian.

This g2-g4 lunge is very much a modern phenomenon. Twenty or so years ago, no-one played like this, at least not in "slow" openings. But then came Shabalov's high-profile discovery of 7.g4 in the Semi-Slav ( 1.d4 d5 2.c4 c6 3.Nf3 Nf6 4.Nc3 e6 5.e3 Nbd7 6.Qc2 Bd6 7.g4!? ), and since then a few similar g-pawn advances have cropped up. Twenty years ago, who would have thought we would be seeing the likes of 1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Nf3 Be7 5.Bg5 O-O 6.e3 h6 7.Bh4 b6 8.g4!? , played by Peter Heine Nielsen; or 1.e4 d6 2.d4 Nf6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g4!? , a typically creative idea from Alexey Shirov. Even in this line of the Nimzo-Indian there are further instances of g2-g4. For example, it can be played as early as move eight (instead of 8.b3 ).

White's idea is fairly straightforward: he wants to attack on the kingside, starting by pushing the knight away from its ideal defensive post by playing g5. And of course White's king is ready to castle queenside. Note also that, for obvious reasons, White's bishop needs to be on e2 rather than d3!

QUESTION: What happens if White chooses a solid approach instead, say with 11.O-O ?

ANSWER: 11.O-O (or 11.Rd1 ) should be met by 11...Be7! . A third move with this bishop might seem like a luxury, but Black's most harmonious development here is with ...d6 and ...Nd7 setting up a typical Hedgehog formation. Leaving the bishop on c5 would make it vulnerable to an exchange with Na4 or, even worse, being trapped via a3 and b4.

EXERCISE: After 12.Rad1 Black has a decision to make: should he choose 12...O-O or 12...d6 ?

ANSWER: The correct choice is 12...d6! . The position does look quite innocuous, especially when compared to 11.g4 , but watch out! Sergei Tiviakov is a true expert in this line, but even he was undone after playing the inaccurate 12...O-O? allowing 13.Ng5! when suddenly he was facing nasty threats against h7. Following 13...Rd8 ( if 13...h6 White plays the crushing 14.Nd5! ) 14.Nd5!! exd5 15.Bxf6 Bxf6 16.Qxh7+ Kf8 17.cxd5 d6 18.Bh5! Bxg5 19.Qh8+ Ke7 20.Qxg7 Rf8 21.Qxg5+ he was unable to hold off White's fierce attack in P.Kiriakov-S.Tiviakov, Port Erin 1999.

This is why Black should be quick to play ...d6 and ...Nbd7, so that the f6-knight is supported by its partner, and not be in such a hurry to castle. Let's take a look at a couple of possibilities after 12...d6! :

(a) 13.Ng5!? Nbd7! ( not falling for 13...O-O 14.Nd5! ) 14.f4!? h6 15.Nge4 O-O 16.f5 was an aggressive reaction by White in J.Rowson-E.Moldobaev, Calvia Olympiad 2004, but here Black gained good counterplay with 16...d5! 17.fxe6 fxe6 18.Ng3 Bd6 .

(b) 13.Rd2 is a more likely response, aiming for a slower build-up: 13...Nbd7 14.Rfd1 Rc8 ( after 14...O-O 15.Ng5 Rfd8 the Nd5 tricks no longer work and Black's position is resilient, but delaying castling doesn't give White such an obvious plan ) 15.Qb1 ( 15.Ng5 d5 16.cxd5 Nxd5 17.Nge4 N7f6 was level in K.Thorsteins-M.Gurevich, Akureyri 1988 ) 15...h6 (preventing Ng5 for good) 16.Nd4 O-O 17.f4 Nc5 18.Bf3 with a typically tense Hedgehog-type position.

I.Campbell-A.Nokso Koivisto, correspondence 1990, continued 18...Rfd8 ( another idea is 18...Rfe8 intending 19.e4 Bf8 20.g4 e5! with counterplay ) 19.e4 Qb8 ( I would be tempted by 19...Bf8 , to answer 20.g4 with 20...d5! 21.e5 Nfe4 ) 20.g4 Qa8 21.Re1 Here Black played the ambitious 21...g5?! which is a good way to fight for the dark squares in similar positions, but in this instance 22.Nd5! exd5 23.exd5 would have been a promising piece sacrifice. Instead another typical Hedgehog break, 21...b5!? , looks like a good way to justify Black's earlier play, and he enjoys some compensation after 22.cxb5 axb5 23.Ndxb5 Qa5 .

After that discussion on quieter lines, let's return to the game and to the position after Carlsen's aggressive and direct 11.g4 :

QUESTION: Should Black accept the pawn offer?

11...Nxg4?!

ANSWER: A general question on whether Black should take on g4 is not an easy one to answer. It sounds like a cop-out, I know, but it all depends on the specifics of the position. Sometimes he should; sometimes not; and sometimes both options are of equal value.

In this Nimzo line, though, it seems that grabbing on g4 is probably a bit too bold, especially here when Black has a better and more practical solution in 11...h6! . This move is covered in the next game, but first of all let's see how Carlsen deals with the pawn capture.

12.Rg1

The game has shifted a gear, and White's rook has suddenly become a very interested party!

12...Nxh2

Black goes "all in". Alternatives suggest that this is probably the right decision:

(a) 12...Nf6 13.Rxg7 is not a good justification for 11...Nxg4 . Material equality is re-established and all Black has achieved is to activate White's rook.

(b) 12...Ne5 13.Nxe5 Qxe5 14.O-O-O with two obvious ideas in Nd5 and Na4.

(c) 12...f5 13.h3 Nh2!? 14.Nxh2 ( Rybka prefers 14.Nd5!? ) 14...Qxh2 15.Rxg7 also looks promising for White.

13.Ng5!

With 13.Ng5! Carlsen leaves the h2-knight out on a limb and also potentially vulnerable. Shaposhnikov is never really able to solve this problem successfully.

13...Nc6

14.O-O-O Ne5?

Black's position was already difficult; it's just got a whole lot worse.

14...f5 would prevent White's next move and give Black the option of ...Ng4 if necessary, even though 15.Na4 still looks nice for White. 14...d5 is also better than the text.

15.Nce4!

The knight on h2 is cut off from the rest of its army and Black is in serious danger of losing material.

17...Nef3

After 17...f6 18.Ne4 Nef3 19.Rxg7 the black knights on f3 and h2 look rather comical!

Now it's White who is a pawn ahead, and Black still has major problems safeguarding his pieces. White is winning.

White doesn't have to wait until move 11 to reveal his aggressive intentions, and the g2-g4 lunge has been seen on numerous moves. In most situations, the best practical decision for Black is probably to decline the pawn.

QUESTION: What is Black's plan?

ANSWER: First of all 11...h6 slows down the g5 advance, so the knight is safe on f6 for the time being. Black is hardly likely to castle kingside now, given that g4-g5 would then carry too much force. Instead her plan is to follow up with ...Nc6 and probably ...0-0-0, and she will rely upon her active development to generate counterplay.

12.Rg1

It's difficult for White to do anything without this move, breaking the pin on the knight and aiding a possible g5 advance.

12...Nc6 13.O-O-O

EXERCISE: 13.O-O-O is an inaccurate move which allows Black to seize the initiative. Try to find a good idea for Black.

13...O-O-O

ANSWER: Partac missed a chance to strike with the pawn break 13...b5! .

This is a desirable pawn break because:

1. It opens lines on the queenside, where the white king lies.

2. An exchange on c4 will weaken White's pawn structure and/or lead to tactical possibilities.

3. Black's king hasn't committed to either side; if the queenside opens up after ...b5, the centre might even be the safest place for him.

A possible continuation could be 14.h4 Nb4! 15.Qb1 bxc4 16.bxc4 (forced, as otherwise f3 would be hanging) 16...Rb8 and suddenly things are looking very rosy for Black. One nice line is 17.g5 hxg5 18.hxg5 Ne4! 19.Nxe4 Bxe4 20.Qxe4 Nxa2+ 21.Kc2 Qb6 with huge mating attack threats on b3 and b2.

Of course 13...b5 only works because of the tactic 14.cxb5? Nb4! which lands White in big trouble on the c-file, so we have to go back a move to try to work out how White can avoid this. The obvious choice to rule out ...Nb4 possibilities by playing 13.a3 . This is indeed sensible, and after 13...Ne5! 14.Nxe5 Qxe5 15.Rg3 we've reached a position with roughly level chances.

White could also alter the timing of a2-a3 because ...Nb4 only really becomes dangerous after White has castled queenside. 13.h4 h5!? ( if 13...Nb4 14.Qb1! and a2-a3 next ) 14.g5 Ng4 15.a3 Be7?! , M.Gagunashvili-Zhang Zhong, Sydney 2008, allowed White to unleash the pseudo-sacrifice 16.Nd5! exd5 17.cxd5 . White regains the piece because of the pin and keeps a slight edge after 17...O-O 18.dxc6 Qxc6 19.Qxc6 dxc6 20.Ne5 as indicated by Gagunashvili.

Looking for an improvement for Black in this game, instead of retreating with 15...Be7 I could suggest 15...Nce5!? 16.Nxe5 Qxe5!? ; or 15...Rc8 16.Ne4 Bf8! and if 17.O-O-O? then 17...Na5! suddenly exposes White's soft spots.

Wang Rui - Tiviakov,S [E38]

It seems appropriate to include one of Sergei Tiviakov's games in this line, as he has been one of its leading practitioners. The brain behind the idea of 6...Qb6, however, belongs to Ukrainian Grandmaster Oleg Romanishin. His creative mind has invented so many ideas, in the Nimzo-Indian and in other openings.

In this game we'll consider some lines where White chooses Bd3 instead of Be2.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Qc2 c5 5.dxc5 Bxc5 6.Nf3 Qb6 7.e3 Qc7 8.Bd3

The game's actual move order was 8.b3 b6 9.Bb2 Bb7 10.Bd3 , but as I mentioned in the Carlsen-Shaposhnikov game, I feel this is a slightly risky move order as it allows the possibility of 10.Nb5! . Interestingly, Tiviakov has played both 9...Bb7 and 9...a6 , so it's unclear how much of a threat he views 10.Nb5 as.

After 9...a6 10.Bd3 Bb7 a key line is 11.Ne4!? ( or 11.O-O Be7 12.Ne4 ) 11...Be7 ( 11...Bb4+ 12.Ke2 Be7 is also playable ) .

Here are two examples, both from Tiviakov's games:

(a) 12.O-O Nc6 13.a3 Ng4!? 14.Ned2 ( if 14.Bxg7 Rg8 with good compensation down the g-file ) 14...Bf6 15.Be4 Bxb2 16.Qxb2 f5 17.Bd3 O-O 18.h3 Nce5 19.Nxe5 Nxe5 and Black is fine, J.De Jong-S.Tiviakov, Hilversum 2008.

(b) 12.Nxf6+ Bxf6 13.Bxf6 gxf6 splits Black's pawns but this isn't hugely significant when the king can go elsewhere, plus the open g-file becomes a source of counterplay: 14.Be4 Nc6 15.Qc3 Ke7! 16.O-O-O b5! 17.Nd4 ( 17.c5!? ) 17...Nxd4 18.Rxd4 Bxe4 19.Rxe4 Rhg8 20.g3 Qc6 21.f3 Rg5 was roughly level in J.Delemarre-S.Tiviakov, Leeuwarden 2003, and 21...d5 might be even better.

QUESTION: Going back to move 8, what does Black do against 8.a3 , intending b2-b4?

ANSWER: Pretty much the same thing. In fact, Black has no objection to White employing this plan, for a couple of reasons:

1. In many lines, e.g. when Black employs a Hedgehog set-up, the bishop retreats to e7 in any case, so Black could view a3 and b4 as pushing the bishop back to where it wants to go.

2. In typical Hedgehog positions White's a3/b4 pawn advance grants him extra space on the queenside, but it also leaves the c4-pawn lacking natural protection and Black can use this as a potential source of counterplay.

An example of how play could develop is 8...b6 9.b4 Be7 10.Bb2 a6 11.Be2 Bb7 12.O-O d6 13.Rac1 Nbd7 14.Rfd1 Rc8! (notice that Black again fully organizes his queenside before castling) 15.Qb1 Qb8 16.Qa1 O-O 17.Rd4!? Qa8 (creating a nice symmetry!)

18.Rcd1 Rfe8 19.Ne1 (I.Rogers-R.Berzinsh, British League 2007) and here Rogers suggests 19...Bf8! and if 20.Bf3?! ( 20.f3 d5 is level ) then 20...Bxf3 21.Nxf3 e5 22.Rh4 Qb7 with an edge for Black the pawn on c4 needs constant attention and this hampers White.

7.c6 is one of the most amazing opening ideas in recent times. The position after 6...Na6 had been known for many years, with hundreds of games played in that time. It took the creative genius of Alexander Morozevich to discover such an idea, and since he unleashed his novelty in 2008, quite a few other players have followed suit.

QUESTION: What's the point of 7.c6? Doesn't it just waste a move? And doesn't it also allow Black to gain more control of the centre?

ANSWER: White does seem to be wasting a move just to give up a pawn (albeit one that was already doomed). This is probably why no-one had thought of playing such a crazy-looking move. However, when you look at it a bit more carefully, it becomes apparent that 7.c6 is not stupid at all, for the following reasons:

1. Black's knight is more than likely coming to c5 in any case, so that takes care of the "lost tempo" issue.

2. By playing c5-c6 White forces Black to alter the pawn structure and close the c-file. This can be pretty useful for White, if nothing else because his c-pawn is unlikely to come under the same pressure it would do on a half-open c-file.

Let's briefly summarize older moves, which generally haven't posed a threat to Black:

(a) 7.Bg5 transposes to the next game.

Dos Hermanas, 1992

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Qb3

4.Qb3 is called the Spielmann Variation. Despite the fact that it looks very similar to 4.Qc2 , it's nowhere near as popular and very much remains a sideline of the Nimzo-Indian.

QUESTION: Why is that the case? It even seems more aggressive than 4.Qc2 , since Black is forced to deal with the threat to his bishop.

ANSWER: 4.Qb3 is certainly not a bad move and it has on occasion been played by strong grandmasters. The real reason for its lack of popularity is that it doesn't challenge Black to any great extent as long as he knows what he's doing with most lines just leading to an equal position. Of course many White players are happy to accept an equal position as long as it's one which they know well, and then try to use their understanding to outplay opponents in the middlegame this is an opening strategy never to be underestimated.

A key point in Black's favour is that, in general, b3 is an inferior square for the queen than c2. During this game we'll examine some of the reasons for this.

QUESTION: What about 4.Bd2 instead of a queen move? How should Black meet this?

ANSWER: My feeling is that 4.Bd2 deserves a bit more respect than it actually gets.

Sure, it's a quiet move and Black has a number of good ways to meet it. But it does do a specific job and it certainly can't be refuted.

The line I'm going to suggest here is 4...d5 , not least because it fits with our Rubinstein coverage in which White could also play an early Bd2 and transpose. After 5.e3 O-O White has two main choices:

(a) If 6.a3 Black can play it like a QGD with White's bishop passively placed on d2. For example, 6...Be7 7.Nf3 c5 ( 7...b6 and ...Bb7 is also fine ) 8.dxc5 Bxc5 9.Be2 dxc4 10.Bxc4 a6 11.O-O b5 12.Ba2 Bb7 13.Rc1 Nbd7 14.Qe2 Rc8 15.Rfd1 Qe7 and Black has the more active position, I.Nemet-L.Brunner, Suhr 1990.

(b) 6.Nf3 is more flexible, reaching a Rubinstein with an early Bd2. If we continue with our usual 6...c5 as in Chapter 3, then White's only real way to justify an early Bd2 is with 7.a3 Bxc3 8.Bxc3 , otherwise Bd2 might become a wasted move. After 8...cxd4 9.Bxd4 Nc6 10.cxd5 ( if 10.Bc3 Ne4 ) 10...Nxd5 11.Be2 Re8 12.b4 e5 13.Bb2 e4 14.Nd4 Ne5 the position was roughly level in R.Stern-Z.Almasi, Dresden 2007.

Another move Black should consider playing is 6...b6 .

In the main lines ...b6 is a decent alternative to ...c5, and here there's the added bonus that Bd2 isn't a particularly threatening move. Two examples:

(b1) 7.cxd5 exd5 8.Bd3 Bb7 9.O-O Nbd7 10.Qc2 a6 ( preventing Nb5 in preparation for ...Bd6; 10...c5 is also okay ) 11.Ne5 ( 11.a3 Bd6 12.b4 Qe7 and if possible ...Ne4 is Black's plan ) 11...Nxe5!? ( 11...Bd6 12.f4 c5 is the alternative, keeping more tension ) 12.dxe5 Ne4 13.Bxe4 dxe4 14.Rfd1 Bxc3 15.Bxc3 Qe7 16.Rd2 Rad8 with a level position, A.Yusupov-R.Dautov, Dresden 1998.

(b2) After 7.Bd3 Bb7 8.O-O Black can steer the game towards the Parma Variation by playing 8...dxc4 9.Bxc4 Nbd7 . For example, 10.Qe2 c5 11.Rfd1 cxd4 12.exd4 Rc8 13.Bd3 Bxc3 14.bxc3 Qc7 15.Rac1 Rfe8 and we reach a position very similar to those discussed in Lautier-Carlsen (Game 9).

As you can see from all these examples, Black is not refuting 4.Bd2 , just getting reasonable positions. Now let's return now to 4.Qb3 :

4...c5

The most natural response to 4.Qb3 , as it both defends the bishop and hits d4.

5.dxc5

White often plays 5.Nf3 Nc6 6.dxc5 transposing to 5.dxc5 Nc6 6.Nf3 below.

QUESTION: With the queen on b3 instead of c2, White is able to answer 4...c5 with 5.d5 . Is this a good idea?

ANSWER: Even though White can play d4-d5, it's not great. Black is probably going to get a favourable Leningrad-type position. For example, 5...O-O 6.Bg5 exd5! (much better than blocking with ...e5 and ...d6, as Black can exploit his development lead gained because of Qb3) 7.cxd5 d6 8.e3 ( or 8.e4 h6 9.Bxf6 Qxf6 10.Bd3 Qg6 11.g3 f5! 12.exf5 Bxf5 with a plus, K.Spicak-M.Grabarczyk, Warsaw 1997 ) 8...Nbd7 9.Nf3 h6 10.Bh4 Nb6 with pressure against d5. If 11.Rd1 Black can play 11...Bd7! embarrassing White's queen by threatening ...Ba4!.

5...Nc6!

Yudasin doesn't recapture straight away. We'll soon realize why.

6.Bg5

One key point behind Black's move order is that after 6.Nf3 he doesn't have to recapture on c5 with the bishop. Instead 6...Ne4! demonstrates a drawback of 4.Qb3 in comparison to 4.Qc2 . White is more or less compelled to trade off his bishop with 7.Bd2 Nxd2 8.Nxd2 , but this position is known to be fine for Black. For example, 8...Bxc5 9.e3 O-O 10.Be2 b6 11.O-O Bb7 12.Nde4 Rb8 , L.Barczay-M.Tal, Miskolc 1963 or 8...f5!? (to prevent Ne4) 9.e3 Bxc5 10.Be2 b6 11.O-O Bb7 J.Fries Nielsen-C.Ward, Copenhagen 1994 ; in both cases Black is fully equal. Akopian's 6.Bg5 is designed to prevent the ...Ne4 idea.

QUESTION: What happens if White forces the bishop to capture on c5 by playing 6.a3 ?

ANSWER: After 6...Bxc5 we discover another drawback of the queen being on b3.

Normally with the queen on c2 there would be the possibility at the right moment to play b2-b4, as we've seen before, but here this option doesn't exist unless White is willing to move his queen again. This position is also fine for Black, even though he can't play ...Qb6 to force e2-e3. For example, 7.Nf3 ( remember the trick 7.Bg5? Bxf2+! ) 7...O-O 8.Bg5 Nd4!? ( there's also nothing wrong with 8...b6 9.e3 Bb7 ) 9.Nxd4 Bxd4 10.e3 ( if 10.O-O-O then 10...Bxc3! 11.Qxc3 Ne4! 12.Bxd8 Nxc3 13.bxc3 Rxd8 ) 10...Qa5! 11.exd4 Qxg5 12.g3 d5 13.Bg2 dxc4 14.Qxc4 Bd7 with level chances, N.Konovalov-A.Zhigalko, Minsk 2008. In this position White erred with the greedy 15.Bxb7 and after 15...Rab8 16.Qa6 e5! Black seized the initiative.

6...h6

Black has a couple of alternatives here if he wants to complicate the game:

(a) 6...b6!? is a gambit I previously suggested in 'Easy Guide to the Nimzo-Indian', and is in fact a common theme in this line. I still feel Black gets reasonable compensation after 7.cxb6 Qxb6 8.Bxf6 gxf6 9.e3 Rb8 or 9.a3?! Nd4! 10.Qd1 Bxc3+ 11.bxc3 Qb2! .

(b) 6...Nd4 and now either 7.Qd1 h6!? or 7.Qa4 h6!? . In the latter case, 8.Bh4 would transpose to the next note.

7.Bxf6!

It's unusual for White to give up his dark-squared bishop, but on this occasion Akopian's decision is justified.

7.Bh4 allows Black to seize the initiative in two possible ways:

(a) 7...g5 8.Bg3 Ne4! 9.e3 Bxc3+ 10.bxc3 Qa5 11.Bd3 Nxg3 12.hxg3 Qxc5 13.Nf3 b6 and White's pawn weaknesses are worse than Black's, S.Agdestein-J.Hjartarson, Reykjavik 1996.

(b) 7...Nd4!? 8.Qa4 ( if 8.Qd1 then 8...Nf5! 9.Bxf6 Qxf6 10.Qd3 b6! 11.cxb6 axb6 with great activity ) 8...b6! 9.cxb6 Bb7! 10.bxa7 Bc6 is another enticing gambit possibility.

The game C.Goncalves-L.Almiron, correspondence 2005, is entertaining: 11.Qd1 Nf5 12.Bxf6 Qxf6 13.Qd3 Rxa7 14.e3 O-O 15.Nge2 Rfa8 16.a3 Nd6 17.O-O-O Rxa3! 18.bxa3 Rxa3 19.Kb2 Qe5 20.f3 Qa5 21.Rb1 Ne4! 22.fxe4 Bxe4 23.Qd4 e5! and Black won; after 24.Qxe4 Rxc3! White's king is getting mated.

7...Qxf6 8.Nf3

QUESTION: Should Black capture on c5 to regain his pawn, or on c3 to give White tripled c-pawns?

8...Bxc3+

ANSWER: I can't give an easy answer to this one, as neither approach is clearly better than the other. Yudasin chooses to give White the tripled pawns but as we'll see it's not so easy to pick them off if it was Black would enjoy a clear edge.

After 8...Bxc5 White can finish his development smoothly and a roughly equal position arises. For example, 9.e3 b6 10.Be2 Bb7 11.Ne4 Qe7 12.O-O O-O 13.Rfd1 d6 14.Qc3 Rac8 15.a3 a5 , V.Akopian-E.Schmittdiel, Groningen 1991.

Two other lines are worth mentioning here:

(a) 8...O-O 9.e3 a5!? (intending a nasty ...a4-a3) 10.a4 was played in A.Hoffman-S.Slipak, Villa Martelli 1997.

EXERCISE: Black could recapture the pawn with 10...Bxc5 , but can you suggest a more creative and favourable way to do so?

ANSWER: Slipak played the incredible move 10...Nb8!! . This knight retreat looks ridiculous at first sight but becomes logical once you realize that ...Na6xc5 will leave the knight perfectly placed. After 11.Be2 Na6 12.O-O Nxc5 13.Qc2 Bxc3 14.bxc3 d6 15.Rfd1 e5 Black enjoyed a clear positional edge.

Unfortunately for Black, White has better options than the accommodating 10 a4?!, such as 10.a3 or simply 10.O-O-O which more or less nullifies the ...a5 idea.

(b) The gambit 8...b6!? proved to be hugely successful in the game R.Stern-K.Landa, German League 2007. The game lasted only two more moves: 9.cxb6 axb6 10.e3?? and now the bombshell 10...Ra3!! (see following diagram) and White felt obliged to resign.

Instead of 10.e3?? White must play 10.Rc1 after which Black has some compensation but the onus is on him to justify the pawn sacrifice.

9.Qxc3

Akopian chooses to exchange queens. In either case, Black must decide how to combat the tripled pawns. It's not quite as simple as it initially looks, chiefly because the knight on c6 is not ideally placed for this battle.

After 9.bxc3 we can borrow Slipak's idea above and play 9...Nb8!? . Again it looks strange to begin with, but once the knight reaches c5 it makes perfect sense and 10.Qa3 Na6 11.g3 ( or 11.Rd1 Qf5! Har Zvi ) 11...Nxc5! 12.Bg2 d6 is fine for Black.

9...Qxc3+ 10.bxc3

EXERCISE: Devise a plan for Black against the tripled pawns.

ANSWER:

10...b6!

Yudasin sacrifices a pawn so that he can more easily attack White's remaining pawns on the queenside and this guarantees Black a level position. Yudasin also suggests ...b6 ideas against 9.bxc3 , for example 9...b6 or 9...Qe7 10.Qb5 b6 , and these look good too.

Without the support of the queen, the ...Nb8 plan isn't so effective here, and after 10...Nb8 11.Nd4 Na6 12.Nb3 Black will probably have to play ...b6 under less favourable circumstances.

11.cxb6 axb6 12.e3 Ra4!

An important move, much stronger than 12...Ba6 . Black has to use the rook to attack c4.

Key Notes

1. After 4.Qc2 c5 5.dxc5 Bxc5 6.Nf3, Black's 6...Qb6 (see Carlsen-Shaposhnikov, Schuurman-Partac and Wang Rui-Tiviakov) is designed to force White to block in his bishop with e2-e3. Black then regroups and normally employs a Hedgehog-style formation.

2. White can play very aggressively with an early g2-g4 (see Carlsen-Shaposhnikov and Schuurman-Partac). The evidence suggests that Black should usually decline taking the pawn and instead look for counterplay with speedy development.

3. Black is normally faced with the issue of whether or not to play an early ...a6 to prevent Nb5 possibilities. It's often a good idea to do so (see Carlsen-Shaposhnikov and Schuurman-Partac), but in some cases (Wang Rui-Tiviakov) Black is able to do without the move.

4. 4.Qc2 0-0 5.Nf3 c5 6.dxc5 can be met by 6...Na6!. The ...Na6xc5 plan is an effective one for Black here because White's knight is already committed to f3. This means that ...Ne4 becomes a strong resource which White cannot quickly prevent with f2-f3 (see Braun-Papp).

5. 4.Qc2 0-0 5.Bg5 c5 6.dxc5 can also be met by 6...Na6!. This time White can play f2-f3 but the ...Na6xc5 plan is still effective due to tactical reasons (see Igla-Kosintseva).

6. Black should treat 4.Bd2 (see Akopian-Yudasin) with some respect, but should also search for transpositions into known lines where Bd2 is a harmless option.

7. Remember why 4.Qb3 (Akopian-Yudasin) is an inferior version of 4.Qc2: White's queen doesn't control the vital e4-square and blocks the possibility of an early b2-b4. Be on the lookout for ...Ne4 ideas and possible ...b6 gambits.
