Chapter Four. Rubinstein Variation: 4.e3 Other Lines

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3 O-O

In this chapter we'll cover alternatives to the mainline Rubinstein, 5.Bd3 d5 6.Nf3 c5 7.O-O . White has a number of interesting options and they lead to a variety of different positions and pawn structures:

(a) 5.Bd3 d5 6.a3 is a clever idea. White plays a super-delayed Saemisch Variation with Black committed to ...d5. This means Black must choose a completely different approach to the one seen in Chapters 1 and 2. Luckily for Black, there are promising options for him here, as we see in Nguyen-Adams (Game 14) and Conquest-Naiditsch (Game 15).

(b) 5.Bd3 d5 6.cxd5 exd5 is a solid option for White. By fixing the central pawn structure very quickly, White eliminates some of dynamism from the position and aims for a slow manoeuvring game (see Giorgadze-Almasi).

(c) 5.Bd3 d5 6.Nge2 is another reasonable option. White develops the king's knight to e2 which, as we'll see, is a common place for it in this chapter (see Milanovic-Baklan).

(d) Last, but certainly not least, there's the immediate 5.Nge2 - the Reshevsky Variation. At first sight it looks a little odd to block the f1-bishop's development, but this is not a bad line for White and certainly better than it looks! See Gelfand-Kramnik (Game 18) and Mamedyarov-Nisipeanu (Game 19).

Game14
Nguyen Ngoc Truongson - Adams,M [E49]
Dresden Olympiad, 2008

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3 O-O 5.Bd3 d5 6.a3 Bxc3+ 7.bxc3

We've already seen the Saemisch ( 4.a3 ) and the delayed Saemisch ( 4.e3 O-O 5.a3 ). This is the super-delayed Saemisch!

QUESTION: Why does White wait until now to play a2-a3?

ANSWER: White has waited for Black to commit himself, with ...0-0 but more importantly with ...d5. This means that Black can no longer choose the traditional – and appealing – anti-Saemisch plan of peppering the c-pawn with the likes of ...b6, ...Ba6 and ...Nc6-a5, and has to try something else. Fortunately for Black there are some other good plans.

7...dxc4!

QUESTION: Why the exclamation mark? Shouldn't Black keep the tension with something like 7...c5 , as he does in the 4.e3 main lines?

ANSWER: The short answer is that it's very rarely a bad idea to play ...dxc4 once White's light-squared bishop has moved from f1. Black is in effect gaining a tempo by forcing the bishop to move twice in short succession.

The long answer is a bit more complicated. With this particular position and pawn structure, it's actually in White's interests to clarify the structure by exchanging on d5. So 7...c5 is met by 8.cxd5! exd5 and now 9.Ne2! .

This is an excellent move. The knight belongs on e2 rather than f3 here, for the following reasons:

1. White has a long-term plan to utilize his central pawn majority and push forward with f2-f3 and e3-e4. If this plan can be carried out successfully, White has excellent chances of creating a powerful attack in the centre and on the kingside.

2. With White's knight on f3 Black would have an obvious way to activate his c8-bishop, with a strong pin on g4. Here there is no such idea, as ...Bg4 would merely run into White's plan of f2-f3. In fact there isn't really a decent square for the bishop on the c8-h3 diagonal, which justifies White's decision to "free" it by exchanging on d5!

Black can solve the problem of what to do with his bishop by playing 9...b6! 10.O-O Ba6! . Still, ever since Botvinnik showed the potential of White's position in a famous victory over Capablanca, players have been clamouring to play this line as White. This is fully understandable given how the game went: 11.Bxa6 Nxa6 12.Bb2?! ( 12.Qd3! is better ) 12...Qd7 13.a4 Rfe8? ( 13...cxd4 14.cxd4 Rfc8 is better ) 14.Qd3 c4? .

"This is a really serious positional blunder. Black evidently assumed that White would be unable to advance the e-pawn later, and Black's superiority would tell on the queenside ... However, Black's superiority on the queenside happens in this case to be of no great consequence, and the breakthrough e2-e4 proves inevitable. Black should have contented himself with the modest defence 14...Qb7 " – Botvinnik.

The game continued 15.Qc2 Nb8 16.Rae1 Nc6 17.Ng3 Na5 18.f3 Nb3 19.e4 Qxa4 20.e5 Nd7 21.Qf2 g6 22.f4 (the white pawn roller causes Black all sorts of problems and Capablanca was virtually forced to play his next move) 22...f5 23.exf6 Nxf6 24.f5! (from now on White's kingside attack is totally relentless) 24...Rxe1 25.Rxe1 Re8 26.Re6 Rxe6 27.fxe6 Kg7 28.Qf4 Qe8 29.Qe5 Qe7 .

Now follows one of the most famous double piece sacrifices in the whole of chess history: 30.Ba3! Qxa3 31.Nh5+! gxh5 32.Qg5+ Kf8 33.Qxf6+ Kg8 34.e7 Qc1+ 35.Kf2 Qc2+ 36.Kg3 Qd3+ 37.Kh4 Qe4+ 38.Kxh5 Qe2+ 39.Kh4 Qe4+ 40.g4 Qe1+ 41.Kh5 1-0, M.Botvinnik-J.Capablanca, Rotterdam 1938.

Opening theory has moved on to some extent 11.f3 and 11...Re8 12.Ng3 Bxd3 13.Qxd3 Nc6 14.Ra2 or 14.Bb2 is more common now), but the general feeling remains that this is not an easy line for Black to play. Most prefer to avoid it given that other options – 7...dxc4! – are available.

Let's now return to the position after 7...dxc4 :

8.Bxc4 c5

Only now does Black make his usual pawn break.

9.Ne2

White can also play 9.Nf3 and this is covered next game.

As usual Black doesn't need to fear 9.dxc5? as after 9...Qa5 he regains the pawn with at least an equal position.

9...Qc7

As we saw in the previous chapter, the queen often settles on c7. Moving the queen to c7 as early as possible makes sense because it sets up an X-ray attack on the c4-bishop and White has to deal with this straightaway.

10.Ba2

EXERCISE: Take a good look at the position after 10.Ba2 and try to devise an effective plan of development for Black.

ANSWER:

10...b6!

This is an excellent choice by Adams, which assures Black a fully playable position.

QUESTION: What about 10...e5 here, adding pressure to White's centre and freeing the c8-bishop?

ANSWER: This is also playable but I prefer Adams' plan. The problem with 10...e5 is that it doesn't just free the c8-bishop; it also frees the bishop on a2 which suddenly becomes a major player. After, say, 11.O-O Rd8 12.Bb2 Nc6 13.Qc2 I slightly prefer White, and Black's best chance of equalizing probably entails playing ...Be6 to exchange White's powerful bishop at the cost of a pawn weakness.

QUESTION: So in general, the plan with ...b6 is better than ...e5?

ANSWER: Not necessarily. In fact it all depends on where White's bishop retreats

If White chooses 10.Bd3 instead of 10.Ba2 , then 10...e5! becomes much more attractive because Black no longer has to worry about a bishop bearing down the a2-g8 diagonal. An example: 11.O-O Nc6 12.Bb2 Be6! (it's Black's bishop which seizes the diagonal) 13.h3 Rfd8 14.Rc1 Na5 .

Black has a solid position with counterplay in the centre and on the queenside, V.Inkiov-S.Kindermann, Plovdiv 1984.

Conversely, 10...b6 – although still not bad – becomes less enticing since White's bishop on d3 is better placed to meet the fianchetto plan, as will become clear in the main game

11.O-O Ba6!

An excellent move and part of Black's plan. The bishop gleefully grabs the diagonal which its counterpart has given up.

If 11...Bb7 White blunts the bishop with 12.f3! and then further with e3-e4! with an edge for White.

12.Re1

White feels obliged to break the pin on the e2-knight as quickly as possible.

QUESTION: It's yet another decision for Black. Should he develop with 12...Nc6 or 12...Nbd7 ?

ANSWER:

12...Nc6!

Adams' plan beginning with 10...b6 becomes clearer after this move. On many occasions in the previous chapter we saw that d7 is the best square for the knight, but this isn't one of them. Here the key difference is that with the bishop on a6 Black has a natural and effective plan of ...Nc6-a5 and possibly ...Bc4, seeking light-square action on the queenside.

12...Nbd7 13.Ng3 cxd4 14.cxd4 Rac8 15.e4 Qc2 16.Bf4 Qxd1 17.Raxd1 Bc4 18.Bb1 was slightly better for White in J.Ramon Perez-M.Roeder, Zaragoza 2001.

13.Ng3 Rad8

QUESTION: Is this the right rook to d8?

ANSWER: There could be a whole book written about the "which rook?" question. Often, over the board, the thing that matters most is not to spend half an hour deciding!

It could be, though, that 13...Rfd8 is more accurate if Black carries on as in this game. If Black chooses this, following 14.Bb2 Na5 15.e4 Bc4 16.Bxc4 Nxc4 17.Bc1 he can retreat the knight to e8 without blocking the king's rook: 17...Ne8! 18.e5 cxd4 19.cxd4 Rd5 20.Qg4 Qd7 21.Ne2 f6 22.Qf3 Rc8 23.exf6 Nxf6 was fine for Black in I.Sokolov-Z.Almasi, Sibenik 2008.

14.Bb2 Na5

Typical Nimzo-Indian strategy and something similar to what we've seen in the Saemisch chapters. Black isn't winning a pawn on c4 but he is nearly as happy to win the square. Adams' plan is to play ...Bc4, swap off the light-squared bishops and then use c4 as an outpost, probably for the knight, and the c-file for counterplay.

Adams' method is cleaner, though not necessarily objectively better than 14...e5!? as played in I.Sokolov-J.Polgar, Hoogeveen 2006, when Black pressures White centre at the cost activating two white minor pieces – the a2-bishop and the g3-knight which now has f5 available. Notice here that the a8-rook to d8 does look like the right decision.

15.Rc1?!

Nguyen Ngoc Truongson doesn't fancy life without his light-squared bishop but his attempt to keep it proves to be misguided.

15.e4! is the move that White has been building towards and it remains the right idea. After 15...Bc4! 16.Bxc4 Nxc4 17.Bc1! (threatening Bg5) 17...cxd4 18.cxd4 Rd7 19.Bg5 Ne8 the chances were roughly level in T.Iljin-S.Khader, Alushta 2009.

15...Bc4 16.Bb1?

It's never easy to admit making a mistake, but it wasn't too late to do so with 16.Bxc4! Nxc4 17.Qb3 .

16...Nb3

Of course! What else could White have been expecting? Adams' queenside plan beginning on move 11 has worked wonders, albeit with a little help from his grandmaster opponent. Nguyen Ngoc Truongson decides to give up the exchange but this never looks like working and Adams slowly grinds out a win.

17.e4

17.Rc2 is probably better objectively, but White can hardly move. What's more, it would have made a mockery of his earlier decision to keep the light-squared bishops on the board!

Conquest,S - Naiditsch,A [E53]
Pulvermuehle, 2004

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3 O-O 5.Bd3

QUESTION: There's been no mention yet of 5.Nf3 . Does this just transpose to the main lines?

ANSWER: In the vast majority of cases it does: after 5...d5 6.Bd3 , or alternatively 6.a3 Bxc3+ 7.bxc3 c5 (diagram) 8.Bd3 dxc4 9.Bxc4 transposes to the game. Black could try to exploit this move order with 8...Qc7!? and if 9.cxd5 cxd4! 10.cxd4?? ( 10.O-O! is better, sacrificing a pawn ) then 10...Qc3+! wins a piece – a trick more than one player has fallen for.

QUESTION: Instead of 8.Bd3 , shouldn't White fix the pawn order first with 8.cxd5 exd5 and only then 9.Bd3 just like in Botvinnik-Capablanca?

ANSWER: In this case, as we've already seen, White's knight would much prefer to be on e2, and Black can reach a comfortable position by playing 9...Bg4! 10.O-O Nc6 or 10...Nbd7 .

QUESTION: So does the 5.Nf3 move order just give White fewer options than 5.Bd3 ?

ANSWER: Overall it does, but there is one way for White to try to justify 5.Nf3 and that is to delay the development of the f1-bishop, for example with 8.Bb2!? .

Black doesn't want to take on c4 yet, allowing White to develop his bishop in one go, and after 8...Nc6 White could keep the game of cat-and-mouse going with 9.Rc1!? again challenging Black to find a useful move other than ...dxc4:

(a) One solid option here is 9...Re8 . Black prepares ...e5 after ...dxc4, and it's also a useful move if White plays cxd5. After 10.Bd3 dxc4 11.Bxc4 e5 12.dxe5 Qxd1+ 13.Rxd1 Nxe5 14.Nxe5 Rxe5 15.Be2 Bd7 16.c4 Re7 17.Bxf6 gxf6 18.Rd6 Kg7 19.Kd2 Bc6 20.Kc3 Re6 21.Rd2 f5 the endgame was close to equal in E.Geller-B.Spassky, Riga 1965.

(b) Another possibility is 9...Qc7 . For example, 10.cxd5 exd5 11.dxc5!? ( 11.Bd3 Bg4! ) 11...Rd8 ( 11...Bg4!? ) 12.Qc2 Qa5 13.Be2 (H.Wirthensohn-S.Tatai, Zürich 1984) and here 13...Qxc5 14.O-O Be6 looks okay for Black.

So in summary, 5.Nf3 very often transposes to main lines but watch out for ways to exploit inferior move orders and be aware of tricky lines where White delays developing the f1-bishop.

Now let's return to 5.Bd3 :

5...d5 6.a3 Bxc3+ 7.bxc3 dxc4 8.Bxc4 c5 9.Nf3 Qc7

EXERCISE: Which of these three possible ways to safeguard the bishop should White choose? a) 10.Bd3 ; b) 10.Ba2 ; c) 10.Qe2 . (Hint: Think of tactical reasons rather than positional ones.)

Just as against 9.Ne2 in the previous game, Black plays this useful queen move with an indirect threat against the c4-bishop.

ANSWER:

10.Ba2!

White actually has to be a bit careful here. 10.Bd3? , which was possible in the previous game, loses a pawn to 10...cxd4! since 11.cxd4?? loses to 11...Qc3+! , now possible without a knight on e2. Remember this trap, as many have fallen for it.

If 10.Qe2?! Black can employ a similar trick with 10...cxd4! 11.cxd4? b5! winning a piece because of 12.Bxb5 Qc3+ . White can cut his losses with 11.Nxd4 but 11...e5 12.Nb5 Qc6 is still good for Black; for example, 13.O-O a6 14.a4! axb5! 15.axb5 Qxc4! 16.Qxc4 Rxa1 and Black's rook and two knights outweighed White's queen in D.Weise-W.Unzicker, Nuremberg 1959 .

10...b6

Again Black chooses this move to help with queenside development.

11.O-O

11...Nbd7

QUESTION: Why didn't Naiditsch carry out the same plan as the one chosen by Adams in the previous game, with 11...Ba6 followed by ...Nc6-a5?

ANSWER: This plan is less effective with White's knight on f3 instead of e2. For example, after 12.Re1 Nc6 White can play 13.e4! straightaway and if 13...Na5 14.Bg5 it's becoming obvious that White has got a much better version of the previous game. So Naiditsch chooses the more traditional ...Bb7, ...Nbd7 set-up. The good news for Black is that with a knight on f3, White isn't geared up to play f2-f3 and e3-e4 which he would like to do against ...Bb7 plans.

QUESTION: Is there any reason why Naiditsch chose 11...Nbd7 first, instead of 11...Bb7 , or doesn't it matter?

ANSWER: I feel that 11...Nbd7 is probably more accurate, as 11...Bb7 allows White an added possibility of 12.Ne5!? planning the desirable f2-f3 and e3-e4, and 12...Nbd7 13.Nxd7 Qxd7 14.f3 Rfd8 15.Bb2 intending e3-e4 is perhaps a bit better for White.

12.Bb2 Bb7

EXERCISE: Find a strong move for Black if White plays 13.Rc1 here.

13.Qe2

ANSWER: Did you spot that Black was threatening something after 12...Bb7 was played? 13.Rc1 fails to meet the threat which is 13...Ng4! when suddenly there are mating ideas on h2. There's no fully satisfactory way for White to continue here: 14.Re1 Bxf3 15.Qxf3 Qxh2+ won a pawn in N.Golovlev-A.Beliavsky, Alushta 2009; 14.g3? Qc6 leaves White terribly exposed on the long diagonal; while 14.d5 exd5 15.Bxd5 Rad8 still gives White problems after 16.c4 Ndf6 or 16.Bxb7? Nde5! .

13.c4 is certainly possible, and here 13...Rfd8 ( 13...Ng4 no longer works as White has 14.d5! ) 14.Re1 cxd4 ( 14...Be4 is another option ) 15.exd4 reaches positions similar to those discussed in the Lautier-Carlsen game of the previous chapter: 15...Rac8 16.h3 Qf4! (this move again) 17.Re3 Nc5!? 18.Rc1 Nce4 19.Qe2 Nd6 20.Rd1 Nf5 21.Red3 Be4 22.Rb3 offered roughly level chances in J.Descovich-P.Coleman, correspondence 1996.

Stuart Conquest's choice of 13.Qe2 also meets the threat, as now 13...Ng4 is simply answered by 14.e4! .

13...e5

This move – attacking the centre and gaining space – is Black's most ambitious course of action, although as noted before there's a risk associated with opening the a2-g8 diagonal when a white bishop is lurking there. Then again, it's often difficult to win games without taking any risks, especially as Black, and Naiditsch is rewarded for his bold play in this game.

If Black wants to play it solid then 13...cxd4 might be the way to go. As we've seen before, normally White prefers recapturing with the c-pawn, and that would be the case here even if after 14.exd4 Black didn't have the strong 14...Ng4! . So White plays 14.cxd4! .

EXERCISE: Find a good plan and move for Black.

ANSWER: As usual, exchanges favour Black, and especially the exchange of light-squared bishops. So 14...Bd5! is a good move. After 15.Bxd5 ( if 15.Rac1 Qb7 16.Bb1 to avoid the exchange, Black could even insist with 16...Be4 ) 15...exd5 16.Rfc1 Qb7 17.a4 Rfc8 18.Ne5 Rxc1+ 19.Rxc1 Rc8 20.Rxc8+ Qxc8 Black safely equalized in J.Dorfman-A.Khalifman, German League 2001.

Let's return to Naiditsch's choice in the game: 13...e5 .

14.Rfd1

Both players now slowly build up, improving their rooks and maintaining the tension for the moment. At each move there are a number of possibilities for either side to release the tension, by advances or exchanges, so this is a tricky position to play and evaluate.

14...Rad8

It feels a bit too early for 14...e4?! and 15.Nh4 Rac8 16.Nf5! looked better for White in Nguyen Anh Dung-H.Van Riemsdijk, Manila Olympiad 1992 – even more so after 16...Nb8? 17.c4! (one bishop blocked but another comes to life) 17...Qd7? 18.g4! Ne8 19.dxc5 Qe6 20.cxb6 Rc5 21.bxa7 Nc6 22.Rd5 and Black had to resign.

15.Rac1

Another useful move, especially as exchanges on the c-file would expose the black queen.

15...h6

EXERCISE: White's army is fully mobilized but what to do next? Find a way to improve White's position.

With 15...h6 Naiditsch naturally wanted to prevent Ng5, especially in answer to ...Rfe8, but Conquest finds another strong way to activate this piece:

ANSWER:

16.Nh4!

White manages to improve one of his pieces. The knight is coming to f5, which is an even more attractive square now that Black has weakened with ...h6. Notice that Ng6 is also a possibility now.

16...Rfe8

This is a critical moment in the game. One option for White that both sides have to constantly evaluate is d4-d5, and perhaps this was the best moment to play it: 17.d5! .

Let's try to assess the changes after White's advance. White has gained a passed pawn and threatens to solidify it with e3-e4 and/or c3-c4. If White is allowed to play e3-e4 in particular, he gains considerable space and Black's position becomes a bit cramped. On the other hand the position closes, for the time being at least, and White loses some potential for dynamic play with his bishops – the one on a2 is immediately blocked. All in all I suspect White stands a bit better after, say, 17...e4! (a vital move, gaining space and the e5-square, and preventing e3-e4) 18.Nf5 Ne5 19.c4 Qd7 20.Ng3 .

I should say that specifics are crucial when trying to evaluate position-changing advances. Here, unlike on the next move, 16.d5 is less effective. 16...e4! gains a key tempo, and after 17.Nh4 Black can play the clever temporary pawn sacrifice 17...c4! intending 18.Qxc4 Nc5! or 18.Bxc4 Ne5 , in either case regaining the pawn with very good play for Black. The line between success and failure of an idea is sometimes paper thin!

17.Nf5 Nf8!

A strong move, covering d5 a third time. The moment for the d5 advance has been lost and White has to think of another plan, which is not that easy. The intention behind White's next move is sound – to play e3-e4 – but Naiditsch's response is very strong. Instead White should probably keep the status quo for the moment, with a roughly level position.

18.f3 e4!

Naiditsch doesn't allow e3-e4 and instead makes the advance himself, securing good activity in the centre.

ANSWER:

An attacking "retreat" is never the easiest thing to spot. White's next move is the only way to safeguard the knight, as knight retreats allow 20...Bg4 skewering queen and rook.

20.g4 h5!?

Game16
Giorgadze,G - Almasi,Z [E48]
Yerevan Olympiad, 1996

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3 O-O 5.Bd3 d5 6.cxd5

We've already seen in some situations that it's useful for White to carry out this exchange in the centre.

Here he does it straight away, not giving Black any chance for ...dxc4, e.g. 6.a3 Bxc3+ 7.bxc3 dxc4! .

6...exd5 7.Nge2!

Once again the e2-square is the best place for this knight, for the following reasons:

1. Having released Black's light-squared bishop so early by the exchange in the centre, White should try to limit its scope and certainly avoid a pin after Nf3 Bg4.

2. White has a clear-cut plan in the middlegame of advancing his pawn centre with f2-f3 and e3-e4.

7...Re8!

QUESTION: This looks like a useful move but shouldn't Black be attacking the centre with 7...c5 , as per usual?

ANSWER: No, it's a slight change of plan! White's early clarification with 6.cxd5 is aimed against 7...c5 . On the other hand, the plan Almasi chooses in this game exploits the negative points of 6.cxd5 ; for example, here the rook moves to the newly opened e-file.

White has good chances of getting an edge after 7...c5 . 8.O-O Bd6

QUESTION: Why did Almasi retreat this bishop? Why not at least leave the decision until later and instead develop a queenside piece?

ANSWER: Now that White has a) at least temporarily incarcerated his dark-squared bishop with e2-e3 and b) freed Black's light-squared bishop with cxd5 exd5, Black can look upon 3...Bb4 as being a job well done. There is no longer any obligation or desire to trade the bishop for a knight. There is nothing left to do on the b4-square and d6 is clearly a very active post for it. The bishop points menacingly in the direction of the white king, and of course there is no longer c4-c5 to worry about either. The position is a bit like a QGD Exchange Variation, but a more passive version for White – his bishop is stuck on c1 rather than g5 – and a more active one for Black. There are further reasons why the bishop retreats so quickly but I don't want to give away any more clues just yet!

1. White will unlikely be able to play a successful e3-e4 with the tension created by ...c5. In most cases it will lead to an unfavourable clearing of the centre, or worse.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3 O-O 5.Nge2

QUESTION: We've seen Ne2 before but never so early. Why does White play Ne2 before Bd3, blocking in the bishop?

ANSWER: It does look at little strange at first sight but White wants to solve the issue of the b4-bishop – by playing a2-a3 – before doing anything else, and before Black can react fully in the centre (for example, with 5.Bd3 d5 6.Nge2 dxc4! as in the previous game).

5...d5!

This is a good reply. In answer to a2-a3, Black will hardly want to give up his dark-squared bishop and help White's development in the process so a bishop retreat is inevitable. 5...d5 ensures that Black gains a solid foothold in the centre before the bishop has to retreat.

It's quite convenient for Black in the main lines of the e3 Nimzo-Indian – the ones covered in this book. He plays 5...d5 whether White plays 5.Bd3 , 5.Nf3 or 5.Nge2!

QUESTION: In that case, couldn't Black play ...d5 a move earlier, with 4...d5 followed by ...0-0 ?

ANSWER: Considering different move orders is an excellent way to improve our understanding of chess openings. 4...d5 is indeed possible, with transpositions to the main lines after 5.Nf3 O-O and 5.Bd3 O-O . 4...d5 might even make White think twice about playing 5.Nge2 in view of 5...dxc4!? .

But let's not stop there! When considering changes in move order we really have to take into account extra options for both sides, not just one, otherwise we're not doing our job properly. One question to ask is what happens if White plays a delayed Saemisch with 5.a3! Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 ?

Previously we've reached this position but with Black already castled and with a white bishop on d3, and Black plays 7...dxc4! (see Games 14-15). But what does Black play in our new position? We don't want to take on c4 and allow the bishop to recapture in one go, but if 6...O-O White might get in first with 7.cxd5! ( rather than 7.Bd3 dxc4! which is what we want ) 7...exd5 8.Bd3! c5 9.Ne2! when we are back in the Botvinnik-Capablanca position we discussed – and avoided – in Nguyen-Adams (Game 14)!

Of course there are other possibilities, for example Black can even play 5.a3 Be7 . The main point, though, is that we've discovered both sides have extra options after 4...d5 , and if Black wants to play the main lines 4...d5 can't be used as an easy way to discourage 5.Nge2 .

Now let's return to our move order, 4...O-O 5.Nge2 d5! :

6.a3 Bd6!?

Until a few years ago virtually everyone played 6...Be7 in this position. If White does nothing, then of course d6 is likely to be a more active square for the bishop. But it does rather invite White next move, which is surely the critical response:

7.c5 Be7

QUESTION: This looks horribly passive for Black. Who would want to play this position?

ANSWER: Well, to name a few players who have been happy to reach this position as Black, I would start with Kasparov, Anand, Kramnik and Topalov! It seems that many of the world's top players worked out that Black has nothing to be afraid of in this position. Black does seem to be a bit cramped but he has enough resources to reach a perfectly acceptable position, as we see in the game.

QUESTION: What is Black's plan?

ANSWER: Black must look for early pawn breaks, otherwise he really could end up getting slowly squashed. The two key pawn breaks are:

1) ...e5 in the centre; and

2) ...b6 to fight back on the queenside, usually meeting b2-b4 with ...a5.

Black often combines both of these plans, as we'll see in the game. A crucial advantage from Black's point of view is the positioning of White's knight on e2. White would dearly love to have the knight on f3. It wouldn't have to move again to assist development and could play a starring role in preventing Black from breaking in the centre with ...e5. Of course, had the knight been on f3, Black would have answered 6.a3 with 6...Bxc3+! rather than 6...Bd6 .

Key Notes

1. In the super-delayed Saemisch, after 4.e3 0-0 5.Bd3 d5 6.a3 Bxc3+ 7.bxc3 Black's most reliable option is 7...dxc4! 8.Bxc4 c5. The immediate 7...c5 allows White to reach a position after 8.cxd5! exd5 9.Ne2! which has been recognized as promising for White since a famous Botvinnik-Capablanca game (see the notes to Nguyen-Adams).

2. In the same line, after 7...dxc4! 8.Bxc4 c5 Black usually plays 9...Qc7 to indirectly attack the c4-bishop, regardless of whether White plays 9.Ne2 or 9.Nf3. As a rule of thumb, if the bishop retreats to a2 Black chooses a plan involving ...b6 and ...Ba6 or ...Bb7, whereas if the bishop retreats to d3 Black normally goes for a plan involving a quick ...e5 and ...Nc6. If White plays 9.Nf3 the bishop must choose a2 because of tactical reasons (see Nguyen-Adams and Conquest-Naiditsch).

3. If White plays 4.e3 0-0 5.Bd3 d5 6.cxd5 exd5, Black takes a different approach. He keeps his dark-squared bishop, normally moving it back to d6, and treats the position like a favourable QGD where White's c1-bishop is less active than normal. A crucial idea to remember is the one of meeting f2-f3 with ...c5!, as Almasi does in Giorgadze-Almasi (Game 16).

4. A solid way to meet 4.e3 0-0 5.Bd3 d5 6.Nge2 is with 6...dxc4! 7.Bxc4 e5!?, utilizing a tactical point in order to force the freeing advance at an early stage. I like the approach shown by Baklan in Milanovic-Baklan (Game 17), where Black keeps the tension with 8...Nc6 rather than exchanging pawns in the centre.

5. In the Reshevsky Variation, Black has no need to fear the temporary lack of space in the critical line 4.e3 0-0 5.Nge2 d5 6.a3 Bd6 7.c5 Be7. Other positional factors, such as White's slow development, usually allow Black to gain sufficient queenside counterplay or break free with ...e5, as Kramnik does to good effect in Gelfand-Kramnik (Game 18).
