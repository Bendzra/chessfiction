Chapter Three. Rubinstein Variation: 4 e3 Main Line

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3

4.e3 , the Rubinstein Variation, is one of the two most popular replies to the Nimzo-Indian (the other is 4.Qc2 ). In most cases White's plan is to develop his kingside quickly and to castle, leaving issues such as central control and attacking the bishop with a2-a3 until later.

QUESTION: Why block in the dark-squared bishop with 4.e3 ? Shouldn't White develop more actively with 4.Bg5 or 4.Nf3 , as he normally does in the Queen's Gambit?

ANSWER: Both of these moves are recognized options, and are covered later on. But neither 4.Bg5 nor 4.Nf3 are anywhere near as popular as 4.e3 .

QUESTION: Why is that the case?

ANSWER: That's a good question, and one which is not easy to answer without delving into concrete lines. For the moment, I can say that 4.Bg5 is of course a move White would like to play, but it allows significant counterplay after ...c5. 4.Nf3 looks more flexible than 4.Bg5 or 4.e3 , but is it really? In many lines of the Nimzo-Indian White likes to develop this knight elsewhere, or to play f2-f3 controlling the key e4-square (as we have already seen a few times). So if we were being unkind we could say that 4.Nf3 is both committal and doing nothing to control e4!

Of course things are far more complex than these generalizations suggest, and White has chances of gaining an advantage after either 4.Nf3 or 4.Bg5 , but by and large Nimzo-Indian players aren't too scared of these moves.

Let's return to 4.e3 and briefly consider White's development options. It's reasonable to assume that the light-squared bishop is best placed on d3, but notice that the knight has two decent squares: f3 and e2.

4...O-O

Black has other good moves here, including 4...c5 and 4...b6 , but in this book I'm focussing on the most popular choice, 4...O-O .

5.Bd3

This is the most natural follow-up to 4.e3 , and if Black does nothing White is even ready to play e3-e4 if he wants to.

In this chapter I'm covering the main line after 4...O-O , while the next one deals with alternatives such as 5.Nge2 .

5...d5

Black finally puts a pawn in the centre.

QUESTION: After ...d5, isn't it like a Queen's Gambit Declined?

ANSWER: The pawn structure certainly is, but the pieces are in different places and this means the positions and plans are not the same.

Let's assess the main differences:

1. White's dark-squared bishop is stuck inside the pawn chain instead of being actively placed on f4 or g5 (as it would be in a QGD after, say, 1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Bg5 Be7 ). This is certainly favourable for Black. For one thing, there is much less pressure on his centre and this allows him to play more actively.

2. Black's dark-squared bishop is on b4 rather than e7. Even though it's more "actively" placed, this probably helps White slightly. With a well timed a2-a3, White can either force it to retreat or, more likely, gain the bishop pair after a trade on c3.

6.Nf3

Other choices for White, here and on the next move, will be discussed in Chapter 4.

6...c5

And another pawn! If there were a bishop on g5, exerting pressure on the d5-pawn, Black would have to think twice about this advance. We could say that Black is exploiting the lack of pressure by White in the centre, and taking the opportunity to create his own pressure.

A less popular, but good alternative is 6...b6 7.O-O Bb7 .

7.O-O

We've reached a key position which has occurred thousands of times, and at all levels. Black has quite a choice of options at this point, and we're going to focus on two of them:

(a) 7...dxc4 8.Bxc4 Nbd7 , the Parma Variation, enjoys a reliable reputation and has been utilized at one time or another by most of the top grandmasters who have played the Nimzo-Indian, such as Anand, Karpov, Leko, Adams, Kasparov and Topalov. Understanding the general ideas and tactics is more important than learning specific moves orders, but there are still one or two forcing lines. See Lautier-Carlsen, Wiacek-Coleman, Onischuk-Eljanov and Chamorro Areses-Versili.

(b) 7...Nc6 8.a3 Ba5!? (see Baburin-Kosten) leads to quite different positions. The main continuation is very sharp, and this option might be appealing if you are looking for a more tactical battle in a slightly less theoretical line.

Game 9
Lautier,J - Carlsen,M [E55]
FIDE World Cup, Khanty-Mansiysk, 2005

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3 O-O 5.Bd3 d5 6.Nf3 c5 7.O-O dxc4 8.Bxc4 Nbd7

QUESTION: Why did Carlsen choose to develop the knight to d7? Why not c6?

ANSWER: A few reasons:

1. 8...Nbd7 marks the beginning of Carlsen's queenside development plans. He intends to develop his light-squared bishop on the long diagonal with ...b6 and ...Bb7, and on d7 the knight won't block the bishop's path.

2. Black will want to exert pressure down the c-file with ...Rc8 and/or ...Qc7. If the knight is on d7 it won't block the c-file.

3. The knight adds support to the c5-pawn, giving Black more options in the event of dxc5.

9.Qe2

This is White's most popular try against the Parma Variation. First of all he intends to activate his king's rook on d1. This is clearly a tempting square for the rook: it supports the d4-pawn, indirectly opposes the black queen and sets up active possibilities in the centre such as d4-d5, dxc5 and e3-e4.

9...b6

Carlsen doesn't waste any time delaying the development of the bishop.

10.Rd1

And Lautier carries out his mini-plan. Alternatives for White will be discussed in the next game.

10...cxd4

QUESTION: This exchange in the centre frees White's c1-bishop. Isn't it better, or at least more flexible, to keep the central tension with 10...Bb7 ?

ANSWER: 10...Bb7 is probably the move Black would like to play, but this allows White to demonstrate one of the strengths of the Qe2, Rd1 plan: 11.d5! .

White's idea is that after some exchanges on d5, the bishop on b4 will be left rather out on a limb, hitting thin air. For example, 11...exd5 12.Nxd5 Nxd5 13.Bxd5 Bxd5 14.Rxd5 followed by e3-e4.

The tactical justification behind White's idea is that 11...Bxc3 can be met by the zwischenzug 12.dxe6! when White regains the piece by force and has good chances of emerging with some advantage, e.g. 12...Ba5 13.exd7 Qc7 14.e4 Nxd7 15.Ng5 and again Black's dark-squared bishop is out of the game, L.Polugaevsky-T.Petrosian, Leningrad 1960.

11.exd4

Lautier accepts an isolated queen's pawn (IQP) position and frees the c1-bishop, but the structure is about to change again.

Keeping the symmetry with 11.Nxd4 Bb7 causes Black no real problems and should lead to a level position. For example, 12.Bd2 a6 13.Be1 Qe7 14.a3 Bd6 15.f3 b5 16.Ba2 Rac8 , S.Gligoric-V.Smyslov, Amsterdam 1954.

Offering a pawn with 12.e4!? is sharper, but Black is still fine: 12...Qc8!? ( if 12...Bxc3 13.bxc3 Nxe4 White has the shot 14.Nxe6! ; however, this is still very playable for Black after 14...fxe6 15.Bxe6+ Kh8 16.Rxd7 Qf6 17.Rxb7 Qxe6 intending 18.f3 Qc8 with an unclear position, as indicated by Golubev ) 13.f3 Ne5 14.Bb3 Bxc3 15.bxc3 Ba6! with good counterplay, S.Shankland-I.Farago, Budapest 2009. This is why Black played 12...Qc8 ; blunted by f2-f3 and e3-e4, Black's light-squared bishop moves to a more active diagonal.

11...Bxc3

QUESTION: Why did Carlsen trade on c3 now? Shouldn't Black wait for White to play a2-a3? Can he keep this bishop?

ANSWER: Black often does delay taking on c3 and instead chooses 11...Bb7 , but ...Bxc3 is definitely part of his plans. If Black delays ...Bxc3 for too long, or tries to avoid it completely, he runs the risk of being hit in the centre by d4-d5, as we have already seen.

For example, 12.Bg5 Rc8?! 13.d5! and now 13...Bxc3 14.dxe6! (this idea again) 14...Bxf3 15.gxf3! or 13...exd5 14.Nxd5 Bxd5 15.Bxd5 with Black struggling in both cases. However, Black can – and should – meet 12.Bg5 with 12...Bxc3! 13.bxc3 Qc7 ( or 13...Rc8 ) with similar play to the main game.

QUESTION: So why didn't Carlsen wait one more move before exchanging?

ANSWER: I suspect the reason for Carlsen's decision to take on c3 at this earliest moment was to eliminate all d4-d5 possibilities. That is, after 11...Bb7 White can play 12.d5!? and now:

(a) 12...exd5 13.Nxd5 is again a bit better for White, e.g. 13...Re8 14.Qc2 Nxd5 15.Bxd5 Bxd5 16.Rxd5 Qe7 ( 16...Rc8? 17.Qd1! leaves Black's knight in trouble ) 17.Bg5 f6 18.Rad1 Nf8 19.Be3 and White's pieces are the more active, N.Sedlak-G.Sax, Zalaegerszeg 2010.

(b) 12...Bxc3! 13.dxe6! Bxf3! 14.gxf3! ( if 14.Qxf3 then 14...Ne5! 15.exf7+ Kh8 16.Rxd8 Nxf3+ 17.gxf3 Raxd8 18.bxc3 with an equal position, e.g. 18...Rd7 19.Ba3 Rfxf7 20.Bxf7 Rxf7 when White's extra pawn is counterbalanced by his wreck of a structure, L.Portisch-J.Donner, Hamburg 1965 ) 14...fxe6 15.bxc3 Qc7 16.Ba3!? ( after 16.Bxe6+ Kh8 17.Be3 Nc5 Black again enjoys decent compensation in view of White's ruined pawns ) 16...Nc5 with a complex and roughly equal position, L.Aronian-S.Karjakin, Bilbao 2009.

It seems that 12.d5 isn't quite as effective here as it is in some positions. So in summary, 11...Bb7 is certainly playable as long as Black is intending to take on c3 in most cases anyway and is ready to meet the complications of 12.d5 .

12.bxc3 Qc7

12...Bb7 is also played here, but given that Black normally follows up with ...Qc7 a transposition is likely.

Let's assess the imbalances of the position after 12...Qc7 :

1. Both sides are almost fully developed, and their pieces are harmoniously placed.

2. White enjoys a bit more space, but Black certainly isn't cramped. The trade of one set of minor pieces has helped Black in this respect.

3. White has the bishop pair in a fairly open position, but Black's remaining bishop will be on a very good diagonal.

4. Black has no structural weaknesses.

5. White possesses what's known as an isolated pawn couple (the c3- and d4-pawns).

The final point is a key positional feature of the position. Depending on specifics, the isolated pawn couple could prove to be either a strength or a weakness. The pawn couple controls key central squares, offers White space and might become a dynamic weapon advancing in the centre. On the other hand, the pawns are static weaknesses. The c3-pawn is backward, but when it advances to c4 (creating "hanging pawns") both pawns are vulnerable to some extent.

As a rule of thumb, the more pieces that are exchanged, the more prominent the static weakness of this pawn couple becomes. So in general Black should be happy with piece exchanges, as long as they don't involve concessions.

13.Bb2

QUESTION: This bishop looks very passive on b2. Why did Lautier move it to this square?

ANSWER: The simple reason is that White wants to move his bishop from c4, which at the moment is vulnerable to attack and also blocking the c-pawn, so first he defends the c3-pawn to avoid ...Qxc3. White then intends to play c3-c4 more or less straight away, after which the bishop on b2 becomes more useful. Not only does it protect the d4-pawn, it also exerts indirect pressure on e5 and the bishop's power down the long diagonal may be released at the right time by a d4-d5 advance.

Still, 13.Bb2 is hardly the only option for White. Another is 13.Bd2 . For example, 13...Bb7 14.Bd3 Rfe8 (lining up the possibility of ...e5, which White's next move prevents) 15.Re1 Rac8 16.a4!? .

QUESTION: What's the purpose of 16.a4 ?

ANSWER: White is preparing to play a4-a5 at some moment, the benefits of which are twofold:

1. White eliminates one of his pawn weaknesses; and

2. Black is left with a pawn weakness himself, whether he allows an exchange on b6 or takes on a5. The latter case, usually avoided by Black, results in White's c-pawn becoming passed.

QUESTION: How should Black react?

ANSWER: There's not a great deal Black can do to directly prevent a4-a5, but he can focus on his own positional goals. As mentioned earlier, Black's position tends to improve with piece exchanges, because these reduce the dynamism in White's position and emphasize the static weaknesses.

EXERCISE: Look at the position after 16.a4 and try to find two different plans for Black seeking to force a favourable exchange of minor pieces.

Black could just play 16...Bxf3 here, but that would be a mistake because it accepts too many concessions: Black trades his strong bishop, leaves White with two bishops versus two knights and is left with many light-squared weaknesses.

ANSWER: Black can play either 16...Bd5 or 16...Nd5 . Let's take a look at these ideas in turn:


(a) The ...Bd5 plan:

Black's plan after 16...Bd5 is to play ...Bc4! forcing an exchange of light-squared bishops. Even though Black's bishop is a good piece, he is usually very happy to trade it for its counterpart which is probably even stronger given that it forms one half of a bishop pair. More specifically, if Black can exchange bishops it gives him the opportunity to dominate and occupy the light squares in the centre with moves such as ...Qc4 and ...Nd5, piling up on the c3-pawn and seizing the advantage.

In a few games White has been sufficiently wary of this promising plan and has changed the dynamics of the position with the sharp 17.Ne5! (preventing ...Bc4 for the moment) 17...Nxe5 18.dxe5 Nd7 19.Qh5 .

The game has moved on with Ne5. White still suffers from static weaknesses (we can add e5 to the list now), but at the moment Black's pressing concern is how to deal with White's initiative on the kingside. This is a type of situation Black could easily face in the Parma Variation, though he will often try to avoid it.

EXERCISE (multiple choice): How should Black deal with the attack on h7?
The options are: (a) 19...Nf8 ; (b) 19...g6 ; (c) 19...h6 ; (d) 19...f5 .

ANSWER: The first thing to note is that 19...h6? , inviting the obvious sacrifice 20.Bxh6! , is usually very risky and here 20...gxh6 21.Qxh6 Nf8 22.Re3 Ng6 23.h4 gives White a very strong attack.

19...g6 leaves Black's kingside full of dark-square holes which are very difficult to defend. T.Hillarp Persson-C.Ionescu, Batumi 1999, continued 20.Qh4 Bc4 ( if 20...Nxe5 then 21.Bf4 is an awkward pin ) 21.Bc2 Qb8? 22.Bg5 Bd5? , and here White could have won immediately with 23.Qh6! (threatening Bf6) 23...Kh8 24.Re3 Rg8 25.Qxh7+! Kxh7 26.Rh3+ Kg7 27.Bh6+ Kh8 28.Bf8 mate. Black did defend poorly in this game, but 19...g6 doesn't really look very appealing to me.

19...Nf8 is the move Black often relies on in this type of position because it allows him to defend h7 without incurring any weaknesses. This move looks better than both 19...h6 and 19...g6 , although with 20.Re3! intending Rh3 or Rg3 White can keep his initiative going on the kingside.

19...f5! might well be the best move. After 20.exf6 Nxf6 21.Qh4 Black has accepted a weakness of his own (the e6-pawn) but crucially he is able to "defend actively", for want of a better phrase. After 21...Bc4 ( 21...Qd8!? ) 22.Bc2 Qf7 23.Bg5 Bd5 24.Re3 ( 24.Bxf6 gxf6 25.Re3 might be more testing ) 24...Rc4! Black had good counterplay in R.Henley-L.Evans, New York 1977.

In conclusion, Black's position is not that easy to play after 19.Qh5 , but 19...Nf8 and particularly 19...f5 offer the best chances.


(b) The ...Nd5 plan:

This move is a vital resource for Black in the Parma Variation. In this particular position, with 16...Nd5!? Black threatens to win a pawn with ...Nxc3 or to force a favourable trade of one of White's bishops with ...Nf4.

Here 17.c4 Nf4! 18.Bxf4 Qxf4 is just the type of position Black is aiming for. The exchange of minor pieces has dented White's ambitions and left only the worry of how to adequately protect his hanging pawns.

White can try to mix things up with 17.Ne5 Nxe5 18.dxe5 but Black gains quick and strong counterplay down the central files here; for example, 18...Red8 ( or even just 18...Nxc3 ) 19.c4 Qc6! , and if 20.cxd5 Qxd5 21.Be4 then 21...Qxe4 22.Qxe4 Bxe4 leaves Black a pawn ahead.

17.Qe4 also prevents both threats. Black should reply with either 17...Nf8 or 17...N5f6 18.Qh4 e5! with a decent position, but he should avoid 17...N7f6 18.Qh4! Nxc3? 19.Rec1 .

In conclusion, out of the two approaches, in this particularly line I feel that 16...Nd5 is safer and more promising than 16...Bd5 .

After that long discussion on Black's general strategy and positional aims, let's return to the game and the position after Lautier's 13.Bb2 .

13...Bb7 14.Bd3

Lautier removes the bishop from the vulnerable c4-square and prepares to play c3-c4. After 14.Rac1?! , for example, White must be willing to allow his kingside pawns to be smashed by 14...Bxf3 .

In fact after 14.Rac1 White should be more worried by 14...Ng4! .

This is a key tactical idea to remember, as it works very well in certain situations. If 15.h3? Black can win a pawn with 15...Bxf3 16.Qxf3 Nxf2! .

Against 14.Bb3 Black could consider 14...Qf4!? followed by ...Rac8 – the queen is quite well placed on f4 and provides useful nuisance value.

QUESTION: What about the 14...Ng4 idea in answer to 14.Bb3 ?

ANSWER: Sometimes the ...Ng4 idea is ineffective because White can simply invite the check on h2, and this is one of those occasions. After 15.h3! Bxf3 16.Qxf3 Qh2+ 17.Kf1 Ngf6 Black can't do anything with his queen and he has achieved very little in giving up bishop for knight.

In general, ...Ng4 ideas are more likely to work if the bishop is still on c4; or if the rook is still on f1, when ...Qh2 is checkmate rather than just check.

Let's get back to the game and the position after 14.Bd3 :

14...Nd5!

A strong move by Carlsen. He actively seeks an exchange with ...Nf4, after which he would stand at least equal.

QUESTION: How about 14...Ng4 in this position?

ANSWER: It's even worse here, due to the tactic 15.Bxh7+! Kxh7 16.Ng5+ .

As a general point, Black has to be careful not to become too passive. The following game serves as a warning as to what might happen if he does, and a reminder of White's attacking chances: 14...Rfe8 15.c4! (it's too late for ...Nd5!) 15...Rac8 16.Rac1 Nf8 (...Nd7-f8-g6, eyeing f4 again, is a typical manoeuvre, but White responds energetically...) 17.Ne5! Ng6 18.Qe3 Nxe5 19.dxe5 Qc6 20.Qh3! Ne4 ( 20...Nh5! looks better, as 21.Bxh7+ Kxh7 22.Qxh5+ Kg8 is far from clear ) 21.Re1 Ng5 22.Qg4 h6 23.Re3 (now White's attack runs very smoothly) 23...Rc7 24.h4 Nh7 25.Rg3 g6 26.h5 g5 27.f4 Qc5+ 28.Kh2 Qe7 29.Ba3! f5 30.exf6 Qxf6 31.Rf1 Rg7 32.fxg5 Qxg5 33.Bg6! Qe5 34.Bf7+ Kh8 35.Qxg7+ and Black was forced to resign in S.Yuferov-V.Zilberstein, USSR 1969.

Returning to the game after Carlsen's 14...Nd5! :

15.Qe4!

Lautier defends against both ...Nf4 and ...Nxc3 for the moment by threatening mate on h7.

If 15.c4?! Nf4 16.Qe3 Nxd3 17.Qxd3 Bxf3 18.gxf3 Rac8 Black's position is looking very good.

15...N7f6 16.Qe5

Lautier feels obliged to offer an exchange of queens.

An earlier game, B.Melander-K.Tikkanen, correspondence 1986, went 16.Qh4 Nf4! ( again Black must avoid 16...Nxc3 17.Rdc1 ) 17.Bf1 Bxf3! (this exchange is obviously more appealing now that it smashes White's kingside pawn structure, and even more so because Black can immediately take advantage of this) 18.gxf3 N6h5! 19.Re1 f5! 20.Bc1 Rf6 and out of seemingly nowhere Black had a strong attack. The game continued 21.Bxf4 Nxf4 22.Kh1 Ng6 23.Qg3 Qxc3 24.Rac1 Qxd4 and Black won.

16...Rac8 17.c4 Nf4! 18.Bf1 Bxf3

Carlsen chooses the same unbalancing exchange. Unlike the previous note, the queens are exchanged and Black doesn't build up a quick attack on the kingside. However, Carlsen still manages to achieve significant play with his knights.

19.gxf3 Rfd8 20.Qxc7 Rxc7 21.a4!

21.a4! is a good move by Lautier. White must seek action on the queenside to counterbalance Black's play on the other wing.

21...h6!

Planning ...Nh7, and then either ...Ng5 or ...Nf8-g6-h4, in either case helping Black's attack on the kingside.

Preventing Ra8.The battle is hotting up. White will likely win the b6-pawn, but once the knight on f8 is fully activated Black's knight pair will cause chaos.

Carlsen presents Lautier, who was perhaps in time trouble, with a difficult decision to make.

30.Ba6?

Lautier must have been worried about Black's activity after 30.dxe5 Rxc5 but this is what he had to try.

ANSWER:

By ignoring it. Black has his own, more serious threats, i.e. checkmate! 31.Bxc8 Nde2+ 32.Kf1 Rd1 mate, or 31.Bxd4 Rxd4 32.Bxc8 Rd1 mate.

Wiacek,K - Coleman,P [E55]
Correspondence, 2007

We saw the idea of d4-d5 more than once in the notes to the previous game, with the general feeling being that Black should try to take steps against this plan. Here we'll see what happens if White plays it straight away after 9...b6.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3 O-O 5.Bd3 d5 6.Nf3 c5 7.O-O dxc4 8.Bxc4 Nbd7 9.Qe2 b6 10.d5!?

This move leads to very sharp positions where knowledge of a few critical variations is required. Don't worry though. Current theory suggests that Black is fine, and indeed few White players seem willing to travel down this line – perhaps because Black sacrifices a pawn and gets quite a lot of play in return.

One other move worth mentioning here is 10.dxc5 which until recently was considered completely toothless in view of 10...Bxc3! 11.bxc3 Nxc5 .

The idea of answering dxc5 with ...Bxc3 is worth keeping in mind, as it crops up so often in the Nimzo-Indian. The position after 11...Nxc5 is at least equal for Black, for these reasons:

1. The knight is firmly entrenched on an excellent outpost on c5;

2. White's queenside pawns are split; and

3. Black's pieces will be well coordinated after ...Bb7.

The reason for some recent interest in 10.dxc5 is the discovery, after 10...Bxc3 , of the little in-between move 11.c6! – a theme of attacking the d7-knight we've already seen in a similar guise with d5xe6. This isn't particularly scary for Black; it's just a much better idea for White than allowing 11.bxc3 Nxc5 .

The game A.Grischuk-S.Grigoriants, Mainz (rapid) 2009, continued 11...Bb4 12.cxd7 Qxd7 (the bishop wants to go to b7) 13.a3 Be7 14.e4!? Bb7 15.e5 Nd5 16.Rd1 Rfd8 17.Rd4 Rac8 with a roughly equal position. White will try to use his extra space on the kingside to build up an attack, but Black's pieces – especially the knight on d5 – are well positioned for defence and counterattack.

For White's main choice, 10.Rd1 , see the previous game.

10...Bxc3!

Black should avoid 10...exd5 11.Nxd5 which, as we saw in similar examples in the previous game, leaves the bishop on b4 awkwardly placed.

11.dxe6!

Once again this in-between capture is the tactical justification behind White's d5 advance.

11...Ne5!

A key move: Black is willing to sacrifice a pawn or two in order to seize the initiative.

QUESTION: What about 11...Ba5 here?

ANSWER: Again the bishop on a5 isn't ideally placed but this does look playable for Black – at least it's more favourable than 10...exd5 11.Nxd5 . For example, 12.exd7 Qxd7 (the bishop wants to go to b7) 13.Rd1 Qe7 14.e4! (this is possible since taking on e4 allows tactics with Bg5 or Bd5) 14...Bg4 is a decent alternative to the text if Black for some reason doesn't fancy the main line.

12.exf7+

White's d-pawn continues its demolition act.

12.bxc3 avoids the complications but 12...Nxf3+ 13.Qxf3 Bxe6 14.Bxe6 fxe6 gives Black no problems at all.

12...Kh8

QUESTION: Why not 12...Nxf7 ?

ANSWER: Black plans to capture on f7 at some point, but at the moment initiative is everything. 12...Nxf7? loses a lot of momentum and after 13.bxc3 Black has no compensation for the pawn.

13.bxc3 Bg4!

EXERCISE: Take a good look at this position and try to find White's best move here. (Hint: Look at all of White's pieces.)

13...Bg4 is a vital move for Black. The awkward pin on the f3-knight, coupled with the possibility of smashing White's kingside pawn cover, constitutes a major part of Black's compensation for his two-pawn investment.

ANSWER:

14.e4!

White absolutely must get his c1-bishop into the game to help with defence and 14.e4 is the only way to do so.

Kilkenny Masters, 2008

In this final game of the chapter we'll look at an alternative for Black to the Parma Variation.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3 O-O 5.Bd3 d5 6.Nf3 c5 7.O-O

7.a3 Bxc3+ 8.bxc3 dxc4 9.Bxc4 reaches a position discussed in the next chapter (Conquest-Naiditsch (Game 15)).

7...Nc6 8.a3

This is by far the most common choice for White. Other moves are playable of course, but from a theoretical viewpoint they are considered to be fairly harmless.

For example, 8.dxc5 Bxc5 9.a3 a6 10.b4 Bd6 11.cxd5 exd5 is an active IQP position for Black in which White would probably prefer to have his bishop on e2 – as a measure against ...Bg4 – rather than d3.

8.cxd5 exd5 and only now 9.a3 doesn't help White. For a start Black can transpose to the main game with 9...Ba5 , but he can also exploit the pawn exchange in the centre by playing 9...Bxc3 10.bxc3 Bg4! with a fine position.

8...Ba5!?

This was called "the Reykjavik Variation" by Gligoric because it featured in the first game of the famous 1972 Fischer-Spassky World Championship match. Previously, though, it had already been employed by players such as Tal, Petrosian and Smyslov – not a bad line-up! I feel that it's quite a good secondary line to include as a back-up to the Parma Variation. It's tricky, not overly theoretical – there's only one critical line, where Black sacrifice a pawn for the initiative – and it could easily catch some opponents off guard.

QUESTION: What is Black's idea with 8...Ba5 ?

ANSWER: There are a few points behind 8...Ba5 :

1. Instead of clarifying the situation, which is what the main line does to some extent with 8...Bxc3 9.bxc3 , Black wants to keep the tension for at least one further move.

2. By refusing to capture on c3 Black avoids strengthening White centre, and in particular the d4-pawn.

3. It's not easy for White to develop freely without releasing the tension himself. For example, 9.Qe2 or 9.Qc2 would allow Black to take on c4 and then on d4.

4. If given another move, Black might take on c4 and d4 to give White an IQP. By playing ...Ba5, Black creates the possibility to attack the d4-pawn with ...Bb6.Of course you may have noticed that 8...Ba5 does leave the c5-pawn unprotected, and accepting this offer is seen as the critical test of this line.

9.cxd5!

Baburin heads straight down the sharp main line.

QUESTION: What happens if White takes on c5 immediately?

ANSWER: On 9.dxc5 Black's bishop changes its mind and captures on c3 after all: 9...Bxc3! 10.bxc3 .

White is a pawn ahead at the moment, but his queenside pawns are so unhealthy this is unlikely to last for long. One example was 10...e5 11.cxd5 Qxd5 12.c4 Qxc5 13.Qc2 Rd8 14.Be2 Bg4 and Black was better in A.Iljushin-L.Aronian, Sochi 2005.

QUESTION: What happens if White doesn't release the tension in the centre?

ANSWER: In this case it's usually Black who releases the tension, and an IQP position arises. Some examples:

(a) If 9.Na4 it makes sense for Black to head for an IQP position, since a4 is a strange place for the white knight in such positions – c3 is usually the best square for it: 9...cxd4! 10.exd4 dxc4 11.Bxc4 Ne7 12.Qe2 Ng6 13.Nc3 (the knight was doing nothing on a4, so it drops back to its normal square) 13...Bxc3! (reaching the structure we saw in Lautier-Carlsen) 14.bxc3 b6 with a comfortable position for Black, D.Khismatullin-A.Lastin, Olginka 2011.

It's worth seeing a few more moves, as it soon gets better: 15.Bb3?! Bb7 16.h3?! Rc8 17.Bd2 Ne4! (Remember: exchanges improve Black's position!) 18.Qe3 Nxd2 19.Nxd2 Nh4! 20.Ne4?! Nf5 21.Qd3 Bxe4 22.Qxe4 Rxc3 and Black won.

(b) 9.Ne2 was Spassky's choice against Fischer. Fischer's response was fine but heading for an IQP is an equally viable option for Black, for similar reasons to those after 9.Na4 : 9...dxc4 10.Bxc4 cxd4 ( 10...Bb6 11.dxc5 Qxd1 12.Rxd1 Bxc5 13.b4 Be7 14.Bb2 Bd7! 15.Rac1 ( if 15.Bxf6 Bxf6 16.Rxd7?? the rook hangs on a1 ) 15...Rfd8 was equal in the Spassky-Fischer game) 11.exd4 h6 (preventing Bg5) 12.Bf4 Bd7 13.Rc1 Ne7 14.Ne5 Rc8 15.Qd3 Bc6 16.Nxc6 Rxc6 17.Qf3 Nfd5 18.Be5 Bc7 with a very solid position, A.Onischuk-R.Hess, US Championship, Saint Louis 2009.

(c) 9.h3!? has been played quite a few times.

QUESTION: This move looks very strange, almost irrelevant? What is White doing?

ANSWER: 9.h3 is a clever move, with a number of points:

1. Black's major resource in our main game, ...Bg4, is ruled out. So White is most definitely intending to take on d5 and c5 if Black does nothing to prevent it.

2. If Black plays ...Qe7 to defend c5, some of the pressure on the d4-pawn is lifted and this gives White to opportunity to improve his position with Qe2 and Rd1.

3. Black can – and probably should – head for an IQP position where h2-h3 has little value, but at least White is comforted by the fact that his queen's knight hasn't been lured from the c3-square.

Here's an example of the IQP position: 9...dxc4 10.Bxc4 h6!? (preventing Bg5 after the exchange of pawns on d4) 11.Qd3! ( if 11.dxc5 then 11...Bxc3! 12.bxc3 Qa5 ) 11...cxd4 12.exd4 Bc7 13.Re1 Ne7 ( 13...a6 14.Ba2 Qd6 and ...Rd8 is an alternative way to play it ) 14.Bd2 b6 15.Rad1 Bb7 16.Ne5 a6 17.Bc1 Nfd5 with a typical IQP battle raging in S.Del Rio de Angelis-M.Adams, Spanish League 2009. Play continued 18.Ba2 Rc8 19.Bb1 f5!? (this advance leaves weaknesses behind, but it's not an uncommon resource for Black and here he is compensated by his active pieces) 20.Ba2 b5 with a roughly equal position.

11.bxc3 Bg4!

QUESTION: Why doesn't Black just regain the pawn like in Iljushin-Aronian above? He can do this with 11...Qa5 , can't he?

ANSWER: 11...Qa5 does regain the pawn, but it's quite a bit different to Iljushin-Aronian:

1. Black's centre pawn is on d5 rather than e5 which favours White, who gains a solid outpost on d4.

2. In the Iljushin-Aronian game Black gained quick counterplay with a rook on the d-file and the threat of ...e4. He lacks that counterplay here.

Together, these points add up to a small advantage for White. For example, 12.Qc2 Qxc5 13.a4! (threatening Ba3) 13...Re8 14.Ba3 Qa5 15.Rab1 Qc7 16.c4 , J.Timman-J.Kaplan, Amsterdam 1974.

Of course regaining the pawn on c5 remains a key idea for Black, especially if White reacts to 11...Bg4 passively...

Key Notes

1. In the main line of the Parma Variation White adopts a plan of 9.Qe2 and 10.Rd1. We normally reach positions where White has an isolated pawn couple on c3 and d4. Lautier-Carlsen (Game 9) covers various plans and tactics for both sides in this type of position.

2. Players should be aware of early d5 advances by White in a number of positions, usually as a response to ...b6. See Lautier-Carlsen, Wiacek-Coleman and Chamorro Areses-Versili for various d5 possibilities.

3. In the isolated pawn couple positions, Black occasionally has the opportunity to punish inaccurate play by White with the tactic ...Ng4, with threats against h2 (see the notes to Lautier-Carlsen).

4. The sharpest line of the Parma Variation is 9.Qe2 b6 10.d5!? (Wiacek-Coleman). Black certainly needs to remember a few move sequences here, but the good news is that theory suggests this line is fine for Black. Furthermore, it looks more fun to play as Black than as White.

5. The main alternative to 9.Qe2 is 9.a3, which leads to a different pawn structure and subtle positional play. Eljanov's treatment of the position in Onischuk-Eljanov is a good model for Black.

6. 9.Qb3 has similar motives to 9.Qe2. Differences between the two moves are highlighted in Chamorro Areses-Versili.

7. The Reykjavik Variation (Baburin-Kosten) is a good back-up option for Black, especially for those seeking a more tactical battle in a slightly less theoretical line.
