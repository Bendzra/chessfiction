Chapter Eight. Kasparov Variation: 4.Nf3

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Nf3 c5 5.g3

This variation is named after Garry Kasparov because he employed it in his world championship matches against arch-rival Anatoly Karpov. It led to a surge in popularity in a line which had previously been considered quite harmless.

The kingside fianchetto doesn't put Black's position under any immediate pressure at all, and this means he enjoys a host a playable options. However, White does hope that the presence of the fianchettoed bishop on the long diagonal will help to create some long-term pressure, and unusually for the Nimzo-Indian this tends to occur on the queenside. In this respect the Kasparov Variation is similar to other d4/c4/g3 openings, for example the Catalan.

Going back a move, 4...c5 is Black's most popular response to 4.Nf3 and it's the one I've chosen to focus on in this book. It's also the move that fits in best with other lines covered here (in contrast 4...b6 , because of the transpositional possibilities, would fit well if Black wanted to play 4.e3 b6 and 4.Qc2 O-O 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.Qxc3 b6 ).

QUESTION: So what happens if White answers 4...c5 with 5.e3 ?

ANSWER: Then Black is well positioned to transpose to the main lines of the Rubinstein, for example with 5...d5 6.Bd3 O-O or 5...O-O 6.Bd3 d5 . Although 5.g3 is the most popular response to 4...c5 , 5.e3 is also quite common so there is some chance of this transposition occurring.

QUESTION: 5.d5 looks like a logical move. What does Black play against this?

ANSWER: 5.d5 is actually quite rare which I find surprising because it's not a bad move, especially if White plans to answer 5...d6 with 6.Bg5 which could transpose to the previous chapter after 6...h6 7.Bh4 Bxc3+ 8.bxc3 e5 (see the note to White's 8th move in Stocek- Eljanov).

5.d5 d6 6.Bd2 is also possible but looks fairly harmless after 6...O-O 7.e3 Bxc3 8.Bxc3 Ne4 9.dxe6 Bxe6 10.Qc2 Nxc3 11.Qxc3 Nc6 12.Be2 Qe7 13.O-O Rfd8 14.Rad1 d5 , L.Szabo-S.Reshevsky, Dallas 1957, or 7...exd5 8.Nxd5 Nxd5 9.cxd5 Bxd2+ 10.Nxd2 Nd7 11.Be2 Nf6 12.Bf3 b5 , L.Pachman-R.Kholmov, Havana 1965; with level chances in both cases.

QUESTION: Can White play a delayed Saemisch with 5.a3 ?

ANSWER: 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 is also rare, as Saemisch players don't like to have their knight on f3, for good reason as we've seen earlier in the book.

Black has quite a few options to reach a good position here, including 6...Nc6 7.Qc2! (to play e4 in one go) 7...O-O 8.e4 d5! 9.e5 Ne4 (this is a favourable version of the 4.Qc2 O-O 5.e4 line, in that White has played an early Nf3) 10.Bd3 cxd4 11.cxd4 Qa5+ 12.Bd2 Nxd2 13.Qxd2 dxc4 14.Bxc4 b6 15.Qxa5 Nxa5 16.Ba2 Bb7 17.Ke2 Rfd8 , I.De la Calle-J.Schmenger, correspondence 2006; or 6...Nc6 7.Qc2 d6 8.e4 e5 9.d5 Ne7 and ...Ng6.

Finally, Black also has more than one good reply to 5.Bg5 . Perhaps the most thematic is 5...Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Qa5! intending to meet 7.Qc2 with 7...Ne4! and Black already has a fairly comfortable position. For example, 8.Bd2 Nxd2 ( 8...f5!? ) 9.Qxd2 O-O 10.e4 d6 11.Be2 Nc6 12.Rd1 e5 and Black was better in A.Linares-B.Lalic, Coria del Rio 2006.

After that discussion on alternatives, let's return to the Kasparov Variation and the position after 5.g3 :

5...Bxc3+!? 6.bxc3 Qa5

In this chapter I've decided to cover something slightly more offbeat. This is partly because the lack of immediate pressure on Black's position means there are many playable lines against the Kasparov Variation, not just the main line. Also, the positions reached after 6...Qa5 have a strong Nimzo-Indian feel to them. With Black inflicting White with the doubled pawns and fighting for light-square control, it's quite similar to the Saemisch; whereas in the main lines the c4-pawn is exchanged very quickly and it's more like a Symmetrical English (indeed it can transpose from that opening). Finally, I'm not totally convinced the current main line is that much fun for Black. It has become very theoretical (I know, "main line"!) and although Black is very solid, more solid than in other lines, the positions are still in-between "equal" and "slightly better for White".

For example, if we travel deep into the main line we get 5...cxd4 6.Nxd4 O-O 7.Bg2 d5 8.cxd5 Nxd5 9.Qb3 Nc6 10.Nxc6 bxc6 11.O-O Qa5 12.Bd2 Bxc3 13.bxc3 Ba6 14.Rfd1 Qc5 15.e4 Bc4 16.Qa4 Nb6 17.Qb4 Qh5 and White tries to make something of his two-bishop advantage.

Those wanting to play this line as White should check out the Kramnik-Anand game from their 2008 World Championship match - a classic positional squeeze from the master of squeezing!

Returning to 5...Bxc3+!? 6.bxc3 Qa5 , even my description of "offbeat" might be unfair here. Although I could find less than 100 games reaching this position, 5...Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Qa5 has been played by the likes of Kramnik, Korchnoi, Topalov, Ponomariov, Timman and numerous 2600-rated grandmasters. My feeling was that if it's good enough for world champions to play it's certainly good enough for us to study!

Game 38
Lenderman,A - Kacheishvili,G [E20]
Copper State Open, Mesa, 2009

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Nf3

QUESTION: What happens if White plays the immediate 4.g3 ?

ANSWER: If 4.g3 Black can still play 4...c5 because White's best move here is 5.Nf3 protecting the d4-pawn and transposing to the main line.

4...c5 5.g3 Bxc3+

QUESTION: Why does Black take on c3 so early without any provocation from a2-a3?

ANSWER: Black wants to fix the structure before he plays ...Qa5. The problem with the immediate 5...Qa5 is that after 6.Bd2! an exchange on c3 is no longer desirable for obvious reasons, so Black seizes the opportunity now.

6.bxc3 Qa5 7.Qd3

EXERCISE: Take a good look at this position, devise a plan of development for Black and decide on the first move.

7.Qd3 is one of White's main options to protect the attacked c3-pawn. We will discuss alternatives later on.

ANSWER:

7...b6!

Black's bishop belongs on the long diagonal, opposing its counterpart. Going back a couple of moves, there's a similar line Black can play with 5...b6 6.Bg2 Bb7 7.O-O Bxc3 8.bxc3 but in our line Black aims to use his queen more actively.

QUESTION: Why didn't Black play 7...cxd4 ? Because of the pin White can't recapture with the c-pawn and is left with doubled isolated pawns. Wasn't that the idea behind 6...Qa5 ?

ANSWER: No it wasn't. In the Kasparov Variation White is often willing to accept this chronic pawn weakness if the position is opened up for his bishops. After 8.Nxd4 O-O 9.Bg2 Nc6 10.Nb3 Qh5 11.Ba3! , I hope you can see what I mean.

Both bishops are on impressive diagonals, White is ahead in development and he dominates some dark squares. This is something Black should try to avoid, and the best way to do so is to maintain the tension in the centre.

QUESTION: Could Black play 7...Ne4 , using tactics to attack the c3-pawn again?

ANSWER: This has been played a few times, including once by Korchnoi. If White is forced to play 8.Bd2 then Black's move is justified. However, it seems that White can give up the c3-pawn by playing 8.Bg2! Qxc3+ 9.Qxc3 Nxc3 10.dxc5 with good chances for an edge. For example, 10...Na4 11.Nd4 Nc6 12.Nb5 O-O 13.Ba3 with typical queenside pressure, Y.Kuzubov-Z.Efimenko, Port Erin 2006.

8.Bg2 Bb7 9.O-O

EXERCISE: This is already a critical moment in the game, with Black facing a decision: Should he play 9...O-O or 9...Be4 ?

ANSWER:

9...Be4!

The e4-square is often a good place for this bishop, because

1. The bishop is protected by the knight, thus making it less vulnerable to tactics on the long diagonal; and

2. Black is no longer susceptible to d4-d5 ideas trying to block the bishop out of the game.

It's vital that Black plays this move here, and of course it helps that it gains time by attacking the queen. The problem with delaying ...Be4 in favour of 9...O-O?! is that 10.Bg5! is a strong reply and an awkward one for Black to meet. He doesn't want to have his kingside structure compromised after castling there, so 10...Ne4 makes sense. The trouble is that 11.Nd2! utilizes one of those long diagonal tactics I mentioned above and Black is in a spot of trouble.

10.Qe3

With this move the queen keeps an eye on the bishop, but Bg5 is no longer a concern for Black.

Another recent grandmaster clash went 10.Qd1!? h6 ( to prevent Bg5, although other moves – 10...Nc6 or 10...Qxc3 – are certainly possible ) 11.dxc5 O-O!? 12.cxb6 axb6 (Black has good compensation because his pieces are active and White's queenside pawns are terrible) 13.Be3 Nc6 14.Bd4 e5 15.Be3 Rfc8 16.Qc1 d5 17.cxd5 Nxd5 18.Bd2 Qa4 19.Qd1 Bc2 20.Qc1 Be4 21.Qd1 Bc2 1/2-1/2 P.Harikrishna-R.Hess, World Open, King of Prussia 2010.

10...O-O

QUESTION: What should White be trying to achieve here? Should he be looking to force the exchange of the light-squared bishops?

Only now, with Bg5 no longer a worry, does Kacheishvili castle kingside.

11.Nd2?!

ANSWER: Exchanging light-squared bishops is the plan which involves the least amount of hassle, and thus the one that many would choose. But the good news for Black is that it's not the right plan! After the exchange of bishops White's position, devoid of the bishop pair, loses much of its potential dynamism. Furthermore, as we've seen previously in this book, the exchange of light-squared bishops often helps Black in his aim to control the light squares and attack the c4-pawn/square.

A more ambitious plan for White, and indeed one which is now standard for this type of position, is to actively avoid the exchange by vacating the long diagonal with Bh3 (or Bf1) and then force the bishop from its post with Nd2 or Ng5. This might seem like a long-winded plan but it's often quite effective.

The problem for White in this particular position is that his queen is not well placed on e3 to support this plan, and after 11.Bh3 Black has a number of good options, including 11...Nc6 12.Nd2 Bg6 ( 12...Bc2!? ) 13.dxc5 bxc5 14.Nb3 Qa4 15.Qxc5 Rfb8 with good compensation or even 11...cxd4!? 12.cxd4 Qh5! .

In this position something more creative is required to challenge Black, and that could be 11.g4!? .

The pawn lunge threatens to win material with g4-g5 and provides some justification for the queen being on e3. Here are two examples:

(a) 11...Bb7 12.g5 Ne8 ( 12...Nh5!? ) 13.d5!? exd5 14.cxd5 Bxd5 15.Rd1 Bc6 16.c4 with decent compensation for the pawn, G.Battaglini-S.Fedorchuk, French League 2011.

(b) 11...d5 (this looks more natural) 12.g5 Nfd7 13.cxd5 Bxd5 ( 13...exd5!? ) 14.c4 ( 14.Qd3!? ) 14...cxd4 ( 14...Bxc4!? ) 15.Qxd4 ( 15.Qa3!? ) 15...Nc6 16.Qh4 Bxf3! 17.exf3 ( 17.Bxf3 Nde5 18.Be4 Ng6 19.Qg3 Rac8 is good for Black ) 17...Rac8 18.Rd1 Nce5 , E.Ubilava-C.Bauer, San Sebastian 2009. Black is doing well in this position, but clearly there's some scope for other possibilities earlier on for both sides. In any case 11.g4!? certainly doesn't allow Black to play on a Nimzo-Indian auto-pilot mode.

Let's return to the position in the game after Lenderman's 11.Nd2 :

11...Bxg2 12.Kxg2

Not that he has a choice in the matter, but Black didn't hesitate to get those bishops off the board. This is more or less a dream position for most Nimzo-Indian players.

12...d6

12...Nc6!? is also good, as after 13.dxc5 Ng4!? 14.Qf4 Nge5 15.cxb6 axb6 Black again enjoys excellent compensation for the pawn.

13.Ne4 Nxe4 14.Qxe4 Nd7

Kacheishvili has completed his development and can now focus on White's weaknesses.

15.Bf4! e5 16.dxe5 dxe5 17.Bg5!

EXERCISE: Try to find a way forward for Black here.

Lenderman works hard to make things as difficult for Black as possible. 17.Bxe5? would fail to 17...Rae8! 18.f4 Nxe5 19.fxe5 Qxc3 .

ANSWER:

17...Qa4

It's vital for Black to coordinate his pieces and deal with any threats, as White's queenside pawn weaknesses will not go away. The queen is well placed on a4 where she both defends (the knight on d7) and attacks (a2 and c4).

17...Qxc3? would be careless, allowing 18.Rad1 when the knight cannot be defended and doesn't have a decent square to go to. After 18...Nf6 19.Bxf6 gxf6 20.Rd6! it's suddenly White who is better. However, 17...Rfe8! intending to meet 18.Rad1 with the calm 18...Nf8 is another good option.

18.Rad1 Rfe8

To give the knight the f8-square, just as in the note above.

19.h4! Nf8 20.h5 h6

EXERCISE: Try to assess the piece sacrifice 21.Bxh6 .

21.Be3?

Lenderman has played with great energy to distract Black from his winning plan of taking the queenside pawns. Unfortunately for White, in this type of position he needs to seize on any tactical opportunity because once Black consolidates completely there won't be any doubt about the final result.

ANSWER: It looks like White can force a draw here with 21.Bxh6! gxh6 22.Rd6! intending 22...Ne6 23.Rxe6! fxe6 24.Qg6+ Kf8 25.Qf6+ Kg8 26.Qg6+ with perpetual. Assuming Black is trying to win, he could avoid this scenario with either 20...Ne6 or, earlier, 17...Rfe8 .

21...Rad8!

Now there is nothing in the position apart from White's weaknesses, and Kacheishvili begins to gain complete control.

22.Rxd8 Rxd8 23.Qg4 Nh7!

This is the final nail in the coffin for White's initiative. The knight is coming to f6, and 24.Bxh6 – the move which should have been played three moves ago – simply loses here to 24...Qc6+ .

24.f3 Nf6 25.Qf5 Qxc4

Finally a pawn drops, and the end comes fairly quickly.

26.Bxh6 Qxe2+ 27.Rf2 Qd3 28.Qxe5 Re8 29.Qf4 Qxc3 30.Qg5 Nh7 31.Qg4 Qe5 32.Bf4? Nf6!

0-1

33.Bxe5 Nxg4 34.fxg4 Rxe5 is an easily winning rook ending for Black. I really like this game and feel it is instructive for Nimzo-Indian players. Lenderman fought extremely hard to drum up some chances on the kingside, but the reality is that Kacheishvili held all the trump cards after the exchange of light-squared bishops.

Game 39
Neelotpal,D - Adhiban,B [E20]
Asian Continental Ch., Olongapo City, 2010

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Nf3 c5 5.g3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Qa5 7.Bd2

This is the other main way to defend the c3-pawn.

7...b6

Just as against 7.Qd3 , ...b6 is the natural response. However, Topalov has used a slightly different light-square strategy here, and this is well worth considering as an alternative: 7...O-O 8.Bg2 Nc6 9.O-O Qa6! (we've already seen the idea of ...Qa4 but the queen can also use the a6-square to attack c4) 10.Ne5! d5!? (typical Nimzo-Indian strategy: Topalov revises his aim and seeks to control the c4-square rather than win the c4-pawn) 11.Qc2 Re8 12.Bf4 cxd4 13.Nxc6 Qxc6 14.cxd4 b6! 15.Rfc1 Ba6 16.cxd5 Qxc2 17.Rxc2 Nxd5 18.Bd6 with a very small edge for White, E.Bacrot-V.Topalov, Sofia 2006, but soon drawn after 18...Rac8 19.Rac1 Rxc2 20.Rxc2 Rd8 21.Ba3 f5! 22.e3 Rc8 23.Rxc8+ Bxc8 24.Bxd5 (if White wants to retain any winning chances at all in this ending he must keep the two bishops and refrain from this trade) 24...exd5 25.Bd6 Kf7 26.Bb8 a6 27.Bc7 b5 28.a3 g5 29.f4 g4 1/2-1/2.

8.Bg2 Bb7 9.O-O O-O 10.Qc2

EXERCISE: Find a good move for Black.

With 10.Qc2 Neelotpal protects the c3-pawn a second time, freeing up the d2-bishop.

ANSWER:

10...Be4!

After seeing the previous game, hopefully this idea should have been fairly easy to find.

QUESTION: Why did White play 10.Qc2 and allow 10...Be4 ?

ANSWER: I can't be sure, but maybe Neelotpal actually wanted to encourage ...Be4 and then employ the plan discussed in the last game: vacate the long diagonal and then force the bishop back.

Even so, it does seem a bit unnatural to invite ...Be4 so flagrantly and the evidence of the game suggests that Black is well placed to meet White's plan. Overall, I feel that both 10.Qb3 and 10.d5 , covered in the next two games, are more challenging options.

11.Qb2 h6

Preventing Bg5, which might have been an awkward move in some positions now that the e4-square is taken.

12.Rfd1!

QUESTION: Why the exclamation mark for 12.Rfd1 ?

ANSWER: Neelotpal is beginning his plan by vacating the f1-square. He's intending to move the bishop there rather than h3 to offer protection to c4 in some lines and also to avoid being susceptible to ...g5-g4 ideas (after Bf4).

We should realize from the previous game that 12.Ne1? would be the wrong plan for White. After 12...Bxg2 13.Nxg2 Nc6! Black will just focus on White's c4-pawn. This is something White definitely needs to avoid. He might already be positionally lost!

12...Rc8 13.Bf1

EXERCISE: Find a plan for Black and a good first move.

I know, 13.Bf1 does make a strange impression! As explained above, though, it's all part of White's plan. If Black does nothing, White will follow up with either Ne1, f2-f3 and e2-e4, or Bf4 and Nd2.

ANSWER:

13...Qa4!

Once again a4 turns out to be a more-than-useful square for the queen. Not only does she attack c4 from there, she also vacates the a5-square. Give Black two further moves for the plan ...Nc6 and ...Na5 and he will be winning.

14.Bf4 Nc6

Grabbing the pawn with 14...cxd4 15.Nxd4 Qxc4 must have been tempting for Adhiban, but this does give White counterplay with some dark-square action after 16.Nb5! . It's certainly okay for Black, but just like in the previous game he prefers to fully coordinate his pieces before picking on White's weaknesses.

15.Nd2!

Neelotpal is just in time. Any further delay and ...Na5 would have led to real trouble for White. Even so, the resulting position just offers level chances.

15...Bg6 16.dxc5!?

Reclaiming the long diagonal with 16.Bg2 looks the most consistent follow-up. Black's bishop has found a nice new home on g6, but it wouldn't be such a happy piece if White were able to advance with e2-e4. Because of this, Black should probably play 16...cxd4 17.cxd4 d5! with enough counterplay.

16...bxc5

17.Nb3?!

Neelotpal was clearly counting on this attack of the c5-pawn, but he must have overlooked the strength of Black's next two moves.

17.Qb5!? is a better way to justify White's previous move. For example, 17...Qa3! ( Black should avoid straightening out White's pawns with 17...Qxb5?! 18.cxb5 Na5 19.c4 ) 18.Nb3 Rab8! 19.Bc1! ( if 19.Bxb8? then 19...Rxb8 20.Qxc5 Rxb3! ) 19...Rxb5 20.Bxa3 Rbb8 21.Nxc5 Ne5 , although Black is fine here. 17.Bd6! would also be an improvement.

17...e5! 18.Be3

Even here White would still be just about okay after 18.Nxc5 because 18...Qxc4 19.Qa3 exf4?? 20.e3! traps the queen.

18...Rab8!

Now White has an inferior position and it immediately gets much worse:

19.Nxc5??

EXERCISE: Black to play and win.

19.Bxc5 Na5 20.Qa3 Qxa3 21.Bxa3 Nxc4 is good for Black because his pieces are far more active than their counterparts.

ANSWER:

19...Qxd1! 20.Qxb8 Qxf1+!

The key move of the combination. Black's queen wins the battle of the "desperados" against her opposite number and Black emerges a piece ahead.

21.Rxf1 Rxb8

0-1

White chose a more challenging plan against ...Be4 this time, but the bishop found an effective new home on g6 and overall Black's resources were always at least sufficient.

Game 40
Bartel,M - Baklan,V [E20]
Lublin, 2010

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.g3 c5 5.Nf3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Qa5 7.Bd2

White has also tried 7.Qb3 here instead of at move 10, but his lack of control over e4 allows Black to attack from another angle:

7...Nc6! (threatening to take on d4) 8.dxc5 ( not ideal, but 8.Bd2 Ne4 looks okay for Black ) 8...Ne4 9.Bg2 Nxc5 10.Qa3 Qxa3 ( 10...b6 11.Nd4 Bb7 12.Nxc6 Qxa3 13.Bxa3 Bxc6 14.Bxc6 dxc6 15.Bxc5 bxc5 is completely equal, but not 10...d6? 11.Nd4! ) 11.Bxa3 Na4 12.Kd2 (P.Nielsen-V.Epishin, Frankfurt (rapid) 2000) and here 12...Na5! 13.Kd3 b6! 14.Nd4 Bb7 15.Bxb7 Nxb7 looks like a good solution for Black.

7...b6 8.Bg2 Bb7 9.O-O O-O 10.Qb3

Just as with 10.Qc2 , White protects the c-pawn to free the d2-bishop.

10...Ne4

EXERCISE: Try to guess White's next move. (Hint: You may have to give up a piece, at least temporarily.)

The difference with 10.Qc2 is that here 10...Be4 doesn't attack the queen and would be answered by 11.Bg5! .

If 10...Be4 suggests itself against 10.Qc2 , then 10...Ne4 does the same against 10.Qb3 . However, Black should also consider alternatives here. For example:

(a) Ponomariov suggests the calm 10...h6 to prevent Bg5, after which Black can consider ...Be4, ...Ne4 or ...Qa6, depending on circumstances.

(b) 10...Qa6!? was Kramnik's choice when he reached this position against Topalov. This is sensible, not least because the b7-bishop is now protected against tactics on the long diagonal, and of course ...Nc6-a5 becomes a possibility. The game continued 11.Bg5 Ne4 12.d5 d6 13.Rad1 Re8 ( Black needs to avoid 13...Nd7 14.dxe6 fxe6 15.Be7! ) 14.Bf4 Nd7 15.dxe6! Rxe6 16.Bh3 Re7 17.Nh4 with an edge for White, V.Topalov-V.Kramnik, Monte Carlo (rapid) 2003, but there are likely improvements to look out for here; for example, 14...exd5 15.cxd5 Nd7 or 14...h6!? intending 15.dxe6 Rxe6 16.Bh3 Re8 17.Bxd6 Nc6 and ...Na5 .

(c) 10...Nc6 is a move Black would like to play, but 11.d5! might be a strong response: 11...Ne7 12.Bg5! exd5 13.Bxf6 gxf6 14.cxd5 Bxd5 15.c4 Be6 16.Qb2 Kg7 17.Ng5 looked quite dangerous in Y.Gozzoli-C.Navrotescu, Lille 2011.

ANSWER:

11.Ng5!

EXERCISE: Analyse and assess the variations after 11...Nxd2 .

Did you guess correctly? A move which allows a bishop to be captured and a resulting queen and rook fork obviously carries some surprise value. However, tactics involving the two bishops on the long diagonal are very common in this type of position, so there's no doubt Baklan would have considered 11.Ng5 .

11...f5!

Probably a good decision, although I wonder whether Baklan's original intention was to take the bishop. After 11...Nxd2 there follows 12.Qc2! (this mate threat on h7 is usually what makes the Ng5 tactic work) 12...g6! 13.Bxb7 Nxf1 and now:

(a) 14.Rxf1 should lead to an equal position, e.g. 14...Nc6 15.Bxa8 Rxa8 .

(b) 14.Bxa8! is stronger because the knight is trapped on f1 so White will likely end up a piece ahead.

Still, this isn't completely clear because Black has a discovered attack on the g5-knight. For example, 14...cxd4! (this is why 12...g6 was better than 12...f5 ) 15.Ne4 Nxh2 16.Kxh2 f5 17.Ng5 Na6 18.Bf3 and though Black is getting two or three pawns for his piece, White probably still has the advantage.

There's even a third option in 14.d5!? preventing ...Nc6 and cutting off Black's queen from the action. Overall, it's quite understandable that Baklan avoided taking on d2.

12.Nxe4

Possibly White should keep the tension with 12.Bf4 , with chances of an edge.

12...Bxe4!

Forced, and what Black wants to play in any case. 12...fxe4? 13.Qc2! would leave the e4-pawn loose and 13...d5 14.cxd5 exd5 15.c4! only makes things worse for Black.

13.Bxe4 fxe4

EXERCISE: Find a good move for White.

Black is threatening to win a pawn here with ...cxd4.

ANSWER:

14.d5!

Bartel's move not only stops the threat of ...cxd4, crucially it also prevents Black's knight developing to c6. Now Black must work really hard to mobilize his forces, but Baklan finds a way:

14...Qa6!

QUESTION: Why the exclamation mark? Shouldn't Black be trying to complete his development?

ANSWER: With the c6-square unavailable, Baklan plans to develop with ...e5, ...d6 and ...Nd7. If he can arrange this successfully he will reach a good position for the usual reason – White's pawn weaknesses. However, 14...Qa6 is a vital preparatory move, to tie White's queen to the defence of c4. If Black played the immediate 14...e5 White could just play 15.Qc2! intending Qxe4.

15.Bf4!

Preventing ...e5.

15...Re8!

EXERCISE: How should Black recapture if White takes on e6?

15...Re8! is an excellent move, which reminds me a bit of Zhang Zhong's 17...Rf8!! against Sasikiran. Baklan realizes that ...e5 and ...d6 is such a vital plan for Black and is willing to play an unnatural-looking rook move in order to fulfil it.

16.a4

ANSWER: After 16.dxe6 Black should recapture with the pawn, despite this giving him doubled isolated e-pawns. A feature of this and similar positions is that Black's ...Nc6-a5 plan is so strong it's often sufficient even when Black has weaknesses elsewhere.

Key Notes

1. ...Be4 is often a good idea for Black in this line. It safeguards the bishop and makes progress difficult for White (see Lenderman-Kacheishvili and Neelotpal-Adhiban).

2. The exchange of bishops on the long diagonal usually favours Black, who is then free to employ typical Nimzo-Indian light-square strategy and attack c4, as Kacheishvili does with success in Lenderman-Kacheishvili (Game 38).

3. Black should try to keep the position as closed as possible, particularly in the early stages. White's bishops tend to thrive if the position opens up too soon and Black might suffer on the dark squares due to his lack of a dark-squared bishop.

4. Watch out for tactical tricks on the long diagonal and be particularly careful when considering the natural move ...Ne4 (e.g. in Bartel-Baklan).

5. d4-d5 is a sharp possibility for White. If he plays this move he is usually prepared to sacrifice a pawn in return for activity (see Sasikiran-Timman).

6. Look out for opportunities to use the queen effectively: ...Qa4! to increase the pressure on the light squares and on c4 in particular; or ...Qa6! to do exactly the same but also safeguard the bishop on b7.

7. Black should be ready for a transposition to the Rubinstein Variation if White chooses to meet 4...c5 with 5.e3.
