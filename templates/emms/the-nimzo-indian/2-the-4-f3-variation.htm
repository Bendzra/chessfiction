Chapter Two. The 4 f3 Variation

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.f3

4.f3 is another principled response to the Nimzo. White is simply continuing the battle which began at move 1. White has been trying to play e2-e4 and Black has been preventing it, first with 1...Nf6 and then with 3...Bb4 . After 4.f3 White is again ready to play e2-e4.

The 4.f3 Variation is very similar to the Saemisch Variation, and indeed there are transpositional possibilities because in most 4.f3 lines White plays a2-a3 more or less straightaway. For this reason I felt that the Saemisch and 4.f3 chapters should run consecutively. However, if you feel you need a break from Saemisch doubled c-pawn positions, you might wish to skip this chapter for the moment, move on to the next one, and then come back to it later.

Black's three main choices here are 4...d5 , 4...c5 and 4...O-O . In this book I've chosen to focus on 4...O-O , partly because it mirrors Black's approach against 4.a3 - early pressure against the c4-pawn. White either transposes back to the Saemisch with 5.a3 or occupies the centre immediately with 5.e4 . We shall study 5.a3 first and then move on to 5.e4 .

White Plays 5 a3

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.f3 O-O 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3

With 5.a3 White transposes back into the Saemisch Variation. Once again I'm going to focus on the direct and quite natural ...b6, ...Ba6, ...Nc6-a5 approach by Black. However, because Black has already castled, some extra care with the move order is required. I won't give anything else away - let's move on to the next game!

Game 4
Miedema,R - Kulaots,K [E27]
Cappelle la Grande, 2008

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.f3

Note that the actual move order of the game was 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 b6 6.f3 O-O 7.e4 Ne8 8.Bd3 Ba6 9.Nh3 Nc6 10.Qe2 Na5 11.e5 Qh4+ 12.Nf2 , but I've adjusted it in order to consider other possibilities more conveniently.

4...O-O 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Ne8

QUESTION: I realize ...Ne8 is a key idea in the Saemisch, but it looks a bit strange to play it so early. Couldn't Black at least delay it for a move or two?

ANSWER: If Black's plan involves ...Ne8, then playing it as early as this is probably the most accurate. I don't feel that he can gain anything by delaying it, and it seems sensible to eliminate having to deal with, say, 6...b6 7.Bg5!? .

Of course Black has other ways to play, and 6...Nh5 7.Nh3 f5 is a reasonable alternative.

7.e4 b6 8.Bd3

8.Nh3 should probably transpose after 8...Ba6 9.Bd3 or 9.e5 Nc6 10.Bd3 .

8...Ba6

Hopefully readers should know by now why 8...Bb7?! is not the best move here. There's nothing wrong with 8...Nc6 , though, as long as it's followed up by ...Ba6.

9.Nh3

Alternatives are covered next game.

9...Nc6 10.e5

10.O-O Na5 11.Qe2 (or 10.Qe2 Na5 11.O-O ) is covered in Topalov-Leko (Game 6).

10...Qh4+

QUESTION: Why did Kulaots throw in this check?

ANSWER: Just as in Moskalenko-Neverov (Game 3), the queen check acts as a "spoiler", aiming to slow down White's play on the kingside. After 11.Nf2 the knight – temporarily at least – is less actively placed, with Bg5 and Ng5 no longer possible straight away. Black may have to move the queen again later on, but his argument is that this time is worth spending.

The main alternative is 10...Na5 when 11.Qe2 f6 ( 11...Qh4+ is still possible ) 12.O-O again reaches the main line, and Topalov-Leko (Game 6).

However, White has a very interesting and dangerous alternative in 11.Ng5!? f5 ( 11...h6? 12.Nh7 traps the rook ) 12.f4!? h6 . At first sight there seems to be no point to White's play. The knight retreats, Black takes on c4 and then what? However, sacrificing a piece with 13.Qh5! has produced two wins for White in grandmaster games:

13...hxg5 14.fxg5 g6 (otherwise White plays g6) 15.Qxg6+ Ng7 16.Qh6 Qe7 17.g6 Ne8 18.O-O Qg7 19.Qg5 Nf6 ( or 19...Nb3 20.Rf3!? Nxa1 21.Rh3 Nf6 22.Rh6 Bb7 23.Bf4 d6 24.h4 Bc6 25.exf6 Qxf6 26.Qg3 Be8 27.h5 Nb3 28.Rh7 1-0, F.Berkes-I.Lysyj, Martuni 2009 ) 20.Rf3 Bxc4 21.Bf4 Nd5 22.Bxc4 Nxc4 23.Rh3 Nxf4 24.Qxf4 d6 25.Qg5 dxe5 26.Rh7 Qf6 27.Qh5 Rae8 28.g7 Qxg7 29.Rxg7+ Kxg7 30.Qe2 and White won in M.Ulibin-M.Palac, Zagreb 2010.

Initially I thought Black could just ignore the offer and play 13...Bxc4 , but now I feel that White's attack is dangerous here as well: 14.Bxc4 Nxc4 15.h4! Qc8 ( or 15...c5 16.Qg6 hxg5 17.hxg5 Rf7 18.Qh7+ Kf8 19.g6 ) 16.Qg6! hxg5 17.hxg5 Qb7 (Black's only chance is to counterattack, otherwise Qh5 is decisive) 18.Kf2 Qe4 19.Ra2 Qb1 20.Re2 Ncd6! 21.exd6 Nxd6 22.Rh7 Rf7 23.Qh5 Kf8 24.Bd2 Ne4+ 25.Rxe4 Qxe4 26.g6 Qc2 27.Qe2! and White is probably winning.

Of course these games and the above analysis might just be the just the tip of the iceberg. No doubt there are improvements lurking for both sides, and in such a complicated line it's incredibly difficult to assess the positions with any certainty. I'll leave it up to readers who want to try this line to delve further into it.

Let's return to Kulaots' choice of 10...Qh4+ which on reflection does look a whole lot safer!

11.Nf2

I can't believe 11.Kf1 is a serious attempt by White. I would very tempted here to break with 11...f6 .

11...Na5!?

As an alternative to ganging up on c4, the plan with 11...f6 looks decent. Two examples:

(a) 12.f4 fxe5 13.g3 Qe7 14.fxe5 Na5 15.Qe2 ( 15.Qg4!? ) 15...d5 16.Qc2 h6 17.Bh7+ Kh8 18.cxd5 exd5 with a roughly equal position, E.Ovod-T.Kosintseva, Moscow 2007.

(b) 12.exf6 Nxf6 13.O-O Na5 14.Re1!? and here I feel that Black can safely grab the pawn with 14...Bxc4! ( 14...d6 15.Nh3! Bxc4 16.Bxc4 Nxc4 17.Qe2 d5 18.Qxe6+ Kh8 19.Bg5 was better for White in E.Barkovsky-A.Hermlin, correspondence 1986 ) . Yakovich gives 15.Re5 which threatens 16.g3 and 16.Bg5 , but 15...Nd5! seems to be a more than adequate answer according to Rybka. The key point is that the discovered attack with 16.Bg5 Qh5 17.Be7 is effectively met by 17...Rf5! 18.Bxf5 exf5 when if anything Black is better because of his excellent minor pieces and White's awkwardly placed bishop.

12.Qe2 c5!?

Ambitious, direct play – Black makes c4 the focus.

QUESTION: What happens on 12...c5 13.dxc5 ? Does Black play 13...bxc5 or 13...Bxc4 ?

ANSWER: 13...bxc5 is okay, 13...Bxc4 is better, but strongest is neither of these. Remember the little trick 13...Nb3! that we've seen before? 14.Rb1 Nxc5 regains the pawn with a clear positional advantage.

Now that the queen no longer protects b3, another possibility for Black was to get rid of White's bishop pair with 12...Nb3 (instead of 12...c5 ) 13.Rb1 Nxc1 14.Rxc1 .

13.g3

EXERCISE: Black must make a decision. Should the queen retreat back to base or try to remain active on h5?

With 13.g3 White forces the queen away, but this comes at a price of slightly weakening the light squares. This may not seem significant at the moment, but just check out what happens later on!

ANSWER:

13...Qd8!

An excellent decision by Kulaots. The queen has done her job on the kingside and is now urgently required elsewhere, helping Black to battle in the centre and against c4.

We saw a successful ...Qh5 in Moskalenko-Neverov (Game 3), but in that game White's queen was on a4 and rather neglecting the kingside. Here things are different, and after 13...Qh5 I would be worried that Black's queen might act as a hook for any kingside attack by White, for example 14.h4!? f5 15.g4 .

It should be noted that 13...Qe7!? is actually possible too. 14.Qe4 threatens mate and the rook, but doesn't win because of 14...f5! 15.Qxa8 Nc6! followed by ...Nc7 winning the queen for two rooks.

14.Be3?!

Too passive! Miedema protects against the idea of ...cxd4 followed by ...Nb3, hitting a1 and d4, but there are better options.

For a start White can afford the time to castle here. 14.O-O cxd4 15.cxd4 Nb3 isn't actually a threat, because 16.Rb1 Nxd4? fails to 17.Qe4 Nf5 18.g4 . White could also consider something like 14.Ne4!? cxd4 15.Bg5 with unclear play after either 15...f6 16.exf6 gxf6 17.Bh6 Rf7 18.O-O or 15...Qc7!? 16.Be7 dxc3 17.Bxf8 Kxf8 . In the Saemisch Nimzo-Indian White must always be looking to play as actively as possible, even if this means sacrificing pawns. We mustn't forget that the Saemisch is basically a gambit.

14...cxd4

In this case it's wise to exchange pawns before playing ...Rc8. After 14...Rc8 15.dxc5! the idea of 15...Nb3? doesn't work because of 16.Bxh7+! Kxh7 17.Qc2+ .

15.cxd4 Rc8 16.Rc1

EXERCISE: How does Black improve his position? Find a good plan for him.

ANSWER:

16...d5!

An excellent move and another important idea in the Saemisch. Black doesn't have quite enough ammunition to win the pawn on c4 since White can always respond with c5 if Black plays ...Qc7, but he does have enough ammunition to win the c4-square.

17.c5!

The best bet. After 17.exd6?! Nxd6 White's c-pawn is under tremendous pressure, and if 18.c5 there follows 18...Bxd3 19.Qxd3 Nf5 with a very strong position for Black, e.g. 20.cxb6 Rxc1+ 21.Bxc1 axb6 22.Bb2 Qd5 or 20.Ne4 bxc5 21.Nxc5 Nc6 .

Black also gets a good position after 17.cxd5 Rxc1+ 18.Bxc1 Bxd3 19.Nxd3 Qxd5 , again with pressure against d4 and with ideas of ...Nc4 and ...Nc7-b5.

17...Bxd3 18.Nxd3 Nc4

Black has secured the c4-square and enjoys a slight advantage. The next step is to activate his queen and the knight on e8.

19.O-O Qd7

19...Nc7 is perhaps a more accurate move order, as then 20.Nb2 can be answered simply by 20...Nxa3! (compare the next note).

20.cxb6

White could have equalized by challenging the c4-knight with 20.Nb2! since 20...Nxa3?! ( 20...Nxe3 21.Qxe3 or 20...Nxb2 21.Qxb2 look equal ) is met strongly by 21.Qa6! .

20...axb6 21.Bd2?!

21.Nb2 is probably better here too. White's bishop seeks activity on b4 but it turns out to be of a superficial variety.

21...Nc7! 22.Bb4 Rfe8 23.Nb2

Finally it comes, but too late! Black is much better coordinated now and ready to pounce with another active knight.

23...Nb5! 24.Nxc4 dxc4 25.Rxc4

If 25.Rfd1 then 25...Qd5! followed by doubling on the c-file or ...Red8.

25...Nxd4 26.Qa2 b5 27.Rxc8 Rxc8

The threats mount on the light squares, and White is beginning to feel the strain caused by weakening his kingside way back with 13.g3 . For example, if 28.Bd6 there follows 28...Rc3! 29.f4 Rc2 30.Qb1 Qc6 mating on g2 or winning with ...Rc1+.

That bishop on b4 is not a lucky piece. Despite the position opening up it's still being outshone by a knight!

28.Rf2

EXERCISE: Black has a tactical possibility here with 28...Qb7 29.Kg2 Nxf3 30.Rxf3 Qe4 , threatening a decisive ...Rc2+. Does this work? (Warning: difficult!)

28...h6

ANSWER: Yes it does! After 28...Qb7 29.Kg2 Nxf3! 30.Rxf3 Qe4! White can avoid an immediate catastrophe with 31.Qf2 Rc2 32.Bd2 but he is paralysed by pins. Black regains his piece, remains a pawn ahead and will have a near decisive advantage in view of White's weak king and e5-pawn. For example, 32...h6 ( the immediate 32...Qd5 is also good, but White is so tied up Black can afford this luxury ) 33.h4 Qd5 34.Kh3 Rxd2 and Black should win.

You might have noticed another tactical shot with 28...Qb7 29.Kg2 Rc2 intending 30.Rxc2? Qxf3+ 31.Kg1 Qd1+ winning. However, White can put up much more resistance with 30.Qxc2! Nxc2 31.Rxc2 when it won't be easy for Black to convert his advantage.

29.Kg2 Rc4

Despite missing the above tactical shot, Black remains much better but it's not easy to break through. On the other hand, it's also difficult for White not to allow a breakthrough. Kulaots spends the next few moves jockeying, and then he pounces when White makes an error.

30.Qd2 Qd5 31.Bd6 Qc6 32.Bb4 Qc7 33.Bd6 Qc8 34.Qd3 Qc6 35.Rb2?!

35.Bb4 , here and next move, provides a tougher defence.

35...Nf5! 36.Kh3 Rd4 37.Qe2 Rc4 38.Rd2 h5!?

Black finally begins some action.

39.Rb2?

EXERCISE: What's the winning plan for Black?

ANSWER:

39...g5!

Threatening ...g4+ to decisively open up White's king. There's no chance for White to survive this light-square assault – for all purposes he is playing a piece down in this battle because the bishop contributes nothing.

40.Kg2 g4 41.Kf2 Qb6+

0-1

After 41...Qb6+ 42.Kg2 Ne3+ 43.Kf2 Black can win in many ways, including simply 43...Nd1+ and 44...Nxb2 . An excellent positional game by Kulaots, who slowed down White's initiative with 10...Qh4+ , pounced on White's timid 14.Be3 and achieved an advantage with 16...d5! to seize control of the c4-square.

Game 5
Wallinger,M - Dambrauskas,V [E27]
Correspondence, 2004

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.f3 O-O? 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Ne8 7.e4 b6 8.Bd3 Ba6 9.f4

QUESTION: Why f3 and then f4? Isn't White spending too much time on pawn moves in the opening?

ANSWER: Possibly he is, but 9.f4 does have some solid logic to it. Not only does White increase his control of the centre and prepare e5, he also allows the knight to develop on its most natural square, f3 – we've already seen the benefits this brings. The key question is, can White get away with this time-consuming "luxury"?

QUESTION: What should Black play against 9.Ne2 ?

ANSWER: e2 isn't such a great place for the knight in this variation as it gets in the way of White's queen, who usually needs to be on this square to protect the c4-pawn. Black should look to exploit this by attacking c4 without delay. For example, 9...Nc6 10.O-O Na5! 11.f4 ( if 11.Qa4 Nd6 ) 11...Bxc4 12.Bxc4 Nxc4 13.f5 f6 14.fxe6 dxe6 15.Ng3 Qd7 and White had no compensation for the pawn deficit in A.Smirnov-V.Yemelin, Tallinn 2009. Of course White can play 10.Ng3 so that he is in time to meet 10...Na5 with 11.Qe2 , but in that case surely it's better for the knight to go to h3 in one move rather than g3 in two? Black should answer this with 11...c5 followed by ...Rc8 with rapid pressure against c4.

Another option to consider is 9.a4!? , planning to activate the bishop on a3. A key line here is 9...Nc6 10.Ba3 d6 11.f4!? (if 11.Qe2 Black should consider 11...e5 trying to block things up, since one of the reasons behind Ba3 is to be able to meet 11...Na5 with 12.Bb4!? ) 11...Na5 12.Qe2 and now:

(a) 12...c5 13.Nf3 Rc8 14.e5! .

EXERCISE: White has apparently ignored the threat to his c4-pawn. Try to work out why he has done so. (Hint: What has changed after 14.e5 ?)

ANSWER: 14...cxd4? fails to recognize the threat created by White's previous move: 15.Bxh7+! (Remember: with the knight on f3, the Greek Gift sacrifice comes into play!) 15...Kxh7 ( 15...Kh8 is wiser but still a position to be avoided after 16.Bd3 ) 16.Ng5+ with a winning attack, e.g. 16...Kh6 17.Qd3 or 16...Kg8 17.Qh5 .

After 14.e5 Black needs to take measures against the h7-sacrifice. 14...g6 is one way of doing so, although after 15.O-O cxd4 16.cxd4 Bxc4 17.Rac1 White's a3-bishop has come to life and Black has some dark-square weaknesses – White has at least enough compensation for the pawn.

(b) Another option for Black is to put aside ideas of attacking c4 for the moment and to fight back on the kingside with 12...f5 . For example:

(b1) 13.e5 Qh4+!? 14.g3 Qg4! 15.Nf3 ( White should consider 15.Qa2!? ) 15...Bxc4! was a clever pawn snatch by Black in M.Cebalo-G.Dizdar, Sibenik 2006.

(b2) 13.Nf3 g6 14.e5 Ng7 15.O-O , as in L.Liptay-J.Pogats, Hungary 1975, looks a touch better for White. As a possible improvement, I suggest activating the knight with 13...Nf6!? 14.e5 Ne4 15.Bb4 ( or 15.Bxe4 fxe4 16.Ng5 Bxc4 17.Qxe4 g6 ) 15...Qd7 16.O-O Rae8 reaching what looks like a complicated position with chances for both sides. My overall feeling is that 9.a4!? is certainly a worthwhile option for White.

Let's now return to the position in the game after Wallinger's 9.f4 :

9...Nc6 10.e5

Another pawn move, and this one does look a bit too committal to me. Nf3 is a good move that has to be played sooner or later, so why not play it now? After 10.Nf3 Na5 White can then decide whether to play e5 or defend the c4-pawn:

(a) 11.e5 .

EXERCISE: Work out whether or not it is safe for Black to capture on c4.

ANSWER: It is safe. After 11...Bxc4 the Greek Gift sacrifice fails: 12.Bxh7+? Kxh7 13.Ng5+ Kg8! 14.Qh5 and here Black can defend with 14...Bd3! – a key resource to remember! Interestingly, in two games I found reaching this position, Black declined to capture on c4, preferring 11...f5 12.Qe2 d5 transposing to note 'b' below.

(b) 11.Qe2 f5!? ( Black should probably avoid 11...Nd6 because after 12.e5! Ndxc4 the self-pin is awkward, not to mention that 13.Bxh7+ might be strong; 11...c5 is certainly playable though ) 12.e5 d5! 13.exd6 Nxd6 14.O-O ( against 14.Ne5 Black should consider the disruptive 14...Qh4+!? 15.g3 Qh3 , while 14.Qxe6+ Kh8 15.Qe2 – only move! – 15...Bxc4 16.O-O looks equal ) 14...Qe7 15.Ne5 Bb7 ( 15...Nf7!? ) 16.a4 Ne4 17.Ba3 c5 with level chances, V.Moskalenko-G.Timoshenko Alushta 1993.

10...Na5 11.Qe2

11.Nf3 would transpose to the above note.

11...d5!

QUESTION: Why is this move given an exclamation mark?

ANSWER: For the same reasons as in the previous game. Black is again more focussed on winning the actual c4-square than the pawn (although the pawn is always a nice bonus!).

Of course there's a need to be flexible. Here the ...d5 strike is very strong because White played 10.e5 instead of 10.Nf3 . In the case of 10.Nf3 Na5 11.Qe2 , 11...d5?! ( instead of 11...f5 – see above ) 12.cxd5 Bxd3 13.Qxd3 exd5 14.e5 would be less appealing, primarily because of White's mobile pawn majority on the kingside.

12.cxd5

There's no real choice, since 12.exd6? Nxd6 13.c5 (otherwise the pawn is lost) 13...Bxd3 14.Qxd3 bxc5 15.dxc5 Ndc4 16.Qxd8 Rfxd8 looks horrendous for White.

12...Bxd3 13.Qxd3 Qxd5!

QUESTION: Why is taking with the queen better than 13...exd5 ?

ANSWER: Let's assess the key differences:

1. Black's queen becomes very active and helps Black to control the light squares.

2. The black pawn remains on e6 so White lacks a pawn majority on the kingside and will find it very difficult to achieve anything there.

3. A pawn on d5 would give Black a more permanent grip on c4, but in the circumstances this isn't required since Black's pieces can perform a perfectly adequate job controlling c4.

Conclusion: 13...Qxd5 is much stronger!

14.Nf3 c5 15.O-O f5

Pre-empting any Ng5 ideas and clamping down even further on the light squares. Black's position is already very pleasant and it only gets better.

16.a4 Nc7 17.Ba3 Rfd8 18.Qc2 Qb3! 19.Qxb3 Nxb3 20.Rad1 c4 21.Nd2 Nd5 22.Rf3

White should probably try 22.Nxc4 Rac8 23.Nd6 Rxc3 24.Rf2 , although Black still keeps a clear advantage.

22...Rdc8

Black has traded strategical advantages – he now dominates the d5-square instead of c4. In fact this is a dream position for Black. Both knights have excellent outposts whereas White's bishop – just as in our previous game – hits thin air and can't contribute to the main battle. Add to this the weakness of a4 and c3 and we can already say that Black enjoys a strategically winning position. Given that this is a high-level correspondence game, free of over-the-board pressures, you would expect Black's winning technique to be solid, and Dambrauskas doesn't disappoint.

23.Bd6 a6 24.Nb1 b5 25.g3 Kf7 26.Na3 bxa4 27.Nc2 Rc6 28.Nb4

28...Rxd6!

The cleanest. White soon has to return the exchange and Black reaches a winning rook ending with an outside passed pawn.

29.exd6 Nxb4 30.cxb4 Rd8 31.Rc3 a3 32.Rxc4 a2 33.b5 axb5 34.Rc7+ Kf6 35.Ra7 a1Q 36.Rdxa1 Nxa1 37.Rxa1 Rxd6 38.Rb1 Rb6!

As always, the rook is well placed behind the passed pawn.

39.Rb4 Ke7 40.Kf2 Kd6 41.Ke3 Kc6 42.Kd3 Ra6 43.Rb2 Ra3+ 44.Ke2 Kb6 45.Kf2 Rd3 46.Re2 Rxd4 47.Rxe6+ Ka5 48.Re5 g6 49.Ke3 Rd7 50.h4 Ka4 51.h5 b4 52.hxg6 hxg6 53.Re6 b3 54.Rxg6

54...Kb5

There were other ways to win, but Black chooses a king and pawn ending.

55.Rg8 Rd6! 56.Rb8+ Rb6 57.Rxb6+ Kxb6 58.Kd3 Kb5 59.Kc3 Ka4 60.g4 Ka3 61.gxf5 b2

0-1

Another excellent strategic performance from Black's point of view. Dambrauskas seized the opportunity for light-square action with 11...d5! and never looked back.

Game 6
Topalov,V - Leko,P [E27]
Dortmund, 2002

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.f3 O-O 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Ne8 7.e4 b6 8.Bd3 Ba6 9.Nh3 Nc6 10.O-O

The main line. White develops quickly, cuts out resources based on ...Qh4+ and delays pushing his e-pawn until he is better prepared.

10.e5 was covered in Miedema-Kulaots.

10...Na5 11.Qe2

11...c5

QUESTION: What about 11...Nd6 here, piling the pressure on c4?

ANSWER: By now I hope the alarm bells should be ringing when considering such a move. After 12.e5 Ndxc4? ( 12...Nf5! had to be played ) not only has Black walked into the awkward self-pin he needs to try to avoid, White already has all the stars aligned for a successful Greek Gift: 13.Bxh7+! Kxh7 14.Qe4+! Kg8 15.Ng5 Re8 ( or 15...g6 16.Qh4 winning ) 16.Qh7+ Kf8 17.Qh5 Kg8 18.Qxf7+ Kh8 19.Qh5+ Kg8 20.Qh7+ Kf8 21.Qh8+ Ke7 22.Qxg7 mate.

11...Nb3!? has been played and deserves attention. This is an idea we've seen before in similar positions. Black puts his attack on c4 to one side in favour of removing one of White's bishops and thus diluting White's kingside threats. For example, after 12.Rb1 (White's choice in the three games on my database) 12...Nxc1 13.Rbxc1 Qe7 14.e5 Bb7 15.f4 f5 16.exf6 Nxf6 Black enjoyed a perfectly good position in E.Wiersma-J.Timman, London 2009.

When I looked deeper, though, I realized it's not quite that simple because Black also has to consider two other replies: 12.Bg5!? f6 13.Rab1 and even a rook and bishop sacrifice with 12.e5!? Nxa1 13.Bxh7+! Kxh7 14.Ng5+ Kg6 ( or 14...Kg8 15.Qe4 f5 16.Qh4 Qxg5 17.Bxg5 Nb3 18.f4 ) 15.Qe4+ f5 16.exf6+ Kxf6 17.Re1 with crazy complications.

Conclusion: 11...Nb3 might be playable but Black has to be prepared for more than just 12.Rb1!

12.e5

The most aggressive plan.

White can also aim to reduce Black's pressure against the c4-pawn with a timely d4-d5. For example, 12.Be3 Rc8 13.d5 f6 (preparing ...Nd6 without allowing e5) 14.dxe6 dxe6 15.e5! .

QUESTION: Should Black block the kingside with 15...f5 ?

ANSWER: This is a difficult strategic decision to make. It's very tempting to play 15...f5 especially on the evidence of the following example: 16.Bg5?! (a superficial gain; in fact it's White who ends up losing time) 16...Qd7 17.a4 h6 18.Be3 g5! 19.f4 g4 20.Nf2 h5 21.Nd1 Bb7 22.Nb2 Qc6 23.Rfe1 h4! 24.Qf2 Rc7 25.Bf1 Rh7 26.Rad1 Kh8 27.Rd8 Rg8 28.Red1 g3! 29.hxg3 hxg3 30.Qe1 Qc7 31.Be2

31...Rh1+!! 32.Kxh1 Qh7+ 33.Bh5 Qxh5+ 34.Kg1 Qh2+ 35.Kf1 Qxg2 mate, P.Cafolla-B.Socko, Cork 2005. An absolute model game by Black, worth playing over again and again.

This is unquestionably White's most consistent follow-up to 4.f3 - White certainly wants to advance with e2-e4, whereas a2-a3 isn't necessarily vital. Yet these days most players choose 5.a3 . The reason for this is that Black's main response...

QUESTION: Could Black adopt the same "Saemisch" approach we've seen so far in this book?

ANSWER: With the Nimzo, as with most openings, it's wise to show some flexibility and adapt to the circumstances. Black could force a Saemisch by playing 5...Bxc3+?! 6.bxc3 . However, it's obviously risky to enter positions, ones we already know to be sharp, a full tempo down. For example, 6...Ne8 7.Bd3 b6 8.Nh3 Ba6 9.O-O Nc6 10.e5 Na5 11.Qe2 and White, who is threatening a Greek Gift sacrifice here, must have some advantage. In fact it's even worse than being a tempo down, as the missing a2-a3 gives White an added possibility of Ba3!

QUESTION: How about if Black makes a useful Saemisch-type move with, for example, 5...b6 and waits for a2-a3?

ANSWER: The problem with this is that he could be waiting a long time! There's no obligation for White to play a2-a3. He's much better off developing, say with 6.Bg5! and then Black has that awkward pin to deal with. This is the sort of position Black needs to avoid, more so since 5...d5 is such an effective move.

Key Notes

1. After 4.f3 0-0 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3, the plans are noticeably quite similar to much of what we saw in the previous chapter but Black's early castling does make a significant difference.

2. If Black wants to play in typical fashion with ...b6, ...Ba6 and ...Nc6-a5, a vital precursor to this plan is 6...Ne8! (see Miedema-Kulaots, Wallinger-Dambrauskas and Topalov-Leko).

3. Just as we saw in the previous chapter, ...Qh4+ can sometimes be used as an effective spoiling move (see Miedema-Kulaots).

4. Black must always be aware of attacking ideas by White on the kingside, including the Greek Gift sacrifice.

5. The light-square strategy beginning with ...d5! is an important idea for Black, and it's seen in Miedema-Kulaots, Wallinger-Dambrauskas and Topalov-Leko.
