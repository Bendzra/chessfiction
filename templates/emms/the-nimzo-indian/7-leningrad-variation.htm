Chapter Seven. Leningrad Variation: 4.Bg5

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Bg5

In this chapter and the following one we'll study two possibilities previously mentioned in the introduction to Chapter Three, 4.Bg5 and 4.Nf3 .

We'll begin with 4.Bg5 , the Leningrad Variation. There's clearly a sound logic to White's move, since pinning the f6-knight is usually a good idea for White in queen's pawn openings. Furthermore, if Black is not careful White might simply get an improved version of the Rubinstein Variation with his bishop actively placed outside the pawn chain after e2-e3. For example, 4...O-O 5.e3 d5 6.cxd5 exd5 7.Bd3 compares quite favourably (or unfavourably, for Black!) to positions discussed in Chapter 3, and so is something Black should be looking to avoid.

4...c5!

EXERCISE: Find a good response for Black if White plays 5.e3 .

We saw in the previous chapter (after 4.Qc2 O-O ) that that ...c5 is a good response to Bg5, and the same applies here too. In fact it's probably the only good option for Black - if it wasn't for ...c5 far more players would be playing the Leningrad, or maybe far fewer players would be playing the Nimzo-Indian!

5.d5!

5.d5 is the only challenging response.

ANSWER: The reason 4...c5 is so strong is that 5.e3? , ideally a move White would like to play, is more or less refuted by 5...Qa5! when Black's threats will win at least a pawn. For example, 6.Bxf6 Bxc3+ 7.bxc3 Qxc3+ 8.Ke2 gxf6 or 6.Qc2 Ne4! 7.Qxe4 Bxc3+ 8.Kd1 Bxb2 .

Other moves for White, although not in the same category as 5.e3 , are still toothless:

(a) Against 5.dxc5 Black has a number of decent options, including just smashing White's pawns with 5...Bxc3 6.bxc3 . I quite like the look of 5...Na6 again. If 6.e4 then 6...Bxc3+ 7.bxc3 Qc7 ; or 6.Qc2 Nxc5 7.a3 Bxc3+ 8.Qxc3 Nce4 .

(b) 5.Rc1 allows instant equality with 5...cxd4 6.Qxd4 Nc6 although Black will have to grind away in a queenless middlegame after 7.Bxf6 Nxd4 (or 7...Qxf6 8.Qxf6 gxf6 ) 8.Bxd8 Kxd8 9.e3 Nc6 , as in E.Grivas-P.Leko, Heraklion 1997, where White managed to secure the draw... after 111 moves.

5...h6

In Easy Guide to the Nimzo-Indian I recommended a tricky option for Black in 5...d6 6.e3 Bxc3+ 7.bxc3 Qe7!? which bypasses some lines and leads to a different type of position in other cases, and I still believe that this is a decent short-cut for Black to take. However, in this book I've decided to pass over that line, as well as other options involving ...exd5 or the gambit ...b5, to focus entirely on the main line. I feel that not only is the main line fine for Black from a theoretical viewpoint, but studying and playing it also teaches us a considerable amount about these closed positions which are rich in strategic possibilities.

6.Bh4 d6

Many games go 6...Bxc3+ 7.bxc3 d6 8.e3 transposing.

7.e3 Bxc3+ 8.bxc3 e5

This is the key starting point of the mainline Leningrad. Black has inflicted White with doubled pawns and then completely closed the centre.

QUESTION: Does this line differ much from the Saemisch Variation?

ANSWER: There are two key differences:

1. Unlike in the Saemisch, it's very difficult for Black to exert meaningful pressure on the c4-pawn. The bishop could easily go to a6 but the knight's route to a5 is blocked, as is any attack down the c-file. So the c4-pawn, while still a weakness, isn't such a liability in the Leningrad.

2. Black enjoys a far greater share of the centre than in the Saemisch, with more space and a solid position. White is less likely to be able to attack quickly on the kingside.

You can probably guess from these two points, and just by looking at the position, that both sides adopt a deliberate approach with plenty of manoeuvring. Quality of development is just as important, if not more so, than quantity.

At this junction White has a number of options, and we will look in turn at all of 9.Nf3 , 9.Bd3 , 9.f3 and 9.Qc2 .

Game 34
Wiersma,E - Grooten,H [E31]
Netherlands Championship, Hilversum, 2008

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Bg5 h6 5.Bh4 c5

Black often employs this move order instead of 4...c5 5.d5 h6 .

6.d5 Bxc3+ 7.bxc3 d6 8.e3 e5 9.Nf3

The f3-square isn't a great place for the knight in the Leningrad (for example, it's vulnerable to ...e4 attacks), but it's only using it as a stepping stone to reach d2.

9...Bf5!

QUESTION: Why the exclamation mark for this move?

ANSWER: In positions which are primarily closed, control of any open file or diagonal can be of premium importance. Here Grooten doesn't hesitate to seize control of b1-h7 which is often a key diagonal in the Leningrad.

QUESTION: Is there anything in particular that Black should be looking to avoid?

ANSWER: In many situations Black should avoid castling too early, and sometimes shouldn't castle at all. Because the centre is so blocked, in some cases Black's king is actually better placed there than on the kingside.

An example of something Black should be looking to avoid is 9...O-O 10.Nd2 Nbd7 11.Bd3! (seizing the b1-h7 diagonal) 11...Re8 12.O-O Nf8 13.f4 exf4 14.exf4 Ng6 15.Bxf6 Qxf6 16.Ne4 Qd8 17.Qh5 with some advantage to White, C.Hoi-B.Sahl, Tastrup 1997.

QUESTION: Why doesn't White grab the diagonal before Black does, with 9.Bd3 ?

ANSWER: He can and he frequently does. 9.Bd3 is covered next game.

10.Nd2 g5!

This is another reason why Black shouldn't always commit to kingside castling. Rather than just a move to break the pin on the knight, this pawn lunge is the beginning of some typical kingside action and space-gaining by Black.

11.Bg3 Nbd7

EXERCISE: What do you think Black is planning with this knight? Is it staying where it is, going to b6 or going to f8?

12.f3

Wiersma supports a possible e3-e4 advance and gives the g3-bishop some breathing space.

ANSWER:

12...Nf8!

The knight comes to the kingside to support Black's growing activity there. On b6 it would attack White's c4-pawn, but this would have little point given that the pawn is well defended and cannot be attacked further.

If Black's bishop gets blocked out of the game on g6, Black could easily get squeezed by persistent pressure. For example, 12...Bg6 13.e4 Qe7 14.a4 Nh5 15.Bf2 a5 16.Qb1 b6 17.g3 O-O 18.Bd3! (preventing ...f5) 18...Ng7 19.g4! (and again!) 19...h5 20.h4! f6 21.Nf1 Kf7 22.Ne3 Rh8 23.Kd2 and Black was under the cosh in B.Spassky-N.Short, Zuerich 2001.

I'll give the remainder of this game, a painful one for Black, because it should hopefully act as a powerful deterrent to blocking in our bishop! 23...Rh6 24.Nf5 Bxf5 25.gxf5 Rg8 26.Be3 Ne8 27.Qb5 Rh7 28.Rag1 gxh4 29.Qb1 Kf8 30.Rxh4 Rxg1 31.Qxg1 Qf7 32.Be2 Ke7 33.Bf2 Ng7 34.Rh2 Ne8 35.Rg2 Rg7 36.Be3 Rxg2 37.Qxg2 Qh7 38.Ke1 Kf7 39.Kf2 Ng7 40.Qh3 Nf8 41.Qh4 Nd7 42.Kg1 Ke7 43.Kh2 Kf7 44.Qe1 Ke7 45.Qb1 Qh8 46.Qb5 Qh7 47.Qb1 Ne8 48.Qc1 Ng7 49.Qg1 Kf7 50.Kh3 Ne8 51.Kh4 Ng7 52.Bf1 Nf8 53.Bh3 Nd7 54.Qg6+ Kg8 55.Bh6 Qxg6 56.fxg6 Nf8 57.Bf5 Nxf5+ 58.exf5 Nd7 59.Kxh5 e4 60.fxe4 Ne5 61.Bf4 Nxc4 62.Kh6 and Short finally resigned.

13.Bf2 h5

QUESTION: 13...Ng6 looks like a good move. Why did Grooten choose 13...h5 instead?

ANSWER: 13...h5 is not just a space-gaining move, it's also preventative. If 13...Ng6 White could strike with 14.g4! , knocking the bishop off the diagonal and seizing it himself after 14...Bd7 15.Bd3! .

If the centre were open there would be no way Black could get away with spending so many moves developing his kingside activity. But this isn't the case here. The centre is closed, White is building only very slowly, Black's king is completely safe on e8 and he is free to improve his position on the wing.

14.e4

QUESTION: On which diagonal do you think Black's bishop should retreat?

14...Bd7!

ANSWER: I'm convinced this is the correct retreat. There will be the odd occasion where the specifics of the position dictate otherwise, but generally speaking the bishop is better placed on the c8-h3 diagonal once White closes the other diagonal with e3-e4. It's easy to see that on d7 the bishop can influence events on both the kingside and queenside. In contrast, on g6 (or h7) the bishop is blocked by the e4-pawn which would put Black under considerable pressure to force open the kingside to justify the bishop's presence there.

15.Be3!

Finally White makes a move in anger. 15.Be3 is useful because although Black can easily defend the g5-pawn with his next move, it does mean it becomes difficult to arrange the desirable ...Ng6.

15...Rg8 16.g3 h4

Grooten continues to slowly probe on the kingside. He intends to increase the pressure with ...Nh5. White can block things up with 17.g4 but then the most natural response is 17...N6h7 followed by ...Ng6 and eventually ...Nf4.

17.Qb3 b6 18.a4

Wiersma makes some movements on the queenside. The a5 advance is a possibility in some positions, but White has to be careful as this is a double-edged sword: it weakens Black's queenside structure but also gives him a passed a-pawn (or two).

18...Rb8 19.Be2 Nh5 20.g4

Wiersma decides to keep it blocked on the kingside at the expense of allowing a knight into f4 which results in him giving up his good bishop.

QUESTION: What would Black play against 20.Bf2 ?

ANSWER: I feel it would be time to advance with 20...g4 to open further lines, especially as White can no longer meet this advance with f3-f4.

20...Nf4 21.Bxf4 exf4!?

EXERCISE: A critical moment of the game has arisen. Look at the change in pawn structure, consider White's options and decide on his best move.

22.h3?

ANSWER: Often there's only a small window of opportunity in a game and it has to be grabbed with both hands. Black's recapture on f4 altered the pawn structure in a small but significant way. A hole has been left on e5 and if White does nothing – like with 22.h3? – Black will fill it with ...Ng6-e5! to get a monster knight on an impregnable outpost.

The golden opportunity Wiersma missed was to fill the hole with 22.e5! dxe5 , at the same time creating another hole that can be filled on e4 – but by his own knight. What's more, White has made the d5-pawn a protected passed pawn – not a bad deal at all for the investment of a measly pawn. After 23.Ne4 Black can fight to neutralize the knight with 23...Nh7 intending ...Nf6, but I prefer White's position after 24.a5 and this is infinitely better than what he gets in the game.

In effect 21...exf4 was a risky recapture which worked in the game because White didn't exploit his small window of opportunity. 21...gxf4! would have been slightly better for Black, although with good chances of a draw with the position so blocked.

22...Ng6!

Grooten doesn't miss his chance.

23.Kf2 Ne5 24.Rhb1 Ke7

Black is considerably better in this position although it's still difficult to make any real progress. Because the kingside is completely blocked, the only chance is to break through on the queenside but this will be far from easy if White defends accurately.

25.Rb2

If 25.a5 bxa5 26.Qxb8 Qxb8 27.Rxb8 Rxb8 28.Rxa5 Black has the better of the ending, though not necessarily enough to win, after either 28...Rb2 29.Ke1 Rc2 30.Rxa7 Rxc3 or 28...Ra8 29.Ke1 Kd8 followed by bringing the king to b6 .

25...Qc7 26.Nb1! Rgc8 27.Na3 a6 28.Nc2 Rb7 29.Ne1

Preparing Nd3 to challenge the e5-knight, but allowing the ...b5 break. It's debatable whether Black can make any progress at all if White keeps his knight on a3, for example 29.Na3 Rcb8 30.Rab1 Qd8 31.Kg2 Qe8 32.Bd1! . It's possible that Wiersma, the higher-rated player, was actually trying to encourage an opening of the position.

29...Rcb8 30.Qa2 b5! 31.cxb5 axb5 32.axb5 Bxb5

EXERCISE: This is a difficult moment for White. Try to work out what he should do.

ANSWER:

33.c4!

An excellent defensive move by Wiersma. It's obviously tempting for White to exchange off his "bad" bishop, instead of making it worse by putting another pawn on a light square! But actually the bishop does a sterling defensive job protecting White's weak points, including his king. After 33.Bxb5 Rxb5 34.Rxb5 Rxb5 Black would have a better chance of exploiting his advantage. For example, 35.Qa7 Rb2+ 36.Kf1 Qxa7 37.Rxa7+ Kf8 38.Ra6 Nc4! and White is in real trouble.

After 33.c4 play should continue 33...Bd7 34.Rxb7 Qxb7 35.Nd3! (keeping the bishop allows White to get rid of Black's monster knight) 35...Nxd3+ 36.Bxd3 and although Black is still better, a draw is the likeliest result.

Unfortunately from this moment the game is marred by mistakes by both players, I suspect in mutual time trouble. Black plays an unsound combination but wins anyway after it's White who makes the final blunder:

33...Bxc4? 34.Rxb7 Qxb7 35.Bxc4 Nxc4 36.Qxc4 Qb2+ 37.Nc2

37...Rb3

Unfortunately for Black this isn't even threatening ...Rc3, as Ra2 is always an effective answer.

38.e5! dxe5 39.d6+ Kxd6 40.Ra6+ Rb6 41.Ra2?

White should play 41.Ra5! .

41...Qc1?

41...Qb3! is much stronger.

42.Qxf7??

42.Qd3+ should win.

Game 35
Sasikiran,K - Zhang Zhong [E31]
Asian Championship, Udaipur, 2000

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Bg5 h6 5.Bh4 c5 6.d5 d6 7.e3 Bxc3+ 8.bxc3 e5 9.Bd3

This time White grabs the diagonal before Black can so do himself with ...Bf5.

9...e4!?

QUESTION: This looks like a good move if Black can play it. Suddenly he seems to have a lot more space.

ANSWER: The ...e4 advance is an enticing idea. Black gains considerable space in the centre and on the kingside, and also an impressive outpost square on e5 which a knight could use to good effect. Furthermore, White's c4-pawn will become vulnerable after the bishop retreats.

But it's not all good news for Black. After 9...e4 the pawn cannot be taken at this moment because of tactics: 10.Bxe4 g5! or 10.Bxf6 Qxf6 11.Bxe4 Qxc3+ . Its long-term safety, though, will be an issue. Also, White's dark-squared bishop has become a stronger piece. If it moves back to g3 – either forced by ...g5 or of its own accord – its scope is increased by the pawn advance and it directly attacks the d6-pawn. So, in summary, 9...e4 is a move which sharpens the game considerably and gives extra possibilities to both sides.

QUESTION: Could Black delay the advance for a move or two?

ANSWER: If Black is going to play ...e4 then I feel that this is as good a time as any. 9...Qe7 10.Ne2 and only now 10...e4?! is a mistake because it allows White to double Black's pawns with 11.Bxf6! gxf6 . If 9...Nbd7 10.Ne2 and then 10...e4 11.Bc2 transposes to the game, but White could also play 10.f3 transposing to the next game.

10.Bc2

The only real option, since the e2-square is needed for the knight to develop.

10...Nbd7 11.Ne2

11.f4 has been played a few times, but Topalov's response in a game against Ivanchuk looks quite convincing: 11...Nb6 12.Qe2 Bg4 13.Qf1 g5! 14.fxg5 Nh5! 15.a4 hxg5 16.Bf2 (V.Ivanchuk-V.Topalov, Wijk aan Zee 2001) and here either 16...Qf6 or 16...Qe7 would have given Black an edge according to Ivanchuk.

11...Qe7

12.Qb1!

A typical move in this line. White's queen further pressures the e4-pawn and also adds her weight to the b-file. She can also perform the mundane but possibly crucial task of defending the c4-pawn on b3.

Another possibility for White is 12.Ba4!? . The idea is to exchange on d7 and possibly on f6. Ironically this might loosen Black's protection of the e4-pawn despite the fact White's bishop has moved away from attacking it. 12...O-O 13.Bxd7 (otherwise the knight might move and leave the bishop looking silly on a4) 13...Bxd7 and now:

(a) 14.Bxf6 Qxf6 15.Qb1 was prematurely terminated in a draw in J.Hjartarson-G.Sax, Tilburg 1989. Black gets good compensation for the pawn after either 15...Rab8 16.Qxe4 Rfe8 or 15...Rfe8 16.Qxb7 Bg4 .

(b) 14.a4 b6 15.Qc2 and now even with the loss of time, 15...Bc8!? with the typical plan of ...Ba6 is an enticing idea for Black, though not the only option. After 16.O-O?! g5 17.Bg3 Ba6 18.Qa2 Nh5 19.Nc1?! f5! Black was completely dominating in V.Bagirov-G.Kuzmin, Baku 1977. White should really play 16.Bxf6! Qxf6 17.Qxe4 Ba6 and again Black has compensation; for example, 18.O-O Rae8 19.Qd3 Qg6! regaining the pawn by force.

12...O-O

QUESTION: In the previous game we saw that castling quickly should often be avoided, and yet this is exactly what Zhang Zhong has done here. What's the difference?

ANSWER: If Black plays an early ...e4 the situation becomes more dynamic and Black is sometimes required to develop much more quickly in his pursuit of counterplay. Here, for example, the king's rook might need to go to e8 to add protection to the e4-pawn.

13.O-O

White really wants to play Ng3 to surround the e4-pawn, but it's impossible at the moment due to tactical reasons: 13.Ng3? g5! traps the bishop, and the resource 14.Nf5 fails to 14...Qe5 15.Nxh6+ Kg7 16.Bg3 Qxc3+ . However, castling means that ...Qxc3 no longer comes with check, so Ng3 is possible in the future.

EXERCISE: The position after 13.O-O is a key moment in the game. Try to work out a plan for Black.

QUESTION: Could White get around the ...g5 problem by playing 13.Bxf6 Nxf6 and only then 14.Ng3 ?

ANSWER: This is a lazy solution which only leads to trouble for White, as it frees Black's position without him having to make any concessions. White's dark-squared bishop is a strong piece after ...e4 and White should not give it up unless he is getting something concrete in return. After 14...Qe5! 15.Qb3 ( 15.Kd2 Ng4! ) 15...b6 the e4-pawn has survived, Black can slowly but surely improve his position and even a direct attack on the kingside is a possibility.

For example, 16.O-O h5! 17.h3 h4 18.Ne2 g5 19.a4 Kh8 20.Bd1 Rg8 21.a5 g4! 22.hxg4 Bxg4 23.Nf4 Bf3 24.Bxf3 exf3 25.axb6 Rxg2+! 26.Kh1 Ng4 and White resigned in G.Siviero-C.Costa, correspondence 1999 – if 27.bxa7 then 27...Rh2+ 28.Kg1 Qg5 29.Qb8+ Kh7 30.Qxa8 Nxf2+ 31.Kxh2 Qg3 mate.

Finally, against 13.a4 Black can play 13...b6 intending ...Ba6, or even 13...a5!? and then ...b6: e.g 14.Ra2 b6 15.Rb2 ( 15.Bxf6 Nxf6 16.Qxb6? Ba6! ) 15...Ba6 16.Bxf6 Nxf6 17.Qa2 (I.Danilov-C.Florescu, Bucharest 1998) and now instead of the game's 17...Qe5 , Black can step up the pressure on c4 with 17...Ng4!? 18.Rxb6 Ne5 .

13...Re8!?

ANSWER to Exercise: Zhang Zhong chooses to over-protect the e4-pawn. This eventually works out well in the game although I'm not 100% convinced it's the best move in view of Sasikiran's reply.

Notice that 13...b6 , although generally a move Black wants to play, does nothing to prevent White's idea after 13.O-O , namely 14.Ng3! . This position now looks good for White. For example, 14...Ba6 ( 14...g5? no longer works as 15.Nf5 Qe5 16.Nxh6+ Kg7 17.Bg3 is winning for White ) 15.Nxe4 Bxc4 16.Rd1 g5 17.Bg3 Nxe4 18.Bxe4 , G.Gnichtel-T.Baerwinkel, Mehlingen 1999.

On reflection, I feel that Black's best plan here is 13...g5 14.Bg3 Ne5! .

This virtually forces White to trade off his dark-squared bishop due to the pressure on c4, and this, as we've already seen, is a desirable minor piece trade for Black:

(a) 15.Bxe4? Nxc4 simultaneously hits e4 and threatens ...Nd2.

(b) 15.Qb3 b6 16.a4 Ba6 (forcing the trade) 17.Bxe5 Qxe5 can be compared to 13.Bxf6 above. Although not quite as favourable for Black, it's still fine for him. J.Noble-K.Rodriguez, correspondence 2006, continued 18.Ng3 h5!? 19.f4! ( White must fight back on the kingside; 19.Rfe1 h4 20.Nf1 would be pretty grim ) 19...exf3 20.Rxf3 Ng4 21.Bf5 Nxh2 22.Kxh2 h4 23.Rh1 Bc8 24.Kh3 hxg3 25.Qb1 Bxf5+ 26.Rxf5 Qg7 1/2-1/2. After 27.Kxg3 Rae8 28.Kf3 f6 the position is equal.

Let's return to the position in the game after 13...Re8 .

EXERCISE: Try to find a logical response by White to 13...Re8 .

13...Re8 is certainly justified if White plays 14.Ng3 which can now be strongly met by 14...Qe5! threatening both ...g5 and the pawn on c3.

ANSWER:

14.Ba4!

This bishop move, which we saw previously, really suggests itself now. It gains in strength because of the pin on the rook.

14...g5

QUESTION: Does Black have to chase the bishop and weaken his kingside? What about 14...b6 planning ...Ba6 here?

ANSWER: 14...b6 15.Ng3! is again strong for White. In any case ...b6 is less attractive with White's bishop on a4, with ideas of Bc6 and meeting ...Ba6 with Bb5.

One of the reasons I wasn't totally convinced by 13...Re8 was that Black has to play ...g5 in any case, as otherwise it's difficult to come up with a constructive plan (readers can try!). Also, Black will probably have to move his rook again soon in order to break the pin. Having said all this, I haven't yet been able to come up with a significant improvement over White's play or a concrete reason why Black should avoid it!

15.Bg3 Nh5!

Planning ...f5.

16.f4!?

Sasikiran strikes on the kingside. In view of what happens in the game, there's an argument for delaying f2-f4 and first playing 16.h3 f5 17.Bh2 , with an unclear position.

16...exf3 17.Rxf3

EXERCISE: Try to establish what is troubling Black and find a way to deal with this.

ANSWER:

17...Rf8!!

If you chose this move you are showing excellent understanding of the position! 17...Rf8 is psychologically not an easy move to make for two reasons:

1. The rook moves from an active open file to a passive square behind a pawn.

2. Black only played ...Rf8-e8 a few moves ago; now he is moving it back again.

However, a player of Zhang Zhong's class is able both to forget what happened in the past and to see the bigger picture. To improve his position Black must break the pin on the d7-knight as quickly as possible, and all other considerations – like moving the rook back to where it was – are secondary.

Something Black must avoid is the temptation to attack the f3-rook and expose the e3-pawn with 17...g4? . After 18.Rf2 Black has succeeded only in weakening his position further. Besides, 18...Qxe3 19.Bxd6 is bad and 18...Nxg3 19.Nxg3 Qxe3? 20.Nf5! is even worse.

18.Bc2

This doesn't work out well, but after 18.Bxd7 Qxd7 19.e4 Black is in time to organize his forces with 19...Qe7! intending ...Bg4.

18...Nb6!

Proving that ...b6 and ...Ba6 isn't the only way to attack c4.

19.Qd1 Bg4!

Black must avoid 19...Nxc4?? 20.Qd3 .

20.Qd3 f5 21.Rff1 Rae8

What a difference from the previous diagram! Zhang Zhong has managed to fully coordinate his forces and White has been driven backwards. Black enjoys a clear advantage.

22.Bf2 f4! 23.Rae1

If 23.e4 then 23...f3! 24.gxf3 Rxf3 25.Be3 Nxc4! 26.Rxf3 Ne5 wins.

23...Bf5 24.Qxf5!

This is a better practical chance than submitting with 24.Qd1 fxe3 .

Game 36
Simmelink,J - Percze,J [E31]
Correspondence, 2002

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Bg5 h6 5.Bh4 c5 6.d5 d6 7.e3 Bxc3+ 8.bxc3 e5 9.f3

As we saw in the previous game, 9.Bd3 can be answered by 9...e4!? hitting the bishop. With 9.f3 White prepares Bd3 by discouraging ...e4.

QUESTION: Going back a couple of moves, could White play 7.f3 , giving himself the option of playing e2-e4 in one move?

If Black plays this way, he should almost certainly choose 10...Bc8 , e.g. 11.Bd3 Nbd7 12.Ne2 Nf8 13.Bf2 Ng6 14.g3 Bd7 15.Rb1 Qc7 , R.Akesson-A.Ornstein, Uppsala 1985, ( rather than 10...Bh7 , e.g. 11.Rb1 b6 12.Bd3 Nbd7 13.Ne2 Qe7 14.Bc2 g5 15.Bf2 , V.Milov-N.De Firmian, Biel 1994 ). As we've witnessed already (remember Spassky-Short!), the bishop can quite easily get blocked out of the game if it stays on the b1-h7 diagonal after e3-e4.

Key Notes

1. Even though White gets doubled c-pawns in both lines, the Leningrad Variation differs considerably from the Saemisch. Due to various reasons it's much more difficult for Black to exert any meaningful pressure on the c4-pawn, which remains a weakness but not a chronic one. On the other hand, Black gets a much bigger share of the centre and he is very solid on the kingside. Indeed, he often uses action on the kingside as a major source of counterplay (see Wiersma-Grooten and Simmelink-Percze).

2. b1-h7 is a key diagonal and Black should look for opportunities to seize control of it with ...Bf5, especially if White is unable to play e3-e4 as a quick response (for example after 9.Nf3 in Wiersma-Grooten).

3. When White does play e3-e4 to attack the bishop on f5, its most desirable retreat is on the c8-h3 diagonal, usually to d7 or c8. If the bishop is forced back to g6 or h7, Black should try to arrange a rapid ...f5 to reactivate the bishop.

4. The pawn advance ...e4 is often a good idea, but it must be treated carefully. It gives Black greater space and an outpost on e5, but it also sharpens the position and offers more opportunities for White as well as Black.

5. Remember the ...Kd8-c7 plan because in many positions it's a good idea. This king manoeuvre allows Black to get his king to a safe square without having to commit his d7-knight and c8-bishop. On c7 the king plays a useful defensive role, protecting the d6- and b7-pawns.
