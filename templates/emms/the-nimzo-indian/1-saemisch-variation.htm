Chapter One. Saemisch Variation: 4.a3

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4

Let's begin our Nimzo-Indian journey by seeing what happens if White attacks the bishop straightaway:

4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3

4.a3 is known as the Saemisch Variation. Instead of ignoring the bishop on b4, White immediately forces Black to exchange it for the knight. This is certainly quite a radical solution. White uses up a move (a2-a3), one he could otherwise have spent on developing, to force Black to do something he's quite happy to do - inflict White with doubled c-pawns. The advantage from White's point of view is that at least the situation is resolved quickly and he can now focus on other goals. Nevertheless, the Saemisch Variation isn't seen as a major threat to the Nimzo-Indian and isn't hugely popular at the moment.

QUESTION: Then why begin with the Saemisch?

ANSWER: In one way the Saemisch could still be viewed as a critical test of the Nimzo-Indian. White is basically questioning Black's whole opening strategy. He thinks so little of Black's "threat" to take on c3 he is willing to expend a whole move to force Black to carry it out!

Furthermore, I feel that studying the sharp imbalanced positions which the Saemisch Variation provides is a good starting point, and also a basic testing ground for would-be Nimzo-Indian players. If you don't enjoy the positions where you are fighting against the doubled c-pawns, it's possible that the Nimzo-Indian is simply not the opening for you. Equally, it's exactly these types of position which initially attract many players to the opening.

Black has a number of good ways of playing against the Saemisch. For this book I've chosen to study 5...b6 , which I feel is the most principled and perhaps the most ambitious way for Black to start the fight against the doubled pawns. Before I give anything more away, let's move on to the first game!

Game 1
Labensky,I - Brodsky,M [E28]
Ukrainian Championship, Rivne, 2005

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 b6 6.f3

EXERCISE: Where should Black develop his light-squared bishop: b7 or a6?

6.f3 is White's most popular and most ambitious move. Basically he is looking to create an imposing centre as quickly possible, by playing e2-e4. Black's previous move, 5...b6 , didn't directly attack the centre (in comparison to say 5...d5 ) so White has free rein to do this.

ANSWER:

6...Ba6!

QUESTION: Why is 6...Ba6 better than 6...Bb7 ?

ANSWER: Brodsky is not just developing his pieces; he's developing them with a specific plan – to target White's weak pawn on c4. If Black doesn't exploit this opportunity to do so, the reasoning behind 3...Bb4 and 4...Bxc3+ is lost.

For example, 6...Bb7?! doesn't look such a bad move, but after, say, 7.e4 d6 8.Bd3 Nbd7 9.Ne2 O-O 10.O-O Black is somewhat passively placed, in that it's difficult to create real counterplay. Meanwhile, White is free to build up an initiative on the kingside. 10...c5 11.Ng3 Rc8 12.Bb2 Qc7 13.f4! Rfd8 14.e5! Ne8 15.Qe2 , as in A.Chilov-E.Pandavos, Kavala 1999, is the kind of grim position Black must do his utmost to avoid.

7.e4

EXERCISE: Where should Black develop his queen's knight: directly to c6, or to d7 after ...d6?

ANSWER:

7...Nc6!

The best move, and consistent with all of Black's previous play. Remember Black's plan of targeting the c4-pawn? The knight is headed for a5 where it can do just this.

7...d6 8.Bd3 Nbd7 is solid, but again Black has fewer chances to obtain quick counterplay this way. ( Notice that 8...Nc6?? provides a good reminder not to mix plans. Here it's a blunder due to 9.Qa4! Bb7 10.d5 winning a piece. )

8.Bd3

8.Bg5 and 8.e5 are considered in the next two games.

8...Na5!

Brodsky single-mindedly continues his attack on the c4-pawn.

QUESTION: Shouldn't Black finish his development first, say with 8...O-O , before he does anything else?

ANSWER: Black could castle here, and this could even lead to positions discussed in the next chapter ( 4.f3 O-O ). But by attacking c4 as quickly as possible Black is trying to take the initiative. By having to meet the threats to c4 White's own developing plans are stalled.

9.e5

White could defend the c4-pawn immediately, but it's obviously very tempting to advance in the centre and force the f6-knight to move again.

If 9.Qe2 a typical sequence is 9...d6 10.Nh3 .

EXERCISE: The c4-pawn is attacked twice and defended twice. Can you see a mini-plan for Black to attack the pawn for a third time? (Hint: It takes two move moves.)

ANSWER: Black can utilize his queen: 10...Qd7!? preparing ...Qa4, or possibly ...Qc6. Remember this idea, because it crops up in many similar positions. White could respond with 11.Rb1 so as to answer ...Qa4 with Rb4. Here are two contrasting examples from this complex position:

(a) 11...O-O-O 12.Nf2 e5?! 13.c5! Bxd3 14.Nxd3 exd4 15.cxb6 axb6 16.cxd4 Kb7 17.O-O was very nice for White in D.Bocharov-B.Predojevic, Moscow 2011:

1. His centre is strong;

2. The weak c4-pawn has been eliminated; and

3. He enjoys promising attacking chances against Black's king.

(b) 11...c5 is more thematic. First, by preventing Rb4 Black can now consider ...Qa4 again. Second, Black can now bring another piece to bear down on the c4-pawn, with ...Rc8 lining up ...cxd4 to uncover the rook. After 12.O-O ( instead, 12.e5!? dxe5 13.dxe5 Ng8 14.Nf2! planning Ne4 looks more challenging to me ) 12...Qa4! 13.Bf4 Nxc4 14.d5 e5 15.Bg5 Nd7 16.Nf2 f6 17.Bc1 b5 White didn't have enough play for the pawn in V.Belous-V.Fedoseev, St Petersburg 2011.

Returning to the position after 9.Qe2 , Black has a less critical but perhaps safer option in 9...Nb3 .

The point of this move is simply to swap off one of White's bishops after 10.Rb1 , with 10...Nxc1 11.Rxc1 .

QUESTION: Doesn't this reduce the pressure on the c4-pawn?

ANSWER: Yes it does, but on the positive side White has been stripped of one of his major attacking pieces, so Black needn't be so worried about a kingside attack. And of course those weaknesses on the queenside won't go away. After 11...O-O 12.e5 Nh5 ( or 12...Ne8 ) 13.Nh3 Qh4+ the position looks fine for Black.

Let's return to the game and to the position after Labensky's choice of 9.e5 :

QUESTION: Which square should Black's knight choose: h5 or g8?

ANSWER:

9...Ng8!

This retreat could be viewed as the sacrifice Black has to make in order to create such a rapid attack on c4.

Initially 9...Nh5 looks quite attractive, as Black threatens both the c4-pawn and a nasty check on h4. However, 10.Nh3! is a strong reply for White, meeting the threat with a threat of his own. Can you see it?

If Black takes on c4 White can carry out his idea: 10...Bxc4? ( 10...Qh4+ is stronger, but 11.Kf1! threatens to trap the queen with Bg5 and still causes problems; e.g. 11...h6 12.Nf2! and if 12...Bxc4? White again has 13.Bxc4 Nxc4 14.g4! ) 11.Bxc4 Nxc4 12.g4! Qh4+ 13.Nf2 and the knight on h5 is lost.

10.Qa4

10.Qe2 can be answered the same way: 10...c5 and if 11.dxc5? Black plays the clever 11...Nb3! 12.Rb1 Nxc5 with a great position. The knight stands on a fine outpost on c5, but Black could just simply take the bishop on d3 and then pile up on c4 with ...Rc8, ...Qh4+ etc.

10...c5

Let's assess 10...c5 , a key Nimzo-Indian move:

1. It fixes the c4-pawn, although at the moment the pin on the d3-bishop was enough to do this.

2. It gives Black more space in which to operate on the queenside.

3. It allows Black to exert more pressure on c4 with ...Qc7 and ...Rc8.

4. It attack's White's centre: the d4-pawn and, less directly, the e5-pawn.

5. White is given the chance to play dxc5 if the right opportunity arises. If the position opens up, his two bishops could benefit.

6. Black's dark squares, in particular d6, have been weakened.

Black always needs to keep these considerations in mind when deciding whether or not to play ...c5. In this particular instance, I would say the positives heavily outweigh the negatives.

EXERCISE: Can you find a tactic for Black if White plays 11.dxc5 ?

11.Be3

ANSWER: 11.dxc5 can be met by 11...Bxc4! 12.Bxc4 Qh4+! regaining the piece with some advantage; e.g. 13.g3 Qxc4 14.Qxc4 Nxc4 15.cxb6 axb6 16.f4 Ne7 17.Nf3 Nd5 when both of Black's knights are actively placed, whereas White's c1-bishop is a miserable piece. Black will continue pressing for light-square dominance with moves such as ...Ke7, ...Rfc8 and ...Ra4.

11...Qc7

Brodsky defends c5, attacks e5 (thus discouraging dxc5) and also sets up an X-ray threat to the c4-pawn with ...cxd4.

12.Rc1

Labensky defends against ...cxd4 ideas, as now after cxd4 the rook protects c4. All well and good, but the plan is flawed as we shall see in a couple of moves.

Objectively White's best bet here may be to make things messy with 12.dxc5! . One possible continuation is 12...Qxe5 13.Kf2 Qxc3 which just looks good for Black, but White has a remarkable resource in 14.Ne2! and if 14...Qxd3 then 15.Rad1 Qxc4 16.Qxd7+ Kf8 17.Qd6+ forces a draw by perpetual check: 17...Ne7 18.Qd8+! would be embarrassing!

12...Ne7 13.Ne2

EXERCISE: Can you find a very strong idea for Black here?

ANSWER:

13...Qc6!

Brodsky's little queen move has devastating consequences. White can no longer hold on to the c4-pawn. Because White's whole strategy has been to protect c4 before trying anything active, once the c4-pawn does go his position falls apart – he is too passively placed to expect any real compensation.

14.Qc2

If 14.Qxc6 Nexc6 and the c4-pawn drops next move. The problem for White is that 15.dxc5 is very strongly met by 15...Nxe5! . This is why Brodsky played ...Ne7 before ...Qc6, so he could recapture with this knight and put pressure on e5.

14...Bxc4

Not only winning a pawn, but also gaining some key squares, for example d5 for the knight. Black's advantage is already close to decisive, and I won't go into too much detail over the remaining moves.

15.dxc5 Nd5! 16.Bf2 bxc5 17.Bxc4

17.Bxh7 loses to 17...Bxe2 18.Kxe2 g6 19.Bxg6 Nf4+ .

17...Nxc4 18.Qe4 Nde3

The ending is very good for Black despite his extra pawn getting doubled.

19.Qxc6 dxc6 20.Nf4 O-O-O 21.Bxe3 Nxe3 22.Ke2 Nc4!

QUESTION: 22...Nd5 looked logical too. Why did Black choose 22...Nc4 ?

ANSWER: Black's knight on c4 hits e5 and a3, and is much stronger than its counterpart. A key general point to remember is that, whether Black wins or exchanges White's c4-pawn, the square it leaves behind tends to become a strong outpost for Black's pieces, especially a knight.

22...Nd5 23.Nxd5 cxd5 is obviously good for Black, but not as straightforward as the game.

23.Nd3 Rd5 24.f4 Kc7!

Excellent technique. Brodsky gradually improves his position and for the moment leaves his knight on the monster c4-square.

The immediate 24...Nxa3? would carelessly allow White back into the game with 25.c4! Rd4 26.Ra1 Nxc4 27.Rxa7 .

25.Rhf1 Rb8 26.Rc2

After 26.Rb1 Rbd8 27.Rfd1 it's the right time to take on a3: 27...Nxa3 28.Ra1 Nb5 29.c4 Nc3+ wins.

White can temporarily save the a-pawn with 26.a4 , but then 26...Rb3! leaves him completely tied down.

26...Nxa3 27.Ra2 Rb3 28.Rc1 a5 29.Nb2 c4 30.Nd1 Nb5

The a-pawn can't be taken because of ...Nxc3+.

31.Ra4 Ra3 32.Rxa3 Nxa3 33.Ra1 Nb5 34.Ke1 a4 35.Ne3

EXERCISE: Find the best way to win for Black.

If 35.Rxa4 Black had 35...Rxd1+! and 36...Nxc3+ .

ANSWER:

35...Nxc3!

Black is not afraid to trade advantages if it means he can win more easily.

35...Rd3! 36.Nxc4 Nxc3 was also good enough, but Black had to avoid giving White needless counterplay with 35...Rc5? 36.Rxa4 Nxc3 37.Ra7+ .

36.Nxc4

After 36.Nxd5+ cxd5 White has no chance against a dominant knight and three onrushing queenside pawns, especially since the rook has no way of getting active. For example, 37.Kd2 d4 38.Kc2 Kc6 39.Rf1 a3 40.f5 exf5 41.Rxf5 a2 42.Kb2 d3 43.Rf1 Nd5 44.Kxa2 Ne3 ( or 44...d2 . )

36...Rd4

Winning a third pawn.

37.Nb2 Rxf4 38.Ra3 Re4+ 39.Kf2 Re2+ 40.Kf3

After 40.Kf3 Rxb2 41.Rxc3 Black wins immediately with 41...Rb3! .

Black wins so many games like this in the Saemisch Nimzo-Indian, simply because White doesn't have to make any obvious-looking mistakes in the opening for it to happen. White makes a few logical developing moves, protects c4 as best he can, and then... bang! 13...Qc6 and Black has a winning position!

Game 2
Yang,D - Ippolito,D [E24]
SPICE Spring Invitational, Lubbock, 2010

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 b6 6.f3 Nc6 7.e4 Ba6 8.Bg5

8.Bg5 is much more aggressive than 8.Bd3 . Whereas 8.Bd3 prepared defence of the c4-pawn with Qe2, 8.Bg5 sets up attacking ideas such as e4-e5. With Black's dark-squared bishop off the board, the pin on the f6-knight becomes a major issue for Black to deal with in numerous lines.

8...h6 9.Bh4

Of course Black is happy if White releases all the pressure with 9.Bxf6? Qxf6 .

9...Na5

Ippolito chooses the most direct approach – attack c4! Black need not worry about e4-e5, as ...g5 saves the piece.

QUESTION: What happens if Black first completes development by castling kingside?

ANSWER: Early kingside castling is usually inadvisable in this line, and certainly in this position! If 9...O-O?! 10.e5! (forcing a weakness) 10...g5 11.Bf2 Nh5 12.h4! and Black's king is stuck on a side he would really rather not be. White obviously enjoys good attacking chances here.

A decent alternative to 9...Na5 is 9...g5 10.Bf2 Qe7 preparing ...0-0-0. For example, 11.h4 O-O-O 12.d5!? Na5 13.c5!? Bxf1 14.d6! cxd6 15.cxd6 Qf8 16.Kxf1 Ne8 17.e5 f6! 18.Qd3 Kb7 19.Rb1 fxe5 20.Qe4+ Nc6 21.Bxb6! axb6 22.Qe3 Nxd6 23.Qxb6+ Ka8 24.Qa6+ Na7 25.Rb6 Rb8 ( 25...g4! ) 26.Rxd6 g4 27.Rxd7 Qc5 with a very complicated position, M.Carlsen-P.Leko, Monte Carlo (blindfold) 2007.

10.e5

A major alternative here is 10.Qa4 , so we should take a closer look at it.

EXERCISE: Weigh up the pros and cons of 10...c5 and 10...Qc8 . Which do you think is the best move?

ANSWER: Black has to continue developing and organizing an attack on c4. We've already seen that ...c5 is a way to do this, but we've also touched upon its weaknesses. If Black plays 10...c5 here, White will continue 11.e5! g5 (forced) 12.Bf2 Nh5 13.h4! . In the space of a few moves, Black has seriously weakened his dark squares by playing both ...c5 and ...g5. I'm not saying that this position is necessarily bad for Black, only that it compares unfavourably with, for example, the ...c5 plan seen in our previous game.;

In practice Black has chosen 10...Qc8! .

This move is dual-purpose:

1. It takes the sting out of e4-e5 – Black can now simply move the knight rather than play ...g5.

2. It introduces the plan of ...Qb7-c6 which, as we've already seen, can be a very effective idea!

Here are a few practical examples:

(a) Black is not afraid to accept doubled pawns: 11.Bxf6 gxf6 12.Nh3 Qb7 13.Qb4?! ( 13.Nf2 Qc6 14.Qxc6 dxc6 would transpose to the lines after 11.Nh3 ) 13...Rg8 14.Kf2 c5! (an excellent temporary pawn sacrifice, activating Black's pieces) 15.dxc5 Rc8! 16.cxb6 Nxc4 17.Nf4 ( 17.bxa7?? Qxa7+ would lead to a quick mate – Rybka says 8 moves! ) 17...Rc6! 18.Bxc4 Rxc4 19.Qb2 axb6 20.Qd2 Qc7 21.g4 f5! 22.Nh5 Ke7 (Black is already better, but now White blunders) 23.gxf5? Qc5+! and White resigned in M.Hoffmann-P.Harikrishna, Caleta 2011, in view of 24.Qe3 Rg2+! .

(b) 11.Bd3 Qb7 (intending ...Qc6!) 12.e5 Nd5! 13.cxd5 Bxd3 14.Rd1 Qa6 , P.Rainieri de Luca-V.Vehi Bach, Barcelona 2003. Black hasn't won the c4-pawn, but once again he controls the key square, and he continued to do so: 15.dxe6 fxe6 16.Nh3 g5 17.Bg3 O-O-O 18.Nf2 Bg6 19.Rc1 . Now White threatens c3-c4 so Black plays 19...Qc4! 20.Qxc4 Nxc4 with a slight advantage: he has the stronger minor pieces and pawns.

(c) 11.Nh3 might be White's best option. 11...Qb7 and now:

(c1) 12.Nf2 Qc6 13.Qxc6 ( 13.Qb4!? ) 13...dxc6 14.Bxf6 gxf6 15.Ng4 f5 ( 15...Ke7 16.Ne3! ) 16.exf5 exf5 (both sides have weaknesses but I slightly prefer Black here) 17.Ne5 ( 17.Ne3 f4 18.Ng4 O-O-O 19.O-O-O Bxc4 was good for Black in J.Waxman-W.Browne, Fort Worth 1984 ) 17...f6 18.Ng6 Rg8 19.Nh4 f4 20.Kf2 Kd7 21.Nf5 h5 22.Re1 Rae8 23.Rxe8 Rxe8 24.Ng7 Rh8 ( 24...Re3!? ) 25.g3 Bxc4 26.Bh3+ Ke7 27.gxf4 Kf7 28.Nf5 Re8 29.Ne3 Bb5 1/2-1/2 V.McCambridge-G.Sosonko, New York 1984.

(c2) 12.d5!? prevents Black's ...Qc6 plan, so it must be a consideration. Black needs to avoid what happens in the following game: 12...c6 13.Rd1 O-O 14.e5 Nh7 15.Be7 Rfe8 16.d6 Nf8 17.Nf4 c5 18.Bd3 Qc6 19.Qc2! Bxc4 20.Be4 Bb3 21.Qf2! Qb5 22.Qg3! Bxd1 23.Bf6 g5 ( or 23...g6 24.Qh4 Kh7 25.Bg5 ) 24.h4 Bc2 25.hxg5 and Black resigned in M.Pracejus-P.Ellinger, correspondence 1997, in view of 25...Qb1+ 26.Kf2 Qxh1 27.gxh6+ Ng6 28.Nxg6 .

A crushing kingside attack by White! ( Black's long-winded version of the ...Qc6 plan seems to be too slow here, so instead of 13...O-O I would be much more tempted to open the position with 13...cxd5! , trying to exploit Black's slight lead in development and the opening of the c-file )

Let's return to the game and the position after Yang's more direct 10.e5 .

10...g5

The only move.

11.Bf2

11.exf6? gxh4 is good for Black, who just follows up with ...Qxf6.

11...Nh5!

This is stronger than 11...Ng8 which is met by 12.Qa4! followed by a quick h2-h4. Notice that the pawn on g5 makes ...Nh5 more attractive – with f4 protected and another square available on g7, there's very little danger of the knight being trapped.

12.h4

Once again 12.Qa4 to protect c4 is a possibility for White, and here Black has responded with 12...f5!? .

QUESTION: What is the point of 12...f5 ? Shouldn't Black just develop, say with 12...Qe7 preparing 13...O-O-O ?

ANSWER: Having induced ...g5, White's main pawn break to gain activity (or counterplay, if we feel that Black is the aggressor!) is h2-h4, releasing the rook on its home square. 12...f5 not only gains space on the kingside, it also prepares to meet h4 with ...g4!, seizing more space and blocking in White's rook. Some examples:

(a) 13.h4 g4! is fine for Black. If 14.fxg4 fxg4 15.Qc2 Black can sacrifice a pawn: 15...Qe7 16.Qe4 Bb7 17.Qxg4 Qf7 followed by ...0-0-0, ...Rhg8 etc, with tremendous pressure on both sides on the board.

(b) 13.O-O-O Qe7 14.h4 O-O-O ( the immediate 14...g4! is also good ) 15.Kc2 g4! and White is suffering. In the game G.Markzon-G.Kacheishvili, Philadelphia 2010, he tried to mix things up with 16.d5? but Black soon won: 16...exd5 17.Rxd5 Bb7 18.Bd4? Ng3! and White resigned.

(c) 13.exf6 Qxf6 reduces White's centre and activates Black's queen, and 14.Nh3 Nf4 15.Nxf4 gxf4 is already a bit better for Black. Let's follow A.Deze-G.Dizdar, Sibenik 1986, for some model play by Black: 16.h4 h5 17.c5 Bxf1 18.Kxf1 Rg8 19.Rh2 Qf5 20.Rd1 Qd5! .

QUESTION: Why the exclamation mark for 20...Qd5 ?

ANSWER: We saw in our previous game that c4 is a key square, even after the white pawn has disappeared. Control of this square is vital, and now Black's knight is able to influence the game with great effect: 21.Re1 Nc4 22.Qc2 bxc5 23.Qc1 cxd4 24.cxd4 Rf8 25.Re2 Rb8 26.Kg1 Rb3 and Black went on to convert his huge advantage.

12...f5

QUESTION: We saw ...f5 in the note above, and it was played so that Black could answer h4 with ...g4. But why here? Isn't it too late for this?

ANSWER: It's true that Black doesn't have time for ...g4, but 12...f5 still slows down White's play on the kingside. Or, to put it another way, it allows Black to fight back on that wing.

That said, there appears to be little wrong with just taking the c-pawn. After 12...Bxc4 13.hxg5 Qxg5 ( 13...hxg5?? loses a piece due to the pin, after 14.Bxc4 Nxc4 15.g4 ) 14.Nh3 Qe7 15.Bxc4 Nxc4 16.g4 Ng7 17.Qa4 Na5 18.Nf4 Black has been driven back and White undoubtedly enjoys some compensation, but it's probably only enough for the pawn deficit. One interesting line here is 18...h5 19.Bh4 hxg4! 20.Bxe7 Rxh1+ 21.Kf2 Rxa1 22.Bf6 Ra2+ 23.Kg1 Ra1+ 24.Kf2 Ra2+ with perpetual check, as given by Gavrikov.

QUESTION: Black is always taking on c4 with the bishop rather than the knight. Why is this so?

ANSWER: Occasionally Black takes on c4 with the knight, but generally the bishop capture is better, for the following reasons:

1 ...Bxc4 usually leads to an exchange of light-squared bishops, removing one of White's bishop pair and accentuating Black's control of the light squares, and of c4 in particular;

2. The knight is the piece which benefits most from the c4-outpost, and after an exchange of bishops the c4-square becomes available to the knight; and, perhaps most importantly

3. ...Nxc4 often puts Black in an awkward self-pin, with the knight being unable to move without exposing the a6-bishop.

Expanding on the final point, in this position 12...Nxc4? would simply be a blunder.

White just responds with 13.Qa4! winning a piece!

Let's return to the position in the game after Ippolito's 12...f5 :

13.hxg5

This looks the most consistent and logical move, especially since Black is forced to capture with the queen to avoid losing a piece to g2-g4. Let's take a look at a few alternatives:

(a) 13.exf6? Qxf6 is again too accommodating as it allows Black to develop quickly. Let's see how chess legend Tigran Petrosian built up a dominating position from here: 14.c5 Bxf1 15.Kxf1 g4! (now the rook on h1 looks silly) 16.Qd3 O-O 17.Re1 Nf4 18.Qc2 Nc4 (once more the knight utilizes the c4-square) 19.g3 ( 19.fxg4 Nd3! ) 19...Qf5! 20.Rc1 ( 20.Qxf5 allows a beautiful double-knight mate: 20...Nd2# ) 20...Qd3+ 21.Qxd3 Nxd3 22.Rd1 Ndb2 23.Ra1 gxf3 and Black won easily in V.Simagin-T.Petrosian, Moscow 1950.

(b) 13.c5 Bxf1 14.Kxf1 .

EXERCISE: Can you find how Black can complete his development in an active way?

ANSWER: Black can play 14...Qc8! .

Now he is ready to play either ...Qb7 or ...Qa6+ followed by ...0-0-0 connecting the rooks. Black quickly gained a decisive advantage in S.Volkov-I.Lysyj, Tomsk 2006, after 15.Rb1 Qa6+ 16.Qe2? Nc4! 17.Rb4 Ne3+! 18.Ke1 Nxg2+ – not bad work considering White was a Grandmaster rated over 2600!;

Also, the tactics favour Black if White tries anything ambitious: 15.g4 fxg4 16.fxg4 Qb7! 17.Rh2 ( or 17.Qf3 Ng3+! 18.Bxg3 Rf8 ) 17...Nf4 18.hxg5 Nc4! and Black, with his queen and monster knights, enjoys a strong initiative. One nice line is 19.gxh6 Qe4 20.Ra2 ( 20.Nf3 Qxf3! ) 20...Rf8! 21.h7 Ng2! 22.h8Q Nce3+ 23.Ke2 Qxg4+ and mate follows quickly.

(c) 13.g3!? is a sneaky move, creating a hidden threat of 14.hxg5 Qxg5 15.f4! . Black met this in F.Berkes-Z.Almasi, Kazincbarcika 2005, with 13...f4!? ( 13...Kf7 connecting queen and rook so that Black can meet hxg5 with ...hxg5 is also possible ) and after 14.hxg5 Qxg5 15.Nh3 Qg8! 16.gxf4 ( 16.g4 is probably better, but Black still has enough counterplay after 16...Ng3 17.Bxg3 fxg3 , e.g. 18.c5 Bxf1 19.Kxf1 h5 ) the simple 16...Bxc4 17.Bxc4 Nxc4 (Gavrikov) would have been fine for Black.

13...Qxg5 14.Nh3 Qg6

15.g4!?

After this move, which certainly isn't the only option, the position gets very complicated with both kings becoming exposed.

15...fxg4 16.Bd3

Forced, as 16.fxg4?? loses to 16...Qe4+! . But now Black won't have to retreat his knight to the miserable g7-square and counterplay on the kingside is assured.

16...Qf7 17.fxg4 Nf4! 18.Nxf4 Qxf4 19.Qe2

In an earlier game White threw in a tempting check, with 19.Bg6+ .

Ippolito boldly plays for the win. Black would ideally like to castle queenside, but Ippolito was probably concerned about the line 19...O-O-O 20.Be3! Qg3+ 21.Bf2! Qf4 22.Be3! when he must either retreat his queen or accept a repetition of moves.

20...Bb7 21.Rg1?

EXERCISE: Find the move which gives Black a decisive advantage.

ANSWER:

A bolt from the blue! Of course the rook is threatened, but possibly what Yang overlooked before now was the devastating threat of ...Nc1. I know from painful experience that knight moves to empty squares on the eighth rank are notoriously easy to miss.

1.c4 e6 2.d4 Nf6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 b6

Another frequent move order to reach the mainline position is 5...Nc6 6.f3 b6 7.e4 Ba6 .

6.f3

This is a good place to summarize White's main alternatives:

(a) Let's begin with 6.e3 .

QUESTION: Isn't this move a little slow? If White's most promising plan is to advance in the centre, surely he should be trying to play e2-e4 in one go?

ANSWER: It would make sense, but actually it's not quite a simplistic as that.

For example, let's see what might happen if Black continues with the normal plan: 6...Ba6 7.Bd3! Nc6 8.e4 Na5 and we have reached a familiar position (from Labensky-Brodsky) except White's f-pawn is back on f2 rather than f3. Paradoxically, this "missing" tempo actually favours White, because after 9.e5! Ng8 there's no pawn to block the queen, and 10.Qg4! is strong.

Black is forced to make an undesirable concession here with either 10...Kf8 or 10...g6 . It's worth remembering that the role of f2-f3 is to force through e2-e4, but the pawn itself is of limited value on f3 and sometimes even a hindrance!

In view of this variation, Black's most accurate move is probably 6...Bb7 and after 7.f3 Nc6 8.e4 he could just play 8...Ba6 reaching our main line with both sides having played an extra move to get there. There are other options too; for example, 8...d6 (or 8...Na5 ) 9.Bd3 Na5 10.Ne2 ( 10.Nh3!? ; 10.Bg5!? ) 10...Qd7 11.O-O Ba6 12.Ng3 Bxc4 13.Bxc4 Nxc4 14.Qe2 Qc6 and White had no real play for the missing pawn in S.Gligoric-P.Keres, Zuerich 1953.

QUESTION: What happens after 6...Bb7 if White develops with 7.Nf3 ?

ANSWER: In general Nf3 and the pure Saemisch don't mix very well, as the knight is not in a good position to aid the e4 advance. Here this is especially so, since Black can take a grip of the e4-square very quickly. After 7...Ne4! 8.Bd3 f5 9.Qc2 O-O 10.O-O ,

Black has a firm grip of the position and is at least equal. It's worth pointing out that this resembles another Nimzo-Indian line, 1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3 b6 5.Bd3 Bb7 6.Nf3 Ne4 7.Qc2 f5 8.O-O Bxc3 9.bxc3 O-O but here Black is a tempo up since White has "wasted" a move on a2-a3. 10...d6 intending ...Nd7 would be fine, but in K.Borsuk-Y.Kuzubov, Kramatorsk 2001, Black was rewarded after some ambitious play: 10...Rf6!? 11.Nd2 Rh6! 12.f3 Qh4! 13.fxe4 Qxh2+ 14.Kf2 Rg6 15.Ke1 Rxg2 16.Qb1? ( after 16.exf5 Black has perpetual check, but not necessarily anything better ) 16...Qh4+ 17.Kd1 Qg4+ 18.Ke1 fxe4 19.a4 exd3 20.Qxd3 Nc6 21.Ba3 e5 22.d5 e4 0-1.

(b) 6.Bg5 is obviously a useful move to play if White doesn't yet want to commit in the centre. Play continues 6...h6 7.Bh4 Nc6 . Transpositions are possible here; for example, 8.f3 Ba6 9.e4 reaches Yang-Ippolito (Game 2).

QUESTION: What about 8.Nf3 in this position, instead of 8.f3 ?

ANSWER: The development with Nf3 is more favourable here than it was above (although that's hardly a compliment!). The key differences here are:

1. White's dark-squared bishop is more actively placed; and

2. Black cannot get a firm grip of the e4-square.

Nevertheless, Black has nothing to fear, as long as he remains a little flexible: I feel that a good continuation here is 8...Bb7! ( it's time to postpone the plan of hitting c4, since if 8...Ba6 then 9.Nd2! is a good answer – the knight is well placed on d2 against this plan, protecting c4 and covering e4 too ) 9.Nd2 Qe7 10.e3 g5 11.Bg3 d6 12.h4 O-O-O 13.Be2 Nd7 14.Nb3 f5 with a complex position in which Black's kingside activity means he is by no means worse, E.Bacrot-M.Carlsen, Baku 2008. I should note that this game arose via another variation: 1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Nf3 b6 ( 4...c5 is covered in Chapter 8) 5.Bg5 Bb7 6.Nd2 h6 7.Bh4 Nc6 8.a3 ( 8.e3 is more usual) 8...Bxc3 9.bxc3 .

(c) Finally, I should briefly mention the rare try 6.e4!? which isn't a true gambit since 6...Nxe4 7.Qg4 regains the pawn. Here I feel Black should follow 7...Bb7 8.Qxg7 Qf6 9.Qxf6 Nxf6 with a decent position, R.Schlehoefer-I.Varasdy, Dortmund 1987. Certainly Black should take the centre pawn otherwise White would be getting away with not having to prepare e4.

Now let's return to the main line, 6.f3 .

6...Ba6 7.e4 Nc6 8.e5

EXERCISE: Black must make a familiar decision. Should the knight advance to h5 or retreat to g8?

We've previously considered both 8.Bd3 and 8.Bg5 for White. Here Moskalenko chooses the most forcing move, 8.e5 .

ANSWER:

8...Ng8!

8...Nh5?! is inadvisable on this occasion as the knight lacks stability. White replies 9.Nh3! , taking measures against ...Qh4+, and Black is actually struggling to find a good answer to the threat of 10.g4 winning the knight. 9...f5 doesn't help in view of 10.Bg5! .

9.Nh3!

QUESTION: Why the exclamation mark for this move?

ANSWER: The h3-square is an excellent stepping stone for the knight. From here it can:

1. Advance aggressively to g5, where it hits h7 and also eyes the e4-outpost;

2. Retreat to f2, again eyeing e4.

3. Move to f4 and then possibly h5; and

4. Support Bg5.

Instead 9.Bd3 would transpose to Labensky-Brodsky (Game 1) after 9...Na5 .

9...Na5 10.Qa4

10.Bg5 can be met by 10...f6 . Then 11.Bh4 Qe7 ( or 11...Bxc4 12.Bxc4 Nxc4 13.Qe2 b5 14.O-O with some compensation for White ) 12.f4 ( 12.Qa4!? ) 12...Qf7! (to make way for ...Ne7) 13.Nf2 Ne7 14.exf6 Nf5! was a strong pawn sacrifice in F.Liardet-A.Sokolov, Geneva 1996. After 15.fxg7 Qxg7 16.Bg5 h6 17.Qh5+ Qf7! 18.Be2 O-O! 19.Qxf7+ Rxf7 20.Bh5 hxg5 21.Bxf7+ Kxf7 22.fxg5 Nxc4 Black was in complete control.

10...Qh4+!?

QUESTION: Why did Neverov play this check? What does it achieve?

ANSWER: 10...Qh4+ is quite a clever "spoiling" move. White can deal with the check in a number of ways, but each one has to accept a small concession. There's a bit of risk involved for Black because on occasions the queen doesn't have many squares available, but luckily there always seems to be at least one good one!

10...Ne7 is the most common choice, and this can lead to very sharp positions. Two examples:

(a) 11.Bd3 O-O ( 11...h6 is also possible ) 12.Bg5 ( Milov suggests the unclear sacrifice 12.Ng5 h6 13.Nh7!? Re8 14.Bxh6 ) 12...h6 13.Bh4 d5 14.Bb1 g5 15.Qc2 Ng6 16.Nf4!! gxh4 17.Nxg6 Re8 18.Nh8! led to a famous win for White in A.Kotov-P.Keres, Budapest 1950. However, there are some significant defensive resources in this line; one key improvement is 13...Qe8! (instead of 13...d5 ) 14.Bb1 d6! 15.Qc2 Ng6 , as indicated by Knaak, when White's attack looks like it will simply fall flat.

(b) 11.Ng5 h6 ( I feel that 11...O-O is riskier this time, because of 12.Qc2! ) 12.Ne4 O-O 13.Bf4 Nf5 ( the sneaky 13...Qe8!? is again interesting, intending ...d5 against most things, including 14.Bxh6 d5! ) 14.h4!? (intending g4 without allowing ...Qh4+) 14...d5! 15.cxd5 ( if 15.exd6 cxd6 and ...Rc8 or ...d5 is coming ) 15...Bxf1 16.Kxf1 exd5 17.g4!? Ne7! ( Black must avoid 17...Nxh4 because of 18.Nf6+! gxf6 19.Rxh4 fxe5 20.Rxh6 exf4 21.Qc2 f5 22.gxf5 with a very strong attack ) 18.Ng3 Ng6 19.Bc1 f6! and Black gains good counterplay, M.Sharif-J.Sampouw, Jakarta 1978.

The Delayed Saemisch: 4.e3 O-O 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3

White certainly doesn't have to play a2-a3 as early as the fourth move, and many Saemisch players will sneakily delay it a move or two, waiting for Black to show his hand. In this section we will look at the move order 4.e3 O-O 5.a3 , while in the next chapter we'll cover 4.f3 O-O 5.a3 .

[...]

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e3

4.e3 (the Rubinstein Variation) is one of White's most popular replies to the Nimzo-Indian, and is covered in Chapters 3 and 4. Here, though, White is only using it as a means to transpose to the Saemisch.

4...O-O

QUESTION: Could Black anticipate a Saemisch and play 4...b6 here?

Then 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 would lead us back to our previous games.

ANSWER: Yes he could, but White doesn't have to play ball. Instead he could carry on developing with 5.Bd3 or play 5.Nge2 so that he is ready to recapture on c3 with the knight and avoid the dreaded doubled pawns.

None of this means that 4...b6 is a bad move - in fact, it's a perfectly viable line for Black against 4.e3 . In this book, though, I'm focussing on the main move, 4...O-O .

5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 b6

6...c5 7.Bd3 Nc6 8.Ne2 b6 is considered by most sources to be the main line, but 6...b6 has scored well for Black and has been the choice of many strong grandmasters.

Clearly there are similarities between this line and 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 b6 , but there are also two key differences:

1. Because White has played e2-e3 - instead of f2-f3 and e2-e4 - his progress advancing in the centre is delayed.

2. Black is already committed to castling kingside, something he usually delays or even avoids in the 4.a3 Bxc3+ 5.bxc3 b6 line.

Let's try to work out how these differences influence events in the next few moves.

7.Bd3

EXERCISE: Where should Black develop his bishop: a6 or b7? After 7.Bd3 White is ready to play e3-e4. As we've already seen, if White has already played e2-e3, Bd3 is usually a better choice than f2-f3 to prepare the e-pawn's advance.

If 7.f3?! Ba6! 8.e4 Ne8 9.Bd3 White really would be a whole tempo down on a typical Nimzo-Indian line. Normally it's White to play here after 1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 e6 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.f3 O-O 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Ne8 7.e4 b6 8.Bd3 Ba6 , a line we will study in Chapter 2.

ANSWER:

7...Bb7!

As we saw in the notes to Moskalenko-Neverov (Game 3), if White chooses e3 and Bd3 instead of f3 and e4, a certain amount of flexibility is beneficial to Black. In the long run the bishop may end up on a6. However, for the moment on b7 it prevents e4 for at least one further move and it forces White to act over the threat of ...Bxg2.

QUESTION: But what is wrong with the immediate plan of ...Ba6 and ...Nc6-a5?

ANSWER: Let's see: 7...Ba6?! 8.e4 Nc6 9.Nf3! Ne8 10.Qe2 Na5 11.e5! .

Compared to the normal Saemisch set-up, White has been able to develop much more aggressively, with the knight on its most active square (instead of a pawn!). This means there is much greater danger to Black's kingside than normal. Indeed, there is already a significant threat, which Black failed to spot in the following game: 11...d5? ( 11...h6 should be played) 12.Bxh7+! (a classic Greek Gift sacrifice - this works because a knight is on f3 rather than a pawn) 12...Kxh7 13.Ng5+ Kg6 (or 13...Kg8 14.Qh5 and Black must give up his queen) 14.h4! Qe7 15.h5+ Kh6 16.Qd3! Qxg5 (if 16...Rh8 17.Nxf7 mate) 17.Bxg5+ Kxg5 18.Qe3+ Kg4 19.f3+ with mate next move, Y.Kuzubov-L.Koerholz, Ohrid 2009. Overall, Black should be trying to prevent White from achieving this favourable set-up.

8.f3

Blocking the bishop's diagonal with the f-pawn is White's most common choice here, and now e3-e4 is back on the cards. It's worth noting that if Black later plays ...Ba6 we transpose to a mainline position, with both sides having played one extra move (e2-e3-e4 for White and ...Bc8-b7-a6 for Black), and indeed this line is covered in the next chapter.

Alternatively:

(a) We've already seen from Moskalenko-Neverov that Black is happy to meet 8.Nf3 , especially after 8...Ne4! 9.Qc2 f5! clamping down on the e4-square.

(b) 8.Ne2!? is a rare but very interesting gambit.

QUESTION: Should Black accept the pawn offer?

ANSWER: I feel that he should, for the following reasons:

1. White definitely gains some compensation (e.g. open g-file, time), but this is definitely a "real sacrifice", and as such there's a degree of speculation.

2. Just as importantly, if Black declines to take the pawn he accepts a concession in that White will most likely be able to achieve e4 without having to play f3 (e.g. after 8...Nc6 9.e4 ).

So the critical test is 8...Bxg2 9.Rg1 . Here are some examples from games:

(a) 9...Be4 (it makes sense to trim White's attacking potential by exchanging a pair of bishops) 10.Ng3 Bxd3 11.Qxd3 Nc6 12.e4 Ne8 13.h4 f6 14.Ra2 Kh8 15.f3 Qe7 16.Rag2 Qf7 17.Ne2 Na5 was P.Eljanov-V.Chuchelov, Dresden 2007. Clearly White's active pieces equate to reasonable compensation; but Black's position looks resilient, g7 is well protected, he has no obvious weaknesses and of course has the extra pawn!

(b) 9...Bf3 10.Rg3 Be4 (a subtlety rather than a wasted tempo; with the rook on g3 White's knight can't use that square) 11.f3 Bg6!? .

This bishop retreat is more challenging than exchanging on d3 - Black blocks the g-file and any exchange on g6 would block it further: 12.e4 Nh5 ( 12...Nc6!? ) 13.Rg2 (Richard Palliser gives 13.Rh3 f5 14.e5 d6 "when Black has two minor pieces stranded on the kingside, but also some counterplay against White's centre") 13...d5 14.Bg5 Qd7 15.Qb3 dxe4 16.Bxe4 Nc6 17.Qa4 Bxe4 18.fxe4 f6 19.Be3 Ne5! and by this stage all of White's compensation had fizzled out in H.Ganaus-L.Lenic, Sarajevo 2010.

No doubt there are possible improvements for both sides in these lines, but I still feel Black should take the pawn on g2 and force White to show what he's got.

Let's return to the position after White's usual choice, 8.f3 :

8...Nc6

It's back to our Plan A: ...Nc6-a5 and probably ...Ba6 too.

Black does have a sensible alternative in 8...c5 9.Ne2 Nc6 and I'll refer the reader to Dangerous Weapons: the Nimzo-Indian for coverage. I will just reproduce, without notes, the game E.Geller-M.Euwe, Zuerich 1953, which is one of the classic Nimzo-Indian encounters everyone should be aware of: 10.e4 Ne8 11.O-O Na5 12.Ng3 cxd4 13.cxd4 Rc8 14.f4 Nxc4 15.f5 f6 16.Rf4 b5 17.Rh4 Qb6 18.e5 Nxe5 19.fxe6 Nxd3 20.Qxd3 Qxe6 21.Qxh7+ Kf7 22.Bh6 Rh8 (yes, Euwe really did play this move!) 23.Qxh8 Rc2 24.Rc1 Rxg2+ 25.Kf1 Qb3 26.Ke1 Qf3 and Geller resigned.

If Black wants to try a different plan then 8...Nh5!? looks like a reasonable move here. For example, 9.Nh3 Qh4+ 10.Nf2 f5! 11.O-O Nc6 12.Qc2 (L.Polugaevsky-L.Alster, Marianske Lazne 1959) and now 12...Ne7 and Black can play on the kingside.

9.e4

EXERCISE: Black carries out a key Nimzo-Indian Saemisch idea here. Try to figure out what this might be. (Hint: There have already been a few examples of the idea in the notes, but I've deliberately glossed over it so far!)

ANSWER:

9...Ne8!

The knight's unprovoked retreat to e8 is a vital prophylactic idea which arises in many similar Saemisch positions, and it's one that must certainly be remembered.

Once White achieves e3-e4 the possibility of Bg5 opens up, and we've already seen that the pin on the f6-knight is especially awkward if Black has already castled kingside. If Black ignores this threat he could easily land himself in hot water. For example, 9...Na5 10.Bg5! h6 11.Bh4 Ba6 ( 11...g5 12.Bf2 breaks the pin but leaves the kingside weakened) 12.f4 and the threat of e5 is looming. This is definitely something Black should do his best to avoid.

9...Ne8! prevents the pin, but it also possesses other qualities:

1. The sting is drawn from e4-e5, as it no longer arrives with a threat.

2. The knight might later go to d6, where it increases the pressure on the c4 weakness.

3. Black's f-pawn is free to advance, either to hold up White's attack or to strike in the centre with ...f5.

All in all, not a bad return for one little knight retreat!

10.Nh3!

We saw in the Moskalenko-Neverov game that the h3-square is a more active base for the knight than e2.

10...Na5!?

Key Notes

1. A swift attack on the c4-pawn is a major plan for Black in the Saemisch. The most direct way to start the attack is by ...b6, ...Ba6 and ...Nc6-a5. Further pressure can often be applied by ...Qd7-a4 (after ...d6), or by ...c5 followed by ...Rc8 or ...Qc7.

2. Both players should recognize that the Saemisch is basically a type of a gambit. White accepts a major weakness in his pawn structure, leading to a possible loss of a pawn, in return for advantages such as an imposing pawn centre. Those playing Black must recognize that capturing the c4-pawn is sometimes only the beginning of the battle. Equally, White players must realize that it's often unwise to grimly hang on to the c4-pawn, especially at the expense of active possibilities elsewhere (see Labensky-Brodsky (Game 1), for example).

3. If Black wins the c4-pawn, or even if it is exchanged, afterwards the c4-square frequently becomes a vital outpost. It can be a particular effective outpost for a black knight (see Labensky-Brodsky, Game 1).

4. Black must be ready to meet an early attack on the f6-knight with e4-e5 when, depending on the specific circumstances, the knight goes to either g8 or h5. In Labensky-Brodsky (Game 1) and Moskalenko-Neverov (Game 3), the best option was back to g8, whereas in Yang-Ippolito (Game 2) it was to h5.

5. Due to the absence of Black's dark-squared bishop, Bg5 becomes an important idea for White, especially in conjunction with e4-e5. Black often combats this with either ...h6 and ...g5 or by breaking the pin with ...Qc8 (which also intends ...Qb7-c6). In view of the potential danger caused by Bg5, Black usually delays castling kingside and often avoids it altogether.

6. In the delayed Saemisch with 4.e3 0-0 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3, Black's king is already committed to the kingside. To combat the threat of Bg5 after e3-e4, Black plays the key move ...Ne8! which prevents the pin, draws the sting from the e5 advance and also allows the knight the possibility to join the attack on c4 via d6.
