a2-a3

This prepares the advance of the b-pawn to b4.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Be2 Nbd7 6.Nf3 e5 7.O-O O-O 8.Rb1 Re8 9.d5 Nc5 10.Nd2 a5 11.b3

(position after 11...h7-h5 )

To make progress on the queenside White has to evict the black knight from c5. He does this with 12.a2-a3 followed by b3-b4 (Game A44).

a2-a4

With this move White gains space on the queenside and attacks the b5 square.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 c6 6.Be3

(position after 6...a7-a6 )

To prevent Black's queenside expansion by ...b7-b5 White plays 7.a2-a4 , even though this concedes control of the b4 square after 7...a5 (Game A45).

b2-b3

This move protects the c4 pawn and allows the development of the bishop at b2 or a3.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.g3 g6 3.b3

b2-b4

This move attacks the c5 square and in some cases supports the advance of the pawn to c5.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 O-O 5.Be3 d6 6.f3 Nc6 7.Nge2 a6 8.Qd2 Rb8 9.Nc1 e5 10.d5 Nd4 11.Nb3 Nxb3 12.axb3 c5 13.b4 b6 14.bxc5 bxc5 15.Be2 Nh5 16.O-O f5 17.Rfb1 f4 18.Bf2

(position after 18...Bg7-f6 )

With the temporary pawn sacrifice 19.b2-b4 White breaks open the queenside and invades there (Game A64).

c2-c4

This attacks the d5 square.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4

c4-c5

This advance attacks the d6 pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.g3 Bg7 4.Bg2 O-O 5.c4 d6 6.O-O Bd7 7.Nc3 Qc8 8.e4 Bg4 9.Be3 Nc6 10.Qd2 Bxf3 11.Bxf3 h5 12.Nd5 Nh7 13.Bg2 e6 14.Nc3 e5 15.d5

(position after 15...Nc6-e7 )

White plays 16.c4-c5 , with the idea of opening the c-file for his rooks. In the endgame his bishops may become a powerful force (Game A31).

c4xd5

White captures a pawn on d5.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 O-O 5.Be3 d6 6.f3 e5 7.Nge2 c6 8.Qd2 exd4 9.Nxd4

(position after 9...d6-d5 )

With his king not yet castled, White prefers to block the e-file with 10.c4xd5 cxd5 11.e5 , rather than to open it by 10.exd5 (Game 22).

[...]

d2-d4

This pawn move occupies a central square and attacks the e5 and c5 squares.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.g3 O-O 5.Bg2 d6 6.O-O e5 7.d4

d4-d5

This blocks the d6 pawn and gains White space on the queenside.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 g6 4.d4 Bg7 5.e4 O-O 6.h3

(position after 6...e7-e5 )

As in similar positions, taking on e5 does not bring White any benefit. He plays 7.d4-d5 , gaining space and stabilising the pawn structure (Game 49).

d4xc5

White captures a pawn on c5.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 O-O 6.Be3

(position after 6...c7-c5 )

White accepts the pawn offer with 7.d4xc5 dxc5 8.Qxd8 Rxd8 9.Bxc5 . He must continue energetically, otherwise Black's initiative may become dangerous (Game A52).

d4xe5

White captures a pawn on e5, usually with the aim of opening the d-file.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 Be7 6.Bg2 c6 7.O-O O-O 8.e4 Qc7 9.b3 Rd8 10.Ba3 Nf8 11.h3 a6 12.Qc2 Ng6 13.Rad1 Be6 14.Rd2

(position after 14...b7-b5 )

The opponent's queenside activity is of little concern to White, as the black kingside pieces are passively placed. With 15.d4xe5 followed by the exchange of the dark-square bishops he gains an advantage in the centre (Game A1).

e2-e4

The pawn occupies a central square and attacks the d5 and f5 squares.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O

(position after 7...O-O )

White's standard plan is to occupy the centre with 8.e2-e4 , even though it blocks the main diagonal of his king's bishop (Games A13 and A14).

e2-e3

With this move White supports his d4 pawn.

1.c4 Nf6 2.Nc3 e5 3.Nf3 d6 4.d4 Nbd7 5.Bg5

(position after 5...Bf8-e7 )

A difficult variation for Black. White supports his d4 pawn by 6.e2-e3 , and maintains control of e4 with his pieces (Game A50).

e4-e5

This attacks the d6 pawn and the f6 square, and vacates the e4 square for the white pieces.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.e4 Re8 9.h3 exd4 10.Nxd4 Nc5 11.Re1 c6 12.Bf4 Nh5 13.Be3 Nf6 14.Qc2 a5 15.Rad1 Qc7 16.f4 Bd7 17.Nb3 b6 18.Nxc5 bxc5 19.Qd2 Bf8 20.f5 Rad8 21.Bg5 Be7 22.Rf1 Rf8 23.g4 Bc8 24.Qe1 Rd7 25.Qh4

(position after 25...Qc7-d8 )

White has strong pressure on the d-and f-files. With 26.e4-e5 he releases his knight and light-square bishop, which quickly forces a decision (Game 18).

e4xd5

With this move White normally captures a pawn on d5.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 Nbd7 4.e4 e5 5.Nge2 c6 6.g3 g6 7.Bg2 Bg7 8.O-O O-O 9.Rb1 exd4 10.Nxd4 Re8 11.Kh1 Nc5 12.f3 a5 13.b3 d5 14.Ba3

(position after 14...Bg7-f8 )

White has to find the best way of resolving the central tension. He plays 15.e4xd5 cxd5 16.Ncb5 , when his minor pieces on the queenside are actively placed, and Black has still to complete his development (Game 21).

e4xf5

White captures a pawn on f5.

1.c4 g6 2.Nc3 Bg7 3.Nf3 d6 4.d4 Nf6 5.e4 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.O-O Nbd7 8.Re1 c6 9.d5 c5 10.a3 Ne8 11.Bg5 f6 12.Bd2 f5 13.Ng5

(position after 13...Ne8-c7 )

After covering the e6 square, Black is threatening to expand on the kingside with ...f5-f4. White plays 14.e4xf5 , with the idea after 14...gxf5 15.f4 e4 of blockading the passed e-pawn (Game A27).

f2-f3

This defends the e4 pawn and the g4 square.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 c5 3.d5 d6 4.Nc3 g6 5.e4

(position after 5...Bf8-g7 )

White has a wide choice of plans. He opts for 6.f2-f3 O-O 7.Bg5 , securing his centre and deferring for the moment the decision of how to develop his kingside (Game A46).

f2-f4

With this move White attacks the e5 square.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.d4 Nbd7 4.Nc3 e5 5.e4 g6 6.Be2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.d5 a5 9.Ne1 Nc5 10.Qc2 b6 11.Bg5 h6 12.Be3 Ng4 13.Bxg4 Bxg4 14.Nd3 Bd7 15.Rae1 f5 16.Bxc5

(position after 16...b6xc5 )

Despite the opponent's two bishops, White is not afraid of opening the position by 17.f2-f4 , as all his pieces are excellently placed (24).

g2-g3

This defends the f4 square and allows the development of the bishop at g2.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.d5

(position after 7...Nf6-h5 )

White takes away the black knight's outpost at f4 by 8.g2-g3 , planning to leave his king in the centre and attack on the kingside by h2-h4 (Game 33).

g2-g4

With this move White attacks the f5 and h5 squares (g3-g4 has similar aims).

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.h3 O-O 6.Be3 e5 7.d5

(position after 7...Nb8-d7 )

In accordance with his plan, White continues 8.g2-g4 , preventing ...Nh5 and cramping Black on the kingside (Game A19).

g4xf5

White captures a pawn on f5, with the aim of opening the g-file.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nf3 Bg7 4.Nc3 d6 5.e4 O-O 6.h3 c6 7.Bd3 e5 8.d5 cxd5 9.cxd5 Nh5 10.g3 Nd7 11.Be3 a6 12.Be2 b5 13.Nd2 Nhf6 14.a3 Nb6 15.g4 Nfd7 16.h4

(position after 16...f7-f5 )

White plays 17.g4xf5 gxf5 18.exf5 , opening the g-file against the opposing king, although this also allows Black to activate his pieces (Game 46).

h2-h3

This move defends the g4 square.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Bg5 c5 6.d5 Na6 7.Bd3 Nc7 8.Nge2 a6 9.a4 Rb8 10.O-O O-O 11.Qc2

(position after 11...Bc8-d7 )

White is planning to cramp Black still further with f2-f4. He precedes this with 12.h2-h3 , denying the black pieces access to g4 (Game A18).

h2-h4

The pawn attacks the g5 square and may move on further to h5.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 e5 6.Nge2 Nfd7 7.Be3 Bh6 8.Bf2

(position after 8...O-O )

Black's artificial opening play has left him in a difficult position. White begins a direct storming of the kingside by 9.h2-h4 (Game A35).

h4-h5

With this move White attacks the g6 pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.d5 Nh5 8.g3 Na6 9.Nd2 c6 10.Nb3 Nf6 11.h4 cxd5 12.cxd5 b6 13.Bg5 Nc5 14.Nd2

(position after 14...a7-a5 )

Supported by his rook, White plays 15.h4-h5 , planning to weaken the black king's defences by a later hxg6 (Game 33).
