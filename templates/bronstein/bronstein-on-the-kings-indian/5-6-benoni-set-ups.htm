Benoni set-ups

In this section we have grouped together a few games that are quite diverse in nature, but are characterised by the following pawn formation: Black plays ...c7-c5 and White replies d4-d5.

Game 41
White: David Bronstein
Black: Oscar Panno
Interzonal Tournament Gothenburg 1955

This is a curious game. Panno was a very good King's Indian player. I lost many King's Indians as White, because I did not want to reveal the strongest way to play against it. I varied my play a great deal, and as a consequence I often ended up in inferior positions.

Things are quite different now - the top players compete in tournament after tournament and they invariably employ the best variations. But we were obliged to finish well up in every event, and the value of each point was very high.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.Nf3 O-O 5.Bg5 d6 6.e3 c5 7.d5 Na6 8.Be2

White has employed a quiet variation. I thought that, with my pawn at d5, I should be guaranteed a slight advantage.

8...Nc7

A typical knight manoeuvre to prepare ...b7-b5.

9.O-O Rb8 10.a4 a6 11.Bh4

Instead I should have played 11.a5 .

11...b5 12.Nd2 Bd7

13.Ra2

At the time I was very pleased with this move, but what I should have played was 13.Qc2 . In fact White blundered away a pawn (a pure blunder!), although many thought that it was a sacrifice.

13...bxa4 14.Nxa4 Bxa4 15.Qxa4 Rb4 16.Qd1 Nfxd5 17.cxd5 Rxh4 18.Nc4

When the situation had clarified, I realised that, despite the loss of the pawn, White's position was not so bad - Black's rook is cut off and his light squares are weak. Later there was a kind of double play - I did not want to lose, and at the same time I had hopes of retaining winning chances.

18...g5 19.g3 Rh6 20.e4 Bd4 21.Na5 Qd7 22.Bg4 Qb5 23.Nc6 Bf6 24.b3 Rg6 25.Re2 Rg7

A most unusual position for the queen's rook!

26.Re3 Qb7 27.Bf3 Nb5 28.e5 dxe5 29.Nxe5 Bxe5 30.Rxe5 Nd4

White has allowed first the bishop, and now the knight to reach d4, but he is aiming at the e7 pawn.

On the whole the chances are equal, but Black underestimated the power of the white bishop in an open game.

31.Bg2 f6 32.Re3 Qa7 33.Rfe1 a5 34.Rc3 Rb8 35.Qd3 Rf7 36.Rec1 Kg7 37.Bh3 Qb6 38.Rxc5 Nxb3 39.Rc6

39...Qb4

From move 35 onwards there was a time scramble.

Of course, by now I would have been happy with a draw, but Panno wrongly avoided 39...Nxc1 , as he was evidently relying on the strength of his a-pawn.

40.Rd1 a4 41.Be6 Rff8 42.Rc7 Kh8 43.Bf5 Rb7

44.d6 exd6 45.Rxb7 Qxb7 46.Qxd6 Kg7 47.h4 h6 48.h5 Rf7 49.Be6 Re7 50.Qd3 Rxe6 51.Qg6+ Kf8 52.Rd8+

Black resigns

Game 42
White: Bernardo Wexler
Black: David Bronstein
Mar del Plata 1960

This game is noteworthy for the fact that before it began I incautiously promised that I would sacrifice my queen, i.e. I wanted to say that I intended to play an interesting game. This was my first experience with the given variation.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 c5 3.d5 e6 4.Nc3 exd5 5.cxd5 d6 6.e4 g6 7.Nf3 a6 8.a4 Bg4 9.Be2

9...Bxf3

I was loathe to give up my light-square bishop, but at that time I was very much under the influence of the well-known game Nimzowitsch-Marshall, New York 1927, and I thought that it was essential to eliminate as soon as possible the f3 knight, which on no account should be allowed to go to c4.

10.Bxf3 Nbd7 11.O-O Bg7 12.Bf4

12...Qb8

During the game this seemed to me to be a natural move, but then I read somewhere that it was a very important innovation. It turned out that in previous games Black had played ...Qc7 or ...Qe7, but after e4-e5 he had ended up in a bad position.

At that time Bernardo Wexler was the Champion of Argentina; he was 40 years old, a strong and experienced player. White chose the correct plan -that of exploiting the strength of his central pawns. I did not care for the prospect of him playing e4-e5, and as a reserve possibility I planned to play my knight to h5, in order to relieve the tension at the cost of spoiled pawns.

13.Be2 O-O 14.Bg3 Re8 15.Qc2 Qc7 16.f4 c4 17.Kh1 Rac8 18.a5 Qd8 19.Rfe1

This was the most interesting moment. The crafty retreat to d8 provokes White into playing 19.Ra4 , winning the c4 pawn, but then Black has a tactical opportunity: 19...Nc5 20.Rxc4 Ncxe4 . White's best was 19.Bh4 with the threat of Ra4.

19...Rc5 20.Ra4 Rxa5 21.Rxc4 b5 22.Rb4 Qb6 23.Bf3 Qc5 24.Rb3

As the game went, Black acquired some play, but with the strong move 24.Qb3 White had the opportunity to threaten the e4-e5 breakthrough. I would have had to play 24...Nh5 with a tense position.

24...Qc4

This move, with several threats (...Nxd5, ...Nc5 and others), proved to be decisive.

25.Be2

After this natural reply I was able to fulfil my promise to the USSR Ambassador in Argentina, Nikolai Borisovich Alekseev - to sacrifice my queen! Fifteen years later, when we accidentally met in Moscow, the first thing he asked me was: 'Look here, David, just how did that miracle happen?', and it was only then that he greeted me. He was very fond of chess.

25...Nxe4 26.Bxc4 Nxg3+ 27.hxg3 Rxe1+ 28.Kh2 Raa1 29.g4 Rac1 30.Qf2 bxc4 31.Rb7 Bxc3 32.bxc3 Rh1+

White resigns

Game 43
White: Leonid Shamkovich
Black: David Bronstein
39th USSR Championship, Leningrad, 1971

This game is unique, for the fact that in a King's Indian Defence Black castled on the queenside! I was also very pleased with the final combination.

1.c4 g6 2.d4 Nf6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 b6 6.Bd3

6...Bb7

The move 6.Bd3 is my invention. The point is that, if now Black routinely plays to undermine the white centre by 6...c5 , then after 7.e5 he loses material. The first time this occurred was in a game of mine with Lutikov (A36), with the difference that Black castled on move 5 and only after 6.Be3 played 6...b6 . That was back in 1959. More than 10 years later Leonid Shamkovich was trying to test my memory, about which I have no complaints even now!

7.Nge2 c5 8.d5 Nbd7 9.O-O Ne5 10.Bb1 a6

At first sight it seems that Black was wrong not to castle - now he would have been able to take the c4 pawn.

In fact, right from the start I had decided to try an innovative idea for Black - castling on the queenside. This has not been played either before, or since, although the plan is quite logical - on the queenside the pawns block the position, and on the kingside there is the chance of a breakthrough. In the present game that is what happened.

11.Qb3

A poor move: White has no suspicion of his opponent's plans and makes it easier for him to carry them out. There is a very old truth in chess: each of the players must be able to hide his intentions and to guess those of his opponent.

11...b5 12.a4

Of course, the line 12.cxb5 c4 13.Qd1 Qb6+ 14.Qd4 Nxd5! 15.Nxd5 Nxf3+ 16.Kh1 Nxd4 17.Nxb6 Nxe2 18.Nxa8 Bxa8 did not satisfy White.

12...b4 13.Nd1 a5 14.Bg5 h6 15.Bh4 g5 16.Bg3 Nh5 17.Ne3 e6 18.Bc2 Qe7 19.Rad1 O-O-O

The question arises: can White, by going completely onto the defensive, defend against the mounting threats of the black pieces? However reluctant he was to do so, Shamkovich should have exchanged pawns on e6. It is true that this would have opened the f-file for Black, but later there would have been some hopes of opening up the black king.

20.Bb1 Ng6 21.Qc2 Nhf4 22.Qd2 h5 23.b3 Bh6 24.Kh1 h4 25.Bxf4 gxf4 26.Ng4 h3 27.gxh3 Bg5 28.Ng1 e5 29.Rf2 Kc7

From this point White is completely obliged to 'swim with the tide' - he can only wait.

30.Bd3 Bh4 31.Rg2 Bc8 32.Ne2 Rh5 33.Ng1 Rdh8 34.Qe2 Bg5 35.Qc2 Bh6 36.Rf1 Bg7 37.Re2 Nh4 38.Ref2 f5

With his pieces completely mobilised, Black lands a blow in the centre (this would not have happened if White had exchanged on e6).

39.exf5 Nxf5 40.Qd2 Bh6 41.Bxf5 Bxf5 42.Re2 Rg8 43.Rfe1 Bd7 44.Rg2 Bg5 45.Nf2

White's last hope was this desperate attempt to defend the h3 pawn with both of his knights, which serve as a kind of armour-plating on the h-file. Black managed to solve this problem with the help of the elegant manoeuvre ...Bg5-h4-g3, after which the h2 pawn could not be defended.

45...Rgh8 46.Ne4 Bh4 47.Rd1 R5h6 48.Qe2 Bf5 49.Rd2 Kb6 50.Qd3 Qd7 51.Rde2

51...Bg3 52.Qd2 Bxh3 53.Nxh3 Qxh3 54.Nf2

54...Qxh2+ 55.Rxh2 Rxh2+ 56.Kg1 Bxf2+

White resigns

Game 44
White: William Hartston
Black: David Bronstein
Tallinn, 1979

The moral of this game is that, when White in a semi-open position plays passively, his pieces may gradually become uncoordinated.

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 e6 3.c4 c5 4.d5 exd5 5.cxd5 d6 6.Nc3 a6

I am not convinced that this is the most correct move. Black hopes that White will weaken his b4 square, and also that at the necessary moment he will not have the move a2-a3.

7.a4 g6 8.Bg5 h6 9.Bh4 Bg7

A significant element in this system is the development of Black's light-square bishop. The point is that the white d5 pawn seriously restricts this bishop, and so it aims to break out to g4, in order at any moment to exchange itself for the knight at f3. Therefore, when I play this system as White, I try not to allow Black's bishop to go to g4, and I play h2-h3.

10.e3 Bg4 11.Qc2

If 11.Be2 I would have immediately exchanged on f3, otherwise Nd2-c4. 11.Qc2 is slightly dubious; perhaps 11.Qb3 would have been better.

11...Bxf3 12.gxf3 Nbd7

13.a5

A second element is that White was in too much of a hurry to make this move. It allowed his opponent to advance his b-pawn and open the file, which is usually to Black's advantage.

13...O-O 14.f4 b5 15.axb6 Qxb6 16.Ra2 Qb4 17.Bg2

The curious move 16...Qb4 was made in the hope of 17.Ra4 Qb7 , when White cannot play 18.Bc4 because of 18...Nb6 .

17...c4

The critical moment came after this move. White should not have begrudged giving up his dark-square bishop, and should have exchanged on f6. From this point the initiative passed completely to Black.

18.O-O Nc5 19.Rd1 Rfc8 20.Bf1 Rab8 21.f3 Nh5 22.Be1 f5 23.Kh1 Re8 24.Qf2 Qb3 25.Rda1

25...Nd3 26.Bxd3 cxd3 27.e4 Nxf4 28.Bd2 Ne2 29.Qg2 Nxc3 30.bxc3

30...Qxa2 31.Rxa2 Rb1+ 32.Qg1 Rxg1+ 33.Kxg1 fxe4 34.fxe4 Rxe4 35.Rxa6 Re2 36.Ra2 g5 37.c4 Kf7 38.h3 Kg6

White resigns

Game 45
White: Alexander Cherepkov
Black: David Bronstein
USSR Championship Semi-Final Yaroslavl 1982

This game was played in the ancient Russian town of Yaroslavl, in a USSR Championship Semi-Final, where I played, on the whole, not badly, winning eight games. But I was slightly unlucky - I had an absolutely won position against Alexander Belyavsky, and in the event of a win I would have qualified for the final.

Then the Moscow Sports Committee, and 'Dynamo', and the Yaroslavl tyre factory, in which we played, all solicited on my behalf, but nevertheless I was not admitted. At that time in the USSR the spirit of competitive promise dominated, and a young (sometimes not very skilled) player was preferred to a distinguished veteran with an interesting creative style that appealed to the general public.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f4 O-O 6.Nf3

This position seems to be an ideal one for White - he controls the 5th rank (the line beyond the equator), and has brought out his knights towards the centre.

6...c5 7.d5

The undermining move ...c7-c5 is the natural reaction, but White is not obliged to close the centre. 7.Be2 cxd4 8.Nxd4 Nc6 9.Be3 etc. is satisfactory for him.

7...e6

8.dxe6

Here there was a curious nuance: perhaps my opponent did not like 8.Be2 because of 8...exd5 (there was also a new idea - to play 8...b5!? immediately) 9.cxd5 Re8 .

The main drawback for White of the exchange on e6 is that now Black's queen's knight acquires the good square c6.

8...Bxe6 9.Bd3 Bg4 10.O-O Nc6 11.Qe1 Re8 12.Qh4 Nb4 13.Bb1 Bxf3 14.gxf3

If 14.Rxf3 , then Black would have had both 14...d5!? with a sharp and roughly equal game, as well as the complicated 14...b5 , e.g. 15.cxb5 (or the even more fantastic variation 15.f5 Nd7 16.Qxd8 Raxd8 17.Nxb5 Ne5 18.Rf1 Nxc4 19.f6 Bh8 ) 15...d5 16.e5 d4 17.exf6 Bxf6 . There was probably a more real explanation for why Cherepkov avoided taking on f3 with his rook. Black could have replied 14...Nd7! in order then to calmly put pressure on White's central pawns (...Nb6 etc.).

14...Nh5 15.Qg4 a6 16.a4 Qf6 17.Rd1 Bh6 18.Ne2 Rad8 19.Ra3

19...b5 20.axb5 axb5 21.cxb5 d5 22.e5 Qb6 23.Ng3 Nxg3 24.hxg3 Qxb5 25.Kf2 Nc6 26.Qh4 Bg7 27.Rh1 h6 28.Re1 c4

29.e6 Qb6+ 30.Kf1 Nd4 31.f5 g5 32.exf7+ Kxf7 33.Qh5+ Kf8 34.Rae3 Rxe3 35.Bxe3 Qf6 36.g4 Nxf3 37.Bc5+ Kg8 38.Re6 Qf7 39.Qxf7+ Kxf7

40.Re7+ Kg8 41.Ke2 Nh2 42.f6 Bxf6 43.Rb7 Nxg4 44.Bh7+ Kh8 45.Bf5 Re8+ 46.Kd2 Ne5 47.Rh7+ Kg8 48.Rxh6

48...Nf3+ 49.Kc2 Re2+ 50.Kb1 Kf7 51.Rh7+ Bg7 52.Bg4 Re1+ 53.Kc2 Ne5 54.Bd1 Kg6 55.Rh2 Nd3 56.Ba3 Be5 57.Rg2 Rh1 58.Re2 Kf5

White resigns

The events that developed in this game after the 14th move were essentially merely a textbook illustration of how to convert a positional advantage: (a) weakened white pawns; (b) complete control by Black of the h8-a1 diagonal; (c) passive, uncoordinated white pieces. Of course, careful, efficient and strong play was still required of Black, but this is precisely what distinguishes a grandmaster from an ordinary amateur.
