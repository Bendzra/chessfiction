Test 1. Is ...e7-e5 possible? (1.1-1.8)

After Black has prepared a shelter for his king, his next important problem is to contest the centre. Usually (but not always) the best way of doing this will be by advancing his pawn to e5. Here are eight opening positions where it is Black to move, and he has to decide: is ...e7-e5 possible?

[...]

1.1

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2

Solution

1.1 6...e5 is perfectly possible, and indeed is considered the best move. After 7.dxe5 dxe5 8.Qxd8 Rxd8 White is not able to win a pawn ( 9.Nxe5 is met by 9...Nxe4 , uncovering the King's Indian bishop, when Black regains the pawn with the better position) and he can answer 9.Bg5 (threatening 10.Nd5 ) with 9...Re8 (Game A56).

[...]

1.2

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Be2 O-O 6.Bg5

Solution

1.2 Here the immediate 6...e5? is bad: after 7.dxe5 dxe5 8.Qxd8 Rxd8 9.Nd5 White wins material, as the knight at f6 is attacked, and Nxc7 also threatened (Game A38). Black should either prepare ...e7-e5 by playing 6...Nbd7 , or else switch instead to 6...c5 .

[...]

1.3

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 O-O 6.Be3 Nc6 7.Nge2 a6 8.Qd2 Rb8 9.Bh6

Solution

1.3 The immediate 9...e5 can be met by 10.Bxg7 Kxg7 11.d5 , when the knight has to retreat and Black is badly cramped. Therefore he plays 9...Bxh6 10.Qxh6 and only now 10...e7-e5 , when after 11.d5 he has the possibility of 11...Nd4 (Game A55).

[...]

1.4

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.b4 d6 3.d4 g6 4.c4 Bg7 5.Bb2 O-O 6.Nbd2 a5 7.a3

Solution

1.4 White looks to have too many pieces covering e5, but 7...e5 is nevertheless possible, as 8.dxe5 is answered by 8...Nfd7 (a typical manoeuvre when there is a white bishop at b2), uncovering a pin by the King's Indian bishop and enabling Black to regain his pawn. Game 11 continued 9.e3 Nc6 10.Qb3 axb4 11.axb4 Rxa1+ 12.Bxa1 Ncxe5 with a good game for Black.

[...]

1.5

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.g3 g6 3.b3 Bg7 4.Bb2 O-O 5.Bg2 d6 6.d4 c6 7.c4 Nbd7 8.O-O

Solution

1.5 In contrast to position 1.4, here 8...e5 is a blunder which simply loses a pawn after 9.dxe5 Ng4 (if 9...Ne8 White can simply play 10.Qd2 , when because of the pin on the d-file Black cannot regain his pawn: 10...dxe5 11.Nxe5 , and 11...Nxe5 is not possible) 10.Qxd6 (Game A53).

[...]

1.6

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.Bg5 Bg7 4.Nbd2 d6 5.e3 O-O 6.Bd3 Nbd7 7.O-O h6 8.Bh4

Solution

1.6 8...e5 is quite opportune. After 9.c3 Black unpinned his queen with the typical (in this line) move 9...Qe8 , when he was already threatening 10...e4 (Game 8).

[...]

1.7

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3

Solution

1.7 5...O-O is the usual move, but 5...e5 is quite playable (Games 28, 39 and 47). As in similar positions, Black need not fear the exchange of queens after 6.dxe5 dxe5 7.Qxd8+ Kxd8 .

[...]

1.8

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3

Solution

1.8 In fact Black has not yet prepared his king's shelter, but 3...e5 is perfectly possible. This move order, which is seldom seen nowadays, was widely used some fifty years ago in the earlier days of the King's Indian Defence, as it enables Black to avoid some variations which were then considered dangerous for him, and which can arise after the move order 1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 , such as the Saemisch Variation ( 5.f3 ) and the Four Pawns Attack ( 5.f4 ). Black is not afraid of the ending after 4.dxe5 dxe5 5.Qxd8+ Kxd8 , as his king will find a secure shelter at c7 or e7, and meanwhile his strong pawn at e5 gives him control of the central dark squares (Games 13 and A32).
