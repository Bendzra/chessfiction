2.1

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.d4 Nbd7 4.Nc3 e5 5.e4 g6 6.Be2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O

(position after 8.d4-d5 )

How should Black react to the blocking of the centre?

Solution

2.1 There is now an outpost for Black's knight at c5, and he plays 8...a7-a5 , in order to safeguard it (24).

2.2

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.g3 g6 4.Bg2 Bg7 5.O-O Nbd7 6.d4 e5 7.Nc3 O-O 8.d5 a5 9.e4 Nc5

(position after 10.Nf3-e1 ) How does Black activate his game?

Solution

2.2 Black needs to make the traditional King's Indian advance of his f-pawn, but 10...Nh5 is unsatisfactory, as he will be unable to recapture on f5 with his g-pawn. He therefore plays 10...Nf6-d7 and follows up with 11...f5 next move (25).

2.3

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.d5 Nbd7 8.Bg5 h6 9.Bh4 a5 10.Nd2 Nc5 11.O-O c6 12.Rb1 Bd7 13.b3 g5 14.Bg3 cxd5 15.cxd5 b5 16.b4 axb4 17.Rxb4 Qa5 18.Qb1 Na6 19.Rb3 Nc5 20.Rb4 Na6 21.Rb3 Rfc8 22.Qb2 b4 23.Ncb1 Nc5 24.Rxb4 Qxa2

(position after 25.f2-f3 )

Black clearly has the initiative on the queenside. How does he make further progress?

Solution

2.3 It is time for Black to switch his sights to the kingside with 25...Nf6-h5 , aiming for the outpost at f4. White cannot capture the knight there with his dark-square bishop, as after ...e5xf4 the hitherto inactive bishop at g7 would become murderously strong (Game 34).

2.4

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 g6 4.e4 Bg7 5.f3 e5 6.Nge2 Nbd7 7.Be3 a6 8.Qd2 O-O

(position after 9.Ne2-c1 )

White is planning to play his knight to b3 and then complete the development of his kingside. But doesn't this all look rather slow?

Solution

2.4 It is hard to understand White's manoeuvre Ne2-c1-b3, other than as a product of fashion. If he did not want to close the centre by 9.d5 , then would not 9.g3 , Bg2 and 0-0 have been simpler? Black takes advantage of the opponent's retarded development by 9...e5xd4 10.Bxd4 c6 , striving to open the centre as quickly as possible with ...d6-d5 (Game A34).

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.Be3 Ng4 8.Bg5 Qe8 9.d5 f5 10.Nd2 Nf6 11.f3 c5 12.O-O Nh5 13.a3 Bf6 14.Be3 Bh4 15.g3 f4 16.gxf4 exf4 17.Bf2 Bf6 18.Qc2 Qe5 19.Kh1 Qg5 20.Rg1 Ng3+ 21.Bxg3 fxg3 22.Rxg3 Qh6 23.Nb5 Be5 24.Rg2 Rf7 25.Nb3 a6 26.Nc3 Rf4 27.Qd2 Qh4

(position after 28.Nc3-d1 )

Black's blockade is so strong that up till now he has not required the use of his queenside pieces...

Solution

2.5 Now is the time to make use of the queen's bishop, and its entry into the fray by 28...Bc8-h3 decides matters within a few moves (Game A60).

2.6

(position after 11.Bg2-h1 )

White has just declined the exchange of bishops. How does Black maintain his forces in their attacking positions?

Solution

2.6 Black plays 11...h7-h6 , preventing 12.Ng5 , and follows up with ...Ng4 and ...e7-e5 (Game 6).

(position after 22.Nf3-h2 )

Black's pieces are compactly arranged, rather like a compressed spring. Does the exotic position of the white king suggest a way to uncoil it?

Solution

2.7 With 22...Nf6xg4 23.Nxg4 f5 Black releases the pent-up energy of his pieces, and the white king quickly succumbs (Game 36).

2.8

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nf3 Bg7 4.Nc3 d6 5.e4 O-O 6.h3 c6 7.Bd3 e5 8.d5 cxd5 9.cxd5 Nh5 10.g3 Nd7 11.Be3 a6 12.Be2 b5 13.Nd2 Nhf6 14.a3 Nb6 15.g4 Nfd7

(position after 16.h3-h4 )

White has begun a kingside pawn storm. How should Black react?

Solution

2.8 Passive play by Black in the King's Indian is usually fatal in the long run. Here he activates his game with 16...f7-f5 , not fearing the opening of the g-file (Game 46).

2.9

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 g6 4.Nf3 Bg7 5.g3 O-O 6.Bg2 Nbd7 7.O-O e5 8.Qc2 exd4 9.Nxd4 Nb6

(position after 10.b2-b3 )

White's last move has left him vulnerable on the long diagonal. How best does Black take advantage of this?

Solution

2.9 Taking advantage of White's last move ( 10.Rd1 was possible, and if 10...Nxc4 11.Ncb5 ), Black plays 10...c7-c5 , gaining an advantage in the centre after the forcing sequence 11.Ndb5 a6 12.Na3 Bf5 13.Qd2 d5 (Game A22).

White has mobilised all his minor pieces. Is there any drawback to such an ideal development?

Solution

2.10 Taking advantage of White's faulty move order, Black seizes the initiative with 8...Nf6-g4 , so that if 9.fxg4 exd4 , when he regains his piece and the white pawns are broken (Game A54).

2.11

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 Nbd7 4.Nf3 e5 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.e4 c6 9.b3 exd4 10.Nxd4 Re8 11.Bb2 Nc5 12.Qc2 a5 13.Rad1 Qb6 14.Nde2 a4 15.Nc1 Qc7 16.Rfe1 axb3 17.axb3 Be6 18.Nd3 Nxd3

(position after 19.Qc2xd3 )

Black's d-pawn is under fire. Should he passively defend it, or is there some other, more active option?

Solution

2.11 Black is not concerned about the attack on his d6 pawn. With 19...Nf6-d7 he prepares to attack White's queenside by ...Nc5 (Game 17). He will answer 20.Qxd6 with 20...Qb6 , when the white b-pawn falls, and the c-pawn is left without support.

2.12

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.c4 Bg7 4.Nc3 d6 5.e4 Nbd7 6.h3 e5 7.Be3 exd4 8.Nxd4 O-O 9.Nde2 Re8 10.Qc2 Nc5 11.f3 Qe7 12.O-O-O c6 13.Bf4 Rd8 14.g4 Be6

(position after 15.Ne2-g3 )

Black's forces are fully mobilised. How does he give them more scope?

Solution

2.12 To release the energy of his well-developed pieces, Black plays 15...d6-d5 . He is ready to answer 16.g5 with 16...d4 , and if 17.gxf6 Qxf6 18.Nce2 d3 , making a decisive breakthrough (Game A4).

2.13

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.g3 Bg7 4.Bg2 O-O 5.Nc3 d6 6.Nf3 Nbd7 7.O-O e5 8.e4 Re8 9.h3 exd4 10.Nxd4 Nc5 11.Re1 a5 12.Qc2 c6 13.Be3 Nfd7 14.Rad1 a4

(position after 15.Nd4-e2 )

With his last move White made a discovered attack on the backward d6 pawn. Does Black have to defend it?

Solution

2.13 Black ignores the threat to his d-pawn and plays 15...Qd8-a5 (Game 20). The point is that after 16.Rxd6 Ne5 17.b3 he has 17...Bxh3 , when 18.Bxh3 allows 18...Nf3+ , with the possible sequel 19.Kf1 Nxe1 20.Kxe1 axb3 21.axb3 Nxe4 22.Rd3 Qa1+ 23.Nd1 Ra2 24.Qc1 Nxf2 25.Kxf2 Rxe2+ and Black wins.

2.14

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 Nbd7 4.Nf3 c6 5.e4 e5 6.Be2 Be7 7.O-O O-O 8.Qc2 Re8 9.Rd1 Qc7 10.b3 Nf8 11.h3 Ng6 12.Be3 h6

(position after 13.Be2-f1 )

Black's position is cramped. Is there any way for him to relieve the pressure?

Solution

2.14 The best way to ease a cramped position is normally by exchanging pieces. Here Black does this with 13...Nf6-h7 followed by ...Ng5 (Game A21). A similar manoeuvre by White (true, with more offensive aims) is often seen in the Ruy Lopez.

2.15

1.d4 d6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 Nf6 5.f3 e5 6.Nge2 O-O 7.Bg5 exd4 8.Nxd4 Nc6 9.Nc2 h6 10.Be3 Be6 11.Qd2 Ne5

(position after 12.b2-b3 )

Black's h-pawn is attacked. Should he defend it, or is there something more positive he can undertake?

Solution

2.15 With the white king still uncastled, Black hurries to prepare a break in the centre with 12...c7-c6 followed by ...d6-d5. He does fear 13.Bxh6 , which can be met by the typical tactical stroke 13...Nxe4 and 14...Qh4+ (Game A61).

2.16

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 O-O 6.Be3 Nc6 7.Nge2 a6 8.Qd2 Rb8 9.Bh6 Bxh6 10.Qxh6 e5 11.d5 Nd4

(position after 12.O-O-O )

Has White won the battle for the d4 square?

Solution

2.16 After 12...c7-c5 13.dxc6 bxc6 14.Nxd4 exd4 15.Rxd4 White conquers the d4 square, but only at a price - his pieces are uncoordinated, and Black exploits this by 15...Rxb2 , with highly favourable complications (Game A55).

2.17

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Bg5 h6 6.Bh4 O-O 7.f4 c5

(position after 8.d4-d5 )

White has set up an imposing pawn centre, but at the expense of his development. How can Black best take advantage of this?

Solution

2.17 With White's dark-square bishop committed to the kingside, this would have been a good moment for the typical sacrifice 8...b7-b5 9.cxb5 a6 10.bxa6 Qa5 , with fine play for the pawn. Black's actual continuation 8...Qa5 9.Qd2 e6 proved less successful (Game A58).

2.18

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 g6 4.g3 Bg7 5.Bg2 O-O 6.e4 e5 7.d5 c5 8.Nge2 Nbd7 9.O-O Nh5 10.Bd2 f5 11.f3 a6 12.a3 f4 13.g4 Nhf6 14.Be1 h5

(position after 15.g4-g5 )

Black's knight is attacked. How should he respond to this threat?

Solution

2.18 White would desperately like to keep the kingside blocked (e.g. after 15...Nh7 16.h4 ), but Black forestalls him by 15...Nf6-g4 16.fxg4 hxg4 (and if 17.Bh4 Bh6 ), when all his pieces pour into the action behind his mobile pawns (Game 37).
