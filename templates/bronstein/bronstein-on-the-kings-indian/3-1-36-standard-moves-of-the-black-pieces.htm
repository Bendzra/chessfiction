Ng8-f6

1.d4 Nf6

The knight attacks the e4 square, where there is often a white pawn, and vacates g8, allowing the black king to castle.

Nf6-h5

This move simultaneously clears three lines - for the queen, bishop, and f-pawn. At the same time the knight aims for the outpost at f4 and attacks the g3 square.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Be2 O-O 6.Nf3 e5 7.d5 Nbd7 8.Bg5 h6 9.Bh4 g5

(position after 10.Bh4-g3 )

Black plays 10...Nf6-h5 , with the intention of occupying the f4 outpost. He does not fear the discovered attack 11.Nxe5 (or 11.Nxg5 ) in view of 11...Nxg3 (Game A39).

Nf6-d7

From here the knight attacks the c5 and e5 squares, and can also move on to b6 or f8. At the same time lines are opened for the queen, the dark-square bishop, and the f-pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.g3 O-O 6.Bg2 e5 7.Nge2 exd4 8.Nxd4 Nc6 9.Nxc6 bxc6

(position after 10.O-O )

In many King's Indian positions there is no need for Black to hurry with the development of his queen's bishop and rook. Here he played 10...Nf6-d7 , with the specific idea of not allowing White to fianchetto his queen's bishop after b2-b3. Later the knight took up a good post at c5 (Game 19).

Nf6-h7

1.c4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 O-O 5.d4 d6 6.h3 e5 7.d5 Na6 8.Bg5 h6 9.Be3 Nc5 10.Nd2 Nh7

This prepares ...Nh7-g5 and clears the way for a possible ...f7-f5.

Nf6-e8

This move clears three lines, for the queen, king's bishop and f-pawn, and at the same time it defends the d6 and c7 squares.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nf3 Nbd7 4.Nc3 e5 5.e4 c6 6.Be2 g6 7.O-O Bg7 8.d5 cxd5 9.cxd5 O-O

(position after 10.Nf3-d2 )

Black wants to advance his f-pawn. He prepares this with 10...Nf6-e8 , at the same time protecting his d6 pawn in anticipation of White's Nd2-c4 (Game A9).

Nf6-g4

This knight move attacks the squares e3, f2 and h2, and clears the way for the f-pawn to advance.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.e4 c6

(position after 9.Bc1-e3 )

White's last move invited 9...Nf6-g4 , attacking the bishop. After 10.Bg5 f6 11.Bc1 Black was able to make the thematic advance 11...f5 (Game 12).

Nb8-d7

From here the knight attacks the c5 and e5 squares; it may also move on to b6, f6 or f8.

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.c4 Bg7 4.Nc3 O-O 5.e4 d6 6.Be2 e5

(position after 7.d4-d5 )

With 7...Nb8-d7 followed by ...a7-a5 Black secures the c5 square for his knight (Game 34). A modern way of achieving the same aim is by 7...a5 and ...Na6 (Game 30).

Nb8-c6

This defends the e5 square and attacks the white pawn on d4.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.O-O Nc6

Nb8-a6

This prepares ...Na6-c5, or in some cases ...Na6-c7.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 c6 4.e4 e5 5.d5 Be7 6.Be2 O-O

(position after 7.Ng1-f3 )

Black's position is cramped. With 7...Nb8-a6 he begins an original manoeuvre aimed at exchanging his passive dark-square bishop: ...Bd7, ...Qc8 and ...Bd8-b6 (Game A47).

Nd7-f8

This may be a good defensive post for the knight, from where it can also be brought into the action via h7 or e6.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.b3 d6 3.d4 g6 4.Bb2 Bg7 5.Nbd2 Nbd7 6.e3 O-O 7.c4 e5 8.Be2 Re8 9.O-O e4 10.Ne1 Nf8

Nd7-c5

When the white d-pawn has advanced or been exchanged, c5 is often a good post for the knight, from where it attacks e4, d3, b3 and a4.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 Nbd7 4.Nf3 e5 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.e4 c6 9.b3 exd4 10.Nxd4 Re8

(position after 11.Bc1-b2 )

Black plays 11...Nd7-c5 , attacking the e4 pawn, and also planning a queenside offensive with ...a7-a5-a4.

12.Qc2 a5 13.Rad1 Qb6 14.Nde2 a4 15.Nc1 Qc7 16.Rfe1 axb3 17.axb3 Be6 18.Nd3 Nxd3 19.Qxd3 Nd7

(position after 20.Qd3-c2 )

Several moves later in the same game, Black's second knight moves to the same square - 20...Nd7-c5 . White drives away the knight by 21.b4 , but this leaves his c4 pawn weakened (Game 17).

Nd7-e5

Normally played when Black has exchanged pawns ...e5xd4, this knight move attacks the squares g4, f3, d3 and c4.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nf3 Nbd7 4.g3 g6 5.Bg2 Bg7 6.O-O O-O 7.Nc3 e5 8.e4 c6 9.b3 Re8 10.Bb2 exd4 11.Nxd4 Qb6 12.Qd2 Nc5 13.Rae1 a5 14.Nc2 Be6 15.Ba1 a4 16.b4 Ncd7

(position after 17.Nc2-e3 )

Earlier Black's queen's knight occupied c5, but was driven back by b3-b4. Now by 17...Nd7-e5 it attacks the weakened pawn at c4 (Game A5).

Na6-c5

When the white pawn has moved from d4 this can be a good post for the knight, from where it attacks the squares e4, d3, b3 and a4.

1.c4 Nf6 2.Nc3 e5 3.Nf3 d6 4.g3 g6 5.Bg2 Bg7 6.O-O O-O 7.d3 c6 8.Bd2 Re8 9.Rc1 h6 10.Qc2 Na6 11.Rfd1 Nc5

Na6-c7

From here the knight defends the e6 square and attacks d5 and b5.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.Nf3 O-O 5.Bg5 d6 6.e3 c5 7.d5 Na6

(position after 8.Bf1-e2 )

Black's standard plan in this type of position is to advance his pawn to b5. He prepares this with 8...Na6-c7 followed by ...a7-a6 and ...Rb8 (Game 41).

Bf8-g7

The characteristic move of the King's Indian Defence. The bishop is developed on the long diagonal h8-a1. This is also one of the stages in building the 'house' for the king.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7

Bf8-e7

This less aggressive development of the bishop, which also prepares castling, characterises the Old Indian Defence.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.g3 d6 3.d4 Nbd7 4.Bg2 e5 5.c4 c6

(position after 6.O-O )

Black opts for 6...Bf8-e7 . Later, after castling and ...Re8, the bishop retreats to f8, creating pressure on the e-file (Game A63).

Bg7-f8

With this move Black covers the squares on the f8-a3 diagonal, and in some cases allows his rook to come into action on the g- or h-file.

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.Bg5 Bg7 4.Nbd2 d6 5.e3 O-O 6.Bd3 Nbd7 7.O-O h6 8.Bh4 e5 9.c3 Qe8 10.Ne1 d5 11.Nb3 a5 12.a4 Nb6 13.Nc5 Nfd7 14.Qb3 Nxc5 15.dxc5 Nc4 16.Bxc4 dxc4 17.Qxc4 Be6 18.Qe2 Qc6 19.e4 Qxc5 20.Nd3 Qc6 21.Qe3 Rfe8 22.Nc5 Bc8 23.Nb3 b6

(position after 24.Nb3-d2 )

The King's Indian bishop's main diagonal is blocked, so with 24...Bg7-f8 Black switches it to the f8-a3 diagonal, where it later takes up a powerful post at c5 (Game 8).

Bc8-d7

This covers the squares on the e8-a4 diagonal and enables Black to connect his rooks.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 c6 4.e4 e5 5.d5 Be7 6.Be2 O-O 7.Nf3 Na6 8.O-O Bd7

Bc8-e6

This move attacks the opponent's queenside (often, in particular, a pawn at c4) and enables Black to connect his rooks.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nf3 Nbd7 4.g3 g6 5.Bg2 Bg7 6.O-O O-O 7.Nc3 e5 8.e4 c6 9.b3 Re8 10.Bb2 exd4 11.Nxd4 Qb6 12.Qd2 Nc5 13.Rae1 a5

(position after 14.Nd4-c2 )

White's last move was aimed at countering the threat of ...a5-a4, but after 14...Bc8-e6 15.Ba1 a4 16.b4 Ncd7 he was unable to defend his c4 pawn (Game A5).

Bc8-f5

This covers the h7-b1 diagonal, in particular the important central e4 square.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.b3 Re8 9.e3 c6 10.Qc2 Qa5 11.a4 Nf8

(position after 12.Bc1-a3 )

With 12...Bc8-f5 Black developed his bishop with gain of tempo (Game 1). White should have replied 13.Qd2 , temporarily preventing 13...e4 because of the tactical trick 14.Nxe4 .

Bc8-h3

This move, attacking the g2 square, can be the culmination of an attack by Black on the kingside.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.h3 O-O 6.Bg5 Qe8 7.Be2 Na6 8.Nf3 e5 9.d5 Nh5 10.g3 f6 11.g4 fxg5 12.gxh5 gxh5 13.Nxg5 Qe7 14.Rg1 Qf6 15.Rg2 Kh8 16.Bxh5 Bh6 17.h4 Nc5 18.b4 Qf4 19.Ne2 Qxh4 20.Nf7+ Rxf7 21.Bxf7 Bh3

Bc8-g4

The bishop attacks the squares on the h5-d1 diagonal, in particular f3, where it may pin the white knight against the queen.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 c5 3.d5 e6 4.Nc3 exd5 5.cxd5 d6 6.e4 g6 7.Nf3 a6

(position after 8.a2-a4 )

In this type of Benoni position the c4 square is potentially a strong post for White's king's knight. Therefore Black plays 8...Bc8-g4 , being ready to follow up with ...Bxf3 (Game 42).

Rf8-e8

The rook supports the e5 pawn, and makes a direct or potential attack on the white e4 pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Be2 Nbd7 6.Nf3 e5 7.O-O O-O

(position after 8.Ra1-b1 )

To force White to resolve the central pawn tension, Black plays 8...Rf8-e8 . The drawback is that after 9.d5 the rook may have to return to f8, to support ...f7-f5 (Game A44).

Rf8-g8

From here the rook aids Black's kingside pawn offensive by supporting the advance of the g-pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.c4 Bg7 4.Nc3 O-O 5.e4 d6 6.Be2 e5 7.O-O Nc6 8.d5 Ne7 9.Nd2 a5 10.Rb1 Nd7 11.a3 f5 12.b4 Kh8 13.f3 f4 14.Nb3 axb4 15.axb4 g5 16.c5 Nf6 17.Bd2 Ng6 18.c6 b6 19.Be1 Rg8

Rf8-f7

From here the rook defends the 7th rank, and is ready to move to g7 to support the advance of the g-pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nf3 g6 4.Nc3 Bg7 5.e4 Bg4 6.Be2 O-O 7.Be3 Nfd7 8.Ng1 Bxe2 9.Ngxe2 e5 10.O-O Nc6 11.f3 f5 12.d5 Ne7 13.Nc1 f4 14.Bf2 g5 15.Nd3 Nf6 16.c5 Ng6 17.cxd6 cxd6

(position after 18.Ra1-c1 )

To guard against a possible invasion on c7, Black plays 18...Rf8-f7 . The rook might later have supported the kingside offensive, although in fact it found employment by doubling on the c-file (Game A57).

Ra8-b8

From here the rook supports the advance of the b-pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.e4 c6 9.h3 Nh5 10.Be3 Qe7 11.Nh2 Kh8 12.Re1 a6

(position after 13.a2-a3 )

Black does not wish to exchange ...e5xd4, and ...f7-f5 is also ruled out. He therefore plays 13...Ra8-b8 , to initiate play on the queenside by ...b7-b5 (Game 40).

Ra8-d8

When the rook is no longer required on the a-file, Black makes it ready for possible action in the centre.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 O-O 6.Be3 c5 7.Nge2 Nc6 8.Qd2 b6 9.Rd1 e6 10.g3 Ba6 11.b3 Re8 12.Kf2 d5 13.e5 cxd4 14.Bxd4 Nd7 15.cxd5 Ncxe5 16.Nf4 Bb7 17.Be2 exd5 18.Nfxd5 Nf6 19.Nf4 Qe7 20.Rhe1 Rad8

Ra8-e8

With this move Black completes his development and lends added support to his e-pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 O-O 6.Be3 e5 7.d5 Nh5 8.Qd2 Qh4+ 9.Qf2 Qe7 10.Bd3 Na6 11.O-O-O Bd7 12.Nge2 f5 13.exf5 gxf5 14.Qg1 Nf6

(position after 15.h2-h3 )

After his last move White is ready to advance g2-g4. Black plays 15...Ra8-e8 to prepare the dynamic counter ...e5-e4 (Game A43).

Qd8-a5

From here the queen attacks White's queenside, and also in some cases can switch to the kingside along the 5th rank.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.g3 Bg7 4.Bg2 O-O 5.Nc3 d6 6.Nf3 Nbd7 7.O-O e5 8.e4 Re8 9.h3 exd4 10.Nxd4 Nc5 11.Re1 a5 12.Qc2 c6 13.Be3 Nfd7 14.Rad1 a4

(position after 15.Nd4-e2 )

With 15...Qd8-a5 Black posts his queen actively, exploiting the fact that his d6 pawn is invulnerable: 16.Rxd6 Ne5 17.b3 allows the surprise combination 17...Bxh3 (Game 20).

Qd8-b6

Another active post for the queen, from where it puts pressure on the b-file and the a7-g1 diagonal.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.b3 Re8 9.e4 exd4 10.Nxd4 Nc5 11.Re1 a5 12.Bb2 a4 13.Rc1 c6 14.Ba1 axb3

(position after 15.a2xb3 )

After 15...Qd8-b6 the black queen's influence extended not only to the b3 pawn, but also along the diagonal to the white king (Game 15).

Qd8-c7

A modest post for the queen, from where it maintains protection of the d6 pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 Nbd7 4.Nf3 c6 5.e4 e5 6.Be2 Be7 7.O-O O-O 8.Qc2 Re8

(position after 9.Rf1-d1 )

Black is cramped. With 9...Qd8-c7 he moves his queen out of the line of the white rook, and prepares the regrouping ...Nf8-g6 (Game A21).

Qd8-e8

Often played to avoid a pin or attack by the white bishop on the h4-d8 diagonal. The queen may later come into play on the kingside.

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.Bg5 Bg7 4.Nbd2 d6 5.e3 O-O 6.Bd3 Nbd7 7.O-O h6 8.Bh4 e5

(position after 9.c2-c3 )

Moving out of the pin by 9...Qd8-e8 , Black creates the immediate threat of 10...e4 (Game 8).

Qd8-e7

From here the queen is ready for action on the e-file, as well as the two dark-square diagonals.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.e4 c6 9.h3 Nh5

(position after 10.Bc1-e3 )

After the development of the white bishop at e3 there is no longer any threat of a pin on the a3-f8 diagonal, and the queen can be comfortably posted by 10...Qd8-e7 (Game 40).

0-0

With this move the king takes up residence in its 'house', at the same time making the rook ready for action.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f4 O-O

Kg8-h7

From here the king lends added support to the h6 square, moves off the g-file, and vacates g8 for possible manoeuvres of the black pieces.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.d4 O-O 6.Be2 Nbd7 7.O-O e5 8.Bg5 h6 9.Be3 c6 10.h3 Re8 11.d5 c5

(position after 12.Qd1-d2 )

With 12...Kg8-h7 Black defends his h6 pawn and prepares a regrouping of his cramped forces by ...Ng8 and ...Nf8 (Game 36).

Kg8-h8

The king moves off the g-file and vacates the g8 square for possible manoeuvres of the black pieces.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.O-O Nc6 8.d5 Ne7 9.Ne1 Nd7 10.Nd3 f5 11.exf5 gxf5 12.f4 Ng6 13.Be3 Nf6

(position after 14.Ra1-c1 )

14...Kg8-h8 . Moving the king into the corner allows Black's other pieces more manoeuvring space. Later his rook was able to make use of the g-file (Game A41).
