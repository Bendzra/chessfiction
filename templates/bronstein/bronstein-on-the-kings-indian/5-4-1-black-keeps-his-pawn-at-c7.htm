Game 24
White: Georgy Lisitsyn
Black: David Bronstein
13 th USSR Championship Moscow 1944

This game demonstrates one of the ways for Black to play against the closed centre.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.d4 Nbd7 4.Nc3 e5 5.e4 g6 6.Be2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.d5 a5

Black prepares ...Nc5 by for the moment preventing b2-b4. White, in turn, is ready to evict the knight from c5 by Ne1-d3, or to exchange it. Of course, I could have exchanged on d4 earlier, but when the white bishop is developed at e2 I do not like to take on d4.

9.Ne1 Nc5

It used to be thought that, when Black's knight reached c5, he achieved a good position. After all, I was not the first and not the tenth person to play the King's Indian. It was employed by Capablanca and the English Champion Yates, and it was played by Reti, Euwe, Chigorin and even Tarrasch. But it should be realised that this is a good position only compared with the Queen's Gambit, where you are down on your knees, awaiting the invasion of a knight at e5, the doubling of rooks, and so on.

10.Qc2 b6 11.Bg5 h6 12.Be3 Ng4

Georgy Lisitsyn was a very strong player, who wrote three excellent books - on the opening, middlegame and endgame. I confidently played 12...Ng4 , and was very surprised by the exchange of first one bishop for a knight, and then the other.

13.Bxg4 Bxg4 14.Nd3 Bd7

Today, of course, I would have taken the knight at d3 and then played

15.Rae1 f5

16.Bxc5 bxc5 17.f4

A brilliant decision by White - he captured with his bishop (!) on c5 and then played f2-f4. I found myself in a very difficult position, and did not know what to do. In general, at that time I was not afraid of ending up in difficulties, as I always believed that I would find something! That was also the case in the present game.

17...exf4 18.Rxf4 Be5 19.Nxe5 dxe5 20.Rf2 f4 21.Ne2 g5 22.Qc3 Qe7 23.Nc1 g4 24.Nd3

24...g3

The only way to gain some play.

25.hxg3 fxg3 26.Rxf8+ Rxf8 27.Nxe5 Qh4 28.Nf3 Rxf3 29.Qxf3

29...h5

We were both in severe time trouble, with about a minute each for our last fifteen moves. At the time I was very proud of this move.

30.Qe3 Bg4 31.e5 Bf5 32.Rd1 Qh2+ 33.Kf1 Qh1+ 34.Qg1 Qh4 35.Qxc5 Qh1+ 36.Qg1

36...Qh4 37.Rd4 Bg4 38.Rd2 Qg5 39.Qd4 Qf5+ 40.Ke1 Qb1+ 41.Rd1 Bxd1 42.Qxd1 Qxb2 43.Qxh5 Qf2+ 44.Kd1 Qd4+ 45.Ke2 Qe4+ 46.Kd2 Qd4+

Draw agreed

Game 25
White: Cenek Kottnauer
Black: David Bronstein
Prague v. Moscow (round 11), Moscow, 1946

This game is from our historic match with the Czechoslovak team, which took place half in Prague and half in Moscow.

My opponent, an intelligent, clever, athletic man, also played water polo. Then at some point he travelled to a tournament in England, fell in love with a beautiful Englishwoman, and decided to settle down there.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.g3 g6 4.Bg2 Bg7 5.O-O Nbd7 6.d4 e5 7.Nc3 O-O 8.d5

White closes the centre and aims later to build up an attack against Black's queenside. Kottnauer had of course seen my games with Pachman and Zita, and therefore he did not want to play e2-e4, which allows a possible ...e5xd4.

After the immediate closing of the centre Black's only feasible reply is ...a7-a5 and ...Nc5.

8...a5 9.e4 Nc5 10.Ne1 Nfd7

Black's plan is to advance ...f7-f5, and then possibly ...f5-f4 with an attack on the kingside. A nuance of the given variation is that, as long as the white queen is at d1, Black cannot play ...Nh5, since after the thematic ...f7-f5, e4xf5 he cannot reply ...g6xf5. Therefore he has to retreat his knight to d7 or e8, play ...f7-f5 and then return the knight to f6.

11.Be3 f5 12.Qd2 b6

With the aim of answering Bxc5 with ...b6xc5, but this is an unnecessary move that conclusively weakens the light squares on the queenside, and Kottnauer later exploits this factor.

13.Nc2 Ba6 14.Na3 Nf6 15.exf5 gxf5 16.f3 Qe7 17.Rae1 h5 18.Nab5 Nh7 19.Bh3

19...Rae8

19...Qf7 is bad because of 20.f4 and if 20...e4 21.Bxc5 bxc5 22.Rxe4 .

20.Qc2 Bc8 21.Na7

Kottnauer thought that 21.a3 followed by b2-b4 would have been better. However, Black would have replied 21...Qf7 22.b4 Nb7 .

21...Bd7 22.Nc6

If 22.Ncb5 Black has the good reply 22...Na6 .

22...Qf7

23.b3 Bxc6 24.dxc6 Ne6 25.Nd5

White sacrifices his c6 pawn, obtaining a strong position for his knight at d5 and a dangerous attack on the queenside.

25...Nd8 26.a3 Nxc6 27.b4 axb4 28.axb4 Nd8

Not 28...Nd4 in view of 29.Bxd4 exd4 30.Bxf5

29.Ra1 f4

If 29...Ne6 30.Ra7 with the threat of Bxb6. Now White cannot play 30.gxf4 because of 30...exf4 .

30.Ra7

Brilliantly played.

30...Ng5 31.Bc8 Nge6

Time trouble, time trouble. And, of course, fatigue. It is not easy constantly to maintain the tension of a combinative game. After 31...fxe3 32.Rxc7 Nxf3+ 33.Kh1 Qxc7 34.Nxc7 Nd4 35.Rxf8+ ( 35.Qd3 Rxf1+ 36.Qxf1 Rf8 37.Qe1 e2 38.Bh3 Bh6 ) 35...Rxf8 36.Qd3 e2 Black would have emerged unscathed. But where to find the seconds needed to calculate these variations? Accepting the sacrifice ( 31...fxe3 ) would have led to undesirable complications, and so Black chooses a quieter continuation, by which he maintains some initiative.

32.Bxe6 Nxe6 33.Bxb6 Ra8 34.Rxa8 Rxa8 35.Bf2 fxg3

35...Ra3 came into consideration.

36.Bxg3 Nd4 37.Qg2 c6 38.Nb6 Ra6 39.c5 Ra2 40.Qh3 d5 41.Kh1 Ra3 42.f4 e4 43.Nc8 e3 44.Nd6 Qg6

45.b5

A clever idea. White defends very resourcefully and causes his opponent the utmost difficulty in achieving any real gains.

45...Nxb5

Not 45...cxb5 in view of 46.f5 Qg4 47.Qg2 . With the text move Black forces a favourable ending.

46.f5 Qg4 47.Qxg4 hxg4 48.f6 Bf8 49.Nxb5 cxb5 50.f7+ Kh7

51.c6

Although this ending appears to be a simple win, it contains many latent possibilities, and demands of Black very careful play and precise calculation. The win for him is not hard after 51.Bd6 , e.g. 51...Kg7 52.Be5+ Kg6 53.Bd6 e2 54.Re1 Bxd6 55.cxd6 Kxf7 56.Rxe2 Ra6 57.Rb2 Rxd6 58.Rxb5 d4 , but 51.Rc1 sets more difficult problems. Here after 51...d4 52.c6 d3 53.c7 Ra8 54.c8Q Rxc8 55.Rxc8 d2 56.Rd8 b4 57.Kg2 b3 it is unexpectedly White who wins: 58.Rxf8 d1Q 59.Rh8+ . Therefore after 51.Rc1 Black would have continued 51...b4 52.c6 Ra8 as in the game.

51...b4 52.Rc1 Ra8 53.c7 Rc8 54.Rd1 b3

This is the idea - the pawns are immune.

55.Rxd5

Now White loses quickly. The best defence was 55.Kg2 b2 56.Kf1 , but after 56...Kg6 57.Ke2 Kxf7 58.Kxe3 Ke6 Black should in the end win with his extra pawn.

55...e2 56.Rb5

56.Rd2 also fails to save the game after 56...Rxc7 57.Rxe2 Rxf7 .

56...Rxc7 57.Rxb3 Rc1+ 58.Kg2 Bc5

A pretty concluding manoeuvre, in a position where White was still hoping to draw. If instead 58...e1Q 59.Bxe1 Rxe1 60.h3 and Black's extra bishop is unlikely to help him to win.

But now after 59.h3 there follows 59...Rg1+ 60.Kh2 Rxg3 and wins, while if 59.Bf2 Bxf2 60.f8Q Rg1+ 61.Kxf2 e1Q mate.

White resigns

Game 26
White: Alexander Kotov
Black: David Bronstein
Candidates Tournament, Zurich, 1953

A game abounding in various strategic and tactical motifs, where White persistently aims to break through on the queenside, and Black equally persistently strives for an attack on the king.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.g3 Bg7 4.Bg2 O-O 5.Nc3 d6 6.Nf3 Nbd7 7.O-O e5 8.e4 Re8 9.d5

9...a6 10.Ne1 Rb8 11.Nc2 Qe7

After the closing of the centre Black appeared to be preparing play on the b-file. But when White made preparations to oppose this, Black began large-scale manoeuvres on the other side of the board. Why did I avoid playing 11...b5? Because White would have replied 12.cxb5 axb5 13.Nb4 , when Black is left with a weak b5 pawn and a paralysed queenside.

12.b4 Rf8 13.Ne3 Ne8 14.Qc2 Ndf6 15.a4 a5

An important move, otherwise White himself would have advanced his a-pawn, and then opened one of the queenside files (b- or c-).

16.bxa5 Ra8 17.Ba3 Nd7 18.Bh3 h5

The advance of this pawn enabled me to include my knight in the play on the kingside via the route ...Ne8-f6-h7-g5.

19.Ncd1 Rxa5 20.Nb2 Nef6 21.Bb4 Ra6 22.a5 Nh7 23.Bg2 h4 24.Nd3 Ndf6

A sharp, dynamic battle has developed, with approximate equilibrium. But, as is known, ideally correct play in chess does not happen! Here, carried away by my manoeuvres on the kingside, I allow White to break though on the queenside, which 24...Nc5 would have prevented.

25.c5 Ng5 26.Qc4 Bh6 27.Bd2 Ra8 28.Rfc1 Nh5 29.cxd6 cxd6 30.Qc7 Qf6 31.Ne1 hxg3 32.hxg3 Nh3+ 33.Bxh3 Bxh3

34.Ng4

The immediate 34.Qxb7 would have allowed the knight sacrifice 34...Nxg3 35.fxg3 Bxe3+ and 36...Qf1+ , closing in on the white king.

34...Bxg4 35.Bxh6 Rfc8 36.Qxb7 Rcb8 37.a6 g5

38.Qxb8+

I have given too much freedom to the white a-pawn, and here I could have lost to a pretty combination: 38.Rcb1 Rxb7 39.axb7 Rb8 40.Ra8 Qd8 41.Bxg5 f6 42.Rxb8 Qxb8 43.Be3 Bc8 44.Ba7 Qxb7 45.Rxb7 Bxb7 46.Bb8 , attacking the pawns from the rear.

38...Rxb8 39.a7 Ra8 40.Rcb1 Kh7 41.Rb8 Rxa7 42.Rxa7 Kxh6

After Kotov failed to take his chance and the game was adjourned, on the resumption Black several times missed a win. However, he cannot make any progress without sacrificing one of his minor pieces.

43.Rbb7 Kg6 44.f3 Bc8 45.Rc7 Qd8 46.g4 Nf6 47.Kg2 Bd7 48.Nc2 Bxg4 49.fxg4 Nxg4 50.Rxf7 Qb6 51.Rg7+ Kh5 52.Rh7+ Nh6 53.Rac7 Qb3 54.Kf2

54...g4

Here, for example: 54...Qd3 55.Ne3 Qd2+ would have won.

55.Ne3 Qd3

And here 55...Kg5 56.Rcg7+ Kf4 57.Ng2+ Kxe4 58.Rxh6 g3+ .

56.Nf5 Qf3+ 57.Kg1 Qd1+ 58.Kf2 Kg5 59.Nxh6 Qd2+

Draw agreed

Game 27
White: Harry Golombek
Black: David Bronstein
Great Britain v. USSR, London, 1954

This game was played on board two in the second round of this double-round match, one of a series of meetings between the USSR and foreign teams during 1954.

1.c4 Nf6 2.d4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.O-O Nc6 8.d5 Ne7

A modern variation played in a game nearly half a century ago.

9.Bg5 h6 10.Bd2

White deliberately forced a weakening of the castled position ( 9...h6 ), considering it useful for him.

10...Nd7 11.Qc1 Kh7 12.Ne1 f5 13.g3

13...fxe4

It is interesting that here Black plays somewhat against the rules - he allows the white knight to occupy the blockading square e4, but on the other hand he also transfers his own knight to the centre. This occurred for the first time in the present game. It had happened before that White himself captured on f5 and Black took with the knight, but this is not exactly the same thing.

14.Nxe4 Nf5 15.Nc2

15...c6

Here Black has to play very accurately. He begins dislodging the knight from e4 by ...c7-c6 and ...d6-d5.

I often employed the procedure seen in the present game - that of hiding my king behind a bishop and two pawns (for the king two pawns are enough!). One rook operates on the open f-file, and again the queen's rook and queen's bishop do not come into play for a long time.

16.dxc6

With 15...c6 Black invited 16.g4 , but after 16...Nd4 17.Nxd4 exd4 18.Nxd6 d3 19.Bd1 Ne5 White's position is hard to defend.

16...bxc6 17.Bb4 Nf6 18.Bf3 a5 19.Ba3 Qc7 20.Qd2

20...Rd8

The battle is proceeding as follows: White attacks the d6 pawn (Black's main weakness in the King's Indian!), and Black accurately defends it. Hence this very unusual move.

21.Rac1 Be6 22.Qe2 Qf7 23.b3 Nxe4 24.Bxe4 d5

With this move Black seizes the initiative and successfully makes use of his central pawns, and thereby (with ...e5-e4) shuts the opponent's light-square bishop out of the game.

25.cxd5 cxd5 26.Bg2 Rac8 27.Qa6 e4 28.Ne3 Nd4 29.Rxc8 Rxc8 30.Qxa5 Ne2+ 31.Kh1 d4

The culmination of Black's strategy - like an ice-breaker the central pawns split White's position in two.

32.Nd1 Bg4 33.h3 Bf3 34.Qe1 Rc2 35.Kh2 Bxg2 36.Kxg2 Qf3+ 37.Kh2 Be5 38.Rg1 Nxg3

White resigns

Game 28
White: Boris Spassky
Black: David Bronstein
Candidates Tournament, Amsterdam, 1956

In this game I was able to introduce an original queen sacrifice. Asked at the time about my evaluation of the resulting position, I replied that I considered the sacrifice to be correct, and that subsequently there would be players willing to take the side of either White or Black. I myself played the variation again nearly 40 years later in a simultaneous (Game A62).

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 e5 6.d5 Nh5 7.Be3 Na6

I do not like moving my knights to the edge of the board, but in this game I exceeded the norm - in the space of two moves both knights have ended up there.

8.Qd2 Qh4+

After this move I was convinced that White should play either 9.Qf2 or 9.Bf2 . But Spassky confidently advanced his g-pawn, which I took with my knight.

9.g3 Nxg3

In many magazines this move was proclaimed as virtually the move of the century. I wanted to demonstrate that the two bishops are a great force. It seems to me that subsequently Spassky did not play very well.

10.Qf2 Nxf1 11.Qxh4 Nxe3 12.Kf2 Nxc4

13.b3 Nb6 14.Nge2 f5 15.Rhg1 O-O 16.Kg2 Bd7 17.a4 Bf6 18.Qg3 Nb4 19.a5 Nc8

Here I expected any move by White (e.g., 20.Kh1 or 20.Rad1 ), apart from the capture on f5. When Spassky in fact played this, I became nervous and took with the bishop.

20.exf5

20...Bxf5

But Black had available a very powerful move - 20...Ne7! , with the possible sequel 21.Ne4 (or 21.fxg6 Nf5 22.Qe1 Nc2 ) 21...Nxf5 22.Nxf6+ Rxf6 23.Qg5 Raf8 when he has a very strong position. There is a mass of threats - ...Nd4, ...Nc2 and ...Nd3. It seems to me that here Black's position is considerably better, as his pieces are dominant.

21.Ra4

I became further rattled after this move - subsequently I could have played much better.

21...Nd3

Thus either 21...Nc2 or else the immediate 21...Na6 was preferable.

22.Rc4 Nc5 23.Ne4 Na6 24.Nxf6+ Rxf6 25.f4 e4 26.Nc3 Ne7 27.Re1 Raf8 28.b4 c6 29.Nxe4 Bxe4+ 30.Rcxe4 Nxd5 31.Re8 Nac7

And here the obvious 31...Nxf4+ and 32...Nxb4 would have offered better chances of resisting.

32.Rxf8+ Kxf8 33.Kh1 Rf5 34.Qh4 Nf6 35.Qf2 Nb5 36.Qe2 Nd5 37.a6 bxa6 38.Qe8+ Kg7 39.Qxc6 Kh6 40.Qxa6 Nxb4 41.Qb7 Nd3 42.Re7 Nxf4 43.Rxh7+ Kg5 44.Qe7+ Kg4 45.Qe3 Kg5 46.h4+ Kg4 47.Kh2 Nh5 48.Rh6

Black resigns.

Black lost this game, but the idea of the queen sacrifice remains viable to this day.

Game 29
White: David Bronstein
Black: Albert Kapengut
40th USSR Championship, Baku, 1972

My opponent had already established something of a reputation as a theoretician, but in this game a basic opening error led eventually to his defeat.

1.c4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 O-O 5.d4 d6 6.h3 e5 7.d5 Na6 8.Bg5 h6 9.Be3 Nc5 10.Nd2

10...Nh7 11.b4 Na6

This game illustrates how a piece can be shut out of the game. In the given case it was the black knight at a6. Essentially for the entire game White was playing with an extra piece. Black could have prevented this by the standard move 10...a5 .

12.a3 f5 13.Nb3 Nf6 14.c5 Nxe4 15.Nxe4 fxe4 16.Bc4 Qe8

17.c6 bxc6 18.dxc6+ Be6 19.Bxe6+ Qxe6 20.Na5 d5 21.Qb3 Rf6 22.O-O Rd8 23.Nb7 Re8 24.b5 d4 25.Qxe6+ Rexe6 26.Bd2 Bf8

Here there was another important point: Black has obtained three pawns in the centre, but the white rooks are able to outflank and eliminate them, because Black is playing a piece down.

27.Rfc1 Nb8 28.Nc5 Bxc5 29.Rxc5 Rf5 30.Re1 Ref6 31.Rxe4 Rxf2 32.Bxh6 d3 33.Rd5 Re2

34.Rexe5 Nxc6 35.bxc6 Rxe5 36.Rxe5 Rxc6 37.Be3 Rc3 38.Kf2 Kf7 39.a4 Rc2+ 40.Kf3 Kf6 41.Rd5

Black resigns

Game 30
White: Olafur Thorsson
Black: David Bronstein
Reykjavik Open, 1996

This game went unnoticed by the theoreticians, although Black employed a rare manoeuvre with his king's knight: ...Ng8-f6-d7-c5. Usually it is his queen's knight that goes to c5.

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.c4 Bg7 4.Nc3 O-O 5.e4 d6 6.Be2 e5 7.d5

When White closes the centre, you as Black have several options, depending on your mood and your imagination. You can play 7...Nbd7 then ...Nc5, and wait, to see what White will play. You can even make the quiet move 7...Qe7 or the aggressive 7...Nh5 . You do not close the centre by 7...c5 , as you want to keep open the option of the undermining move ...c7-c6. But if you play 7...c6 immediately, then after the inevitable exchange ...c6xd5, c4xd5 the white knight acquires the good square c4. The difference between this variation, and the one with the fianchetto of the bishop at g2, is that there it is harder for White to establish his knight at c4.

7...a5

In chess there is always a slight air of uncertainty - you never know whether you are losing a move, or gaining one. Thus here 7...a5 is either a competent move, or it is a waste of time - suppose that the knight does not go to c5, suppose that it is not needed there? Perhaps the immediate 7...Ne8 would be better? In undetermined positions such as this one (White has not yet castled) I have always harboured doubts: you play 7...Ne8 , and the opponent unexpectedly advances his h-pawn. Then you have to play ...h7-h6, to avoid the opening of the h-file.

8.Bg5 Qe8 9.Nd2 Nfd7 10.g4 Nc5 11.Rg1 Nba6

12.a3

Here White made a typical mistake. He did not play 12.b3 , as evidently he did not care for 12...a4 13.Rb1 axb3 14.axb3 Nb4 , but in this line he had a much stronger move - 13.Qb1!

Now the black knights begin to take up dominant posts on the queenside.

12...a4 13.h4 Bd7 14.h5 h6 15.Be3 g5 16.Nb5

16...Qb8 17.Qc2 c6 18.Nc3 Qd8 19.f3 Qa5 20.Kf2 Rfb8 21.Rgd1 Bf6 22.Rdc1 Bd8

A most unusual occurrence - on Black's kingside the only piece remaining is his king. His dark-square bishop has left there, aiming for the queenside.

23.Bd1

I had foreseen the possibility of the unexpected knight leap on move 24, but if White had played 23.Bf1 Bb6 24.Bg2 it is probable that nothing terrible for him would have occurred.

23...Bb6 24.Be2

24...Ne6 25.Bxb6

Or 25.dxe6 Bxe3+ , when 26.Kxe3 allows 26...Qc5+ 27.Kd3 Qd4 mate.

25...Qxb6+ 26.Kf1 Nf4 27.Nxa4 Qe3 28.Nc3 Nc5 29.Re1

29...Bxg4 30.fxg4 Qg3 31.Bf3 Nh3 32.Ke2 Qh2+ 33.Kd1 Nf2+ 34.Ke2 Nxg4+ 35.Kd1 Nf2+ 36.Ke2 Nfxe4+ 37.Kd1 Nf2+ 38.Ke2 e4 39.Bxe4 (see diagram)

White resigns
