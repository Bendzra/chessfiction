White castles queenside

As regards the play in the middlegame, the relative placing of the kings is an even more important factor than the pawn formation. This section covers games where White castles on the queenside, and Black on the kingside, a situation which often leads to a fierce battle with mutual attacks on the kings.

Game 46
White: Vladimir Makogonov
Black: David Bronstein
Bolshevik Sports Society, Kiev, 1944

This game is interesting for the fact that Black tried to find a defence against the very strong system that Vladimir Makogonov often played - and to some extent he succeeded.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nf3 Bg7 4.Nc3 d6 5.e4 O-O 6.h3

It is this move that characterises the variation named after Makogonov.

6...c6 7.Bd3 e5 8.d5 cxd5 9.cxd5 Nh5

Now, in order to make the planned advance of his pawn to g4, White has to go in for some rather unwieldy manoeuvres.

10.g3 Nd7 11.Be3 a6 12.Be2 b5 13.Nd2 Nhf6 14.a3 Nb6

A notable feature of the opening is that, rather unusually, Black has obtained a strong-point for his knight at c4.

15.g4 Nfd7 16.h4

16...f5

Black is not afraid of opening the g-file for the opponent. It is more important to try and activate his pieces.

17.gxf5 gxf5 18.exf5 Nf6 19.h5

Makogonov thought this was not the best move, because Black was able to play his light-square bishop to h7 and gain counterplay.

19...h6 20.Rg1 Kh8 21.Nf1 Bxf5 22.Qd2 Ng8 23.Ng3 Bh7 24.Bd3 Nc4 25.Bxc4 bxc4

White has eliminated the strong black knight, but now the b-file has been opened for the black rooks, and if he moves his knight from c3, he always has to reckon with the breakthrough ...c4-c3.

26.O-O-O Rb8 27.Nge4 Rf7 28.Rg3 Nf6 29.Nxf6 Qxf6 30.Rdg1 Qf5

The intentions of the two sides are clear - White is attacking the g7 bishop, and Black the b2 pawn.

31.f3 Rb3

In time trouble Black plays rather recklessly. The quiet 31...Rfb7 would have been better.

32.Ne4 c3 33.bxc3 Rfb7 34.Qc2

Were this position to occur in a modern game, any grandmaster with Black would play 34...Bf8 , parrying White's threats while maintaining his own, and would easily realise his advantage.

The enormous number of opening guides helps modern grandmasters to make some 15-20 theoretical moves within the space of a few minutes, leaving them a great reserve of time for the rest of the game. But at the time when this game was played only the Queen's Gambit and the Ruy Lopez had been thoroughly studied, and in all the other openings the correct plans had to be sought during the game itself. Thus the time allotted for 40 moves hardly sufficed for the opening alone, and in major tournaments it was a normal occurrence for players to have perhaps only a quarter of an hour left for their last 10-15 moves, or sometimes even less than 5 minutes.

34...Rxa3 35.Rxg7 Qxf3

With a few more seconds on his clock, Black would surely have found 35...Ra1+ 36.Kd2 , when 36...Rxg7 37.Rxg7 Kxg7 38.Qb2 Qxf3 39.Qxa1 Qxe4 40.Qxa6 Qxd5+ 41.Ke1 leads to a draw, but he can also play more strongly: 36...Rxg1 37.Rxg1 Qxf3 , retaining the initiative.

36.Rxb7 Qxe3+ 37.Kb2 Bxe4 38.Qc1 Rxc3 39.Qxc3 Qxg1 40.Qc8+ Qg8 41.Qc7 Qg2+ 42.Ka3

Black resigns

Game 47
White: Izak Aloni
Black: David Bronstein
Olympiad, Moscow, 1956

When the reader plays through this game, he may find it hard to understand the enormous tension of the occasion. Black's opening play was in the nature of an experiment, based on the simple belief that open lines are more important than material, and soon after the opening he found himself in desperate time trouble. All this took place in the World (Team) Championship, where a loss by one of the Soviet players was considered a disgrace.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 e5 6.Nge2 O-O 7.Be3 Nbd7 8.Qd2 a6

With the idea that 9.O-O-O can be met by 9...b5 . However, if the opponent had replied 9.d5 and only then g2-g4, Black would have found it hard to obtain any play...

9.g4

But this wing attack, made before the centre is stabilised, is premature.

9...exd4 10.Nxd4 c5

A typical King's Indian move: Black believes that the dynamic play he gains for his pieces will outweigh the static weaknesses he creates on the d-file.

11.Nc2 Ne5 12.Be2 Be6 13.Na3 Nfd7 14.O-O-O

14...b5

All Black's actions are standard King's Indian moves! The advance of the queenside pawns has the aim of opening lines, setting White difficult problems.

15.cxb5 axb5 16.Ncxb5 c4 17.Qxd6 Qa5

17...c3 18.Nxc3 Qa5 would have been better, as now White could have played 18.Bd2 , e.g. 18...c3 19.Bxc3 Rfc8 20.Kb1 and it is not clear how Black continues the attack.

This was the one doubtful moment when Black might have lost the game. This is how the analysts reason - they assume that you are playing an ideal opponent, as in a correspondence game. But in a practical game there is the clock ticking away, and the opponent is nervous, you are nervous...

18.Bd4 Rfc8

Now all the black pieces, without exception, are actively placed. White's forces are scattered, and are unable to organise a defence.

19.Bc3

19...Nd3+ 20.Bxd3 Bxc3 21.Nxc3 cxd3 22.Rxd3 Ne5

Would 22...Nc5 have been better? After 23.Re3 Na4 24.Nab1 it is a pity that 24...Nxb2 25.Kxb2 Qxa2+ does not work because of 26.Kc1 , but on the other hand 24...Bxa2 leaves White hard-pushed to find a defence.

23.Re3 Rd8 24.Qe7 Rd7 25.Qf6 Qc5 26.Nc2 Nd3+ 27.Rxd3 Rxd3 28.a3

Instead of this, 28 Rd1 would have given White some drawing chances.

28...Qf2 29.Re1 Rd2

White resigns

Game 48
White: Arthur Bisguier
Black: David Bronstein
Budapest, 1961

This game does not follow a strict King's Indian move order, but the position reached after a dozen moves is very much in keeping with our theme.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 e4

With his fourth move White granted his opponent the possibility of advancing his central pawn with tempo, which is what I did. However, the well-known Yugoslav player Andrija Fuderer - he later gave up chess and became a professor of Chemistry in Antwerp - usually played the very interesting move 4...Nfd7 . He would then continue ...Na6, ...c7-c6, ...Be7, ...0-0, ...Re8 and ...Bf8 (cf., for example, Game A25). At the same time, if Black plays 4...Nbd7, then 5 Bg5 is unpleasant, as it is hard to get rid of the pin (cf. Game A50).

The move played by me is slightly risky; the white knight could also have gone to d2 or g5.

5.Ng1

In the first case Black can sacrifice a pawn - 5.Nd2 e3 with unclear consequences. If instead 5.Ng5 Bf5 , then White has the interesting move 6.g4 when he has the more attractive position, with more space in the centre.

5...h6 6.Bf4 g5 7.Bd2 Bg7 8.e3 Bf5 9.Nge2 Bg6 10.Qb3

In this unusual position I very much wanted to give up my b7 pawn, and it was with difficulty that I restrained myself from doing so.

10...b6 11.Ng3 O-O 12.Be2

White is ready to castle, so now is the moment to strike at the centre.

12...c5 13.dxc5 bxc5 14.f3 d5 15.cxd5 exf3 16.gxf3 Nbd7 17.e4 Rb8 18.Qa3 Re8 19.O-O-O g4 20.Rhg1 Rb4 21.Qxa7 gxf3 22.Bxf3 Ne5

An outwardly very complicated position has arisen, but note that Black's pieces are more usefully placed - his knights have posts in the centre, his bishops are trained on White's weak points, and his rooks have half-open files.

Somewhere around the 20-move mark, when it became quite impossible to work out the variations, Arthur Bisguier, a highly intelligent man with a good sense of humour, decided to take everything that was going, and he was soon three (!) pawns up.

23.Be2 Re7 24.Qxc5 Reb7 25.b3 Qa8

This is the move that I like best in this game. Some analyst may be able to find a defence, but during the game it was difficult even to understand what was threatened.

26.Kc2

Black's heavy pieces are trained directly on the white king's position, and simultaneously the two bishops are mounting a latent attack. The next move of the black rook immediately decides the outcome.

26...Rxe4 27.Ngxe4 Nxe4 28.Rxg6 Nxc5 29.Rg2 Ra7 30.a4 Ng6 31.Bc4 Qc8 32.Rf1 Qh3 33.Rff2 Ne5 34.Bf1 Qh4 35.Rf4 Qd8 36.Rb4 Kh7 37.Nb5 Rd7 38.d6 Qf6 39.Rf4 Qe6 40.Nd4 Qd5 41.Bc3 Ng6

White resigns

Game 49
White: David Bronstein
Black: Viktor Kupreichik
USSR Championship Semi-Final, Minsk, 1983

A difficult game for both sides. It is sufficient to say that White castled only on the 24th move - before that he simply did not have time. I employed, if it can be so expressed, the 'improved Makogonov Variation'.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 g6 4.d4 Bg7 5.e4 O-O 6.h3 e5 7.d5 Na6 8.Bg5 h6 9.Be3 Qe8 10.g4 Bd7 11.Bd3 c6 12.Nd2 Nc5

The point is that Black, after posting his knight at c5, began a battle for the centre. White kept his king in the centre, and at the necessary moment exchanged his dark-square bishop for this knight (Vladimir Makogonov, as a rule, begrudged giving up this bishop, and preferred to give up his light-square bishop, for example, after the manoeuvre ...Nb8-d7-b6-c4).

13.Bc2 a5 14.a3 cxd5 15.cxd5 Rc8 16.a4 Qd8 17.Qe2 Ne8 18.h4 f5 19.gxf5 gxf5 20.exf5 Bxf5 21.Bxf5 Rxf5

22.Bxc5 Rxc5 23.Nde4

The white knight has taken up a strong post at e4, and the bishop at g7, which usually plays an important role in Black's counterplay, in this case proves to be a passive piece.

23...Rc8 24.O-O-O Rf4 25.h5 Nf6

Strategically this game was conducted almost ideally by White, but at this point Black was obliged to play better than in the game: 25...b5 26.axb5 a4 with definite counterplay.

26.Kb1 Qd7 27.f3 Qf5

The white knight at e4 is holding up the advance of the black e-pawn. The black bishop at g7 is restricted by its own pawns and is passively placed. At the same time, the white rooks are ready to begin an attack on the g-file.

28.Ka2 Rxc3

This exchange sacrifice does not help.

29.Nxc3 Rb4 30.Qd3 e4 31.fxe4 Nxe4 32.Nxe4 Rxa4+ 33.Kb1 Rxe4 34.Rhg1

The decisive move, by which White nips in the bud the opponent's attacking pretensions, and himself launches a decisive attack.

34...Kh8 35.Qc2 Qe5 36.Qc8+ Qe8

Black is forced to block with his queen, as otherwise disaster awaits him: 36...Kh7 37.Rxg7+ Kxg7 38.Rg1+ and wins.

37.Rc1 b6 38.Rc7 Bf8 39.Qf5 Re1+ 40.Rxe1 Qxe1+ 41.Ka2

Black resigns

Game 50
White: Malcolm Pein
Black: David Bronstein
London League 1997

This short game is very instructive. In the Saemisch Variation Black must play very energetically. However, White in turn has learned to mount an attack with minimum risk.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 O-O 6.Nge2 e5 7.Bg5 c6 8.Qd2 Nbd7

This position has occurred on many occasions.

9.O-O-O

One of the most famous games in the history of Soviet chess continued 9.d5 c5 10.g4 a6 11.Ng3 Re8 12.h4 Qa5 13.Bh6 Nf8 14.h5 Qc7 15.Bd3 b5 16.O-O-O bxc4 17.Bb1 Bh8 18.Rdg1 Rb8 19.Nf5!! (Tal-Tolush, Moscow 1957). By winning it, the then 20-year-old student from Riga became the Champion of the USSR.

The following year he repeated his success, and he did not stop until 1960, when he became World Champion by defeating the great Botvinnik.

Players with White liked playing this position against the young Fischer. Once 9.d5 was played against him and White gained the advantage. After this Bobby refrained from ...Nbd7 and in a game with Pachman he played 8...Qa5 , but after 9.d5 cxd5 10.Nxd5! he ended up in an inferior position. This happened in 1958 at Portoroz. Many years later the Czech grandmaster was not averse to repeating this very variation in a game with me in 1994 at the Donner Memorial Tournament. However, I quickly exchanged on d4 and organised a break in the centre (cf. Game A61).

9...Qa5

My present opponent, Malcolm Pein, preferred to castle long. One of his games had continued 9...a6 10.Kb1 b5 . I decided to be a little crafty and play 9...Qa5 immediately. Reckoning that for the moment the advance of the b-pawn was not threatened, White quickly replied

10.Bh6

Of course, the move 10.Kb1 was obligatory.

10...b5

This move nevertheless followed, and Black seized the initiative.

11.Bxg7 Kxg7 12.Ng3 b4 13.Nb1 exd4 14.Qxd4 Qg5+

This pretty, long queen check had already occurred in a similar position in the game Stoltz-Petrosian, Bucharest 1953 (cf. Game A16).

15.Nd2 Ne5 16.Ne2

16...c5 17.h4 Qxg2 18.Qxe5 Qxh1 19.Qg3 Nh5 20.Qf2 Kg8 21.f4 Bg4 22.Bg2

22...Nxf4

My opponent had been hoping to trap my queen, but this reply, planned in advance, made everything clear.

23.Qxf4 Qxg2 24.Rg1 Qxe2 25.Rxg4 Rae8 26.h5 Re5

White resigns
