a7-a6

This prevents a white knight from going to b5 and prepares the possible advance of the b-pawn to b5.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.g3 O-O 5.Bg2 d6 6.O-O e5 7.d4 Nbd7 8.e4 a6

a7-a5

This move attacks the b4 square. The pawn may move on to a4, to attack a pawn at b3.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.d5 Nbd7 8.Bg5 h6

(position after 9.Bg5-h4 )

Black plays 9...a7-a5 , to safeguard his knight outpost at c5 (Game 34).

a5-a4

With this move Black either attacks the white b-pawn, or fixes the b3 square after the opponent has played a2-a3.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.e4 c6 9.d5 Nc5 10.Ne1 cxd5 11.cxd5 a5 12.f3 b6 13.Be3 Nh5

(position after 14.a2-a3 )

Black sets up a blockade on the queenside with 14...a5-a4 (Game 31). White can win a pawn by 15.Bxc5 bxc5 16.Nxa4 , but then 16...Bh6 gives Black active play.

b7-b6

This defends c5. Against restrained set-ups it can prepare the development of the light-square bishop at b7 or a6.

1.c4 g6 2.d4 Nf6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6

(position after 5.f2-f3 )

Black plays 5...b7-b6 , to support the advance of his c-pawn (Game 43). But after 6.Bd3 he must beware of 6...c5? , which loses material to 7.e5 .

b7-b5

With this move Black attacks the c4 pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 c5 3.d5 d6 4.Nc3 g6

(position after 5.e2-e4 )

Not wishing to defend passively, Black sacrifices a pawn by 5...b7-b5 . After 6.cxb5 and a subsequent pawn exchange on a6 he gains active play on the two open files against White's a- and b-pawns (Games A10 and A17). This idea was a forerunner of the Benko Gambit, in which the sacrifice is made even earlier ( 1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 c5 3.d5 b5 ).

c7-c6

This move defends the d5 and b5 squares, and allows the development of the queen at a5, b6 or c7.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 g6 4.e4 Bg7 5.f3 e5 6.Nge2 Nbd7 7.Be3 a6 8.Qd2 O-O 9.Nc1 exd4

(position after 10.Be3xd4 )

After White's time-consuming knight manoeuvre Ng1-e2-c1 it makes sense for Black to break open the centre. He plays 10...c7-c6 , planning to follow up with ...d6-d5 (Game A34).

c7-c5

This move attacks the d4 pawn and allows the development of the black queen, usually at a5.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f4 O-O

(position after 6.Ng1-f3 )

With 6...c7-c5 Black strikes at the white centre. He will answer 7.dxc5 with 7...Qa5 (Game 45).

c6xd5

After the normal recapture c4xd5, this leads to the opening of the c-file.

1.c4 Nf6 2.Nc3 e5 3.g3 c6 4.Nf3 d6 5.Bg2 g6 6.O-O Bg7 7.e4 O-O 8.d4 Qa5

(position after 9.d4-d5 )

To justify his experimental queen development at a5, Black plays 9...c6xd5 10.cxd5 in order to continue 10...b5 (Game 35).

d7-d6

With this move Black defends the e5 and c5 squares.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6

d6xe5

Normally played in reply to d4xe5, this leads to an open d-file, with Black maintaining his pawn at e5.

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.c4 d6 4.g3 Bg7 5.Bg2 O-O 6.O-O Nbd7 7.Qc2 e5 8.Rd1 Re8 9.Nc3 c6 10.e4 a6 11.h3 b5

(position after 12.d4xe5 )

Black recaptures 12...d6xe5 , maintaining his strongpoint in the centre. When White makes this pawn exchange there is always the long-term danger of him being left with a bad light-square bishop (Game A15).

d6-d5

The normal aim of this move is to attack the c4 and e4 pawns.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 g6 4.e4 Bg7 5.f3 e5 6.Nge2 Nbd7 7.Be3 a6 8.Qd2 O-O 9.Nc1 exd4 10.Bxd4 c6 11.Nb3 a5 12.Be3 a4 13.Nd4 a3

(position after 14.b2-b3 )

With 14...d6-d5 Black breaks open the centre. White must aim to castle as quickly as possible (Game A34).

e7-e6

This move attacks the d5 square and allows the possible development of the queen on the d8-h4 diagonal.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.b3 d6 3.d4 g6 4.Bb2 Bg7 5.e3 O-O 6.Be2 b6 7.O-O Bb7 8.Nbd2 Nbd7

(position after 9.c2-c4 )

The modest-looking 9...e7-e6 is a flexible move that gives the queen a comfortable post at e7. After further preparation the pawn later advanced to e5 (Game A33).

e7-e5

This standard King's Indian move attacks the d4 pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5

e5-e4

With this move Black gains space on the kingside, attacking the f3 and d3 squares.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.b3 d6 3.d4 g6 4.Bb2 Bg7 5.Nbd2 Nbd7 6.e3 O-O 7.c4 e5 8.Be2 Re8

(position after 9.O-O )

With 9...e5-e4 Black sets up a central pawn wedge, gaining space for his pieces on the kingside (Game A6).

e5xd4

Black removes the white d-pawn, opening lines for his bishop on the long diagonal and his rook on the e-file, as well as creating posts for his knights at e5 and c5.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 e5 6.Nge2 O-O 7.Be3 Nbd7 8.Qd2 a6

(position after 9.g2-g4 )

White has embarked on a wing attack before first securing his centre. With 9...e5xd4 10.Nxd4 c5 Black obtains dynamic play for his pieces (Game 47).

f7-f5

This move attacks the e4 pawn and threatens in some cases to advance to f4. It can be said, without exaggeration, that the pawn break ...f7-f5 is Black's main threat in the King's Indian Defence.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.g3 Bg7 4.Bg2 d6 5.d4 O-O 6.O-O Bg4 7.Nc3 c6 8.h3 Bxf3 9.Bxf3 e6 10.e4 Nbd7 11.Be3 Qe7 12.Qd2 e5 13.d5 c5 14.Kg2 Ne8 15.g4 a6

(position after 16.Rf1-h1 )

White is threatening to press on the kingside with Ne2-g3 followed by the advance of his h-pawn. Black makes a bid for freedom by 16...f7-f5 17.gxf5 gxf5 18.exf5 e4 , sacrificing a pawn to activate his bishop (Game 38).

f5xe4

Black eliminates the white pawn on e4.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.O-O Nc6 8.d5 Ne7 9.Bg5 h6 10.Bd2 Nd7 11.Qc1 Kh7 12.Ne1 f5

(position after 13.g2-g3 )

13...f5xe4 was an interesting decision, allowing White to occupy e4 with his knight. Black's plan was to eliminate the white d-pawn and finally advance ...d6-d5, gaining control of the centre (27).

f5-f4

This move gains space on the kingside and attacks the squares e3 and g3.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.d4 Nbd7 4.Nc3 e5 5.e4 g6 6.Be2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.d5 a5 9.Ne1 Nc5 10.Qc2 b6 11.Bg5 h6 12.Be3 Ng4 13.Bxg4 Bxg4 14.Nd3 Bd7 15.Rae1 f5 16.Bxc5 bxc5 17.f4 exf4 18.Rxf4 Be5 19.Nxe5 dxe5

(position after 20.Rf4-f2 )

Fine play by the opponent has left Black with chronically weakened pawns. His only chance of counter-play is 20...f5-f4 followed by ...g5-g4-g3 (24).

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.g3 g6

g6xf5

Normally played in reply to a capture by White on f5, this move attacks the e4 and g4 squares.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.O-O Nc6 8.d5 Ne7 9.Ne1 Nd7 10.Nd3 f5

(position after 11.e4xf5 )

Black recaptures 11...g6xf5 , maintaining control of the e4 square. He later regrouped his knights to g6 and f6 and used the open g-file for his rook (Game A41).

g6-g5

With this move Black attacks the h4 and f4 squares and plans the possible follow-up ...g5-g4, attacking a pawn at f3.

h7-h6

With this move Black either defends the g5 square or attacks a white bishop there.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.d5 Nbd7

(position after 8.Bc1-g5 )

With 8...h7-h6 Black drives the bishop back to h4, so that he always has the option of breaking the pin on his knight by a later ...g6-g5 (Game 34).

h7-h5

This move attacks the g4 square and in some cases threatens the further advance to h4 and h3.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.e3 Nbd7 5.Nf3 g6 6.Be2 Bg7 7.b3 O-O 8.Bb2 Re8 9.Qc2 c6 10.O-O Qa5 11.Rfd1 Nf8 12.a3 e4 13.Nd2 Bf5 14.b4 Qc7 15.Nf1 d5 16.cxd5 cxd5 17.Qb3 Red8 18.Rdc1 Qe7

(position after 19.a3-a4 )

With 19...h7-h5 Black began the advance of his pawn to h3, weakening the light squares in the vicinity of the white king. Later his knight from f8 came into play via h7 and g5 (Game 2).

h5-h4

The pawn attacks the g3 square, and threatens in some cases to advance further to h3, attacking the g2 pawn.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.b3 d6 3.d4 g6 4.Bb2 Bg7 5.Nbd2 Nbd7 6.e3 O-O 7.c4 e5 8.Be2 Re8 9.O-O e4 10.Ne1 Nf8 11.Nc2 Bf5 12.d5 h5 13.Nd4 Bd7

(position after 14.Qd1-c2 )

Black advances 14...h5-h4 followed by 15...h3 , weakening the white king's position (Game A6).
