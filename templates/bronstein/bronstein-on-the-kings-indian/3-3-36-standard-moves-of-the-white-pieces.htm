Nb1-c3

From here the knight defends the e4 pawn and attacks the d5 square.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3

Nb1-d2

From here the knight defends the e4 and c4 squares.

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.Bf4 Bg7 4.e3

(position after 4...O-O )

White plays 5.Nb1-d2 , as he plans to over-protect his d4 pawn by c2-c3 (Game 7).

Ng1-f3

This move defends the d4 pawn, attacks the e5 square, and clears the way for kingside castling.

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3

Ng1-e2

This move defends the d4 pawn and clears the way for kingside castling.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Bg5 c5 6.d5 Na6 7.Bd3

(position after 7...Na6-c7 )

Rather than develop his knight at f3, White plays 8.Ng1-e2 , leaving his f-pawn free to advance later to f4 (Game A18).

Ng1-h3

This clears the way for kingside castling, the knight normally aiming for the f2 square.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 O-O 6.Be3

(position after 6...Nb8-d7 )

White has to decide on how best to develop his kingside. Taking advantage of Black's last move, he plays 7.Ng1-h3 , planning to move the knight the good post f2, where it covers the e4 pawn and also g4 (Game A37).

Nf3-d2

This move defends e4 and c4 and may allow the f-pawn to advance.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.g3 Bg7 4.Bg2 O-O 5.Nc3 d6 6.Nf3 Nc6 7.O-O a6 8.d5

(position after 8...Nc6-a5 )

White defends his attacked c-pawn by 9.Nf3-d2 . He will develop his queenside by Qc2, b2-b3 and Bb2 (Game A26).

Ne2-g3

From here the knight defends the e4 pawn and attacks the squares f5 and h5.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.h3 O-O 6.Be3 e5 7.d5 Nbd7 8.g4 Nc5 9.Qc2 c6 10.Nge2 cxd5 11.cxd5

(position after 11...Qd8-a5 )

White defends his attacked e4 pawn by 12.Ne2-g3 , at the same time preparing g4-g5 without allowing the black knight to go to h5 (Game A19).

Nf3-e1

This move is normally part of a regrouping manoeuvre that takes the knight to d3 (or c2). It also frees the f-pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.O-O Nc6 8.d5

(position after 8...Nc6-e7 )

White plays 9.Nf3-e1 , switching his knight to the effective central square d3, and (in anticipation of Black's ...f7-f5) allowing his f-pawn to advance (Game A41).

Nc3-e4

From here the knight blocks the e5 pawn and attacks the d6 pawn and several of the dark squares in the opponent's position (the same applies to Nd2-e4 and to the capture by the knight on e4).

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 e5 6.d5 Nh5 7.Be3 O-O 8.Qd2 f5 9.exf5 gxf5 10.O-O-O a6 11.Bg5 Qe8 12.Nh3 Nd7 13.g4 fxg4 14.fxg4 Nf4 15.Ne4

Nd2-b3

From here the knight defends the squares d4 and c5.

1.c4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 O-O 5.d4 d6 6.h3 e5 7.d5 Na6 8.Bg5 h6 9.Be3 Nc5 10.Nd2 Nh7 11.b4 Na6 12.a3

(position after 12...f7-f5 )

With 13.Nd2-b3 White supports the advance of his pawn to c5, while also incidentally allowing his bishop to retreat in the event of ...f5-f4 (29).

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.c4 Bg7 4.Nc3 O-O 5.e4 d6 6.Be2

Bf1-g2

The bishop moves to a ready-made post on the long diagonal, preparing kingside castling.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2

Bf1-d3

This defends the e4 pawn and prepares kingside castling.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 O-O 6.Be3

(position after 6...b7-b6 )

White played 7.Bf1-d3 , when Black's planned 7...c5 encountered the unexpected 8.e5 , winning material (Game A36).

Bg2-f1

Normally played to switch the bishop to a more promising post if the long diagonal is blocked.

1.c4 Nf6 2.Nc3 e5 3.g3 c6 4.Nf3 d6 5.Bg2 g6 6.O-O Bg7 7.e4 O-O 8.d4 Qa5 9.d5 cxd5 10.cxd5 b5 11.Re1 Bd7 12.a3 b4 13.Na2 bxa3 14.Nc3 Qb6 15.Rxa3

(position after 15...Nb8-a6 )

At g2 White's bishop is blocked by his own central pawns. He plays 16.Bg2-f1 to bring it into action on the queenside, where the main battle is about to develop (Game 35).

Bc1-d2

A modest post for the bishop, but one from where it covers two diagonals.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 g6 4.g3 Bg7 5.Bg2 O-O 6.e4 e5 7.d5 c5 8.Nge2 Nbd7 9.O-O

(position after 9...Nf6-h5 )

Developing the bishop at e3 would invite an attack on it by ...f7-f5-f4. White therefore plays 10.Bc1-d2 (Game 37).

Bc1-e3

This defends the d4 pawn and in some cases prepares queenside castling.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.e4 c6 9.Be3

Bc1-f4

From here the bishop attacks the e5 and d6 squares.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.e4 Re8 9.h3 exd4 10.Nxd4 Nc5 11.Re1

(position after 11...c7-c6 )

White's strategy is aimed at removing the strong knight from c5. He plays 12.Bc1-f4 , threatening 13.Nb3 (Game 18).

Bc1-g5

This attacks (and often pins) the knight on f6 and in some cases prepares queenside castling.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Be2

(position after 5...O-O )

6.Bc1-g5 tries to inhibit Black's counterplay by the potential pin on his knight. Thus 6...e5? loses material after 7.dxe5 dxe5 8.Qxd8 Rxd8 9.Nd5 (Game A23).

Bc1-b2

The bishop defends the d4 pawn and puts pressure on the long diagonal.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.b3 d6 3.d4

(position after 3...g7-g6 )

With 4.Bc1-b2 White makes an additional attack on the e5 square, although this did not prevent Black from later advancing his e-pawn (Game A6).

Bc1-a3

From here the bishop attacks the squares on the a3-f8 diagonal.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.d4 d6 5.g3 O-O 6.Bg2 Nc6 7.O-O Rb8 8.h3 a6 9.a4 Nd7 10.a5 e5 11.d5 Ne7 12.e4 b6 13.axb6 cxb6 14.b4 a5 15.bxa5

(position after 15...b6xa5 )

A bishop is normally a slightly better piece than a knight, but sometimes one has to make the exchange. White plays 16.Bc1-a3 Nc5 17.Bxc5 dxc5 , when he obtains a strong pawn in the centre that is constantly threatening to advance (Game A28).

Be3xc5

This exchange (normally of a black knight) is aimed at increasing White's control of the central light squares.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 g6 4.d4 Bg7 5.e4 O-O 6.h3 e5 7.d5 Na6 8.Bg5 h6 9.Be3 Qe8 10.g4 Bd7 11.Bd3 c6 12.Nd2 Nc5 13.Bc2 a5 14.a3 cxd5 15.cxd5 Rc8 16.a4 Qd8 17.Qe2 Ne8 18.h4 f5 19.gxf5 gxf5 20.exf5 Bxf5 21.Bxf5

(position after 21...Rf8xf5 )

With 22.Be3xc5 Rxc5 23.Nde4 White takes control of the e4 square, leaving the black bishop boxed in (Game 49).

Rh1-g1

This move is normally made to occupy an open file.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nf3 Bg7 4.Nc3 d6 5.e4 O-O 6.h3 c6 7.Bd3 e5 8.d5 cxd5 9.cxd5 Nh5 10.g3 Nd7 11.Be3 a6 12.Be2 b5 13.Nd2 Nhf6 14.a3 Nb6 15.g4 Nfd7 16.h4 f5 17.gxf5 gxf5 18.exf5 Nf6 19.h5

(position after 19...h7-h6 )

With 20.Rh1-g1 White occupies the open file directly opposite the black king (Game 46).

Rf1-e1

From here the rook defends the e4 pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.g3 Bg7 4.Bg2 O-O 5.Nf3 d6 6.O-O Nbd7 7.Nc3 e5 8.e4 c6 9.h3 a6 10.Qc2 b6 11.Be3 Qe7 12.Rad1

(position after 12...Bc8-b7 )

White completes his development with 13.Rf1-e1 while maintaining the tension, leaving Black in an uncomfortable position (Game A49).

Rf1-d1

This defends the d4 pawn and exerts pressure on the d-file.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nf3 Bg7 4.g3 O-O 5.Bg2 d6 6.O-O Nbd7 7.Qc2 e5 8.Rd1

Ra1-b1

From here the rook supports the advance of the pawn to b4.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Be2 Nbd7 6.Nf3 e5 7.O-O

(position after 7...O-O )

With 8.Ra1-b1 White moves his rook off the diagonal of the black bishop, planning to expand later on the queenside by b2-b4 (Game A44).

Ra1-c1

From here the rook occupies an open file or supports the advance of the pawn to c5.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.Nf3 O-O 6.Be2 e5 7.O-O Nc6 8.d5 Ne7 9.Ne1 Nd7 10.Nd3 f5 11.exf5 gxf5 12.f4 Ng6 13.Be3

(position after 13...Nd7-f6 )

White plays 14.Ra1-c1 , with the intention of later advancing his pawn to c5 and opening the c-file (Game A41).

Ra1-d1

With this move White defends his d4 pawn and exerts pressure on the d-file.

1.c4 Nf6 2.Nc3 g6 3.e4 d6 4.d4 Bg7 5.f3 e5 6.Nge2 c6 7.Bg5 Nbd7 8.d5 O-O 9.Qd2 Qb6 10.Be3 Qc7 11.g4 h5 12.g5 Ne8 13.h4 a5 14.b3

(position after 14...Nd7-c5 )

With 15.Ra1-d1 (threatening 16.Bxc5 dxc5 17.d6 ) 15...Na6 16.Na4 White forces the opponent to block the queenside by 16...c5 , after which his hands are freed for manoeuvres on the kingside (Game 39).

Ra1-e1

From here the rook defends the e4 pawn and prepares for action in the centre.

1.Nf3 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.d4 Nbd7 4.Nc3 e5 5.e4 g6 6.Be2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.d5 a5 9.Ne1 Nc5 10.Qc2 b6 11.Bg5 h6 12.Be3 Ng4 13.Bxg4 Bxg4 14.Nd3

(position after 14...Bg4-d7 )

In anticipation of the opening of lines on the kingside after his planned f2-f4, White brings his last piece into play - 15.Ra1-e1 (24).

Qd1-c2

The queen moves off the back rank, defending the e4 pawn.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nfd7 5.e4 c6 6.Be2 Be7 7.O-O

(position after 7...O-O )

With 8.Qd1-c2 White adds support to his e4 pawn and allows his queen's rook to come into play at d1 (Game A25).

Qd1-d2

The queen supports the dark-square bishop, allows the rooks to become connected, and prepares for possible queenside castling.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f3 e5 6.d5 Nh5 7.Be3 O-O 8.Qd2

Qd1-e2

The queen moves off the back rank, allowing the rooks to become connected, and defends the c4 and e4 pawns.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.f4 c5 6.dxc5 Qa5 7.Bd3 Qxc5 8.Nf3

(position after 8...O-O )

White needs to take his king into safety. He plays 9.Qd1-e2 , planning Be3, after which he can castle kingside (Game A20).

0-0-0

The king moves to a safer position and brings the queen's rook into play on a central file.

1.d4 Nf6 2.Nf3 g6 3.c3 Bg7 4.Bf4 O-O 5.Nbd2 Nh5 6.Be5 f6 7.g4 fxe5 8.gxh5 exd4 9.cxd4 gxh5 10.e3 d5 11.Bd3 Bf5 12.Bxf5 Rxf5 13.Nh4 Rf6 14.Qxh5 e6 15.Rg1 c5 16.Ndf3

(position after 16...Nb8-d7 )

To bring his queen's rook into the attack, White plays 17.O-O-O . The possible opening of the c-file does not concern him, as his king will be safe enough at b1 (Game A42).

Ke1-f1

The king moves away from possible threats on the a5-e1 diagonal or the e-file.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 g6 3.Nc3 Bg7 4.e4 d6 5.h3 O-O 6.Be3 e5 7.d5 Nbd7 8.g4 Nc5 9.Qc2 c6 10.Nge2 cxd5 11.cxd5 Qa5 12.Ng3 Bd7 13.Bd2 Rfc8 14.Rb1 Qd8 15.Qd1 a5 16.Qf3 Rab8 17.g5 Ne8 18.h4 f6 19.Be2

(position after 19...Ne8-c7 )

White wants to connect his rooks, but without moving his rook off the h-file. He therefore plays 20.Ke1-f1 , and later moves his king to g2 (Game A19).

Kg1-h1

The king moves off the g1-a7 diagonal.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 Nbd7 4.e4 e5 5.Nge2 c6 6.g3 g6 7.Bg2 Bg7 8.O-O O-O 9.Rb1 exd4 10.Nxd4

(position after 10...Rf8-e8 )

White plans to advance his f-pawn. To avoid a possible pin on the dark-square diagonal, he first plays 11.Kg1-h1 (Game 21).

Kg1-h2

The king moves off the gl-a7 diagonal, normally lending added protection to a pawn on h3.

1.d4 Nf6 2.c4 d6 3.Nc3 e5 4.Nf3 Nbd7 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.e4 c6 9.h3 exd4 10.Nxd4 Nb6 11.b3 d5 12.cxd5 cxd5 13.exd5 Nbxd5 14.Nxd5 Nxd5 15.Bb2 Qa5 16.Qf3 Rd8 17.Rfd1 h5 18.a3 Qb6 19.Rd2 h4 20.Rad1

(position after 20...Nd5-f6 )

White consolidates his king's position by 21.Kg1-h2 (Game A3).
