Game 5
Ruger - Gebhard
Dresden 1915
Giuoco Piano

1.e4 ...

The most important target in the opening is to develop the pieces as last as possible in order to occupy and control the centre.

White's first move fixes a pawn in the centre and, as the first step in get-ting his pieces off the back rank, opens avenues for his queen and bishop.

1. ... e5

Black equalizes the pressure in the centre and releases two pieces for action. He must be content with this, as he cannot hope at this early stage to wrest the initiative away from White by force.

2.Nf3 ...

It is good strategy to make developing moves that embody threats, as it cuts down the choice of reply. Against the passive 2.Nc3 , which develops a piece but attacks nothing. Black can choose from among such good responses as 2...Nf6 , 2...Nc6 and 2...Bc5 . Likewise, if White's second move is the quiet 2.Bc4 , Black has three good options in 2...Nf6 , 2...Bc5 and 2...c6 (with a view to an early ...d5).

2. ... Nc6

Black saves his e-pawn and develops a piece. The knight comes into play efficiently: its action is directed toward influencing the centre. It de-tends the e5-pawn and attacks d4.

3.Bc4 (D) ...

The bishop comes out, taking up a post where it commands a diagonal leading towards Black's king. It attacks a pawn which is peculiarly vulnerable as it is defended only by the king.

This does not mean that White will capture the pawn in the next few moves, but the threat is always there. Many a short brilliancy owes its life to similar captures in which the bishop is sacrificed simply to draw the king out into the open where White's other pieces can get at him.

3. ... Bc5 (D)

Black follows suit, bringing his bishop out to the most suitable square.

In the opening, a bishop is best placed for attack when it controls a diagonal passing through the centre or when it pins a hostile knight and renders it immobile. Defensively, the bishop would do a fine job at c7, where it radiates power in several directions, making an intrusion by an enemy piece difficult.

4.c3 ...

Supporting an advance of the d-pawn with a view to controlling the centre. White postpones castling as his king is in no danger.

4. ... Nf6

A powerful counter to the threat: Black develops a piece and attacks a pawn.

5.d4 ...

White meets this by attacking a piece.

5. ... exd4

Practically forced, since 5...Bd6 guarding the e-pawn is clumsy, since it blocks the d-pawn, while 5...Bb6 permits 6.dxe5 Nxe4 7.Bxf7+ Kxf7 8.Qd5+ and White regains his piece, remaining a pawn ahead. White could also play 7.Qd5 , attacking the knight while threatening mate, instead of 7.Bxf7+ .

6.cxd4 ...

White's array of pawns looks impressive, but can the centre be maintained?

6. ... Bb4+

Far superior to the timid 6...Bb6 after which Black is crushed somewhat like this: 7.d5 Nb8 8.e5 Ng8 (both knights return home ingloriously) 9.O-O Ne7 10.d6 Ng6 11.Ng5 O-O (Black's king is protected by three pawns and the knight, in four moves the knight and one pawn disappear, while the remaining two pawns are pinned and helpless) 12.Qh5 (threatening 13.Qxh7# ) 12...h6 13.Qxg6 (again threatening mate - the white queen is of course taboo) 13...hxg5 14.Bxg5 Qe8 15.Bf6 (D) and White, exploiting two pins, forces mate within two moves. This is the final position of this line:

After the actual move. White must get his king out of check, and if possible save the threatened pawn.

7.Nc3 ...

White prefers this, which offers a pawn, to 7.Bd2 Bxd2+ 8.Nbxd2 , when the e-pawn is protected.

7. ... O-O

“Chess is not for timid souls,” wrote Steinitz in a letter to Bachmann. Castling early in the game is generally sound strategy. In this case it is inappropriate, as White's centre is formidable and must be destroyed. Black must play 7...Nxe4, accepting the offer of a pawn, and take his chances of surviving whatever attack might follow. It cannot be worse than this passive castling, which results in both his knights being evicted from their fine posts. Pillsbury gave as his rule, which he said was absolute and vital: “Castle because you want to or because you must, but not because you can.”

8.d5 (D) ...

Ordinarily, such moves are sus-pect, as d5 should be occupied by a piece, but here the pawn dislodges the knight and prevents Black forever from freeing his game by ...d5

8. ... Ne7

As good as there is. The shift to a5 is countered by 9.Bd3 , and the knight is stranded at the side of the board.

9.e5 ...

Now a shot at the other knight!

9. ... Ne4

Retreating to e8 does not look at-tractive, so Black plays to exchange knights.

10.Qc2 ...

Protects his own knight, which was twice attacked, and threatens the enemy's.

10. ... Nxc3

Practically forced, as defending the knight by 10...f5 allows 11.d6+ and the other knight falls.

11.bxc3 ...

This recapture gains a tempo: Black's bishop must lose a move retreating, while White's advantages multiply. Now he enjoys an open b-file for his rook and an extra diagonal for his dark-squared bishop.

11. ... Bc5 (D)

Naturally, this offers better chances than moving to a5, the only other flight square.

A comparison of the two sides' positions shows that White's is decidedly superior. Me has more pieces in play, and they have far more mo-bility than Black's. He also has better prospects of bringing more pieces into the battle.

The recommended recipe now is to occupy Black with threats - keep him on the run and give him no time to put up effective resistance.

12.Ng5! ...

Ostensibly to scare Black by the threat of mate; actually, the purpose is to induce a weakening advance of one of the king's retinue of pawns.

It one of these pawns does move. Black is done for! For example, 12...f5 13.d6+ wins a piece, or if 12...g6 , then 13.Ne4 forces 13...Bb6 , when 14.Bh6 drives the rook to c8, and 15.Nf6+ wins the exchange.

12. ... Ng6 (D)

The only defence! The kingside pawns remain intact, but Black has been compelled to place his pieces where White wants them. Under such circumstances the chances of Black putting up a good light are not loo bright.

White must avoid Black's error of castling as a matter of routine development. Such a quiet course would give Black time to play 13...d6 or 13...h6 , beating the knight back. White must not give his opponent a moment to catch his breath. He must attack, attack, attack!

13.h4 ...

Threatening to move on to h5, dislodging the knight, and then mate with the queen

13. ... h6

What else is there? Black tries to drive off the menacing knight. He cannot do this by 13...f6 as 14.d6+ Kh8 15.Nf7 wins the exchange.

14.d6! ...

A powerful stroke! Black's bishop is cut off from the defence, while a line is opened for White's bishop. White now threatens 15.Qxg6 , as the pinned f-pawn could not capture the queen.

14. ... hxg5

Black might as well take whatever material he can.

15.hxg5! ...

The knight will not run away (the penalty being 16.Qh7#). Meanwhile, the rook has a lovely open file, and the queen still threatens to capture the knight.

15. ... Re8

The king needs more room!

16.Qxg6 ...

Regaining the piece without relaxing the pressure. White threatens one mate in one, and two mates in two.

16. ... Rxe5+

Black consoles himself by equalizing the material and gives one despairing check before he yields.

17.Kf1 ...

Simplest, as the mate threats are still on tap. There was little chance that White would blunder by 17.Kd2 Qxg5+ 18.Qxg5 Rxg5 , when all the good work has gone for naught.

1-0

Black can avoid mate on the move by 17...Qe8 (incidentally threatening mate himself!), only to fall into 18.Qh7+ Kf8 19.Qh8# .
