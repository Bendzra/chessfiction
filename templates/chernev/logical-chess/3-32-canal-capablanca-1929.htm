Game 32
Canal - Capablanca
Budapest 1929
Queen's Indian Defence

1.d4 ...

A favourite of modern players, this is one of the best possible ways to begin the struggle for mastery of the centre - by occupying it with a pawn and permitting two pieces to come quickly into play.

1. ... Nf6

Unlike 1...d5 this does not meet the opponent head on, but it dues prevent him from continuing with 2.c4 and has the added merit of developing a piece at once to its proper square in the opening

2.c4 ...

There are many good points about this move:

1) It restrains Black from an immediate 2...d5 , as the reply 3.cxd5 forces a recapture with a piece and destroys his short-lived pawn-centre.

2) It opens the c-file for the use of White's major pieces.

3) It makes a diagonal available to the queen.

4) It enables the two pawns standing side by side to get a grip on four squares on the tilth rank.

2. ... e6 (D)

Black's f8-bishop is to be developed aggressively, to influence affairs in the centre. If White replies 3.Nc3 , for example, in order to follow up with 4.e4 . Black pins the knight and makes 4.e4 impossible.

3.Nf3 ...

White cannot enforce the advance of the c-pawn, so he makes a normal developing move and awaits events.

3. ... b6

Further restraint on the project! Black intends to place his bishop at b7, where it attacks from a distance. The bishop will add its strength to that of the knight in the control of the critical e4-square.

4.g3 (D) ...

White's best course is to oppose bishops on the long diagonal as one does with rooks on an open file In setting up an equal opposing force, he takes the sting out of the enemy bishop's attack.

4. ... Bb7

Strictly a modern concept! In olden times they filled up the centre with pawns and then fianchettoed the bishop, with the result that the bishop was deprived of any usefulness. Nowadays the bishop is free to strike all along the diagonal, unhampered by fixed pawns cluttering up his path.

5.Bg2 ...

With the completion of the fianchetto. White's kingside is cleared, and he is ready to castle.

5. ... Bb4+

Before attempting to undermine the centre by ...c5. Black develops a piece. Not only does the bishop's move expedite castling, but it also brings about a favourable exchange of bishops.

6.Bd2 ...

This is preferable to 6.Nbd2 , a passive interposition which gives Black time to castle and then attack the centre by 7...d5.

6. ... Bxd2+

Black proceeds with the exchange as the best means of freeing his somewhat cramped position. Instead, withdrawing the bishop to c7 throws him on the defensive and lets White retain the initiative.

7.Nbxd2 (D) ...

Theorists and critics of master

chess recommend that White capture with the queen instead. Despite their shrieks that the knight belongs at c3 in order to help attack the d5-square, the players themselves persist in recapturing with the knight They advance as argument that in doing so another minor piece is developed, but the real reason might be sheer stubbornness - rebellion against authority. In any case, if it is less effective than 7.Qxd2 , it is not to be so roundly condemned.

7. ... O-O

Before taking any decisive action in the centre. Black sees to the welfare of his king and removes him to a safer part of the board.

A strong alternative is 7...c5 challenging White's pawn-centre. To this. White cannot reply 8.d5 as it loses a pawn.

8.O-O (D) ...

White makes his king's position more secure and presses the h1-rook into service.

8. ... c5!

A strong move, whose object is to undermine White's centre.

9.dxc5 ...

White has little choice. Moving the pawn to d5 loses a pawn, supporting it with 9.e3 (in order to recapture with a pawn and maintain a pawn in the centre), ties down the c-pawn to the job of defence. The chances then of the e-pawn's ever reaching e4 are rather dubious.

9. ... bxc5

Black has gained three advantages by a simple exchange of pawns:

1) He has exchanged a centre pawn for a less central pawn.

2) His c-pawn controls d4, making it impossible for White to settle a piece on that square.

3) His queen and a1-rook will he able to operate with good effect on the newly opened b-file

10.Qc2 ...

In most eases the function of the queen in the opening is not so much to be moving around as it is to get off the hack rank and out of the way, so that the rooks can get together and work on the centre files.

With the departure of the queen, the f1-rook can occupy d1, exert pressure on the d-file, and make life uncomfortable for Black's forces on that tile.

10. ... Nc6 (D)

The knight develops toward the centre and intensifies the pawn's grip on d4.

11.Rfd1 ...

The tempting 11.e4 meets its refutation in 11...e5 (strengthening the pressure on d4) followed by 12...Qe7 and 13...Nd4 . White would then face a problem: the pressure of the knight at d4 is almost intolerable, but removing it lets Black recapture by 14...cxd4, leaving him with a passed pawn on the d-file.

11. ... Qb6!

Now there are three pieces bearing down on d4, while the queen herself gets a purchase on the open b-file.

12.a3 (D) ...

White has no wild dream of starting a counterattack with 13.b4; mainly he wants to rule out ...Nb4 at some later stage.

12. ... Rab8

Fine positional play! One is apt to forget that a rook's first duty is to seize upon any file that is open, not only since the rook's attack extends the whole length of the file but because the rook has a convenient avenue for its progress to other points.

13.Rab1 ...

White's rook also goes to b1, but what a world of difference there is in the two moves! White's rook has no open file; it has little prospect of increasing its mobility, and its function is purely defensive. In protecting the b-pawn, though, it relieves the queen of a responsibility.

13. ... Rfc8

The rook was doing nothing at f8, so it is transferred to a file where it is likely to become useful.

There was no hurry about playing 13...a5 since White is in no position to try for the break by 14.b4 .

14.e4 ...

White's move has a superficial appearance of strength. In reality, the pawn occupies a square that should be kept clear. White's pieces lose a great deal of mobility in not being able to make use of c4 as a jumping-off point to their various destinations.

14. ... e5

Stilling 15.e5 , White's e-pawn's lust to expand.

Notice how Black has reinforced his hold on d4. This point now has the power of the queen, a knight and a couple of pawns trained on it.

15.Qd3 ...

White hopes for some counterplay along the d-file, either by 16.Qd6 or in manoeuvring his knight by way of f1 and c3 to d5. In the meantime, he prevents Black from playing 15...Nd4 , the response to which is 16.Nxe5 .

15. ... d6

A witty, almost impudent, reply. Black guards the c-pawn (so that he can play 16...Nd4 ) with a pawn which itself is unprotected!

White, of course, must not take the d-pawn, the penalty for 16.Qxd6 being 16...Rd8 , winning the queen

16.Nf1 ...

Indicating that he wants to deploy the knight to c3 and then d5. Excellent strategy, if White can carry it out.

16. ... Nd4! (D)

However, Black gets there first!

Not only does he establish an outpost at d4, but he also hinders White from continuing his manoeuvre. If White tries 17.Ne3 , then 17...Bxe4 18.Nd5 Bxd3 19.Nxb6 Nxf3+ 20.Bxf3 Rxb6 21.Rxd3 e4 wins a piece neatly.

17.Nxd4 ...

The advanced knight cramps his style, so White gets rid of it

17. ... exd4

Black is happy to exchange: he has a passed pawn on the d-file, which in the course of time might become a queen. More than that, the pawn guards the exits. It hinders White's knight from coming out at c3 and puts an end to its ambition to reach d5

The passed pawn at d4 is apparently the logical candidate for promotion to queen, but there are surprises to come - many of them!

18.b4 ...

Suddenly White bursts forth! His chief threat is 19.bxc5 Qxc5 (Black-must recapture with the queen, as she is doubly attacked) 20.Rxb7 Rxb7 21.e5 , and the discovered attack will net White two pieces for a rook.

How does Black meet this threat? Obviously not with 18...cxb4 , as 19.Rxb4 gives White the better of it. Also not by 18...Qc7 , when after 19.bxc5 dxc5 (to protect the passed pawn) 20.f4 threatening 21.e5 follows, and White's pieces spring to life.

Capablanca's actual reply initiates a remarkable combination. He lets White obtain an advantage in material in return for a position which looks tar from promising - except to a Capablanca!

18. ... Qc6! (D)

Triple attack on the c-pawn! This forces White either to defend the pawn tamely, or else to go ahead boldly with the combination he planned.

19.bxc5 ...

White plays to win! He takes a pawn off, opens a file for his rook, and threatens to remove the passed d-pawn.

19. ... dxc5

Black must recapture with the pawn to preserve the precious passed pawn.

20.Rxb7 ...

This tempting combination wins two pieces for a rook.

Has Capablanca been caught off-guard, or does he see much further into the resources of the position than his opponent?

20. ... Qxb7

Black captures with the queen to maintain control of the valuable open file.

21.e5 (D) ...

Discovered attack on the queen, and direct attack on the knight.

21. ... Qb3!

A remarkable offer to exchange queens! Usually, the side that is ahead in material tries to clear the board and simplify to an ending.

22.exf6 ...

White rejects 22.Qxb3 as it leads to 22...Rxb3 23.exf6 Rxa3 , when Black is left with two passed pawns.

22. ... Qxd3

The idea of this is to force the white rook off the last rank. The rank then becomes available to Black's rook as a point of entry, enabling it to get behind White's pawns.

23.Rxd3 ...

White has no choice.

23. ... Rb1!

The knight is pinned as a start, and White has to scramble frantically to break the pin.

Against passive play, Black continues with 24...Re8 followed by 25...Ree1 and 26...Rec1 . This wins the c-pawn since White cannot protect it by 27.Bd5 without abandoning the knight. After capturing the pawn. Black would have two dangerous connected pawns rushing to become queens.

24.Bd5 ...

White's plan is clear, he protects the precious c-pawn and vacates a square for his king. Following 25.Kg2 , the knight, no longer pinned, can come into the game.

24. ... Rcb8

The doubled rooks give Black undisputed possession of the all-important b-file.

White is now faced with two threats:

1) 25...Rc1 intending 26...Rbb1 . The attack on the knight would compel the bishop to return to g2, whereupon Black picks up the c-pawn.

2) 25...R8b3 , forcing an exchange of rooks, after which one of White's queenside pawns must fall.

25.Kg2 ...

White unpins the knight, which has been a spectator tor the last ten moves.

25. ... R8b3!

A daring concept! Black offers to exchange rooks and let White remain with two pieces to his one!

26.Rxb3 ...

White is happy to oblige!

26. ... Rxb3 (D)

Now the a-pawn is in peril.

27.Nd2 ...

White could not save the a-pawn (if 27.a4 , then 27...Rb4 28.a5 Ra4 ), so the knight counterattacks by going after a pawn.

27. ... Rxa3

Winning a pawn and simultaneously creating a passed pawn on the a-file. Will this be the one to become a queen?

28.Ne4 ...

Now White's pieces spring into action! The threat is 29.Nxc5 , knocking the support out from under the d-pawn and giving himself a passed c-pawn.

28. ... a5!

Much better than 28...Ra5 which tics the rook down to defending a pawn and makes his role in the ending a subordinate one.

29.Nxc5 ...

After this capture. White's own passed pawn begins to look menacing.

29. ... gxf6

Not so much to gain a pawn as to give the king more freedom. If at once 29...Kf8 , then 30.Nd7+ drives the king hack to g8, as 30...Ke8 allows 31.fxg7 winning for White.

29...d3 allows 30.Kf3 followed by 31.Ke3 , when White wins the d-pawn.

30.Kf1 ...

The king turns back to head off the passed pawns.

30. ... a4

Black does not fear a double attack on this pawn by 31.Bc6 . He would refute this by 31...Ra1+ followed by 32...a3 , and the pawn has moved another step forward.

31.Ke2 (D) ...

The king comes closer, definitely putting an end to any danger from the d-pawn.

How does Black proceed?

31. ... Ra1!

By scaring White with the threat of queening the a-pawn! Black intends 32...a3 , followed by 33...a2 , 34...Re1+ (to make way for the pawn without loss of time) and 35...a1Q .

32.Nd3 ...

White blockades the d-pawn and prepares to advance his own passed pawn.

32. ... a3

Back at the seventeenth move it seemed that the d-pawn would reach the eighth rank and become a queen The d-pawn is blockaded, and it now appears that the a-pawn is the one that will be promoted.

But will it be the a-pawn?

33.c5 ...

White's pawn can cause trouble, too!

33. ... a2

Threatening to win on the spot by 34...Re1+ 35.Nxe1 a1Q .

34.Kf3 ...

The king steps aside to evade the check.

34. ... Rd1

This move, which attacks the knight as well as threatening to queen the pawn, adds to White's difficulties.

35.Bxa2 ...

White must destroy this dangerous creature at all costs!

35. ... Rxd3+

With this capture Black nets a piece for his a-pawn.

36.Ke4 ...

White does not fall into the trap 36.Ke2 losing his passed pawn after 36...Rc3 .

36. ... Rd2

The text-move attacks the bishop and the f-pawn. Moreover, it revives the d-pawn's prospects.

Is this pawn to be the candidate after all?

37.Bc4! ...

An admirable spot for the bishop.

Black can now easily go wrong by 37...Rxf2 38.c6 Rb2 (if 38...Rc2 , then 39.Kd5 wins for White) 39.c7 , and White's pawn crashes through!

37. ... Kf8! (D)

The king takes over the job of restraining the dangerous pawn.

38.f3 ...

Advancing the c-pawn is useless 38.c6 Ke7 and the pawn can go no further. With the text move. White offers his h-pawn in return for Black's d-pawn.

38. ... Rxh2

Black falls in with the suggestion

Instead of grimly holding on to the passed pawn, he is willing to simplify, confident that his superiority is enough to secure the win. This requires a belief in justice on the chessboard, and confidence in one's ability to mete it out properly.

39.Kxd4 ...

With this capture. White's prospects look brighter. His king and bishop are in close attendance on the passed pawn and will bend all their efforts to escort it to the far side of the board.

39. ... Ke7

Ready to blockade the pawn.

40.Bd3 ...

Apparently with the idea of switching the bishop to c4 From there it guards his f-pawn, prevents Black's doubled pawns from advancing, and is prepared to protect his c-pawn when it reaches c6.

40. ... h5 (D)

Threatening 41...Rg2 42.g4 h4 , when Black wins

Is the h-pawn to be the passed pawn that becomes a queen?

41.Ke3 ...

The king comes closer to rescue the g-pawn.

41. ... Rg2

In forcing the king to defend the g-pawn. Black draws him farther away from his own passed pawn.

42.Kf4 ...

The only move. Against 42.g4 the win is easy: 42...h4 43.Bf1 Rc2 44.Kd4 Rf2 45.Bh3 Rxf3 46.Bg2 Rg3 47.Bf1 h3 , etc.

42. ... Rg1

Now to get behind the passed pawn! In the ending, the rook does its best work behind the enemy's pawns. Its power to strike extends all along the line, so that no matter how far the pawn advances on the file, it can never escape from the rook's attack.

43.Be4 ...

Preparing to protect the pawn when it reaches c6. If at once 43.c6 , then 43...Rc1 44.Be4 (but not 44.Bb5 Rc5 45.Ba4 Rc4+ winning the bishop), and we have the position reached a little later in the game by a transposition of moves.

43. ... Rc1

To the uninitiated, this appears strange! Why help the pawn move farther up the board?

Black's idea is to compel the pawn to move to a light square. This will be the bishop down to its defence and considerably limit the bishop's sphere of activity.

44.c6 (D) ...

The pawn moves up to come under the bishop's protection.

44. ... Rc3!

A tremendously effective move! It reduces White to a state of zug-zwang - compulsion to move. What this means is that White might hold the game, except that he must make a move, and any move he makes is fatal because it disturbs the position unfavourably!

45.c7 ...

Voluntarily giving up the pawn he cannot save! The alternatives are extremely interesting:

1) 45.Kf5 Rc5+ (to drive the king back) 46.Kf4 Ke6 47.Ke3 (a bishop move loses the pawn at once, while 47.g4 allows 47...h4 giving Black a passed pawn) 47...f5 48.Bd3 Rxc6 and Black wins.

2) 45.Bd5 Rc5 46.Ke4 ( 46.Be4 Ke6 leads to play as in line '1') 46...f5+ 47.Kd4 Rxd5+! 48.Kxd5 f4! 49.Kc5 (or 49.gxf4 h4 50.Kc5 Kd8 51.Kb6 Kc8 wins) 49...fxg3 50.Kb6 g2 51.c7 g1Q+ 52.Kb7 Qb1+ 53.Kc8 Qb6 54.f4 Qa7 55.f5 Qa8# .

45. ... Rxc7

Removing a potential danger. The ending still requires winning, and the manner of achieving the win is an illustration of smooth, flawless technique. The demonstration is as lucid and accurate as though it were the solution of a composed endgame study.

46.Bd5 ...

To keep Black from moving his king to the line square e6.

46. ... Rc5

The rook intends to pursue the bishop until it leaves the diagonal overlooking the c6-square.

47.Ba2 ...

Understandably, the bishop tries to slay on the diagonal as long as possible. If instead 47.Bb3 , then 47...Rb5 forces 48.Ba2 ( 48.Bc4 Rb4 pins the bishop, while 48.Ba4 Rb4+ also wins the bishop) 48...Rb2 49.Bd5 Rb4+! 50.Kf5 ( 50.Be4 Ke6 51.Ke3 f5 wins, or if 50.Ke3 then 50...f5 followed by 51...Kf6 and Black wins) 50...Rb5 51.Ke4 f5+ 52.Kd4 Rxd5+! 53.Kxd5 f4 54.gxf4 h4 , when the pawn cannot he stopped!

47. ... Rb5! (D)

Complete domination! The bishop has no moves!

48.Ke3 ...

Or 48.Ke4 f5+ 49.Kf4 Kf6 50.Ke3 (the bishop cannot move, and 50.g4 loses to 50...hxg4 51.fxg4 Rb4+ 52.Kf3 fxg4+ ) 50...Rb4 51.Bd5 f4+ 52.gxf4 h4 53.Kf2 Rb2+ 54.Kg1 Kf5 55.Bxf7 Kxf4 56.Bd5 Kg3 57.Be4 Ra2 58.Kf1 h3 and Black wins

48. ... Ra5

Allowing the bishop one square on the a2-g8 diagonal.

49.Bc4 ...

The bishop could not go to b3 on account of the ensuing pin, and 49.Bb1 is answered by 49...Ke6 50.Kf4 Ra4+ 51.Ke3 ( 51.Be4 f5 wins the bishop) 51...f5 52.Bc2 f4+ 53.gxf4 (or 53.Kd2 fxg3 ) 53...Ra3+ 54.Kd2 Ra2 55.Kc1 Rxc2+ and Black wins.

49. ... Rc5

Dislodging the bishop from the diagonal leading to c6.

50.Ba6 ...

50.Kd4 Rg5 wins a pawn at once, while 50.Ba2 is refuted by 50...f5 , clearing f6 for Black's king.

50. ... Ke6

Another step nearer the centre and White's remaining pawns.

51.Kf4 (D) ...

White puts up a hard light! He is ready to pounce on 51...f5 with 52.Kg5 .

51. ... Rc3!

Again restricting White to a move by the bishop. Playing 52.Ke4 succumbs to 52...f5+ 53.Kf4 Kf6 , etc.

52.Bf1 ...

White must keep watch over c4, otherwise (if he plays 52.Bb7 , for example) 52...Rc4+ forces his king to retreat while Black's can advance

52. ... f5

Finally! This pawn moves up a square and vacates f6 for the king.

53.Ba6 ...

The number of moves White has left is dwindling! 53.g4 fxg4 54.fxg4 h4 55.g5 h3 56.Kg4 h2 57.Bg2 Rc1 wins for Black, while after 53.Kg5 Rxf3 54.Bc4+ Ke5 55.Kh4 f4 Black again wins easily.

53. ... Kf6

Stalemating White's king. What remains now is to force him hack to the third rank while Black's king moves up to the fourth.

54.Bb7 ...

If the bishop stays on the longer diagonal, for instance by 54.Be2 , Black plays 54...Rb3 and then drives the king back by 55...Rb4+ .

54. ... Rc4+

Compelling the king to retreat.

55.Ke3 ...

The only move.

55. ... Kg5

With the pretty threat 56...f4+ 57.Kd3 ( 57.gxf4+ Rxf4 gives Black a passed pawn, while 57.Kf2 Rc2+ 58.Kg1 fxg3 gives him two) 57...fxg3 58.Kxc4 Kf4! , and Black wins neatly.

56.Kf2 (D) ...

A thrust at the rook by 56.Bd5 allows this finish: 56...f4+ 57.Ke2 Rc2+ 58.Kd3 Rc7 (simple but brutal ), and Black wins.

With the text-move. White retreats in order to head off the potential passed pawn, whichever one that may be!

56. ... f4!

This is the key move in all cases! Black threatens to win at once by 57...Rc2+ 58.Kg1 fxg3 .

57.Kg2 ...

Ready to reply to the check with 58.Kh3 saving the pawn.

57. ... f5! (D)

Passed pawns must be pushed This pawn, which stood patiently at f7 for 56 moves, is, believe it or not, the one that was destined to become the passed pawn that wins the game! 0-1

White does not wait for the proof: After 58.Kh3 fxg3 59.Kxg3 h4+ (a likely-looking candidate, but not the final choice) 60.Kh3 Rc3 61.Bd5 Kf4 62.Kxh4 Rxf3 63.Bxf3 Kxf3 64.Kh3 f4 65.Kh2 Ke2 the pawn marches straight through to be queened.

The whole game is beautiful, and the ending so fascinating that I could not resist a detailed analysis of its many fine points. A study of it is far more rewarding in entertainment and instruction than the playing over of a host of 'brilliancies' that feature a flinging about of pieces in wanton display.
