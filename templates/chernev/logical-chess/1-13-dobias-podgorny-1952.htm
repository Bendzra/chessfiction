Game 13
Dobias - Podgorny
Prague 1952
French Defence

1.e4 ...

This is one of the best ways to be-gin what is a race and a struggle: a race to get the pieces out rapidly and onto squares where they can operate most efficiently; a struggle to gain control of the centre.

At one stroke the e-pawn takes up an important square in the centre and attacks two others, while the queen and bishop control eight more squares.

1. ... e6

This quietly aggressive move pre-pares to dispute White's centre by 2...d5 .

This defence has the merit of avoiding the many strong openings White can play after the customary 1...e5 reply.

2.d4 ...

Of a similar move, Philidor in his Chess Analysed (1791) says, “This Pawn is played two Moves for two very important Reasons: the first is, to hinder your Adversary's King's Bishop to play upon your King's Bishop's Pawn; and the second, to put the Strength of your Pawns in the Middle of the Exchequer, which is of great Consequence to attain the making of a Queen.”

We may still follow the advice if not the spelling.

2. ... d5

An attack on the e-pawn which immediately challenges the centre.

3.Nc3 ...

Of the various courses open to White (advancing the e-pawn to e5, exchanging pawns, sacrificing his centre pawn or protecting it) he takes that which enables him to develop a piece and maintain the pressure.

3. ... dxe4

Temporarily allowing White more freedom of action, but Black hopes to play ...c5 later and destroy the troublesome d-pawn.

4.Nxe4 ...

The recapture leaves White with a slight edge in his centralized knight and pawn position.

4. ... Nd7 (D)

Preparing a support for the g8-knight's development at f6. If White then exchanges knights, Black can recapture with the d7-knight.

If he played instead 4...Nf6 then comes 5.Nxf6+ and Black must either break up his kingside pawns by 5...gxf6 or capture with the queen and risk its being bothered by the minor pieces. A sample of what could happen (after 4...Nf6 5.Nxf6+ Qxf6 ) is this little trap: 6.Nf3 Bd7 (to seize the long diagonal) 7.Bd3 Bc6 8.Bg5 Bxf3 9.Qd2! Qxd4 10.Bb5+ and White wins the enemy queen.

5.Nf3 ...

The best possible way to put the king's knight to work - by developing it at 1*3 where it has enormous influence on the centre, and where it stands peerless in defence of the castled king.

5. ... Be7

A noncommittal developing move (it brings a piece off the back rank and helps the king castle quickly) but not so good as the conventional 5...Ngf6 .

If Black tries instead the fianchetto development of his c8-bishop (tempting in view of White's exposed knight), there is a pretty trap he can stumble into: 5...b6 6.Bb5 Bb7 7.Ne5! Bxe4 (or 7...Bc8 8.Bg5 Nf6 9.Nc6 , winning the queen) 8.Bxd7+ Ke7 9.Bc6! and Black must lose some material.

6.Bd3 ...

This is probably sharper than 6.Bc4 but either move places the bishop in a good spot and clears the first rank for kingside castling.

6. ... Ngf6

Black too prepares to get his king into safety by developing (at long last) his g8-knight.

7.Qe2 ...

White develops a piece and supports his central knight strongly with queen and bishop.

This is more restraining on Black's cramped position than 7.Nxf6+ Bxf6 , when Black can initiate an attack on White's centre with ...c5.

7. ... O-O

The king seeks security in the corner. There was no easy freeing manoeuvre in 7...Nxe4 8.Bxe4 Nf6 since 9.Bxb7 Bxb7 10.Qb5+ followed by 11.Qxb7 wins a pawn for White.

8.O-O ...

White's castling is less to escape danger than to let the h1-rook take an active part in the game.

White's position is so promising as to offer him a good attacking line in 8.Bg5 Nxe4 9.Qxe4 g6 (certainly not 9...Nf6 to prevent mate, as 10.Bxf6 wins on the spot) 10.h4 , when White can castle on the queenside and storm the enemy bastions with his kingside pawns.

8. ... Nxe4 (D)

Black exchanges to get some elbow - room.

9.Qxe4! ...

Takes command of the board with a threat of mate! Offhand it looks risky to make this capture and leave the queen exposed to harassment by the minor pieces, but Black is in no position (in either sense of the word) to make trouble. He has all he can do to stay alive!

9. ... Nf6

Naturally. Black does not wish to advance one of the kingside pawns, for instance 9...g6 , unless compelled to, but what's wrong with the move he plays, 9...Nf6? Does it not bring the knight to its best square, guard against mate, beat off the queen, and free his own queenside?

Indeed it does all these things, and under the circumstances it is probably Black's best move. It is strange that a move made under duress often does not have the same positive effect as a move made of one's own volition.

10.Qh4 ...

After this. Black's knight, which to be sure stands on a good square, must remain on that square to guard against mate.

10. ... b6 (D)

The c8-bishop, barred by Black's very first move from coming out to the kingside, seeks other means to lake part in the battle. Development at b7 looks attractive, as from there it commands the long diagonal.

11.Bg5! ...

Excellent strategy! White attacks the most important defensive piece, the knight that guards against mate. The specific threat is 12.Bxf6 Bxf6 13.Qxh7# .

A simple threat and easy to meet - all Black has to do is play 11...g6 or 11...h6 .

What then is White aiming at?

The hidden purpose is to force Black to move one of the kingside pawns, to avoid being mated. A move by either of these pawns creates a looseness in the defensive structure that can never be repaired. It weakens the position organically as it makes a breach that can never be closed. The pawn that advances can never go back to its former position in the line of defensive pawns.

11. ... g6

On the alternative 11...h6 White wins by 12.Bxf6 Bxf6 13.Qe4 , when the threat of mate wins the rook on a8, an innocent bystander.

12.c4 ...

A very good move! To begin with, it prevents Black playing 12...Nd5 , to remove by exchange White's attacking pieces. Offensively, it prepares an advance of the d4-pawn, which will break up Black's pawn structure at e6. Once this is done. White's rook will have a point of entry on the e-file.

12. ... Bb7

Black has no effective counterattack. The best he can do is to keep on developing pieces on the most favourable squares to make a hard fight of it.

13.d5 ...

Threatening, after the preparatory 14.Rad1 , to take the e6-pawn 15.dxe6 so that the forced recapture by 15...fxe6 deprives Black's g-pawn of one of its props.

13. ... exd5 (D)

This seems to win a pawn, as should White recapture by 14.cxd5 the reply 14...Nxd5 not only holds on to the pawn but compels an exchange of bishops, taking the edge off White's attack.

14.Rfe1! ...

This unexpected zwischenzug (in-between move) threatens immediate victory by 15.Rxe7 Qxe7 16.Bxf6 Qd6 17.Ng5 h5 18.Qxh5! gxh5 19.Bh7#!

14. ... h6

Black offers a pawn to deflect one of the pieces bearing down so heavily on his f6-knight and c7-hishop.

There was no relief in 14...Kg7 to support the knight, as White responds with the brutal 15.Bh6+ winning the exchange.

15.Qxh6 ...

But not 15.Bxh6 , which permits 15...Ne4 driving off White's queen.

White now plans 16.Bxg6 fxg6 17.Qxg6+ Kh8 18.Nd4 (threatening to win by 19.Ne6 , 19.Nf5 or 19.Re3 followed by 20.Rh3+ ) 18...Qe8 19.Qh6+ Kg8 20.Nf5 Rf7 21.Bxf6 winning easily.

15. ... Ng4

White has a neat win against the defence by 15...Ne4 . He plays 16.Bxe7 Qxe7 17.cxd5 (threatening to capture the pinned e4-knight next) 17...Bxd5 18.Bxe4 Bxe4 19.Rxe4! Qxe4 20.Ng5 , when Black must lose his queen or be mated.

The text-move is of course an attempt to chase the queen away.

16.Qh4 ...

White supports the bishop, attacks the knight and threatens to win by 17.Bxe7 . What more can be achieved by one move?

16. ... Bxg5

Returning the knight to f6 leads to catastrophe, viz.: 16...Nf6 17.Rxe7 Qxe7 18.Bxf6 and White attacks Black's queen while threatening 19.Qh8# .

17.Nxg5 (D) ...

Once again the theme song mate at h7.

17. ... Nf6

And Black must dance to the tune, by bringing the knight back to f6.

Here too Black posts the knight on a good square, but not of his own free will.

18.Qh6 ...

This restricts Black more than would manoeuvring the rook over to h3 by way of e3. For example, after 18.Re3 Re8 19.Rh3 Kf8 the king escapes immediate disaster.

After White's actual move, if Black tries 18...Re8 he falls into 19.Bxg6 Rxe1+ 20.Rxe1 fxg6 21.Qxg6+ Kh8 22.Nf7# .

18. ... d4

To prevent 19.Re3 , and to give the bishop more play on the long diagonal.

How does White continue the attack? Can he bring up the reserves without losing too much time? Or can he weaken the defensive formation and make it vulnerable to immediate assault?

Is there a hint in the last question? Yes, indeed!

Black's chief defence lies in his knight, which guards against the mate at h7, and the f-pawn supporting the all-important g-pawn. If only White could strike at these two defenders - threaten them, remove them, get them owl of the way somehow...

There is a move, the kind that looks absurd at first glance!

Hint No. 2: A master player looks at every move he would like to make, especially the impossible ones.

19.Re6!! ...

Threatening to capture the knight, and then mate with the queen.

19. ... Re8 (D)

Had Black played 19...fxe6 , there would have followed 20.Qxg6+ Kh8 21.Qh6+ Kg8 22.Bh7+ Kh8 23.Bf5+ Kg8 24.Bxe6+ Rf7 25.Bxf7# .

It is interesting to note how the startling 19.Re6 move not only threatens to take the knight hut also exploits the fact that the f-pawn dare not capture the rook and abandon the defence of the g-pawn.

Black's last move is intended to clear the f8-square for the king, if White should take the knight and then check at h7 with his queen.

20.Bxg6 ...

Breaking through the pawn barrier! White's threat is 21.Bxf7# .

1-0

There is no defence:

1) 20...fxe6 21.Bf7# .

2) 20...fxg6 21.Qxg6+ Kh8 22.Nf7# .

3) 20...Qd7 21.Bh7+ Nxh7 (or 21...Kh8 22.Qxf6# ) 22.Qxh7+ Kf8 23.Qh8# .
