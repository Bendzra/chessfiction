Typical Najdorf Themes

Najdorf play is exceptionally rich in thematic ideas, and I shall be highlighting these basic weapons in the Najdorf player's arsenal throughout the book. The more of them you are familiar with, the more skilfully and successfully you will be able to handle Najdorf positions, and many Sicilian situations in general.

In this short chapter, I would like to highlight some of the most central of these themes, some highly concrete and others of a more conceptual nature. Each position is drawn from one of the main illustrative games in this book, so for more details you can refer to the game in question.

Timing

Vasily Ivanchuk - Veselin Topalov
Morelia/Linares 2008

Black has just played 11...b4! at a moment when White is not in a position to execute the thematic Nd5 sacrifice.

12.Na4

The knight must therefore move to the edge of the board. The sacrifice 12.Nf5?! should be met by 12...bxc3 13.Nxg7+ Kd8! .

12...Nbd7 13.f3 O-O 14.Rfd1 Kh8

Black has avoided the first wave of White's attack and safeguarded his king. See Chapter 1, Game 2 for the details.

Counterattack

Viorel Iordachescu - Andrei Maksimenko
Serbian Team Ch, Zlatibor 2006

19...Rfc8!

The rook move is more logical than locking in the dark-squared bishop with 19...f6?! 20.Bb2 Bf7 21.Nd4 d5 22.exd5 Qe5+ 23.Be2 , when White has an edge.

20.c3 Bc4 21.Rc1 Bxe2 22.Bxe2 (D)

22...Bxd4 23.Qxd4 a5!

Black's queen's rook now has more scope.

24.Kd2

24.b5?! Qc5 25.Qxc5 Rxc5 is pleasant for Black since the white bishop has little to do.

24...axb4 25.cxb4 Qd8

The chances are equal. See Chapter 2, Game 4 for further information.

Ulan Ude 2009

24...Rxa3!

27.Bd4 Nfd7 28.Qb2 Qa8

Attacking Vulnerable Pawns

Evgeny Alekseev - Leinier Dominguez
Biel 2008

13...h5!?

Black becomes active on the kingside before White plays Ng3. The solid 13...Ba6 14.Re1 O-O is also playable.

14.gxh5 Rxh5 15.Nd4?!

White exploits Black's undefended rook to plant his knight on c6, but this turns out to be ineffective. Another sharp idea is 15.Nf4!? Rh4 16.Ne6 fxe6 17.dxe6 Rb8 18.Qd3!? Kf8 .

15...Nf6 16.Nc6 Qc7 (D)

White would usually expect to have an advantage in most Sicilian lines when he obtains an advanced knight outpost, but there are several reasons why Black is OK here:

• The exchange of one set of knights on the d5-square followed by White's recapture with his e4-pawn has closed the d-file and limited White's options for infiltrating Black's position.

• White's natural pawn-break f4 can now be met by ...e4, keeping the centre closed.

• The powerful knight on c6 is offset by White's weakened kingside: the split f- and h-pawns leave the white king extremely vulnerable.

See Chapter 4, Game 10 for the game continuation and additional details.

Fighting for Space

Arkadij Naiditsch - Boris Gelfand
Dortmund 2007

13.Rfd1

White's set-up was pioneered by Karpov, whose original idea 13.Rfc1 is well met by 13...Nc5 14.Nxc5 dxc5 15.f3 c4 16.Na4 Nd7 , since White cannot now establish a queenside bind.

13...Nc5 14.Nxc5 dxc5 15.f3

15.Nd5 can be met by 15...Bxd5 16.exd5 Bd6 17.c4 Nd7 followed by kingside expansion with ...f5.

15...Rfd8 16.Qe1 Rxd1 17.Bxd1 c4

Black has secured an equal game: he has plenty of space on the queenside and counter-play with ...Bb4. See Chapter 5, Game 12 for more information.

Simplification

Sergei Tiviakov - Suat Atalik
Turin Olympiad 2006

14.Nf5 Be6

More solid than 14...gxf5 15.Bxh5 fxe4 16.Bxf4 Be6 17.Be2 , when Black's vulnerable kingside affords White compensation.

15.Nxe7+ Qxe7 16.Bxh5 Nxh5 17.Bxf4 Nxf4 18.Rxf4 Rac8

Hanging on to the f4-pawn was a clever ploy by Black to reduce White's attacking chances: to recover the sacrificed pawn he had to exchange three sets of minor pieces, and the chances are now equal. See Chapter 6, Game 4.

Exchange Sacrifice on c3

Alexei Shirov - Viswanathan Anand
Morelia/Linares 2008

13...Rxc3!?

This type of exchange sacrifice is one of the most important themes in many Sicilian lines, and the Najdorf is certainly no exception. Black eliminates any attacking ideas involving a Nd5 sacrifice, weakens White's queenside and makes the e4-pawn more vulnerable. In many cases, the positional compensation is sufficient even when the white king is castled kingside.

14.bxc3 Qc7

The queen guards e5 while preparing ...Nb6-a4.

15.Kb1 Be7 (D)

16.e5!?

with tremendous complications. See Chapter 7, Game 16 for additional details.

Queenside Attack

Daniel Stellwagen - Tiger Hillarp Persson
Malmo 2008

14.g4

White is spending a lot of time seeking king-side play. It is not easy for Black to bring his king to a completely safe home, so his best option is to fight for the initiative by counterattacking against the white king.

14...Bb7 15.Bg2 O-O-O 16.Kb1

White tries to tuck his king away, anticipating Black creating pressure on the half-open c-file.

16...Nc5 17.Rhe1

White must avoid 17.Nxf6? , as he loses material after 17...h3! 18.Bf1 Bg7 .

17...Be7 18.Qh3 Kb8 19.f5 e5 20.Nb3 a5!?

The position is dynamically balanced. The black a-pawn is a powerful battering-ram, and targeting the dark squares around the white king is totally logical given the fact that White has exchanged off his bishop that operates on that colour squares. See Chapter 8, Game 18.

Kingside Attack

Alexei Shirov - Wang Hao
Russian Team Ch, Dagomys 2009

22...Nde5?

Black had to play 22...a5! 23.b5 Nce5 24.Bb3 a4 25.Bd1 Ng6 . Now White's attack smashes through.

23.Rxh6!! 1-0

The key line is 23...Qxh6 24.Nf6+ Kh8 25.Bg5 Qh7 26.Nxh7 Kxh7 27.Qe4+ Kg8 28.Bd5 Bd7 29.Rf4 . See Chapter 9, Game 20 for additional details.

Central Breakthrough

Evgeny Nayer - Nick de Firmian
World Open, Philadelphia 2009

In one of the sharpest lines of the Najdorf, Black had gone astray several moves earlier by allowing the white knight into d5; here White has too many pieces massed in the centre, and must seek a vigorous way to smash through Black's defences.

20.Rg7! Nxe4 21.Bxe6! (D)

21...Bxd5

21...fxe6 22.Nc7+ Kd8 23.Ncxe6+ Kc8 24.Rxb7 Kxb7 25.Qxe4+ also leads to disaster for Black.

22.Bxf7+ Kd8

Or 22...Bxf7 23.Qxe4+ , mating.

23.Nb3 Nd2+ 24.Rxd2 Bxf3 25.Nxa5 Rh6 26.Rg6

White has an extra pawn and a dominant position.

See Chapter 10, Game 23 for additional details, and to see how Black could have avoided this calamity.
