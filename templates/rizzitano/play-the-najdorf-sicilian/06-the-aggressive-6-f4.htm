6 The Aggressive 6.f4

6.f4 is an appealing choice for attacking players; the footsoldiers side-by-side on e4 and f4 control the centre and support a potential kingside pawn-storm. White is also able to meet ...e5 by Nf3 without impeding his f-pawn; the knight may then be deployed to the kingside via Ng5 or Nh4-f5 to support a kingside attack. The downside of the f-pawn advance is that White loses the possibility of advancing the pawn more modestly to f3 to support his e4-pawn. The f-pawn advance also weakens the g1-a7 diagonal; after White has castled kingside, this gives Black several possibilities for creating counterplay with ...Qb6+.

6.f4 experienced a surge in popularity during the late 1980s and early 1990s as White searched for a fresh weapon with which to combat the Najdorf; one of the attractions was the wide-open field for developing new ideas. John Nunn's recommendation of 6.f4 in his Beating the Sicilian series also contributed to the popularity of this line. We shall focus on Black's traditional Najdorf reply 6...e5 . The major alternative 6...e6 transposes to a line of the Scheveningen (ECO code B82).


Game 13 (Navara-Maksimenko) features the main line 6.f4 e5 7.Nf3 Nbd7 8.a4 Be7 9.Bd3 O-O 10.O-O exf4 11.Bxf4 . White recaptures his pawn and offers the b2-pawn in order to lure the black queen away from the centre. Black accepts the sacrifice and White masses his forces on the kingside in preparation for a kingside assault. Navara misplays the attack and Maksimenko overlooks an opportunity to consolidate his advantage by harassing the white queen. Maksimenko follows up with a reckless pawn advance in front of his king, and after a further miscue his advantage disappears and Navara is able to steer the game into a drawn rook endgame. The middlegame demonstrates the importance of remaining vigilant and minimizing any opportunity for the opponent to generate counterplay.


In Game 14 (Tiviakov-Atalik), White varies with 6.f4 e5 7.Nf3 Nbd7 8.a4 Be7 9.Bd3 O-O 10.O-O exf4 11.Kh1 . The idea behind the king move is to defer recapturing the pawn in order to limit Black's queenside counterplay. Atalik responds by defending the f4-pawn; the goal is not to retain the pawn, but rather to compel White to exchange minor pieces in order to restore material equality. Several sets of exchanges give way to one of the characteristic Najdorf structures, where White has an isolated e4-pawn and knight versus Black's isolated d6-pawn and bishop. Both players respond with some accurate moves and the result is a well-played draw.

Game 13 [B93]
David Navara - Andrei Maksimenko
Bundesliga 2007/8

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.f4

White grabs space on the kingside and prepares a retreat for his d4-knight behind the f-pawn.

6...e5 (D)

This is Black's consistent Najdorf response; other options include 6...Nbd7 and 6...Qc7 , but I shall not be examining these moves here. Black's most important alternative is 6...e6 , transposing to a line of the Scheveningen (ECO code B82).

7.Nf3

Alternatives:

(a) 7.Nf5 (a rare move that could be an effective surprise weapon) 7...d5! (the stem game of this line saw 7...Bxf5 8.exf5 Nc6 9.g4!? with sharp play in Fischer-J.MacDonald, Montreal simul 1964) 8.Ne3!? and then:

(a1) 8...d4? 9.fxe5 Ng8 10.Bc4! is very dangerous for Black; for example, 10...Nh6 11.Ned5 Nc6 ( 11...dxc3? 12.Bg5! Qa5 (forced; 12...Qd7? 13.Nb6 with crushing threats or 12...Qxg5? 13.Nc7+ Ke7 14.Qd6# ) 13.b4 is dreadful for Black) 12.Bxh6 dxc3 13.Bg5 Qa5 14.b3 with an advantage for White as he will remain a pawn ahead.

(a2) 8...Nxe4 9.Nxe4 dxe4 10.Qxd8+ Kxd8 11.fxe5 (Losev-Dubinsky, Moscow 1997) and now 11...Be6 12.Bc4 Bxc4 13.Nxc4 Nd7 is equal - Arizmendi and Moreno.

(a3) 8...exf4 9.Nexd5 Nxd5 10.Qxd5 Bd6 with roughly equal chances.

(b) 7.Nb3 (the knight is marginalized here) 7...Nbd7 8.Bd3 Be7 9.O-O b5 10.Kh1 Bb7 11.Qe2 O-O 12.Bd2 Re8 and Black has smoothly completed his development, Radulov-Sigurjonsson, Lucerne Olympiad 1982. One of the ideas behind the 6.f4 Variation is to prepare a retreat for the white knight behind the f-pawn; the combination of f4 and Nb3 does not work well because the knight cannot readily participate in a potential kingside attack.

7...Nbd7 (D)

8.a4

White invests a tempo to grab some space and restrain Black's ambitions on the queenside; if Black is allowed to play ...b5, White is often compelled to respond with a3 to prevent Black from driving the c3-knight away from the centre with a timely ...b4 pawn advance.

Alternatives:

(a) 8.Bc4 Be7 9.a4 transposes to the note to White's 9th move.

(b) 8.Bd3 b5 (for 8...Be7 9.a4 , see the main game) 9.O-O Be7 10.Kh1 O-O 11.a3 Re8 12.Qe1 ( 12.Nh4 Nc5 13.fxe5 dxe5 14.Nf5?! Bxf5 15.Rxf5 ( 15.exf5 Nxd3 16.cxd3 Qd4 is also depressing for White because of his vulnerable d3-pawn) 15...Nfxe4! 16.Nxe4 Nxe4 and Black has won a pawn, Guliev-Van der Stricht, Moscow 2004) 12...Bb7 13.fxe5 dxe5 14.Bg5 Qb6 15.Nh4 g6 with equal chances, Henao-Browne, US Open Ch, Los Angeles 1991.

8...Be7 (D)

9.Bd3

White has not been able to demonstrate any advantage after 9.Bc4 Qa5! 10.Qe2 ( 10.O-O?? Qc5+ picks up the bishop) 10...O-O 11.O-O exf4 12.Bxf4 Ne5 (note how the black queen lends support to the e5 strongpoint) 13.Nd5 ( 13.Bxe5 dxe5 14.Kh1 Qc7 with a balanced game, Stellwagen-Nunn, Amsterdam 2006) 13...Nxd5 14.Bxd5 Be6 15.Bxe6 Nxf3+ 16.Qxf3 fxe6 17.Qb3 Qc5+ 18.Kh1 Qc8 19.Rad1 ( 19.a5 Qd7 20.Rad1 Rac8 is also harmless for Black) 19...b5! with equal chances, Leko-Kasparov, Sarajevo 1999.

9...O-O 10.O-O exf4

Another major option is 10...Nc5 11.Kh1 (the king steps aside as 11.fxe5 dxe5 12.Nxe5?? Qd4+ wins the knight) 11...exf4 , transposing to note 'b' to Black's 11th move in Game 14.

11.Bxf4

11.Kh1 is covered in Game 14.

11...Qb6+ 12.Kh1 Qxb2 (D)

13.Qe1

The queen heads for the kingside. This has become established as the main line, but there are two important alternatives:

(a) 13.Qd2 (the queen appears to be misplaced here) 13...Qb6 14.Nd5 Nxd5 15.exd5 Nc5 and then:

(a1) 16.Rae1 Qd8 17.h3 ( 17.Be3!? ) 17...Bd7 18.a5 Rc8 left White with inadequate compensation for the pawn in Adams-Van Wely, Garmisch Partenkirchen rapid 1994.

(a2) 16.Ng5 Bxg5 17.Bxg5 f6 18.Be3 Qc7 19.Bxc5 Qxc5 20.Rab1 with the idea of Qb4 enabled White to equalize in Vogt-Gelfand, Halle 1987.

(b) 13.Nd5 (White clarifies the central pawn-structure before committing his queen to a particular square) 13...Nxd5 14.exd5 and now:

(b1) 14...Qb6 15.Qe2!? (for 15.Qd2 see line 'a'; 15.Qe1 transposes to note 'b' to White's 14th move) 15...Bf6 16.Rab1 Qc7 17.Bg5 Qd8 ( 17...g6 18.Bh6 Qd8 19.Bxf8 Qxf8 20.Nd2 Bd8 was agreed drawn in Haslinger-Rowson, British Ch, Douglas 2005, but White should play 21.Nc4 with an edge) 18.Qe4 g6 19.Qh4 h5 (D).

Now:

(b11) 20.Rb4 Bxg5 21.Nxg5 Ne5 22.Rbf4 f5 (Gross-Womacka, Feldbach 1997) and now Gallagher's suggestion of 23.Qg3 should be met by 23...Ng4 , when Black has adequate defensive resources.

(b12) 20.Bxf6 Qxf6 ( 20...Nxf6 , Filippov-Al Modiahki, Hyderabad 2005, and now 21.Ne5 Nxd5 ( 21...Kg7 22.Rb6! leaves Black paralysed) 22.Qxd8 Rxd8 23.Nxf7 Rd7 24.Ng5 favours White as Black has difficulty unravelling his queenside) 21.Ng5 Qe7 22.g4 b5 23.Rbe1 Ne5 24.gxh5 Bb7 25.Be4 with an edge for White as his king is more secure.

(b13) 20.h3!? (a new move to prise open the kingside) 20...Bxg5 21.Nxg5 Ne5 22.g4! favoured White in Arakhamia-Gormally, European Union Ch, Liverpool 2008.

(b2) 14...Nc5! (the safest choice, eliminating White's dangerous light-squared bishop) 15.Qe2 Nxd3 16.Qxd3 Qb4 17.Bg5 (D), and here:

(b21) 17...f6!? (this was a 1995 Informator 65 suggestion by Gelfand) 18.Bd2 Qg4 (another reasonable move is 18...Qc5!? , Boucher-Aykent, ICCF corr. 1996) 19.Rae1 Bf5 20.Qb3 Rfe8 is fine for Black as White's queenside pawns are vulnerable.

(b22) 17...Bxg5 18.Nxg5 f5!? (this was suggested by Ki.Georgiev and Kolev as an improvement over 18...Qh4 19.Ne4 (Leko-Gelfand, Belgrade 1995) 19...Qe7 20.Rae1 f5 21.Nc5 Qf6 22.Ne6 Bxe6 23.dxe6 Rae8 with a slight advantage for White according to Gelfand; however, I think Black can hold the position after 24.Qxd6 Qe7 as White's e6-pawn is blockaded) 19.Rfe1 Bd7 ( 19...Qh4 20.Ne6 Rf7 also looks reasonable) 20.Ne6 ( 20.Re7? Rae8 was given by Ki.Georgiev and Kolev as an example of the weakness of White's back rank) 20...Rf7 and Black retains his extra pawn.

We now return to 13.Qe1 (D):

13...Qb6

This is Black's most popular choice: having snagged a pawn, the queen withdraws from any potential trouble along the b-file. Alternatives:

(a) 13...Qb4?! (this queen retreat is too provocative and the queen quickly becomes marooned on the queenside) 14.Rb1 Qc5 15.Nd5 Nxd5 16.exd5 Bf6 17.c4! Qc7?! 18.Qg3 Ne5 19.Bg5 Bxg5 20.Bxh7+! Kxh7 21.Nxg5+ Kh6 22.Qh4+ Kg6 23.Rb3!? with a mating attack for White, Vogt-Womacka, East Germany 1989.

(b) 13...Nc5!? (this move is more resilient than its reputation) 14.Rb1 Nxd3 15.cxd3 Qc2 ( 15...Qa3?? loses to 16.d4 ) 16.e5 ( 16.d4 Re8 17.Rf2 Qd3 18.Rc1 Bf8 19.Rd2 Qc4 was fine for Black in Axelsson-de Firmian, Stockholm 1993; 16.Rd1 Qb3 17.Nd4 Qb6 18.Be3 , with compensation for the material, was suggested by Ki.Georgiev and Kolev, and this looks reasonable in view of 18...Qd8 19.Nf5 Bxf5 20.Rxf5 Rc8 21.Rb1 Qd7 22.Bd4 with kingside pressure) 16...Nh5 17.Nd5 Bd8 18.Bg5 (D) and here:

(b1) 18...f6? was played in Nijboer-Armas, Wijk aan Zee 1989, but then 19.e6! Re8 (after 19...fxg5 20.Nd4 White wins as the black king is in a mating-net) 20.e7 Bxe7 21.Nxe7+ Kf8 22.Qe4 gives White a decisive attack.

(b2) 18...Be6! 19.Ne7+ Bxe7 20.Bxe7 Nf4! 21.Qg3 ( 21.Rf2 Qxa4 is fine for Black) 21...dxe5 22.Bxf8 Rxf8 23.Nxe5 Bd5 with roughly equal chances as Black has good piece-play in return for his small material investment; this line could use some practical tests.

We now return to the position arising after 13...Qb6 (D):

14.Bg5

Alternatives:

(a) 14.Rb1?! Qd8 15.e5 dxe5 16.Nxe5 Nc5 17.Bc4 Be6 18.Bxe6 fxe6 19.Nc4 (Savon-Vaulin, Katowice 1993) 19...Qe8 20.Nb6 Rd8 favours Black thanks to his extra pawn.

(b) 14.Nd5 Nxd5 15.exd5 Qd8 and then:

(b1) 16.Qg3 and here Black has two promising moves:

(b11) 16...Nc5 17.Rae1 ( 17.Nd2!? looks more prudent, to preserve White's pawn-structure) 17...Nxd3 18.cxd3 Bf5 19.Re3 Re8 left White with insufficient compensation for the pawn in de Firmian-Van Wely, Akureyri 1994.

(b12) 16...Nf6 17.Qh4 Ne4 18.Qh5 g6! ( 18...Nf6 led to a draw by repetition in Barglowski-Bobras, Polish Team Ch, Lubniewice 2003) 19.Qh6 Re8 20.Bxe4 Bf8 21.Qg5 f6 22.Qg3 Rxe4 and again White has insufficient compensation for the pawn.

(b2) 16.c4 (White expends a tempo to prepare a retreat for his light-squared bishop) 16...Nc5 17.Bc2 Bg4 18.Qg3 Bh5 (this is the most precise continuation, by which Black plays to neutralize White's dangerous light-squared bishop) 19.Rae1 (the stem game in this line saw 19.Nd4 Bg6 20.Nf5 Bxf5 21.Bxf5 Bh4 22.Qg4 g6 with an edge for Black, M.Negele-G.Mueller, corr. 1998) 19...Bg6 20.Bxg6 fxg6 21.Rxe7 ( 21.Bg5?? Rxf3 22.Qxf3 Bxg5 23.Qf7+ Kh8 gave Black a decisive advantage in Panchanathan-Saravanan, Nagpur 2002 as the white rooks have no entry-points) 21...Qxe7 22.Bxd6 Qe2 23.Re1 Qxc4 24.Bxf8 Rxf8 with an advantage for Black as he has retained his extra pawn and White's remaining pawns are vulnerable.

14...Nc5

This knight move aims to eliminate White's potent light-squared bishop. This was a 1994 Informator 62 suggestion by Van Wely after he played 14...Qd8 15.Qh4 Re8 ( 15...h6!? 16.Bxh6 gxh6 17.e5 Nxe5 18.Qxh6 ( 18.Nxe5? loses to 18...Ng4!! ) 18...Neg4 19.Qg5+ Kh8 20.Qh4+ Kg7 21.Qg5+ with a draw by repetition) 16.e5!? (this is a common motif in this line: White opens the b1-h7 diagonal for his light-squared bishop) 16...dxe5 17.Bxf6 Nxf6 18.Ng5! ( 18.Nxe5?! Be6 gave Black a large advantage in Kveinys-Van Wely, Moscow Olympiad 1994; Black has the bishop-pair to go along with an extra pawn) 18...h6 19.Nxf7! Kxf7 20.Bc4+ (D).

Now:

(a) 20...Be6 ( 20...Kf8?? 21.Qh5 mates) 21.Bxe6+ Kxe6 22.Qc4+ Kd7 23.Rad1+ Bd6 24.Rxf6 gxf6 ( 24...Qxf6 25.Ne4 Qf4 26.Nc5+ Kc8 27.Qd5 Bxc5 28.Qxc5+ Kb8 29.Qd6+ Ka7 30.Qc5+ Kb8 31.Qd6+ is a draw) 25.Ne4 Ke7 26.Nxd6 Qxd6 27.Rxd6 Kxd6 28.Qb4+ Ke6 29.Qxb7 with a likely draw as it is difficult for the black king to find shelter.

(b) 20...Kg6 21.Bd3+ Kf7 22.Bc4+ with a draw by repetition - Van Wely.

This example illustrates several typical Sicilian attacking themes and underlines the importance of exchanging White's light-squared bishop.

15.Qh4 Nxd3 16.cxd3 (D)

16...Qa5

Another reasonable move is 16...Qd8!? followed by ...h6 to repel White's dark-squared bishop. This idea awaits a practical trial.

17.Ne2?!

White is systematically driven back after this knight retreat.

The most dangerous try is 17.e5 . Then:

(a) 17...Qxc3 18.Rac1 Qb2 19.Rb1 Qc3 20.Rbc1 Qb2 1/2-1/2 Bielczyk-Vera, Cappelle la Grande 2008.

(b) 17...dxe5! 18.Ne4 Qd8 19.Nxe5 Nd5 20.Rxf7 Rxf7 21.Nxf7 Kxf7 22.Qxh7 Bxg5 23.Qh5+ Kg8 24.Nxg5 Nf6 25.Qf7+ Kh8 26.Rf1 Bg4 27.Qg6 Bh5 28.Nf7+ Kg8 29.Nh6+ Kh8 30.Nf7+ with a draw by perpetual check.

17...h6 18.Bd2 Qd8 19.Qe1 Bd7 (D)

20.Nfd4 Ng4 21.Qg3 Bf6

Black should exploit the white queen's lack of squares with the direct attack 21...Bh4! 22.Qf3 ( 22.Qxd6 Nf2+ 23.Kg1 Nxd3 24.Ra3 Bg4 favours Black thanks to his powerful bishop-pair) 22...Ne5 23.Qe3 Bg5 24.Nf4 Qb6 with an advantage for Black as White has no compensation for his missing pawn and he is caught in an uncomfortable pin.

22.Rab1 Be5 23.Bf4 Rb8 24.Nf3 Bxf4 25.Nxf4 f5?!

The pawn advance needlessly weakens the kingside. Black should instead consolidate his advantage with 25...Nf6 26.Nd4 Qa5 27.Nfe2 Qe5 , when White has no compensation for his material deficit.

26.Nd4!

Navara seizes the opportunity to stir up some trouble on the kingside.

26...Qf6 27.Nxf5 Bxf5 28.Nd5 (D)

28...Qe6?

Black should keep the knights on the board and exchange queens with 28...Qe5! 29.Qxe5 ( 29.exf5 Qxg3 30.hxg3 Rbe8 favours Black because of White's boxed-in king) 29...Nxe5! ( 29...dxe5?! 30.Ne7+ Kh7 31.Nxf5 is equal) 30.exf5 ( 30.Ne7+ Kh7 31.Nxf5 Nxd3 32.Nxd6 Rxf1+ 33.Rxf1 b5 34.axb5 axb5 also favours Black) 30...Nxd3 and Black retains winning chances thanks to his extra pawn.

29.exf5 Qxd5 30.Qxg4 Rf6 31.Rf3 b5 32.Qe4 Qxe4 33.dxe4 Re8

If 33...b4 , then 34.a5 enables White to maintain the balance.

34.axb5 axb5 35.Rxb5 Rxe4 1/2-1/2

Game 14 [B93]
Sergei Tiviakov - Suat Atalik
Turin Olympiad 2006

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.f4 e5 7.Nf3 Nbd7 8.a4 Be7 9.Bd3 O-O 10.O-O exf4

For comments on the moves up to here, see Game 13.

11.Kh1 (D)

11.Bxf4 was covered in Game 13.

11...Nh5!?

Black defends the f4-pawn at the cost of moving his knight to the edge of the board; one of the ideas is to induce White into wasting precious tempi to restore material equality. Black has two major alternatives:

(a) 11...Ne5 12.Bxf4 Qc7 and now:

(a1) 13.Qd2 Be6 14.Nd4 Rac8 15.Nce2 Rfe8 with equal chances, Tal-Rizzitano, National Open, Chicago 1988. This game is fully annotated in Understanding Your Chess.

(a2) 13.Qe1 Be6 14.Nxe5 dxe5 15.Bg5 Kh8 16.Rd1 Rad8 with a balanced game, Mallahi-Andriasian, Sevan 2006.

(a3) 13.Bg5 (this introduces a dangerous exchange sacrifice, against which Black must play precisely to hold the balance) 13...Be6 14.Nxe5 ( 14.a5 Nfg4! 15.Bf4 Nxf3 16.Qxf3 Ne5 17.Qg3 Rac8 is equal, J.Polgar-Wojtkiewicz, Budapest Zonal 1993) 14...dxe5 15.Bxf6 Bxf6 16.Rxf6! gxf6 17.Qh5 Kg7 18.Rf1 Rh8 19.Qh4 ( 19.Ne2 was a 1998 suggestion by Joe Gallagher, to which 19...Rhg8 looks like an adequate defence) 19...Qe7 20.Nd5 Bxd5 21.exd5 (D).

21...a5! (Black must blockade the queenside; this was an improvement over the careless 21...Rad8? 22.c4 a5 23.Be4 b6 24.b4! axb4 25.a5 with a deadly pawn-roller, Glek-Malishauskas, Tallinn 1986) 22.c4 b6 23.Be4 Qd6 24.b4 axb4 25.a5 (White lures the black rook off the back rank) 25...Rxa5 26.Qg4+ with perpetual check, S.Gonzalez-A.Petrosian, Bogota 1987.

(b) 11...Nc5 12.Bxf4 Bg4 (an important manoeuvre: as we shall see, one of Black's major ideas is ...Bh5-g6 to put pressure on e4) and then:

(b1) 13.Qd2 Rc8 and here:

(b11) 14.Nd4 d5! 15.e5 Nfe4 16.Qe3 Nxd3 (1/2-1/2 Gdanski-Kuczynski, Ksiaz 1998; but Black should play on) 17.Qxd3 Nxc3 18.bxc3 Qd7 gives Black good chances to exploit White's weakened queenside.

(b12) 14.Rae1 Re8 and now:

(b121) 15.Nd4 Qb6!? (this was a 2004 recommendation by Arizmendi and Moreno) 16.b3 (after 16.Bg5? Nxd3 17.Qxd3 Qxb2 Black simply wins a pawn, Ahn-Feller, Charleroi 2006) 16...Nxd3 17.Qxd3 Qc5 18.Bd2 Bd8 19.Nd5!? Nxd5 20.exd5 Bf6 21.Nf5 Be5 22.c4 with equal chances as White has managed to safeguard his queenside pawns.

(b122) 15.h3 Bh5 16.Nd4 Bg6 ( 16...Qb6!? ) 17.Nf5 Bxf5 18.exf5 d5 with good play for Black as he has smoothly completed his development, Zi Jing Wong-Palliser, British League (4NCL) 2008/9.

(b2) 13.Qe1 Rc8 (D) and now:

(b21) 14.Qg3 Bh5 15.Be3 Bg6 16.Bd4 d5! 17.Bxf6 ( 17.exd5 Nxd3 18.cxd3 Nxd5 19.Qe5 Nf6 favours Black as he has the superior pawn-structure to go along with his bishop-pair) 17...Bxf6 18.e5 Nxd3 19.cxd3 ( 19.exf6 Nxb2 ) 19...Be7 "and Black has nothing to complain about" - Arizmendi and Moreno.

(b22) 14.Nd4 Qb6! and then:

(b221) 15.Be2?! Bxe2 16.Qxe2 Rfe8 (the alternative 16...Qxb2!? should lead to the same position after 17.Nf5 Rfe8 , since 17.Bd2? loses to 17...Ncxe4! ) 17.Nf5 Qxb2 with an edge for Black, J.Polgar-Anand, Buenos Aires 1994.

(b222) 15.a5! Nxd3 16.axb6 Nxe1 17.Raxe1 Rc4 offers Black counterplay according to Anand in his 1994 Informator 62 notes. 18.Nf5 Bxf5 19.exf5 Re8 is comfortable for Black.

We now return to 11...Nh5!? (D):

12.Nd4

The white knight aims for the f5-square.

Alternatives:

(a) 12.Ne1 (this retreat fails to impress as the knight is headed in the wrong direction) 12...Ne5! 13.Be2 ( 13.Qxh5?? Bg4 traps the queen) 13...Nf6 14.Bxf4 Be6 and then:

(a1) 15.Nf3 and here Black has a choice between speculative or solid moves:

(a11) 15...Qb6!? 16.Nxe5 ( 16.Qd4 Qxd4 17.Nxd4 Rac8 is equal) 16...dxe5 17.Bxe5 Rfd8!? ( 17...Rac8?! was tried in Friedel-Esserman, Berkeley 2008, but 18.Nd5! favours White) 18.Qc1 Rac8 gives Black easy development in return for the pawn.

(a12) 15...Qc7 16.Nd4 (Meier-Guelpen, corr. 1995) 16...Rac8 with a solid position for Black thanks to the strongpoint on e5.

(a2) 15.Nd3 Ng6 16.Bg3 d5 (Black has already equalized) 17.exd5 ( 17.Bf3 d4 18.Ne2 Ng4 19.Bxg4 Bxg4 was also fine for Black in Bauer-Gelfand, French Team Ch 1999/00) 17...Nxd5 18.Nxd5 Bxd5 19.Bf3 Bf6 1/2-1/2 Groszpeter-Varga, Gyula 1998.

(b) 12.Nd5 and now:

(b1) 12...Ndf6 13.Nxf4 Bg4 14.c4 Re8 is equal, Arakhamia-Areshchenko, Caleta 2005.

(b2) 12...Nc5 13.Nd4 ( 13.Nxf4 Nxf4 14.Bxf4 Bf6 15.c3 Re8 is fine for Black, Petrovic-Tringov, Novi Sad 1986) 13...g6 14.Nxf4 Nxf4 15.Bxf4 Bg5 16.Qd2 Bxf4 17.Qxf4 Ne6 with an even game, Dvoirys-A.Shneider, Podolsk 1993.

12...g6 (D)

12...Ndf6 13.Be2 g6 is another route to the position seen in the main game.

13.Be2

White can also play the immediate 13.Nf5 Ndf6 14.Nxe7+ Qxe7 15.Bxf4 Nxf4 16.Rxf4 (the exchange of two sets of minor pieces has reduced any potential threat to Black's king-side) 16...Be6 (or 16...Qe5 17.Qd2 Be6 18.Raf1 Nd7 with a solid position for Black as his queen cannot be dislodged from its centralized post) 17.Qe2 Rac8 (another idea is 17...Nd7 18.Bc4 Nb6 with equal chances - Ki.Georgiev and Kolev) 18.e5 Nd7 (Black must avoid 18...Nd5? 19.Nxd5 Bxd5 20.Rd4 , when White will win a pawn, Dvoirys-Averchenko, Tomsk 1997) 19.exd6 Qxd6 with equality.

13...Ndf6 (D)

14.Nf5

The challenge for White is to find a way to recover the f4-pawn without allowing excessive simplification. The alternative is 14.Bxh5 Nxh5 15.Nd5 Bg5 16.Qf3 ( 16.Nxf4 Nxf4 17.Bxf4 Bxf4 18.Rxf4 d5 is equal, Sokolov-Gelfand, Yugoslav Team Ch, Ulcinj 1997) 16...Bd7 17.Ne2 Bc6 18.Nexf4 Nxf4 19.Bxf4 Bxd5 20.exd5 Rc8 and the players soon agreed to a draw in J.Polgar-Gelfand, Linares 1997.

14...Be6

The bishop takes control of the vital d5-square; this is Black's safest choice.

Alternatives:

(a) 14...gxf5 15.Bxh5 fxe4 16.Bxf4 Be6 17.Be2 gives White reasonable compensation for the pawn in view of Black's vulnerable king-side.

(b) 14...Re8 15.Nxe7+ Qxe7 16.Bxf4 Nxf4 17.Rxf4 Be6 is equal, Friedel-Ludwig, USA Ch Qualifier, Tulsa 2008.

15.Nxe7+ Qxe7 (D)

16.Bxh5

Tiviakov elects to exchange one of the black knights before recovering the f4-pawn; this prevents Black from establishing a knight outpost on the e5-square as in note 'a' below.

Alternatives:
