10 Main Line: 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Be7

The Main Line, 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Be7 , was the focal point of the 6.Bg5 variation during the 1970s and early 1980s; attention then shifted to the Poisoned Pawn Variation. Several of the critical Main Line positions were somewhat neglected during the past two decades, but there has been an upsurge in attention during recent years as players have re-examined some older positions and developed new ideas for both sides with the assistance of analytical chess engines. After the usual continuation 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.O-O-O Nbd7 , White has an important choice between continuing his development with 10.Bd3 (Game 22), or initiating a kingside pawn-storm by 10.g4 (Game 23).

Both lines are critical and highly theoretical, so it is important to work through the lines and understand the details before venturing into these sharp positions. Strong nerves are required by both players in the Main Line: White is often called upon to give up material (for example, via the Nd5 piece sacrifice) in order to maintain the initiative, and Black often finds his king under attack in the middle of the board. A thorough knowledge of these types of positions is an important step toward developing an understanding of the subtle balance between time and material in pursuit of the initiative.


Game 22 (Mamedov-Areshchenko) features the developing move 10.Bd3 , to which Black responds with 10...h6 (the Browne Variation) 11.Bh4 g5 to kick White's dark-squared bishop away from the kingside. The players follow the main line for several moves. Areshchenko expands on the queenside with 17...b5 (17...Bd7 is safer), and Mamedov counters several moves later with 20.a4 to try to break open Black's queenside. The queenside battle escalates as Black sacrifices an exchange to keep the initiative; the middlegame is a typical back-and-forth Najdorf battle. The end of the struggle is marred by time-pressure, but the game illustrates the importance of keeping the initiative, even at the cost of some material.


In Game 23 (Nayer-de Firmian), White opts for the aggressive 10.g4 and Black selects the 13...Nc5 line. Nayer plays Perenyi's dangerous 16.Rg1 and after 16...h5 he opts for the crafty 17.Bh3. de Firmian goes astray and Nayer is able to unleash a cascade of sacrifices to shatter Black's king position. Black resists but ends up a pawn down in a sharp middlegame; a further error allows White to finish off the game in style. This battle is rather one-sided and there are improvements for Black in the notes, but the game is a stark reminder of the latent power in White's set-up: Black must play extremely accurately or he can quickly find himself in a difficult situation.

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Be7 (D)

8.Qf3

Another common transposition is 8.Qe2 h6! 9.Bh4?! (but 9.Bxf6 Bxf6 10.O-O-O Qc7 is comfortable for Black, Kim-Lanin, St Petersburg 2007), when Black has 9...Nxe4! - for the details, see note 'a' to White's 7th move in Game 15.

8...Qc7

The queen move is directed against the e5 pawn-break. Less popular is the older line 8...h6 (nowadays Black tends to avoid this move until White has played Bd3) 9.Bh4 and then:

(a) 9...Qc7?! 10.O-O-O Nbd7 11.Be2! (White's light-squared bishop is particularly effective on e2 after Black has played ...h6 because from here the bishop controls g4 and also gives White various tactical opportunities involving the h5-e8 diagonal; White can also transpose into the game continuation by playing 11.Bd3 ) 11...Rb8?! (Black should prefer the more recent idea 11...O-O!? 12.Qg3 Nc5 13.Bf3 e5 14.Nf5 Bxf5 15.exf5 Rac8 with equal chances, Sammour Hasbun-Ludwig, US League 2009; I expect further developments in this line) 12.Qg3 Rg8 (Black's dual rook moves make a bad impression) 13.Rhf1 g5 14.fxg5 Ne5 ( 14...Qa5?! 15.Nxe6 fxe6 16.gxf6 Rxg3 17.Rd5!! (this combination highlights the weakness of Black's kingside after he has played ...h6) 17...Qxd5 18.Nxd5 Nxf6 19.Nxf6+ Bxf6 20.Bxg3 wins for White, Weinstein-De Fotis, US Open Ch, Chicago 1973) 15.Nf3 hxg5 16.Bxg5! (this was suggested by Nunn in 1996; 16.Nxg5?! Qc5 ( 16...Rg6? 17.Qf4! b5 18.Nxf7! b4 19.Bxf6 bxc3 20.Bxe7 1-0 Bowie Reed-Douthwaite, corr. 1992) 17.Kb1 is only somewhat better for White) and now White wins in all lines: 16...Nh5 17.Qh3! Rxg5 ( 17...Nxf3 loses to 18.Bxe7 - Nunn) 18.Nxe5 Rxe5 19.Bxh5 or 16...Nxf3 17.Bxf3 Nh5 18.Bxh5 .

(b) 9...g5 10.fxg5 Nfd7 (D) is the famous Gothenburg Variation, which was the prototype of the method employed by Black in our main game.

At first sight, Black's play looks like sheer madness, but the logic is that he is trying to secure a grip on the vital e5-square. If he succeeds in planting a knight firmly on this fine central post, then he is unlikely to have any major problems, even if this comes at a small cost in material or structure. Therefore White tends to react vigorously, either striking combinatively before Black can establish a knight on e5, or else to remove the knight from this square once it arrives. The critical lines are:

(b1) 11.Nxe6!? fxe6 12.Qh5+ Kf8 13.Bb5! (the logic behind this extraordinary move is that the bishop will eliminate one black knight or the other, unless Black takes the bishop on b5, but then the extra tempo will grant White's attack a decisive impetus) 13...Rh7! 14.O-O+ Kg8 15.g6 Rg7 16.Rf7! Bxh4 17.Qxh6 Rxf7 18.gxf7+ Kxf7 19.Rf1+ ( 19.Qh7+ is a draw) 19...Bf6 20.Qh7+ ( 20.e5 dxe5 21.Ne4 Qb6+! 22.Kh1 axb5 23.Nxf6 Ke7 24.Rd1 Nxf6 25.Qg7+ Ke8 26.Qg6+ Ke7 1/2-1/2 Zivkovic-Martinovic, Zenica 2008) 20...Ke8! (the older move 20...Kf8? loses to Dale Kirton's idea 21.e5 dxe5 22.Be2! with a decisive attack, T.Schmidt-Limayo, ICCF e-mail 1999) 21.Qg6+ Kf8 22.e5 ( 22.Qh6+ is a draw) 22...dxe5 23.Bxd7!? ( 23.Ne4?! Qb6+ 24.Kh1 axb5 25.Nxf6 Ke7 26.Qe8+ Kd6 27.Qxc8 and now the strong improvement 27...Ra4! was introduced in Sevecek-Cranbourne, ICCF e-mail 2001; then 28.b4 is relatively best, although White is struggling for a draw) 23...Nxd7 24.Ne4 Qe8!? 25.Qh6+ Ke7 26.Nxf6 Nxf6 27.Qxf6+ Kd6 28.Rd1+ Kc6 led to a draw in Daurelle-Rousset, France 2007. Interested readers are directed to the NIC Yearbook 85 Forum.

(b2) 11.Qh5 Ne5 12.Bg3 Bxg5 13.Be2 ( 13.Nf3 is another approach) 13...Qb6 14.Rd1 (D) and here:

(b21) 14...Qxb2?! 15.O-O Rf8?! ( 15...O-O 16.Na4 Qb4 17.Nf3 favours White) 16.Nxe6! (this is an improvement over 16.Ndb5?! axb5 17.Nxb5 Ke7 with sharp play, Iskov-T.Kjeldsen, Odense 1975) 16...Bxe6 17.Rxd6 Bxa2 18.Nxa2 Nbd7 19.Nc1 Bxc1 20.Re6+ Kd8 21.Rxe5! Nxe5 22.Bxe5 gives White a strong attack in return for the exchange as the black king is caught in a central crossfire.

(b22) 14...Nbd7 15.Nxe6 (the strength of Black's central knight outpost is seen in the line 15.O-O Nf6 16.Qh3 Qxb2 17.Bf4 Bxf4 18.Rxf4 Nh7 19.Nd5!? exd5 20.Qxh6 Qa3 21.Qg7 Rf8 22.Qxh7 Bd7 23.exd5 O-O-O with an unclear position) 15...Nf6 16.Nd5 (Poenisch-H.Schreiber, corr. 1987) 16...Qa5+! 17.b4 Nxh5 18.Nec7+ Kd8 19.bxa5 ( 19.Bxe5!? is an interesting try, though after 19...Qxa2 20.Bxh5 dxe5 21.Nxa8 Ke8 no clear end is in sight) 19...Nxg3 20.hxg3 Ra7 is roughly equal as Black's misplaced rook is offset by White's horrible pawn-structure.

The Gothenburg Variation is easy to condemn on general grounds but very hard to refute with concrete analysis. Its main drawback may be no more than the fact that White can force a draw in line 'b1' above; however, this is a type of practical 'defect' that applies to many sharp tactical opening lines.

9.O-O-O Nbd7 (D)

10.Bd3

The bishop is generally well-placed on d3 because it supports the e4-pawn, defends the c2-pawn and takes aim at Black's kingside. One potential drawback is that White's centralized knight is now unprotected.

Alternatives:

(a) 10.g4 is the subject of Game 23.

(b) 10.f5 e5 11.Nb3 ( 11.Nde2 b5 12.Ng3 Bb7 is also comfortable for Black) 11...b5 12.a3 Bb7 13.h4 Rc8 is equal, Ader-Fischer, Santiago 1959.

(c) 10.Qg3 (White applies indirect pressure on the g7-pawn; this line was popular during the 1970s and 1980s, but interest in the queen move waned as the correct response was discovered) 10...h6 11.Bh4 g5 (Black is able to utilize the vulnerable position of White's queen and bishop to liberate his position) 12.fxg5 Nh5 ( 12...Rg8 is a sound alternative) 13.Qe3 Qc5 (this is the key idea: Black recovers the pawn with a solid position) 14.Kb1 hxg5 15.Bf2 Ne5 16.Qd2 Qc7 17.Nf3 Nxf3 18.gxf3 Bd7 with a balanced game, Kupreichik-Beliavsky, USSR Ch, Leningrad 1974, and many later games.

(d) 10.Be2 b5! (Black should avoid 10...h6?! 11.Bh4 , which transposes to note 'a' to Black's 8th move above) 11.Bxf6 Nxf6 12.e5 Bb7 13.Qg3 (Keres's original queen sacrifice 13.exf6 Bxf3 14.Bxf3 Bxf6 15.Bxa8 d5 (now the white bishop is unable to retreat) 16.Bxd5 Bxd4 17.Rxd4 exd5 18.Nxd5 Qc5 19.Re1+ Kf8 20.c3 h5! left White struggling for a draw in Keres-Fischer, Bled/Zagreb/Belgrade Candidates 1959) 13...dxe5 14.fxe5 Nd7 15.Bxb5 (White should settle for 15.Bf3 O-O 16.Bxb7 Qxb7 17.Rhe1 Nc5 with a queenside initiative for Black, Morgado-Ricardi, Mar del Plata 1988) 15...axb5 16.Ndxb5 Qc5 17.Qxg7 Qxe5! (Black returns some material to extinguish White's initiative) 18.Nc7+ Qxc7 19.Qxh8+ Nf8 20.Qg8 Ra5 21.Rhf1 Bg5+ 22.Kb1 Bxg2 23.Rg1 Qxh2 24.Qg7 Rf5 with an edge for Black as the two bishops are superior to the white rook, Grimm-Pershin, ICCF corr. 2002.

We now return to 10.Bd3 (D):

10...h6

Lines with ...h6 by Black are generally known as the Browne Variation.

The equally-popular alternative is 10...b5 11.Rhe1 Bb7 (the provocative advance 11...b4?! 12.Nd5 gives White a more favourable version of the knight sacrifice) 12.Qg3 b4 13.Nd5 exd5 14.exd5 (the pawn recapture is more dynamic than 14.e5 dxe5 15.fxe5 Nh5 16.Qh4 (the queen sacrifice 16.e6 Nxg3 17.exf7+ Kxf7 18.Rxe7+ Kg8 19.hxg3 also leads to a draw) 16...Bxg5+ 17.Qxg5 g6 18.e6 Nc5 19.exf7++ Kxf7 20.Rf1+ Kg8 21.Nf5 Ne6 22.Nh6+ Kg7 23.Nf5+ 1/2-1/2 Lassen-G.Nielsen, Danish corr. Ch 1982, and several later games) 14...Kd8 15.Nc6+! (White offers another pawn to open lines in the centre; this idea of Kotronias's has revived interest in this line) 15...Bxc6 16.dxc6 (D).

Now Black must decide how to deal with the pawn on c6:

(a) 16...Nb6 17.Bh4 Rg8 18.Re2 Qxc6 19.Bxh7 Re8 20.Qxg7 Qc4 (after 20...Nxh7?! , as played in E.Berg-Negi, Wijk aan Zee 2007, 21.Qxf7! Ra7 22.Qxh7 Rc7 23.Rde1 Nd5 24.g3 favours White as Black can't unravel his pieces) 21.Rxe7 Qxf4+ 22.Re3! Qxe3+ ( 22...Qxh4? loses to 23.g3 ) 23.Kb1 Qe6 24.Bxf6+ Kc7 25.Bg5 gives White reasonable compensation for his slight material deficit in view of Black's exposed king.

(b) 16...Qxc6 17.Bxf6 Nxf6 18.Qxg7 Re8 19.Qxf7 Ra7 20.Rxe7 Raxe7 21.Qxf6 Kc7 22.Qd4 Qb6 ( 22...Qc5 23.Qxc5+ dxc5 24.Bxa6 also favours White) 23.Qxb6+ Kxb6 24.Kd2 Kc5 25.f5 with an endgame edge for White as the bishop and two pawns are superior to the rook, Navara-Shirov, Prague rapid 2005.

(c) 16...Nc5 17.Bh4 Rg8 18.Bxh7 Rh8 19.Qxg7 Rxh7 20.Qxf6 Rxh4 (not 20...Bxf6?? 21.Bxf6+ Kc8 22.Re8+ , when White mates) 21.Qxf7 Rh8 22.Re5 Na4 23.Re3 Ra7 24.Rde1 (D).

Black has two extra pieces, but his king is in a jam. Now:

(c1) 24...Re8 25.f5 Qxc6 26.f6 (Kotronias-A.Shneider, Korinthos 2004) 26...Rc7 ( 26...Nc5 27.Kb1 keeps up the pressure) 27.R1e2 Qb5 28.fxe7+ Kc8 29.h4 favours White as the pawns are superior to the knight.

(c2) 24...Qxc6! 25.Rxe7 Rxe7 26.Qxe7+ Kc8 27.h3 ( 27.h4 Nc5 is unclear, Poli-Buessing, ICCF corr. 2007) 27...Nc5 with roughly equal chances, Hebels-Chopin, ICCF e-mail 2006. I expect there will be further developments in this line.

11.Bh4

White has two important alternatives:

(a) 11.h4?! (D) is an attacking idea that has been played in more than 100 games. The pawn-thrust has attracted a bit of a cult following over the years: the idea is not so easy to refute and Black's best defence has never been clearly established. He can try:

(a1) 11...hxg5 12.hxg5 Rf8 (the strength of White's attack is apparent after 12...Rxh1?! 13.Rxh1 Ng8?! 14.Rh8 Kf8 15.Qh3 d5 16.e5 Bxg5 17.Rxg8+ with a mating attack, Fiorentini-Villa, corr. 1984) 13.gxf6 Bxf6 14.Nde2 Nc5 15.Kb1 Bd7! ( 15...Rb8? 16.g4 favours White, Silman-Taijan, Lone Pine 1979) 16.g4 O-O-O ( 16...g5!? 17.Rh6 Bg7 18.fxg5 Qd8 is unclear) 17.g5 Be7 18.Rh7 Rg8 with roughly equal chances.

(a2) 11...b5 12.Bxf6 Nxf6 13.e5 Bb7 14.Qh3 ( 14.Qg3 dxe5 15.fxe5 O-O! with a slight advantage for Black "shows the dark side of h4" - Nunn) 14...Nd5 15.Bg6! (this is an improvement over 15.Nxe6? fxe6 16.Qxe6 Nxf4 17.Bg6+ Kd8 with an edge for Black, Czaya-Born, corr. 1978) 15...Nxc3 ( 15...Nxf4 16.Nxe6! ) 16.Bxf7+ Kxf7 17.Qxe6+ Kf8 18.Qf5+ Kg8 19.Qe6+ Kf8 20.Qf5+ with a draw by repetition.

(a3) 11...Nc5!? 12.f5 hxg5 13.hxg5 Rxh1 14.Rxh1 (D) and now:

(a31) 14...Nfd7?! 15.Rh8+ and here:

(a311) 15...Bf8 16.fxe6 and then:

(a3111) 16...Ne5? 17.Nd5! Ncxd3+ 18.Kb1 Qa5 19.Qf1!! (Vigorito faced this shocker in an online blitz game; the line was originally believed to favour Black after the inferior continuation 19.exf7+?? Nxf7 20.Rxf8+ Kxf8 21.cxd3 (R.Burgess-R.Booth, Australian corr. Ch 1983) 21...g6 , when Black has an extra rook) 19...Nb4 20.e7 Qxa2+ ( 20...Ng6 21.exf8Q+ Nxf8 22.Nb3 with a clear plus for White) 21.Kc1 Ng6 22.exf8Q+ Nxf8 23.Nb3 Qa4 24.g6! and White wins - Vigorito.

(a3112) 16...Nxd3+! 17.Kb1 N3e5 18.g6 Nf6 19.Qxf6 Nxg6 20.Nd5 gxf6 21.Nxc7+ Kd8 22.Rxf8+ Nxf8 23.e7+ Kxe7 24.Nxa8 b5 is a spectacular line, with a likely draw.

(a312) 15...Nf8 16.f6 gxf6 17.gxf6 (D) and then:

(a3121) 17...Bd8? 18.Qf4 , with a strong attack, has been played several times.

(a3122) 17...b5?! 18.Bxb5+ axb5 19.Ncxb5! Qa5 20.b4 Qxb4 21.Nc7+ Kd7 22.fxe7 Qe1+ 23.Qd1 Nd3+! (this is an improvement over 23...Qe3+? 24.Kb1! ( 24.Qd2? Qg1+ 25.Qd1 Qe3+ was a draw by repetition in Tsuboi-Paolozzi, Brazilian Ch, Sao Luis 1981) 24...Kxe7 25.Nf5+ exf5 26.Nd5+ Kd7 27.Nxe3 , when White has a decisive material advantage) 24.cxd3 Qe3+ 25.Kb1 Rb8+ 26.Ncb5 Qxd4 27.exf8N+ Ke7 28.Ng6+ fxg6 29.Rh7+ Kd8 30.a4 favours White, but he may not be winning.

(a3123) 17...Qd8! 18.fxe7 Qxe7 19.Qe3 Bd7 20.Kb1 O-O-O is equal.

(a32) 14...Ng8! 15.f6 Nxf6! 16.Rh8+ Kd7 17.gxf6 (D) and now:

(a321) 17...Nxd3+?! 18.Qxd3 Bxf6 was assessed by Nunn as unclear back in 1996. Now White could consider 19.Nf3 Qa5 ( 19...Bxc3 20.bxc3 Qc5 21.Rg8!? is annoying) 20.g4!? (now White has possibilities of Rh5 and/or g5 in some lines) 20...Bxc3 21.bxc3 Qa3+ (the idea is to give Black the option of ...Qb5+ if necessary) 22.Kb1 Qc5 23.Rh7 Ke7 24.Rxg7 Bd7 25.Ng5 ( 25.e5!? ) 25...Qg1+ ( 25...Rf8 26.Qf3 ) 26.Kb2 Qb6+ 27.Kc1 Qg1+ with a repetition.

(a322) 17...Bxf6! looks good for Black, as theory's recommendation 18.Bc4 is strongly answered by 18...Qb6! (this is even better than 18...Bxd4 19.Nb5! (F.Larsen-Kubach, corr. 1984) 19...Bxb2+ 20.Kxb2 , when 20...Na4+ 21.Kb3 Nc5+ 22.Kb2 Na4+ is a draw by repetition, while 20...Qb6 tries for more) 19.Nde2 Kc7 with a large advantage for Black thanks to his extra pawn and pair of bishops.

(b) 11.Qh3 (White maintains the position of his bishop by pinning Black's h6-pawn; this move was popular during the 1970s but has since fallen out of favour) 11...Nb6 (this move controls d5 and makes way for ...Bd7 followed by queenside castling; there are also some tactical possibilities based upon the vulnerable position of the white queen) 12.f5 e5 (D) and here:

(b1) 13.Nb3 Bd7 14.Be3 ( 14.Kb1 Bc6 15.Rhe1 Nbd7 with a balanced game, Naiditsch-Kempinski, Bundesliga 2007/8) 14...Na4! (not 14...Nc4? 15.Bxc4 Qxc4 16.Nd2 Qc6 17.Bg5! , which favours White as he will land one of his knights on d5, Timman-Hamann, Amsterdam 1975) 15.Nxa4 ( 15.Bd2?! Nxc3 16.Bxc3 a5! (this is the usual recipe for harassing a white knight on b3) 17.a3 Bc6 ( 17...b5!? ) 18.Rhe1 b5 gave Black a queenside initiative in Timman-Portisch, European Team Ch, Plovdiv 1983) 15...Bxa4 16.Qg3 Bc6 17.Qxg7 Rg8 18.Qxh6 Nxe4 with equality.

(b2) 13.Nde2 Bd7 14.Kb1 Bc6 15.Be3 d5! (it is usually a good sign for Black if he can liberate his position with ...d5) 16.Bxb6 Qxb6 17.exd5 Ba3 18.b3 Bxd5 (Black must avoid 18...Nxd5? 19.Nxd5 Bxd5 20.Nc3 Bc6 21.f6! gxf6 22.Bc4 with a strong attack as the black king has no safe haven) 19.Nxd5 Nxd5 20.Be4! ( 20.Bc4 Nf6 21.Nc3 O-O is equal, Nunn-Browne, Tilburg 1982) 20...Nf6 21.Qf3 O-O! 22.Bxb7 Ra7 23.Be4 a5 gave Black reasonable compensation for the pawn in Nunn-Sadler, Hastings 1997/8.

We now return to 11.Bh4 (D):

11...g5

The g-pawn thrust is a common motif in the Najdorf: by diverting White's f4-pawn, Black secures the e5-square for one of his knights.

12.fxg5

White has also tried to crack open the centre with 12.e5?! gxh4 13.exf6 Nxf6 14.f5 ( 14.Rhe1 Bd7 15.Re3 Rg8 16.Rde1 Qb6 17.Nf5 Bf8 18.Qf2?! ( 18.Nxh4 O-O-O favours Black) 18...O-O-O and White's attack has backfired, J.Anderson-Hunt, Hastings 2008/9) 14...e5 15.Nde2 Bd7 16.Be4 Rc8!? 17.Bxb7 Bc6 (another idea is 17...Rb8!? 18.Be4 a5 ) 18.Bxc6+ Qxc6 19.Qxc6+ Rxc6 20.Rhf1? ( 20.Nd5!? ) 20...Rg8 21.g3 (Khlusevich-Neverovsky, Moscow 1986) 21...Ng4! favours Black as White's kingside is under heavy pressure.

12...Ne5 13.Qe2 (D)

13...Nfg4

The knight advance has proved to be more reliable than the retreat 13...Nh7 . Then:

(a) 14.Bg3 hxg5 15.Rhf1 Nf6 16.Nf3 Nfd7 ( 16...Nh5!? ) 17.Kb1 b5 is equal, Ivanchuk-Vallejo Pons, Amber Rapid, Monte Carlo 2005.

(b) 14.Nf3 hxg5 15.Bf2! Nf8 16.Nxe5 dxe5 17.Be3 Bd7 ( 17...Rg8?! 18.Qf2 Bd7 (Hort-Bobotsov, Wijk aan Zee 1970) 19.Rhf1! f6 20.Be2 favours White as Black has issues unravelling his kingside; note that after 20...Ng6? 21.Bxg5! Black loses a pawn) 18.Qf2 Ng6 (Kostro-Kavalek, Polanica Zdroj 1968) 19.g3 Rc8 20.Rdf1 Rh7 21.Kb1 with an edge for White thanks to his more secure king position.

14.Nf3 (D)

White challenges Black's strongpoint on the e5-square. This is more critical than 14.h3 hxg5 15.Bg3 Nf6 16.Kb1 Bd7 17.Bf2 O-O-O 18.Nb3 (Szabo-Browne, Amsterdam 1972) 18...Nh5 with a balanced game.

14...hxg5

The immediate pawn recapture is more popular than the older 14...Nxf3 . Then:

(a) 15.Qxf3 hxg5 ( 15...Ne5 16.Qh5 Ng6 17.Bg3 hxg5 18.Qf3 Ne5 reaches the same position) 16.Bg3 Ne5 17.Bxe5 dxe5 18.Rdf1 Rh7 19.h4 gxh4 20.Qg4 Bf8 21.Rxh4 Rg7 22.Qh3 ( 22.Qe2 Bd7 is comfortable for Black) 22...Qe7 23.Rh7 ( 23.Rh5!? ) 23...Qg5+ 24.Kb1 Bd7 with equality, Suarez Real-Andres Gonzalez, Oviedo 2006.

(b) 15.gxf3 hxg5 16.Bg3 Ne5 (D).

This position has occurred more than 150 times in practice, but to date White has been unable to demonstrate any advantage:

(b1) 17.h4 gxh4 18.f4 Nxd3+ 19.Rxd3 Bd7 20.Qe1 Bb5! 21.Rd1 h3 is equal, Ljubojevic-Browne, Wijk aan Zee 1976.

(b2) 17.f4 gxf4 18.Bxf4 Bd7 19.Rdf1 Rh7 20.h4 O-O-O 21.h5 Rdh8 with equality, Hrabusa-Bindrich, European Under-18 Ch, Sibenik 2007.

15.Bxg5

White opens up the kingside and wins a pawn at the cost of leaving his knight temporarily misplaced. Black has no problems equalizing after 15.Bg3 ( 15.Nxe5 Nxe5 16.Bg3 Bd7 17.Rhf1 b5 is comfortable for Black) 15...Bd7 ( 15...Nxf3 16.gxf3 transposes to note 'b' to Black's 14th move above) and now:

(a) 16.Rhf1 Qc5 (another idea is 16...f6 17.h3 Nxf3 18.Rxf3 Ne5 and Black has a solid position thanks to his strongpoint on the e5-square, Walek-Mozny, Czech Team Ch 2002/3) 17.Nxe5 Nxe5 18.Kb1 Rc8 ( 18...b5!? ) 19.Bf2 Qa5 is equal, Shirov-Ivanchuk, Leon rapid 2008.

(b) 16.h3 Nxf3 17.hxg4 Rxh1 18.Rxh1 Nh4 19.e5!? Bc6 20.Rf1 (D) and then:

(b1) 20...Bxg2 21.Rf2 Bc6 22.Qf1 Qb6!? ( 22...dxe5 23.Rxf7 Ng2 24.Rh7 Nf4 (Bengtsson-Powles, IECC e-mail 2000) 25.Rh8+ Bf8 26.Rh7 Be7 with a repetition) 23.exd6 Qe3+ 24.Kb1 Qxg3 25.dxe7 Bg2 26.Qe1 Rc8 with roughly equal chances.

(b2) 20...Nxg2 21.Qf2 ( 21.exd6 Bxd6 22.Bxd6 Qxd6 23.Ne4 ( 23.Be4 Nf4 ) 23...Bxe4 24.Bxe4 Nf4 25.Qc4 was given by Nunn as unclear in 1996; now Black could consider 25...Rd8!? 26.a4 Qb6 , when White seems to have insufficient compensation for the pawn) 21...Nf4 22.Bxf4 gxf4 23.Qxf4 Kd7 ( 23...O-O-O 24.exd6 Qxd6 25.Qxd6 Rxd6 26.Rxf7 Bg5+ 27.Kb1 Rd4 and Black's bishop-pair compensates for his missing pawn, Vuckovic-Vujacic, Yugoslavia 1999) 24.exd6 Qxd6 25.Qxd6+ Kxd6 26.Ne4+ Ke5 27.Rxf7 Re8 and again Black's bishop-pair compensates for his pawn, Pillhock-Cutillas Ripoll, corr. 2000.

(c) 28.Bd3 Kc7 followed by ...Rha8 with a mating attack.

11...Nxf6

Black has a couple of alternative recaptures, and there is room in line 'b' for independent investigation:

(a) The pawn recapture 11...gxf6 has been out of fashion for years. Many games have followed the main line 12.f5 Ne5 13.Qh3 O-O 14.Rg1 Kh8 15.Rg3 Rg8 16.Nce2 Bd7 17.Nf4 with an edge for White because of the weakness of Black's e6-pawn.

(b) 11...Bxf6 12.Bxb5 ( 12.g5 gives Black a choice between transposing to the main game with 12...Be7 or taking an independent path by 12...Bxd4!? 13.Rxd4 Bb7 14.Be2 O-O 15.Rhd1 Rac8 with sharp play, Siugirov-Swiercz, European Under-14 Ch, Sibenik 2007) 12...O-O ( 12...Rb8 13.Bxd7+ Bxd7 14.g5 Bxd4 15.Rxd4 Qb6 16.Qd3 Qxb2+ 17.Kd2 Bb5 18.Qe3! favours White, Sulskis-Nayer, Groningen 1998, and several later games) 13.Bxd7 Bxd7 14.g5 Be7 15.h4 Rfb8 16.f5 Qb7 17.Nb3 (I think 17.b3!? also favours White because he keeps his knight in the centre) 17...exf5 18.Nd5 Bf8 19.exf5 a5 "with counterplay" according to Jonathan Rowson in his New In Chess Yearbook 54 article on this line. It seems to me that White can forge ahead with 20.h5! a4 ( 20...Bxf5 21.Qxf5 a4 22.Rhg1 Kh8 23.h6 axb3 24.hxg7+ Bxg7 25.Nf6 Bxf6 26.Qxf6+ Kg8 27.g6! fxg6 28.Rxg6+ hxg6 29.Qxg6+ with a decisive attack for White as he will eventually have queen and three pawns versus two rooks) 21.h6! g6 ( 21...axb3 22.hxg7 Bxg7 23.f6 bxa2 24.Ne7+ Kf8 25.fxg7+ mates) 22.fxg6 fxg6 23.Nf6+ Kh8 24.Qxb7 Rxb7 25.Nd4 and White retains his extra pawn with a crushing bind. Black needs to come up with an earlier improvement in this line.

12.g5 Nd7 (D)

13.f5 (D)

This aggressive advance has replaced the older line 13.a3 Rb8 14.h4 b4 15.axb4 Rxb4 16.Bh3 Qc5! (Black concedes a tempo, but draws the white knight away from the centre; this move has proven to be more reliable than the alternatives 16...Nc5 , 16...Qb6 and 16...O-O ) 17.Nb3 Qb6 18.h5 Nc5 19.Nxc5 dxc5! with a comfortable position for Black, Bellin-Portisch, Tees side 1972, and many later games.

13...Nc5

13...Bxg5+ has a solid reputation, but I believe that White can obtain an advantage with accurate play. Black temporarily wins a pawn, but the resulting positions tend to favour White because he can usually exchange Black's light-squared bishop and dominate the light squares in the centre and kingside. 14.Kb1 Ne5 15.Qh5 (D) and now:

(a) 15...h6?! 16.Nxe6! (the knight capture forces the exchange of Black's light-squared bishop) 16...Bxe6 17.fxe6 O-O 18.h4 Bd8 19.Nd5 Qb7 20.Bh3 Kh7 (L.Rowley-V.Pedersen, Australian Under-18 corr. Ch 1989) 21.Bf5+! g6 22.Rhg1 with a winning attack for White.

(b) 15...Bf6 16.Nxe6! Bxe6 17.fxe6 favours White.

(c) 15...Qd8 16.h4! (D).

This is White's strongest reply; after Black plays the inevitable ...g6 to drive the queen away, White will follow up with h5 to prise open the kingside. Black has two tries:

(c1) 16...b4 17.hxg5 bxc3 18.Nxe6 Bxe6 19.fxe6 g6 20.Qh6 Qb6 ( 20...cxb2 (A.Stuart-Thorn Leeson, corr. 1999) 21.exf7+ Kxf7 22.Bh3 with a strong attack) 21.b3 fxe6 22.Qg7 ( 22.Qh3 intending 22...Ke7 23.Qxc3 , as recommended by Nunn, is a strong alternative) 22...O-O-O 23.Rh3 Kb8 24.Rxc3 favours White as his active pieces and safer king are more significant than Black's centralized knight.

(c2) 16...Bf6 17.fxe6 O-O 18.Bh3 Kh8 (Black should avoid the weakening 18...g6?! 19.Qe2 Re8?! 20.exf7+ Kxf7 21.h5 with a strong attack, Nunn-T.Heinemann, Bundesliga 2001/2; this game is annotated by John Nunn in Grandmaster Chess Move by Move) 19.Nd5 fxe6 20.Bxe6 Bb7 21.Rdf1 Qe8 22.Qd1 Bxd5 23.Bxd5 Rc8 24.Ne6 Rf7 25.Rf5 favours White according to Nunn, Ilyes-Irzhanov, ICCF e-mail 2000.

(d) 15...Qe7 16.Nxe6! (the reader should recognize a common theme in these lines: exchange Black's light-squared bishop!) 16...Bxe6 17.fxe6 g6 18.exf7+ Kxf7 19.Qh3! Kg7 20.Nd5 Qd8 21.Qc3!
