7 Gelfand Variation: 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Nbd7

6.Bg5 was the most popular Najdorf line for many years, before being supplanted by the English Attack during the 1990s. However, there has been a recent revival in the more aggressive bishop development as players have taken a fresh look at some older positions and developed new ideas for both sides. Black's traditional reply is 6...e6 and after 7.f4 we begin our coverage with 7...Nbd7 .

The knight move was first played in the 1950s, but over time the variation has become closely identified with Boris Gelfand. The grandmaster from Belarus has achieved excellent results with this line in high-level games for more than twenty years. Black delays the development of his dark-squared bishop with ...Be7 in order to accelerate his queenside expansion with ...b5. This is a provocative concept and White has several interesting sacrificial ideas to attack Black's king position. The most critical lines start after 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.O-O-O b5 10.Bd3 Bb7 11.Rhe1 Qb6 . The Gelfand Variation is a very topical line and ideas are developing at a rapid pace.


Game 15 (E.Berg-Negi) covers the sharp tactical line beginning with 12.Nd5 . White sacrifices a bishop for three pawns and attacking chances as the black king is marooned in the centre. Black misses an opportunity to contest the open d-file and obtain a slight advantage. Berg plays very aggressively and keeps up the pressure, while Negi misses an additional opportunity to repel the attack. After a further error, White is able to infiltrate with his major pieces and Black is forced to sacrifice his queen for a rook and bishop to prevent mate. Berg quickly finishes off the game as the queen is too powerful on an open board. This game features some exciting back-and-forth tactics and demonstrates how important it is for the defender always to be alert to possible tactics directed against his king. In this game we also consider alternatives for both sides, starting with Black's 6th move.


In Game 16 (Shirov-Anand), White chooses the relatively quiet alternative 12.Nb3 . Black responds with 12...Rc8 and after 13.Qh3 he sacrifices the exchange with 13...Rxc3!? ; this is a typical Sicilian Defence motif whereby Black invests a small amount of material in order to reduce White's attacking opportunities and break up White's queenside pawn-structure. Shirov advances in the centre with 16.e5!? to open lines for his rooks, but after 16...dxe5 he goes astray with 17.f5?!, when Anand is able to safeguard his king in the centre of the board. Black misses an opportunity to increase his advantage, and then White overlooks a chance to equalize. Anand concludes the game very forcefully with a nice combination to exploit Shirov's weak back rank. The main theme of this game is White's inability to activate his rooks, which allowed Black gradually to increase his advantage and create irresistible mating threats.

Game 15 [B96]
Emanuel Berg - Parimarjan Negi
Malmo 2007

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 (D)

6...e6

Alternatives:

(a) 6...Nc6 leads to positions more akin to the Richter-Rauzer, to which it will probably transpose, so I shall not cover it here.

(b) 6...Nbd7 is an older line that has not been developed much in recent years. The most promising response is 7.Bc4 ( 7.f4 e6 is equivalent to 6...e6 7.f4 Nbd7 ) 7...Qa5 8.Qd2 e6 9.O-O-O b5 10.Bb3 Bb7 11.Rhe1 O-O-O 12.a3 , when theory favours White.

7.f4

White has a couple of minor alternatives:

(a) 7.Qe2?! (the queen runs into a tactical problem on this square) 7...h6! 8.Bh4 Be7 9.f4 Nxe4! 10.Bxe7 Nxc3 11.Qc4! (White sacrifices a pawn to keep his structure intact) 11...Kxe7 12.Qxc3 Re8! (Black prepares to castle 'by hand') 13.O-O-O Kf8 14.Nf3 ( 14.g4?! e5! 15.Nf5 Bxf5 16.gxf5 Nc6 favours Black, Blazi-Keller, Marktredwitz 1984; 14.Kb1 Kg8 is also fine for Black, as is 14.Be2 Qb6 , Shabalov-Bryant, Las Vegas 2008) 14...d5 leaves White with inadequate compensation for the pawn.

(b) 7.Qf3 h6! 8.Bh4 ( 8.Bxf6 Qxf6 9.Qxf6 gxf6 10.f4 h5 11.f5 Nc6 12.Rd1 Bd7 13.Bc4 Ke7 gave Black a comfortable endgame thanks to his bishop-pair in Short-Kasparov, Sarajevo 2000) 8...Nbd7 9.O-O-O Qc7 10.Be2 Be7 11.Rhe1 Rb8 12.Qh3 b5 is equal, Korchnoi-Sadvakasov, Match (game 7), Astana 2003.

We now return to 7.f4 (D):

7...Nbd7

In comparison with the Main Line (Chapter 10), Black delays the development of his king's bishop in order to accelerate his queenside expansion; this is a provocative idea and White has several interesting sacrificial ideas to attack

Black's king position. Black has a wide choice of alternatives:

(a) 7...Qc7 has similar aims, and is covered in Chapter 8: Kasparov Variation.

(b) 7...Qb6 is the Poisoned Pawn Variation - see Chapter 9.

(c) 7...b5 is the Polugaevsky Variation, also examined in Chapter 9.

(d) 7...Be7 is discussed in Chapter 10: Main Line.

(e) 7...Nc6 was popular in the 1990s, but is currently quite rare. Let's briefly examine the main lines after 8.e5 h6 9.Bh4 :

(e1) 9...Nxd4 10.Qxd4 (White must avoid 10.exf6? Nf5 11.fxg7?! Qxh4+ 12.g3 Nxg3 13.gxh8Q? Ne4+ with a mating attack, Sprenger-Danner, Oberpullendorf 2002) 10...dxe5 11.Qxd8+ Kxd8 12.fxe5 g5 13.Bg3 Nd7 14.O-O-O Bg7 15.Be2! and White completes his development and homes in on the vulnerable f7-pawn after 15...Ke7 16.Bh5 , Paramos Dominguez-L.Dominguez, Santa Clara 1999.

(e2) 9...dxe5 10.Nxc6 Qxd1+ 11.Rxd1 bxc6 12.fxe5 Nd5 13.Ne4 Rb8 14.b3 Be7 15.Bg3 with a slight structural advantage for White, Adams-Anand, Linares 1997.

(e3) 9...g5 allows White two attractive ideas:

(e31) 10.fxg5 Nd5 11.Nxd5 exd5 12.exd6 Bxd6 ( 12...Qxd6? 13.Qe2+ Be7 14.Nxc6 bxc6 15.Bg3! favours White as the black king is marooned in the centre, Shabalov-Browne, Las Vegas 1997) 13.Nxc6 bxc6 14.Qd4 Qe7+ 15.Be2 Be5 16.Qa4 Rb8 17.g6 and then:

(e311) 17...Qd6 18.gxf7+ gives White a strong attack after 18...Kxf7 19.O-O+ Kg7 20.Bg3 or 18...Kf8?! (McDonald-Danner, Budapest 1996) 19.a3! (covering b4) 19...Bxb2 20.O-O .

(e312) 17...Qb4+ 18.Qxb4 Rxb4 19.gxf7+ Kxf7 20.O-O+ Kg7 21.Bf2 Rf8 (Bracker-Heigermoser, Kelheim 2007) 22.Rae1 Bxb2 23.Bd3 with slight pressure for White.

(e32) 10.Bg3!? Nd5 ( 10...gxf4?? loses a piece: 11.Nxc6 Qb6 ( 11...bxc6 12.Bh4! ) 12.Bf2 Qxc6 13.exf6 ) 11.Nxd5 exd5 12.Nxc6 bxc6 13.Qe2 Be6 14.exd6 gxf4 15.Bxf4 Bxd6 16.Bxd6 Qxd6 17.O-O-O with an edge for White due to his more secure king, Zjukin-Shishkov, Estonian Team Ch, Tallinn 1998.

8.Qf3

This is White's most popular move, preparing to castle queenside and develop a rapid initiative in the centre.

Alternatives:

(a) 8.Bc4 Qb6 9.Bxf6!? (White aims for quick central pressure; this idea has recently become popular) 9...Nxf6 10.Bb3 and now:

(a1) 10...e5 11.Ba4+!? ( 11.Nde2 exf4 12.Nxf4 Qe3+ 13.Nfe2 Be7 is balanced, Beliavsky-Gelfand, Linares 1994) 11...Ke7 (another idea is 11...Bd7!? 12.Bxd7+ Kxd7 13.fxe5 dxe5 14.Nb3+ Kc7 with approximately equal chances) 12.Nde2 exf4 13.Bb3 Be6 14.Nd4 with good compensation for the pawn on account of Black's misplaced king, Naiditsch-Siugirov, Moscow 2009.

(a2) 10...Qc5 11.Qd3 Be7 12.O-O-O e5 (the simple 12...O-O looks like a reasonable alternative) 13.Nf5 Bxf5 14.exf5 with a slight advantage to White thanks to his grip on the light squares, Kurnosov-Anisimov, European Ch, Budva 2009.

(b) 8.Qe2 Qc7 9.O-O-O b5 and then:

(b1) 10.g4 b4 11.Nd5?! ( 11.Na4!? ) 11...exd5 12.exd5+ Be7 13.Nc6 ( 13.Re1 O-O! 14.Qxe7 Re8 15.Qxe8+ Nxe8 16.Rxe8+ Nf8 favours Black) 13...Nb8! 14.Bxf6 gxf6 and Black was able to withstand the assault in Jobava-Kariakin, Sochi 2007.

(b2) 10.a3 Bb7 11.g4 Be7 12.Bh4 h6 13.Kb1 Rc8 14.Bg2 Qc4 is equal, Bosiocic-Areshchenko, European Ch, Budva 2009.

8...Qc7 9.O-O-O b5 (D)

Black delays the development of his king-side in order to accelerate his queenside attack. Another option is to transpose into the 'Main Line' by 9...Be7 , which we consider in Chapter 10 via the standard move-order 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Be7 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.O-O-O Nbd7 .

10.Bd3

White has a wide range of alternatives here:

(a) 10.Bxf6 (White voluntarily exchanges a set of minor pieces; the goal is to increase the impact of a sacrifice on b5) 10...Nxf6 11.e5 Bb7 and then:

(a1) 12.Qe3!? (White keeps his queen in the centre of the board) 12...dxe5 13.Ncxb5 Qb6 14.fxe5 Nd5 15.Qg3 axb5 (Black must grab the piece as 15...O-O-O? 16.Na3 favoured White thanks to his more secure king in Hector-Van der Stricht, European Team Ch, Plovdiv 2003) 16.Bxb5+ Kd8 (D) and now:

(a11) 17.c4?! and then:

(a111) 17...Rxa2?! 18.Kb1!? (I think this is more challenging than 18.cxd5 Bxd5 19.Kb1 Bc5 20.Nb3 Rxb2+ 21.Kxb2 Qxb5 with easy play for Black, as indicated by Luther) 18...Ra8 19.Qf2!? Bc5 20.Qxf7 Kc8 21.cxd5 Bxd5 22.Qd7+ Kb8 23.Nc6+ Bxc6 24.Qxc6 Ra5 25.Qxb6+ Bxb6 26.Bc4 Rxe5 with equality.

(a112) 17...Bc5! 18.Nb3 Kc7! is worthy of attention, and looks quite promising for Black.

(a12) 17.a4 Kc8 18.Rhf1 is analysis by Luther; I think Black should then play 18...Ba6 19.Rxf7 Bxb5 20.Nxb5 Rxa4 21.Rxd5 exd5 22.e6 Qg1+ 23.Kd2 Rd4+ 24.Nxd4 Qxd4+ with equal chances.

(a2) 12.Qh3 dxe5 13.Ncxb5 (White runs out of firepower after 13.Nxe6? fxe6 14.Qxe6+ Qe7 15.Qb6 Rc8 16.fxe5 Rxc3! 17.bxc3 ( 17.exf6? loses to 17...Qe3+ ) 17...Nd5 18.Bxb5+ axb5 19.Qxb5+ Qd7 20.Qb3 Qc6 with a clear plus for Black, Willumsen-Urkedal, Copenhagen 2006) 13...Qb6! 14.fxe5 Ne4 15.Bc4 Nf2! with sharp play, Carlsen-Gelfand, Biel 2005.

(b) 10.Bxb5 (White sacrifices the bishop from its original square; this idea is dangerous because White has not lost a tempo with a preliminary Bd3 as occurs in several other lines) 10...axb5 11.Ndxb5 Qb8 ( 11...Qb6 is another continuation that has been explored in detail) 12.e5 Ra5 (and here 12...Bb7 is a major possibility) 13.exf6 gxf6 14.Bh6! Bxh6 15.Nxd6+ Ke7 16.Kb1 Rd8 17.Rhe1 Nb6 18.Ncb5 (D) and now:

(b1) 18...Rxb5?! 19.Nxb5 Nc4 20.Qb3 Nd2+ 21.Rxd2 Rxd2 22.Qb4+ Rd6 23.g3 Bd7 24.Qxd6+ Qxd6 25.Nxd6 Kxd6 26.Rd1+ (Konikowski's suggestion 26.b4!? also looks reasonable as 26...e5 27.fxe5+ fxe5 28.Kb2 favours White, as his queenside pawns are ready to roll) 26...Kc7 27.Rf1 (a draw was agreed here in Simmelink-Stuart, corr. 1997, but White should play on) 27...Kb6 28.b4 Kb5 29.Rd1 Bc6 30.Rd6 Bf8 31.c4+ Kb6 32.c5+ Kc7 33.Rd1 with a clear plus for White as his queenside pawns are in motion.

(b2) 18...Ba6 and then:

(b21) 19.Nf5+ Kf8 20.Qc3 Rxb5 21.Qxf6 Rxb2+! (the only move, but it is sufficient to save the game) 22.Qxb2 (White must capture the rampaging rook as 22.Ka1? Rxa2+! 23.Kxa2 Bc4+ 24.Ka1 Qa7+ leads to mate) 22...Nd5 23.Rxd5 Qxb2+ 24.Kxb2 Bg7+ 25.Nxg7 Rxd5 (this is the point of the combination: the white knight is unexpectedly trapped) 26.Nxe6+ fxe6 27.Rxe6 1/2-1/2 Lutz-Gelfand, Dortmund 2002. A possible continuation is 27...Bf1 28.g3 Rb5+ 29.Kc3 Rc5+ with equality.

(b22) 19.Qc3 Rxb5 20.Nxb5 Bxb5 21.Qb4+ Rd6 22.Qxb5 Bxf4 23.a4 (D) and now:

(b221) 23...Qa7 ("double-edged" - Palliser) 24.g3 (I think this is best as White is able to mobilize his pawns) 24...Be5 25.Rxd6 Bxd6 26.a5 Nd7 27.Rd1 with an edge for White thanks to his queenside pawn-roller.

(b222) 23...Qc7 (Kantsler-Kuporosov, Sochi 1979) 24.Rxd6 (recommended by Luther) 24...Qxd6 ( 24...Bxd6 25.a5 Nc4 26.a6 also favours White) 25.a5 Nd5 26.Qb7+ Nc7 27.Re4 with a slight advantage for White in a very complex position. I expect further developments in this line.

(c) 10.e5 (the idea behind this sacrifice is to lure Black's light-squared bishop away from defending the e6-square) 10...Bb7 11.Qh3 dxe5 12.Nxe6 fxe6 13.Qxe6+ Be7 (D).

Now White has a choice - line 'c3' is safest and appears to lead to a draw by perpetual check:

(c1) 14.Bxb5? axb5 15.Nxb5 Qc6 ( 15...Qb6 is another sharp line) 16.Nd6+ Kd8 17.fxe5 Kc7 18.Qxe7 Nd5! (this is stronger than the more popular alternative 18...Rxa2 19.exf6 Ra1+ 20.Kd2 Qd5+ 21.Kc3 Qa5+ 22.Kd3 Qd5+ 23.Kc3 1/2-1/2 Vitolins-Yuferov, Moscow 1972) 19.Rxd5 Qxd5 20.Rd1 Qxe5! 21.Nxb7 Qxe7 22.Bxe7 Rxa2 23.Nd6 ( 23.Kb1? Rha8 24.Ba3 R2xa3 25.bxa3 Rb8 and Black emerges with an extra piece, Stojic-Motoc, Queenstown 2009; 23.Rd3 Re8 with a clear plus for Black) 23...Ra1+ 24.Kd2 Rxd1+ 25.Kxd1 Kc6 with an endgame advantage for Black.

(c2) 14.Nxb5 axb5 15.Bxb5 Be4! 16.c3 ( 16.Rd2? Kf8 17.Bc4 Bg6 favours Black, Kamsky-Gelfand, Linares 1993) 16...O-O-O 17.Rhe1 (White completes his development with tempo; less effective is 17.Qxe7? Qb6 18.Qb4 Qe3+ 19.Rd2 Nb8 ( 19...h6?? 20.Ba6+ 1-0 Van der Wiel-Gutman, Amsterdam 1984) 20.Qc4+ (Pinasco-Jabari, e-mail 1999) 20...Bc6! with a clear plus for Black) 17...Ba3 18.Qa6+ Bb7 19.Qxa3 e4 (1/2-1/2 Foenander-Pilalis, ICCF corr. 2007) 20.Kb1 with a sharp but balanced position.

(c3) 14.Bxf6 gxf6 (not 14...Nxf6? 15.Bxb5+ Kf8 16.fxe5 axb5 ( 16...Bc8 17.Qc6 wins for White) 17.exf6 Bxf6 18.Rd7 Qf4+ 19.Kb1 Bxc3 20.Qe7+ 1-0 Matsuura-Rocha, Brazil 2002; White has a decisive material advantage after 20...Kg8 21.bxc3 Qf6 22.Qxf6 gxf6 23.Rxb7 ) 15.Be2 (White threatens a fatal check on h5) 15...h5 16.Nd5 Bxd5 17.Rxd5 Nb6 (another way is 17...Nc5 18.Qf5 Qc6 19.Qg6+ Kf8 20.Rhd1 Qe8 21.Qf5 Qc8 22.Qg6 Qe6 23.Bxh5 Qg8 24.Rd8+ Rxd8 and a draw was agreed in Van der Wiel-Kasparov, Amsterdam 1991 in view of 25.Rxd8+ Bxd8 26.Qe8+ Kg7 27.Qg6+ Kf8 with a repetition) 18.Bxh5+ Rxh5 19.Qg8+ Bf8 20.Qe6+ 1/2-1/2 Capelan-Polugaevsky, Solingen 1974; many later games followed this path.

10...Bb7 11.Rhe1 Qb6 (D)

The queen moves a second time in order to dislodge White's centralized knight. Black has one last opportunity to transpose to a branch of the Main Line by 11...Be7, a variation discussed in the note to Black's 10th move in Game 22.

12.Nd5

The knight leap is White's most aggressive choice.

Alternatives:

(a) 12.Nxe6? fxe6 13.Qh3 e5 14.Nd5 Bxd5 15.exd5 O-O-O 16.Bf5 Kc7 left White with insufficient compensation for the piece in Geller-Polugaevsky, Portoroz Interzonal Playoff 1973; this was the stem game for 11...Qb6 .

(b) 12.Nb3 is the subject of Game 16.

12...Qxd4

This has generally been considered to be Black's only reasonable move, but line V below is worthy of attention:

(a) 12...Nxd5?? 13.exd5 Nc5 ( 13...Qxd4 14.Rxe6+ fxe6 15.Qh5+ mates) 14.Nxe6 fxe6 15.Qh5+ with a crushing attack.

(b) 12...Bxd5?? 13.exd5 e5 ( 13...Qxd4 14.dxe6 is winning for White) 14.Nc6 and Black is pulverized along the e-file.

(c) 12...exd5 13.Nc6! dxe4! (Swedish IM Nils Grandelius introduced this improvement over the older 13...Bxc6? 14.exd5+ Be7 15.dxc6 Nc5 16.Bxf6 ( 16.Bf5!? Ra7 17.Bxf6 gxf6 18.Rxd6 was also good enough in Rizzitano-Lindsay, Boston 1981) 16...gxf6 17.Bf5 Qc7 (Chiburdanidze-Dvoirys, Tallinn 1980) 18.Re3! intending Rde1 with a crushing attack) 14.Bxe4 Nc5 15.Bxf6 gxf6 16.Qh3 Ne6 17.Bg6! Kd7 18.Bxf7 Kxc6 19.Qxe6 Qd8 20.Qe3 with reasonable compensation for the piece in view of Black's vulnerable king, Maslak-Grandelius, Olomouc 2009.

13.Bxf6 gxf6 14.Bxb5 Qc5 (D)

15.Nxf6+

White can win the black queen in exchange for three minor pieces with 15.b4 Qxb5 16.Nc7+ Ke7 17.Nxb5 axb5 . Now:

(a) 18.Qh5 Rxa2 19.Qxb5 Bh6!! and then:

(a1) 20.e5?! (Shabalov-Gelfand, Bermuda 2004) 20...Rc8! (Golubev) 21.exd6+ Kd8 22.Qd3 Rcxc2+ 23.Qxc2 Bxf4+ and "Black is close to a win" - Gelfand.

(a2) 20.Kb1 Rha8 21.Qxb7 Bxf4 22.c3 Be5!? ( 22...R2a7 23.Qb5 Ne5 is equal) 23.Qc6 R8a3 24.Qc4 Rxg2 25.Re2 Rxc3 "with full compensation for the queen" - Gelfand.

(b) 18.Qd3! Ra6 19.Qxb5 Rb6 20.Qd3 Bh6 21.g3 (D) and here:

(b1) 21...e5 (Jerez Peiez-Czakon, Sitges 2008) 22.a4! exf4 23.Qd2! with a clear plus for White as he has mobilized his queenside pawns.

(b2) 21...Ba6 was first played in Mnatsakanian-Kr.Georgiev, Erevan 1982. Now White should play 22.Qd2! with a clear plus.

(b3) 21...Ra8 22.a3 e5 (Nataf-Lalic, Salou 2004) 23.Qe3! Rba6 24.Rd3 with a clear plus for White as he has successfully defended his pawns and prevented Black from activating his minor pieces. I expect further developments in this line.

15...Kd8 16.Nxd7 Qxb5 17.Nxf8 Rxf8 18.Qa3 Ke8

The king move is more flexible than 18...Rc8? 19.Qxd6+ Ke8 20.Re3! Qc6 21.Qd2 Ke7 22.Qb4+ Kf6 23.c3! ( 23.Qd4+ Ke7 24.Qb4+ repeats the position, but White can play for more) 23...Qc5 24.e5+ Kf5 25.g4+ Kg6 26.f5+ Kg7 27.Qf4 Kh8 28.Qh6 (intending Rh3) 28...Bg2 29.Rd7! with a strong attack, Goh-Chan, Singapore Ch 2006.

19.Qxd6 Qc6 (D)

19...Rc8? transposes to the previous note.

20.Qd4

The white queen retains access to a4. 20.Qd2 Qc7 21.g3 ( 21.f5 Rd8 22.Qb4 Rxd1+ 23.Rxd1 Rg8 is equal, Georgiadis-Perunovic, European Ch, Budva 2009) 21...f6 22.e5 f5 23.Qd6 Qxd6 24.Rxd6 Ke7 25.Red1 Bd5 26.b3 Rfc8 was roughly equal in Luther-Sashikiran, Moscow 2007; Black went on to win an instructive endgame.

20...Qc7 21.f5 Rg8 22.g3

White keeps the black rook at bay. 22.Qa4+ Bc6! (this is more ambitious than 22...Qc6 23.Qd4 Qc7 with a repetition) 23.Qc4 Rxg2 24.fxe6 Rc8 25.exf7+ Qxf7 is fine for Black as all his pieces are active.

22...Rc8 23.Rd2 (D)

23...e5

Black should contest the open d-file with 23...Rd8! 24.Qa4+ Qc6 25.Qa5 Rxd2 26.Qxd2 e5 27.Rd1 Qc7 (Black has a slight advantage according to Berg) 28.Qe2 ( 28.Qe3 Rg4!? ) 28...f6 (after 28...Ke7?! 29.Qh5 White's queen becomes active) 29.Rd3 Rg7 and the black bishop is superior to the white pawns.

24.Qa4+ Kf8

Also possible is 24...Qc6 25.Qb4 f6 26.c3 Qb5 27.Qd6 Qc6 28.Qb4 with a repetition - Berg.

25.Rd7 Qc6 26.Qb4+ Kg7 27.Qd2

The black king is relatively safe in the corner after 27.f6+ Kh8 .

27...Kh8 28.c3

White's king is too vulnerable for 28.Rxf7 Rgd8 29.Qe2 ( 29.Qc3? Qd6! 30.Qe3 Bxe4! is winning for Black) 29...Qh6+ 30.Qe3 Bxe4! 31.c3 ( 31.Qxh6?? Rxc2+ 32.Kb1 Re2+ mates) 31...Qh5 32.Rf6 Qxh2 33.Qxe4 Qd2+ 34.Kb1 Rb8 35.b3 Qxc3 36.Rc6 Rxb3+ 37.axb3 Qxb3+ , leading to a draw.

28...f6 29.Rd1?!

A safer line is 29.Rd6 Qa4 30.Qh6 Rcf8 (both 30...Rxc3+? 31.Kb1 Bxe4+ 32.Ka1 and 30...Qxa2? 31.Qxf6+ Rg7 32.Rb6! favour White) 31.Rxf6 Bxe4 32.Rxe4 Qxe4 33.Rxf8 Qe1+ 34.Kc2 Qe2+ 35.Kb3 Qb5+ 36.Kc2 ( 36.Ka3?? Qc5+ costs White the f8-rook) 36...Qe2+ with a draw by repetition.

29...Rg7

Berg recommends 29...Rc7! 30.Rd8 Rf7 31.Qh6 Qc7 with advantage to Black.

30.Rd6 (D)

30...Qa4??

The queen is too far astray here.

Alternatives:

(a) 30...Qxe4 31.Rxf6 Qg4 32.Qd6 Be4 with roughly equal chances in a complex middlegame.

(b) 30...Qc7 31.Rxf6 Bxe4 32.Qe3 Rd8! and now:

(b1) 33.Re1? Qd7! (now Black switches over to the attack) 34.b3 (not 34.Qxe4? Qd2+ 35.Kb1 Rb7! 36.b3 Rdb8 and White must sacrifice a rook to prevent mate) 34...Bxf5 with an advantage as the black bishop is superior to the white pawns.

(b2) 33.Rxd8+ Qxd8 34.Rxa6 Bxf5 with roughly equal chances.

31.Rd8+ Rg8 32.Qh6! Rxc3+

Black's bishop is lost after 32...Rgxd8 33.Qxf6+ Kg8 34.Rxd8+ Rxd8 35.Qxd8+ Kf7 36.Qc7+ Kf6 37.Qxb7 and White wins - Berg.

33.Kb1!

White's king glides to safety along the back rank. The rook is poisoned: 33.bxc3?? Qa3+ 34.Kc2 Qxa2+ 35.Kc1 Qa3+ 36.Kc2 Bxe4+ 37.R1d3 Bxd3+ 38.Rxd3 Qa4+! ( 38...Qe7?! 39.Qd2 gives White counterplay) 39.Kd2 Qc6 with an advantage for Black because of his passed pawns and more secure king position.

33...Bxe4+ 34.Ka1 Qxd1+

Black's rook and bishop are no match for the white queen, but the black king is unable to fend for himself after 34...Rc1+ 35.Rxc1 Rxd8 36.Qxf6+ Kg8 37.Qxd8+ with a mating attack.

35.Rxd1 Rc6 36.Qh5 Rgc8 37.b3 R6c7 38.Qh6

More precise is 38 Qh4!, but the game continuation is good enough.

38...Rf7 39.Rd6 Rcf8 40.g4 Bb7 41.h4 e4 42.g5 Re8 43.Rxf6 1-0

An entertaining struggle typical of the back-and-forth tactics in this line.

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Nbd7 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.O-O-O b5 10.Bd3 Bb7 11.Rhe1 Qb6

For comments on the moves up to this point, see Game 15.

12.Nb3 (D)

The alternative 12.Nd5 was covered in Game 15.

12...Rc8

Anand seizes the half-open c-file. Shirov had previous experience facing the main alternative 12...b4 13.Nb1 ( 13.Na4 Qc7 14.Nd4 is another sharp line) 13...Be7 14.N1d2 Qc7 15.Qh3! e5 ( 15...Rc8 16.Kb1 e5 17.Nc4 O-O with equality, E.Berg-K.Szabo, Rilton Cup, Stockholm 2007/8) 16.Nc4 with a slight advantage for White, Shirov-Kariakin, FIDE World Cup, Khanty-Mansiisk 2007.

13.Qh3 (D)

Similar positions arise after 13.Kb1 Rxc3!? 14.bxc3 Qc7 15.Bf1 ( 15.Qh3 transposes to the main game) 15...Be7 and Black has adequate compensation for the exchange in view of White's weakened queenside, Timman-Hillarp Persson, Malmo 2008.

13...Rxc3!?

Black sacrifices the exchange in order to weaken White's queenside and eliminate any attacking ideas involving a knight sacrifice on d5. Another idea is 13...b4 14.Nb1 ( 14.Nd5!? ) 14...a5 with sharp play, Belov-Efimenko, European Ch, Plovdiv 2008.

14.bxc3 Qc7

The queen move restrains White's e5 pawn-break and vacates b6 in preparation for the possible manoeuvre ...Nb6-a4.

15.Kb1 Be7 16.e5!? (D)

Shirov opens lines for his rooks in accordance with his preference for active play.

16...dxe5

This is the critical moment.

17.f5?!

White should recapture the pawn with 17.fxe5! Nd5 18.Bxe7 Kxe7 19.Rd2 Nxc3+ 20.Kb2 Nxe5 (this was given by Anand without evaluation in his Informator 102 notes) 21.Qh4+!? Ke8 (the only move as 21...f6?? 22.Qb4+ picks off the knight) 22.Qb4 ( 22.Qh5!? ) 22...Na4+ 23.Ka1 Nc6 with sharp play.

17...Nd5 18.Bxe7

The exchange of dark-squared bishops pulls the black king into the centre to defend the e6-pawn.

18...Kxe7 19.fxe6 (D)

19...fxe6?!

Black should prefer the direct 19...Nxc3+! 20.Kc1 ( 20.Ka1 Nxd1! 21.Rxd1 ( 21.exd7?? Qc3+ mates) 21...fxe6 with a clear plus for Black because he has retained his extra pawns) 20...Nf6 ( 20...Nxd1? loses to 21.exd7 Nc3 22.Qg3! ) 21.Rd2 e4 22.exf7 Qe5 ( 22...Kxf7?! 23.Bc4+! Qxc4 24.Rd7+ Nxd7 25.Qxd7+ Kg6 26.Qd6+ Kf7 27.Qd7+ with a draw by repetition - Anand) 23.Be2 Nxa2+ 24.Kb1 Nc3+ 25.Kc1 Bd5 with a clear plus for Black - Anand.

20.Qg3 g6 21.Rd2 Rc8 22.Qg5+

The white queen retains more options after 22.Qh4+ N7f6 ( 22...Ke8? loses to 23.Qxh7 ) 23.Rf2 e4 24.Bxe4 Nxc3+ 25.Kc1 Qe5 26.Rxf6 Nxa2+ 27.Kd2 ( 27.Kb1 Nc3+ 28.Kc1 Na2+ also repeats) 27...Rd8+ 28.Bd3 Qc3+ 29.Ke3 Qe5+ with a repetition - Anand.

22...Ke8 (D)

23.Qg4?

The queen move is too slow as Black is able to defend his vulnerable e6-pawn.

Alternatives:

(a) 23.Bxg6+? hxg6 24.Qxg6+ Ke7 25.Qg7+ Kd6 26.Rd3 Qd8 27.Nd2 Qh8 repels the attack.

(b) 23.Qh6! N7f6 24.Kc1 (given as unclear by Anand) 24...Qxc3!? (White hangs on after 24...Qe7 25.Rxe5 ) 25.Bxg6+ hxg6 26.Qxg6+ Kd7 27.Qf7+ Kc6 28.Qxe6+ Kc7 29.Qxe5+ ( 29.Rxe5!? ) 29...Qxe5 30.Rxe5 Kd6 31.Re1 Rc3 with roughly equal chances.

23...Nxc3+ 24.Ka1 Bd5

Black's light-squared bishop defends the e6-pawn, blocks the d-file and takes aim at the white king. White has difficulty obtaining counterplay because he is unable to generate any threats with his rooks.

25.Re3 Nf6 26.Qh4 Qe7 27.Bf1 Bxb3 28.cxb3 Nce4!

Black is able to generate decisive threats thanks to the weakness of White's back rank.

29.Rb2

No relief is offered by 29.Rxe4 Qa3 30.Bd3 ( 30.Rb2 Rc1+ 31.Rb1 Rc2 mates) 30...Nxe4 31.Qxe4 Qc1+ 32.Bb1 Qxd2 and Black has a decisive material advantage.

29...Rc1+ 30.Rb1 Qc5 0-1

An instructive game by Anand as the exchange sacrifice worked brilliantly. Shirov was unable to create any significant counterplay and his rooks were helpless bystanders throughout the entire game.
