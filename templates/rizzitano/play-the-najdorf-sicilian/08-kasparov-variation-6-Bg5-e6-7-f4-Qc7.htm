8 Kasparov Variation: 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Qc7

The variations arising from 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Qc7 have not been analysed in as much detail as the more popular Poisoned Pawn or the Main Line, so there is still plenty of opportunity for independent analysis. The move 7...Qc7 was the choice of a young Garry Kasparov; he played several interesting games in this line early in his career before moving on to prefer the Poisoned Pawn Variation. One of the ideas behind 7...Qc7 is to meet White's typical plan involving Qf3 and e5 with ...b5 and ...Bb7; Black is also well-positioned to play ...b4 before White is able to respond with the thematic Nd5 piece sacrifice. Several of these lines can transpose into the Gelfand Variation or the Main Line, so it is beneficial to be familiar with these lines also.


Game 17 (Anand-Kramnik) features Kramnik's first outing with 7...Qc7 ; this was a must-win game for the 14th World Champion. Anand plays the positional line 8.Bxf6 to weaken Black's pawn-structure, and after 8...gxf6 9.f5 Kramnik chooses 9...Qc5 instead of the more usual 9...Nc6 . Black's attempt to unbalance the position with the double-edged novelty 12...exf5!? is met by the precise 13.Qe3!, seizing two critical diagonals and creating some tactical possibilities involving the weakened d5-square. Kramnik goes astray with 17...f5? instead of the more accurate 17...Be6!, which allows Anand to defuse the tension and force an exchange of queens into a slightly advantageous endgame. The players agree to a draw and Anand retains his world championship title by the score of 6½ points to 4½ points. This was a relatively short but interesting encounter because of Kramnik's opening novelty and the tense competitive situation.


In Game 18 (Stellwagen-Hillarp Persson), White chooses the sharp 8.Qf3 . Black plays the timely 11...h5! to grab some space on the kingside before White can clamp down with Nh5. The middlegame is a typical Sicilian slugfest: Stellwagen wins a pawn on the kingside at the cost of tying up his queen and knight along the h-file, while Hillarp Persson strikes out on the queenside with 20...a5!? to loosen up White's king position. The tension culminates in an exciting combination by Black to create mating threats, but White is able to counter precisely and force a draw by repetition. The main theme of this game is the importance of piece activity: Black sacrificed his h-pawn, but in return he was able to draw two of White's pieces out of play and create strong attacking threats on the queenside.

Game 17 [B96]
Viswanathan Anand - Vladimir Kramnik
World Ch match (game 11), Bonn 2008

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Qc7 (D)

The queen moves out of the pin, restrains White's e5 pawn advance and prepares ...b5 and ...Bb7. Kramnik was trailing in the World Championship match by the score of 6 points to 4 points with two games left to play, so in this must-win situation his usual Petroff or Berlin Ruy Lopez would not be suitable choices - critical games demand the Najdorf!

8.Bxf6

White weakens Black's pawn-structure but concedes the bishop-pair; now it will be difficult for Black to castle kingside. Alternatives:

(a) 8.Qf3 is the subject of Game 18.

(b) 8.f5!? (White increases the pressure on e6 at the cost of conceding the e5-square to Black's pieces) 8...Be7 9.fxe6 fxe6 10.Qd3 Nc6 (also reasonable is 10...Ng4!? 11.Bxe7 Qxe7 12.Nf3 Nc6 13.Qd2 O-O and "Black has a good game thanks to his control over e5" - Arizmendi and Moreno) 11.O-O-O Bd7 12.Be2 (Nataf-Bruzon, Capablanca Memorial, Havana 2002) 12...Ne5!? 13.Qh3 O-O 14.Rhf1 Rae8 with a solid position for Black - Arizmendi and Moreno.

(c) 8.Bd3 b5! 9.Qe2 b4! (Black drives the knight away from the centre before White has the possibility of sacrificing the piece on d5) 10.Nd1 Nbd7 11.Nf2 Bb7 12.O-O-O Be7 13.Rhe1 h6! 14.Bh4 (Nisipeanu-Istratescu, World Junior Ch, Mamaia 1991) 14...g5! 15.fxg5 hxg5 16.Bxg5 Qa5 17.Bxf6 Bxf6 "with excellent compensation for the pawn" - Emms.

(d) 8.Qe2 Nc6! (Black exploits the momentary lack of coordination between the white pieces by challenging White's central knight) 9.O-O-O Nxd4 10.Rxd4 Be7 (D).

Now:

(d1) 11.e5?! dxe5 12.fxe5 Nd5 13.Bd2 ( 13.Bxe7?! Nxe7 ( 13...Nxc3!? is possibly even better, contrary to Kasparov's analysis) 14.Ne4 O-O left White with a vulnerable e5-pawn in Ivanchuk-Kasparov, Tilburg 1989) 13...Nxc3 14.Bxc3 b5 with equal chances.

(d2) 11.g3 Bd7! 12.Bg2 h6 13.Bh4 Bc6 was fine for Black in Ljubojevic-Kasparov, Belgrade 1989. This game is annotated by Igor Stohl in Garry Kasparov's Greatest Chess Games, Volume 1.

(d3) 11.g4!? h6 12.Bh4 g5 13.e5!? dxe5 14.Rc4 Qb8 15.fxg5 hxg5 16.Bg3 Nd7 with sharp play, Naiditsch-Swiercz, European Ch, Budva 2009.

8...gxf6 (D)

9.f5

This is the logical follow-up to increase the pressure on Black's centre.

Alternatives:

(a) 9.Qh5?! (the queen excursion is popular but dubious, as now Black is able to force a favourable exchange of queens) 9...Qc5! 10.Qxc5 dxc5 11.Nb3 Bd6 12.g3 Ke7 13.Bg2 Ra7! 14.O-O-O Rd8 15.a4 b6 with a comfortable position for Black as he will follow up with ...Rad7 and ...Bc7, Kamsky-Svidler, FIDE Grand Prix, Baku 2008.

(b) 9.Qd2 Nc6 (Black's kingside pawn-structure has been compromised, so he focuses on preparing to castle queenside; this transposes into a solid line of the Richter-Rauzer Attack (ECO code B67) and is safer than the popular alternative 9...b5 ) 10.O-O-O Bd7 11.Kb1 O-O-O and then:

(b1) 12.Bc4 Kb8 13.Nxc6+ Qxc6 14.Bb3 h5 15.Rhf1 Qc5 16.Qd3 (Topalov-Anand, Dortmund 1997) 16...Be7 17.f5 Rdg8 18.Qh3 Rg4! was recommended by Leko to keep an eye on the e4-pawn; the chances are equal.

(b2) 12.Be2 h5 13.Rhf1 Kb8 14.Rf3 Nxd4 (another idea is 14...h4 with equal chances, Manik-Stocek, Slovakian Team Ch 2007/8) 15.Qxd4 Be7 16.Qd2 Rdg8 looks reasonable for Black, as suggested by Arizmendi and Moreno.

We now return to 9.f5 (D):

9...Qc5

Black has a solid alternative in 9...Nc6 10.fxe6 fxe6 11.Bc4 Nxd4 12.Qxd4 Rg8 13.O-O-O Bd7 14.Bb3 , and now:

(a) 14...O-O-O 15.g3 Be7 16.Na4 Kb8 (after 16...Rg5?! 17.Rd3 White has a slight advantage, Radjabov-Grishchuk, FIDE Grand Prix, Sochi 2008) 17.Nb6 Rg4 is equal.

(b) 14...Rxg2! 15.Qxf6 Qd8 16.Qd4 Qg5+ 17.Kb1 Qc5 18.Qh8 O-O-O 19.Qxh7 Bg7 20.Qh3 Rg6 and Black's bishop-pair provides adequate compensation for the pawn, Smirnov-Nayer, Russian Ch, Novokuznetsk 2008.

10.Qd3

The queen development prepares for queenside castling and covers the sensitive e3-square so that the d4-knight can retreat.

10...Nc6 11.Nb3 Qe5 12.O-O-O (D)

12...exf5!?

Kramnik introduces a new move, but such a double-edged idea is not to everyone's taste since it concedes the d5-square to White's pieces. A more typical plan would be for Black to maintain his control over the central squares via 12...Bd7 13.Nd2 b5 with a balanced position.

13.Qe3!

Anand seizes some dark squares and clears d5 for his rook. The queen move is more incisive than 13.Nd5 Bh6+ 14.Kb1 Kf8 with sharp play: Black's king has been displaced, but his bishop-pair could become powerful.

13...Bg7

Kramnik defends the f6-pawn as 13...fxe4 14.Rd5 Qe6 15.Nd4 Nxd4 16.Qxd4 Bd7 17.Nxe4 gives White a dangerous lead in development.

14.Rd5 Qe7 15.Qg3 (D)

15...Rg8

The alternatives are unappetizing:

(a) 15...O-O 16.exf5 Re8 17.Bd3 Nb4 18.Rd4 Nxd3+ 19.Qxd3 and White has a clear plus thanks to his grip on d5.

(b) 15...Bh6+ 16.Kb1 f4 17.Qh4 Bg5 18.Qf2 f5 19.Bd3 favours White thanks to his superior development.

16.Qf4 (D)

Anand keeps up the pressure and avoids simplification. It is too early to recapture material with 16.Qxd6? fxe4 17.Qxe7+ Nxe7 18.Rh5 f5 19.Rxh7 Be6 20.Nc5 O-O-O , when Black has an edge thanks to his passed e4-pawn.

16...fxe4

Another idea is 16...Be6 17.Rd1 ( 17.exf5?? Bxd5 18.Nxd5 Qe1# ) 17...fxe4 ( 17...O-O-O 18.exf5 Bd7 19.Bd3 with a slight advantage for White due to his control over e4 and d5) 18.Nxe4 Bg4! 19.Rd3 ( 19.Nxd6+?! Kf8 20.Bc4 Bxd1 21.Rxd1 Qe5 is fine for Black) 19...Kf8 20.h3 Be6 and Black can maintain the balance.

17.Nxe4 f5?

Now Black is left struggling for a draw. There was still time to transpose to the note to Black's 16th move by 17...Be6! , as White has nothing better than 18.Rd1 .

18.Nxd6+ Kf8 19.Nxc8 Rxc8 20.Kb1 Qe1+ 21.Nc1 Ne7 22.Qd2!

Black is compelled to exchange queens because of the mating threat on d8.

22...Qxd2 23.Rxd2 Bh6 24.Rf2 Be3 1/2-1/2

Kramnik made Anand a draw offer he could not refuse. Although White has a slight advantage after 25 Rf3, the draw clinched victory in the world championship match for Anand.

Game 18 [B96]
Daniel Stellwagen - Tiger Hillarp Persson
MaImo 2008

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Qc7 8.Qf3

The queen development is White's most popular choice. 8.Bxf6 is covered in Game 17, along with White's 8th-move alternatives.

8...b5 (D)

9.Bxf6

White has a wide range of options:

(a) 9.e5?! Bb7 10.Qg3 Nd5 11.Nxd5 Bxd5 12.f5 dxe5 13.fxe6 fxe6 14.O-O-O (Gorovykh-Brodsky, Peterhof 2009) 14...Nd7 leaves White with insufficient compensation for the pawn.

(b) 9.f5?! (this line has fallen out of favour) 9...b4! 10.Ncb5 (White must avoid 10.fxe6?? bxc3 11.Bxf6 cxb2 12.Rb1 gxf6 13.Qxf6 Qc3+ 14.Kf2 Bxe6 15.Qxh8 Nc6 and Black wins) 10...axb5 11.Bxb5+ Bd7 12.fxe6 Bxb5 13.Nxb5 Qc5 14.Bxf6 fxe6! 15.Nd4 gxf6 16.Nxe6 Qc4 17.Qxf6 Nd7! favours Black as White's attack has run out of steam, Molander-Moreno, Port Erin 2003. My database contains more than 30 games with this position, while Arizmendi and Moreno provide detailed coverage of this line.

(c) 9.Bd3 b4! 10.Nce2 Nbd7 11.O-O-O Bb7 12.Kb1 Be7 13.Rhe1 Nc5 gives Black easy development, Leiber-Wojtkiewicz, Bad Worishofen 1991.

(d) 9.O-O-O b4! (D) and now:

(d1) 10.e5? (this old attacking idea appears to have been refuted) 10...Bb7 11.Ncb5 axb5 12.Bxb5+ Nbd7 13.Qh3 b3! 14.Qxb3 Bd5 15.c4 Ne4 16.Qc2 Bb7 17.Rhe1 Nxg5 18.fxg5 O-O-O 19.Bxd7+ Rxd7 20.Nb5 Qc5 21.Kb1 dxe5! with a clear plus for Black, Yeo-Teran Alvarez, Campillos 2004.

(d2) 10.Nce2 Nbd7 11.g4 Bb7 12.Ng3 Rc8 13.Bd3 Be7 14.Kb1 Nc5 15.Nb3 Na4!? is equal, Becerra-Wojtkiewicz, New York rapid 2003.

(d3) After 10.Nd5!? exd5 11.Bxf6 gxf6 12.exd5 (D) Black has two defensive options:

(d31) 12...Bg7?! was recommended by Arizmendi and Moreno. The critical line is 13.Bd3 f5 14.Rhe1+ Kf8 15.Qe3 Qd8 16.Bxf5 Bb7 (G.Ginsburg-Moreno, Bundesliga 2003/4) 17.Qe4! Nd7 18.Nc6 Qf6 19.Bxd7 Qxb2+ 20.Kd2 Bc3+ 21.Ke2 Bxe1 22.Qe7+! (Arizmendi and Moreno only consider 22.Rxe1 Kg7 and Black escapes) 22...Kg8 (the point of the queen check is that 22...Kg7?? loses to 23.Qg5+ Kf8 24.Qh6+ Qg7 25.Qxd6+ ) 23.Kxe1 and White has a clear plus as Black has difficulty activating his rooks. This line could use some practical tests.

(d32) 12...Qc5 13.Bd3 Ra7 14.Bf5 Bxf5 15.Nxf5 Rc7 16.Rhe1+ Kd8 17.Qe4 Kc8 18.Rd4 a5 19.b3 Na6 20.Rc4 Qb5 with tremendous complications, as first played in Akopian-Lautier, Ubeda 1997. Again, further practical tests are needed.

9...gxf6 10.O-O-O

White has another aggressive but dubious alternative beginning with 10.e5?! d5 11.exf6 b4! (D).

Now:

(a) 12.Nce2 Nd7 13.f5 e5 14.Ne6 fxe6 15.fxe6 Nxf6! (Black returns the piece in order to extinguish White's attack) 16.Qxf6 Bg7 17.Qf5 Rf8! 18.Qh5+ Kd8 19.O-O-O ( 19.Qxh7 Bxe6 also favours Black) 19...Bxe6 20.Ng3 (Adler-Semeniuk, Vilnius 1974) 20...Kc8!? (Arizmendi and Moreno) 21.Kb1 Kb7 favours Black thanks to his bishop-pair and central control.

(b) 12.Nxd5 exd5 and then:

(b1) 13.Qxd5 Bb7 14.Bb5+ axb5 15.Nxb5 Bxd5! (this was a 2004 suggestion of Arizmendi and Moreno) 16.Nxc7+ Kd7 17.Nxd5 Kc6 18.O-O-O Rxa2! 19.Kb1 Ra5 20.Ne7+ Bxe7 21.fxe7 Re8 22.Rhe1 (Geenen-Ringoir, Eupen 2008) 22...Kc7! with an advantage for Black as the piece is stronger than the pawns.

(b2) 13.O-O-O Nd7 14.Bb5 axb5 15.Rhe1+ Kd8 16.Qxd5 Ra6 17.Qxf7 Qxf4+ 18.Kb1 Bc5! 19.Ne6+ Rxe6 20.Qxe6 Kc7 favours Black, Balabaev-Dibley, ICCF corr. 2005.

We now return to the position after 10.O-O-O (D):

10...b4 11.Nce2

11.Nd5!? exd5 transposes to note 'd3' to White's 9th move above.

11...h5!

Black mobilizes his h-pawn and grabs some space on the kingside before White can prevent the advance with Ng3.

12.Ng3 h4 13.Nh5 Nd7 (D)

14.g4

The g-pawn supports the f5 pawn advance. The alternative is to utilize the queen via 14.Qg4 Bb7 (D):

(a) 15.Ng7+? Bxg7 16.Qxg7 Ke7! (Black's king is safe here; now the threat is ...Rag8, trapping the white queen) 17.Qg4 Bxe4 and Black has won a pawn, Bobras-Moranda, Polish Ch, Lublin 2008.

(b) 15.Qxh4 O-O-O 16.f5 e5!? (another option is 16...Nc5 with reasonable compensation for the pawn - Emms; the line-up of white pieces along the h-file does not inspire confidence) 17.Nb3 d5 18.exd5 Nb6 19.Be2 Nxd5 20.Bf3 a5! gives Black a very promising attack - Arizmendi and Moreno.

(c) 15.Nxe6 fxe6 16.Qxe6+ Be7 17.Ng7+ (after 17.e5? fxe5 18.fxe5 , as in Volokitin-Papaioannou, European Ch, Plovdiv 2008, Black should play 18...d5! 19.Ng7+ Kd8 20.Rxd5 Qc6! with a clear plus as he has repelled the attack) 17...Kd8 18.Bc4 and White has sufficient compensation for the piece. I expect further developments in this line.

14...Bb7 15.Bg2 (D)

Another idea is 15.Bh3 O-O-O ( 15...Rc8!? ) 16.Rhe1 Kb8 17.Kb1 Be7 with sharp play, Shirov-Zdebskaya, Warsaw rapid 2008.

15...O-O-O 16.Kb1

White moves his king to safety in anticipation of a black build-up along the half-open c-file.

16...Nc5 17.Rhe1

The f6-pawn is poisoned after 17.Nxf6? h3! 18.Bf1 Bg7 and Black wins material.

17...Be7 18.Qh3 Kb8 19.f5 e5 20.Nb3 (D)

20...a5!?

Hillarp Persson uses the a-pawn as a battering-ram to weaken the dark squares around the white king; this is a typical motif to exploit White's missing dark-squared bishop. Black has also tried 20...Rc8 21.Nxc5 Qxc5 22.Rd2 a5 with sharp play, Kanarek-Swiercz, Polish Ch, Chotowa 2009.

21.Qxh4 a4 22.Nc1 a3 23.b3 Nxe4! (D)

A nice combination to create mating threats on b2.

24.Rxe4!

Stellwagen avoids 24.Bxe4? Qc3 25.Nd3 d5 26.Bf3 Bc5 (with the idea of ...Bd4) 27.Kc1 Rc8 (threatening ...Be3+) 28.Nxc5 Rxc5 29.Re2 Ba6 and Black wins. Note how out of play White's queen and knight are over on the h-file.

24...Bxe4 25.Bxe4 Qc3 (D)

26.Nd3 d5 27.Qf2!

The white queen returns in the nick of time to exploit Black's exposed king.

27...dxe4 28.Qb6+ Ka8 (D)

The black king cannot escape the checks as 28...Kc8 29.Qa6+ Kc7 30.Qa5+ Kd7 31.Qb5+ is also a draw by repetition.

29.Qa6+ Kb8 30.Qb6+ Ka8 31.Qa6+ 1/2-1/2

A well-played game demonstrating the exciting tactics in this line.
