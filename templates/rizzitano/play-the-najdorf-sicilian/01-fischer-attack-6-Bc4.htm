1 Fischer Attack: 6.Bc4

Our coverage begins with one of White's most straightforward attacking lines versus the Najdorf Sicilian. The 11th World Champion Bobby Fischer began playing his namesake line in his early teens; he also defended against 6.Bc4 many times with the black pieces. After the usual moves 6...e6 7.Bb3 Black's traditional continuation has been 7...b5 (Game 2), but during the late 1980s the modern alternative 7...Nbd7 (Game 1) began gaining in popularity, culminating in its use by the 13th World Champion Garry Kasparov during three games of his 1993 PCA World Championship match versus English grandmaster Nigel Short. The position after 6.Bc4 occurred during eight of these Short-Kasparov games, and a close examination of these battles would be beneficial for anyone playing these lines.

The Fischer Attack tends to crystallize into two main ideas: White's attempts to soften up Black's defences along the a2-g8 diagonal versus Black's attempts to prove that White's light-squared bishop is a juicy target for Black's natural queenside expansion with ...b5, sometimes in combination with ...Nbd7-c5. Black's theoretical prospects in the Fischer Attack are quite healthy and to date he has been able to neutralize White's various attacking schemes. During the past decade the line has waned somewhat in popularity, but enterprising players including Ivanchuk, Morozevich, Rublevsky, Volokitin, Nisipeanu and Mueller have been injecting the Fischer Attack with new ideas that continually challenge Najdorf players.

Game 1 (Sigfusson-de Firmian) features the modern 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 Nbd7 . Sigfusson increases the pressure on Black's centre by playing the f4-f5 pawn advance in conjunction with Qf3. de Firmian releases the central tension and stabilizes the central pawn-structure by employing a precise sequence of moves:

• Attack White's d4-knight with ...e5 to drive it away from the centre.

• Exchange White's light-squared bishop with ...Nxb3.

• Attack White's c3-knight with ...b4 so that after the further moves Nd5 Nxd5, exd5 White has occupied the d5-square with a pawn and there is no longer a 'hole' on d5.

This is a typical idea for Black in the Sicilian Defence, but de Firmian is able to introduce it in a new position. The middlegame features very energetic play by Sigfusson to swing his queen's rook to the kingside. Both players go astray in the sharp tactics that follow, and this exciting game ends in perpetual check.


In Game 2 (Ivanchuk-Topalov), Black plays the traditional 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 b5 . After 8.O-O Be7 White selects the piece-based attack beginning with 9.Qf3 and Black counters with 9...Qb6 . Ivanchuk introduces a new idea 15.Kh1!? to remove his king from a potentially dangerous diagonal and he follows up by prising open the a-file and obtaining strong queenside pressure. Topalov defends well, but Ivanchuk misses a couple of opportunities to increase his advantage. Late in the middlegame, Black appears to be under pressure, but an instructive tactical resource enables him to escape from difficulty and the game peters out into a draw. Ivanchuk has introduced another interesting new idea in the Fischer Attack: the note to White's 8th move in this game includes coverage of the alternative development scheme 8.Bg5 Be7 9.Qf3 Qc7 10.e5!? Bb7 11.exd6 Bxd6 12.Qe3 Bc5 13.O-O-O Nc6 and now instead of the traditional capture 14.Bxf6 , Ivanchuk introduced the fascinating queen sacrifice 14.Qxe6+!? fxe6 15.Nxe6 in a rapid game versus Sergei Kariakin. This creative sacrifice is a good example of the opportunities for introducing opening novelties even in well-known positions.

Game 1 [B86]
Sigurdur Sigfusson - Nick de Firmian
Copenhagen 2005

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4

White's light-squared bishop occupies the powerful a2-g8 diagonal and takes aim at the potentially vulnerable e6- and f7-squares. The drawback of this aggressive move is that the bishop may be vulnerable to harassment from Black's minor pieces and pawns; the natural queenside advance ...b5 will now come with gain of tempo.

6...e6 (D)

Black contests the d5-square and shortens the range of the c4-bishop.

7.Bb3

White anticipates the ...b5 pawn advance and waits for Black to commit himself to a particular development scheme. Alternatives:

(a) With 7.a3 , White expends a tempo to prepare a secure retreat for his light-squared bishop, but the loss of time allows Black to mobilize his queenside pieces more quickly. 7...Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.Ba2 b5 10.f4 Bb7 11.f5 e5 12.Nde2 (White appears to have good prospects because of the potential weakness of the d5-square, but Black's position is more than satisfactory thanks to his smooth development and safe king position) 12...Nbd7 13.Ng3 Rc8! 14.Bg5 ( 14.Be3 Nb6 15.Bxb6 Qxb6+ 16.Kh1 Qe3! favoured Black because of his dark-square dominance in Robatsch-Fischer, Capablanca Memorial, Havana 1965) 14...Rxc3! (this thematic exchange sacrifice is the reason why Black refrained from playing an early ...Qc7) 15.bxc3 Nxe4 16.Bxe7 Qxe7 17.Nxe4 Bxe4 gave Black good compensation for the exchange in Ciric-Gligoric, Novi Sad 1965; this evaluation has been confirmed by many subsequent games.

(b) 7.O-O (D).

White employs a cunning move-order which is quite effective against players with only ...Nbd7 lines in their repertoire; now Black must be careful not to respond in mechanical fashion. 7...b5 8.Bb3 Be7 and 7...Be7 8.Bb3 b5 both transpose to Game 2, while 7...Nc6 switches to a line of the Sozin Attack (ECO code B88); note that White cannot play the Velimirovic Attack now that he has committed his king to the kingside. Other tries cannot be recommended:

(b1) 7...Nxe4? (Black is too far behind in development to exploit White's 'omission' of Bb3) 8.Nxe4 d5 9.Bg5! Be7 10.Bxe7 Qxe7 11.Bxd5 exd5 12.Nc3 gives White a crushing lead in development, Arakhamia-Tazhieva, USSR Women's Ch, Podolsk 1990.

(b2) 7...Nbd7? 8.Re1! (White is able to profit from delaying Bb3; the piece sacrifice 8.Bxe6!? fxe6 9.Nxe6 is also dangerous) 8...Ne5 ( 8...Be7? 9.Bxe6! fxe6 10.Nxe6 gives White a devastating attack as he will pocket a third pawn for the sacrificed piece) 9.Bb3 (the bishop is compelled to retreat, but now White will be able to oust the black knight with gain of tempo) 9...Be7 10.f4 Nc6 11.Nxc6 bxc6 12.Qf3 favours White thanks to his lead in development, Zapata-R.Mateo, Decameron 2003.

7...Nbd7

This flexible knight development is characteristic of many Najdorf lines; in the Fischer Attack, the knight typically continues on its journey to the c5-square, where it defends the e6-pawn, exerts pressure on White's e4-pawn and also gives Black the option of exchanging off White's potentially dangerous light-squared bishop.

One important concept to be aware of when learning the Najdorf is to understand the various functions that Black's queen's knight can perform depending upon White's chosen piece and pawn configuration; it can remain on d7 to restrain an e5 pawn advance and bolster the f6-knight, or it can sometimes move to e5 or b6. We shall be exploring these various development schemes throughout the book.

The pawn advance 7...b5 is covered in Game 2; the alternative knight development 7...Nc6 is another transposition to the Sozin Attack (with White retaining the option of castling queenside).

8.f4

White prepares to soften up the a2-g8 diagonal.

8...Nc5 (D)

Black follows through on his plan of attacking the e4-pawn. Another important theme in this line is to determine if and when the e4-pawn can be safely captured without opening too many lines for White's pieces.

9.f5

The f-pawn advance is the most direct approach. Alternatives:

(a) 9.O-O Be7 ( 9...Nfxe4 10.Nxe4 Nxe4 11.f5 has been played in several top-level games including Topalov-Kasparov and Topalov-Short, Euwe Memorial, Amsterdam 1996; experience has shown that White has sufficient compensation for the pawn) 10.e5 ( 10.Qf3 O-O 11.Be3 Qc7 is another sharp line with balanced chances) 10...dxe5 11.fxe5 Nfd7 12.Qh5 Nf6 ( 12...g6 13.Qe2 h5!? 14.Bf4 g5 was suggested by Arizmendi and Moreno; now I consider 15.Bd2! to give White good chances to exploit Black's weakened kingside) 13.Qd1 ( 13.exf6!? Qxd4+ 14.Kh1 gxf6 was given by Sandler in Informator 51; I think White should then play 15.Bf4! f5 16.Rad1 Qg7 17.Qe2 , with good compensation for the pawn because of Black's vulnerable king position) 13...Nfd7 14.Qe2! (this could also have been played on move 12; the alternative is 14.Qh5 Nf6 15.Qd1 1/2-1/2 Sandler-Danailov, Adelaide 1990) 14...O-O ( 14...Qc7 15.Bf4! O-O 16.Rad1 gives White a promising attacking position) 15.Bf4 Qb6 16.Rad1 with central pressure since Black is struggling to complete his development, Golubev-Kovchan, Kharkov 2006. This promising idea needs further tests.

(b) 9.e5 Nfd7!? (Black seeks to keep lines closed; also playable is 9...dxe5 10.fxe5 Nfd7 11.Bf4 b5 12.Qg4!? h5! with sharp play, Short-Kasparov, PCA World Ch match (game 8), London 1993; this game is annotated by Igor Stohl in Garry Kasparov's Greatest Chess Games, Volume 1) 10.exd6 Nf6! (note that this knight manoeuvre is a viable option because Black's dark-squared bishop is still on f8) 11.Be3 Bxd6 12.Qf3 O-O 13.O-O-O Qc7 14.Kb1 Nxb3 15.cxb3 e5 16.fxe5 Bxe5 with a comfortable position for Black thanks to his superior pawn-structure and bishop-pair, Hakki-Sadvakasov, Doha 2006.

(c) 9.Qf3 b5 10.f5 transposes to the main game.

We now return to the position arising after 9.f5 (D):

9...b5

Alternatives:

(a) 9...Nfxe4? (the pawn-grab is too greedy!) 10.fxe6! Qh4+ ( 10...fxe6 11.Nxe4 Nxe4 12.O-O leaves the black king stranded in the centre) 11.g3 Nxg3 12.Nf3 Qh5 13.exf7+ Kd8 14.Rg1 with a crushing advantage for White, Fischer-Bednarski, Havana Olympiad 1966.

(b) 9...e5 (Black voluntarily creates a hole on the d5-square, but he is able to break with ...d5 before White can consolidate) 10.Nde2 Nxb3 11.axb3 d5!? 12.Bg5 d4 13.Bxf6 gxf6 14.Nd5 Bd7 15.O-O Bc6 with sharp play, Sieiro-Gonzalez - Vilela, Santa Clara 1983.

(c) 9...Be7 10.Qf3 O-O 11.Be3 ( 11.O-O is a popular alternative) 11...e5 12.Nde2 b5!? (another approach is 12...Nxb3 13.axb3 b5 ) 13.Bd5 Rb8 14.b4 Na4!? ( 14...Ncd7 15.O-O Nxd5 ( 15...Nb6! , with a slight advantage for Black, was recommended by Kasparov) 16.Nxd5 Bb7 with a balanced game, Short-Kasparov, PCA World Ch match (game 6), London 1993) 15.Nxa4 ( 15.Ba7 Nxd5 16.Nxd5 Ra8 17.Be3 Bb7 is fine for Black) 15...bxa4 16.a3 leaves White slightly better according to Kasparov in Informator 59, but I believe Black can play 16...Qc7 17.c4 a5! with good prospects as White's queenside is collapsing.

(d) 9...Bd7 is recommended by Arizmendi and Moreno to accelerate Black's queenside counterplay by controlling the c6-square. They provide detailed coverage of 10.Qf3 ( 10.Bg5 Be7 is fine for Black as the c6-square is covered) 10...b5 11.O-O Be7 12.fxe6 fxe6 13.e5!? dxe5 14.Nc6 Bxc6 15.Qxc6+ Kf7 with sharp play in Istratescu-Short, Erevan Olympiad 1996 and several later games.

10.Qf3

Another idea is 10.Bg5 b4 11.Na4 Nxb3 12.axb3 e5 (this instructive move sequence should be compared to the game continuation; here Black drives White's c3-knight to the edge of the board, exchanges his c5-knight for White's light-squared bishop and plays ...e5 to drive White's d4-knight away from the centre) 13.Nc6 ( 13.Bxf6 Qxf6 14.Nf3 Rb8 with equality, Tomescu-Vajda, Bucharest 1995) 13...Qc7 14.Nxb4 Rb8 15.c3 Nxe4 16.Be3 Nf6 is equal.

10...e5 (D)

For 10...Bd7 , see note 'd' to Black's 9th move above.

Black evicts the white knight from the centre and relieves the pressure on the e6-pawn. The advance of the e-pawn creates a hole on the d5-square, but Black's next few moves are directed towards eliminating this issue.

11.Nde2

White's most popular choice is to swing the knight toward d5 with 11.Nc6 Qd7 12.Nb4 Bb7 13.Nbd5 (after 13.Bd5 Ncxe4! 14.Bxb7 Qxb7 15.Bg5 Rb8 16.Bxf6 , as in J.Tomczak-Chlost, Polanica Zdroj 2007, Black should play Golubev's 2001 suggestion of 16...Nxc3! ; for example, 17.Qxb7 Rxb7 18.Bxg7 Bxg7 19.bxc3 Kd7 with an edge for Black as he will infiltrate along the half-open c-file) 13...Ncxe4 14.Nxe4 ( 14.Nb6 Nxc3! 15.Qxc3 Qxf5 16.Nxa8 Bxa8 favours Black) 14...Nxd5 15.Bg5 Nb6 16.O-O-O is the critical line according to Golubev. I think Black is fine after 16...Nc4 17.Rhe1 f6 18.Qh5+ Qf7 19.Qxf7+ Kxf7 so White needs some new ideas in this line. The idea of planting a knight on d5 is desirable for White from a strategic perspective, but the manoeuvre costs several tempi and Black's tactical resources appear to be more than adequate.

11...Nxb3!?

White has scored well in a handful of games after 11...Bb7 12.Bg5 , so in conjunction with his next move de Firmian introduces a standard Sicilian Defence idea in a new position.

12.axb3 b4 13.Nd5 Nxd5 14.exd5

The timing of the sequence of moves ...e5, ...Nxb3 and ...b4 is critical and tends to work best for Black if White is forced to occupy the d5-square with a pawn after an exchange of pieces on that square.

14...Be7 15.O-O O-O

Another reasonable idea is 15...Qc7!? to accelerate Black's play along the half-open c-file.

16.Ng3 Bb7 17.Ra4 Qb6+

Safer is 17...a5 to protect the b-pawn. The game continuation leads to tremendous complications.

18.Be3 Qb5 (D)

19.c4!?

A real all-or-nothing move: White allows his queenside to disintegrate in return for opening a path to the kingside for his rook.

19...bxc3 20.f6 Bxf6 21.Nh5 (D)

21...Bd8?!

White's attack looks formidable, but Black has some positional trumps such as his active queen and strong light-squared bishop. Black can hold the position by maintaining the communication between his rooks with 21...Be7! 22.Rg4 , and then:

(a) 22...f5? loses to 23.Rxg7+ Kh8 24.Qg3 Bf6 25.Rxh7+ Kxh7 26.Rxf5 , as in the note to White's 24th move.

(b) Not 22...g6? 23.Nf6+ Bxf6 24.Qxf6 with a mating attack.

(c) 22...Qd3? also loses: 23.Nxg7 Kh8 24.Nf5 Qxd5 ( 24...Bxd5 25.Bh6! is winning for White) 25.Qh3 Qe6 26.Rg7 .

(d) 22...Kh8! 23.Rxg7 Qxd5 24.Qf5 Qe4 (the black queen arrives just in time to shore up the kingside) 25.Rxh7+ Kg8 26.Rg7+ Kh8 27.Qh3 ( 27.Rh7+ Kg8 28.Rg7+ Kh8 repeats) 27...Qh4 28.Qf5 Qe4 with a draw by repetition.

22.Rg4 f5?

Black's last chance was 22...Qxd5! 23.Rxg7+ Kh8 24.Qg3 Qd3 25.bxc3 ( 25.Rgxf7 Rxf7 26.Rxf7 Bf6! 27.Nxf6 Qd1+ 28.Kf2 Qc2+ is a draw by perpetual check) 25...Bh4 26.Qxh4 Qxe3+ 27.Kh1 Qh6 28.h3 with just a slight advantage for White thanks to his more secure king position.

23.Rxg7+ Kh8 (D)

24.Qh3??

Right piece, wrong square. Intuitively, this move appears wrong because it reduces the mobility of a major piece. More concretely, White overlooked the opportunity to create a mating-net by exploiting the weakness of g7: 24.Qg3! Bf6 ( 24...cxb2 25.Rxh7+ Kxh7 26.Qg7# ) 25.Rxh7+ Kxh7 26.Rxf5 (Black has an extra rook but he is unable to ward off White's mating attack) 26...Qe2 (Black desperately tries to control h5, but now White has a long series of checks culminating in mate) 27.Nxf6+ Rxf6 28.Qh4+ Kg8 29.Rg5+ Kf7 (or 29...Kf8 30.Qh8+ Ke7 31.Rg7+ Rf7 32.Bg5+ Kd7 33.Rxf7# ) 30.Qh7+ Ke8 31.Qg8+ Rf8 32.Qe6+ Kd8 33.Bb6# .

24...Qxf1+! 25.Kxf1 cxb2

Black's pesky b-pawn cannot be prevented from promoting, so White is compelled to sacrifice a rook and deliver perpetual check.

26.Rxh7+ Kxh7 (D)

27.Nf4+

Another way is 27.Nf6++ Kg7 28.Qh6+ Kf7 29.Qh7+ Kxf6 30.Qh6+ Kf7 31.Qe6+ Kg7 32.Qh6+ Kf7 with a draw.

27...Kg8

Black must remain alert and avoid the careless 27...Kg7?? 28.Ne6+ Kf7 ( 28...Kg8 29.Qg3+ Kf7 30.Qg7+ is winning for White) 29.Qh7+ Ke8 30.Qg6+ , when White wins.

28.Qg3+ Kh7

Not 28...Kf7?? 29.Qg6+ Ke7 30.Qe6# .

29.Qg6+ Kh8 30.Qh6+ 1/2-1/2

Game 2 [B87]
Vasily Ivanchuk - Veselin Topalov
Morelia/Linares 2008

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3

For comments on the moves up to here, see Game 1.

7...b5 (D)

This is the traditional main line. 7...Nbd7 and Black's other 7th-move alternatives were covered in Game 1.

Black expands on the queenside and prepares to increase the pressure on White's e4-pawn with ...Bb7 in combination with a timely ...b4 pawn advance.

8.O-O

Alternatives:

(a) 8.f4?! Bb7! 9.f5 e5 10.Nde2 and now:

(a1) 10...Nxe4!? is not so easy to refute: 11.Nxe4 ( 11.Bd5 Nxc3 12.Nxc3 Bxd5 13.Qxd5 Nd7 14.Bg5 Be7 15.Bxe7 Kxe7 favoured Black in Grabher-Wojtkiewicz, Liechtenstein 1997) 11...Bxe4 12.O-O Qb6+ 13.Kh1 Qb7 14.Rf2 Nd7 15.Ng3 Bc6 leaves White with insufficient compensation for the pawn, Yasseen-G.Hernandez, Dubai Olympiad 1986.

(a2) 10...Nbd7 11.Bg5 Be7 12.O-O Rc8 13.Ng3 h5!! (Fischer unveils a profound idea to prevent White from increasing the kingside pressure with a timely Nh5; the h-pawn advance also threatens to dislodge the g3-knight) 14.h4 b4 15.Bxf6 Bxf6 16.Nd5 Bxh4 17.Nxh5 Qg5 with a clear plus for Black because of White's stranded knight, R.Byrne-Fischer, Sousse Interzonal 1967.

(b) 8.Bg5 Be7 9.Qf3 Qc7 10.e5!? (a very aggressive idea) 10...Bb7 11.exd6 Bxd6 12.Qe3 Bc5 13.O-O-O Nc6 (D) and now:

(b1) The old line ran 14.Bxf6 gxf6 15.Ne4 (White sacrifices the exchange to expose Black's king; another sharp idea is 15.Nd5 , as analysed by Morgado and Alvarez in New In Chess Yearbook 88) 15...Bxd4 16.Rxd4 Nxd4 17.Nxf6+ Kf8 18.Qxd4 Rd8 19.Qh4 h6 20.c3 Qe5 21.Nh7+ Ke8 22.Nf6+ Kf8 23.Nh7+ , leading to a draw by repetition, A.Kogan-Sutovsky, Ashdod 2006.

(b2) 14.Qxe6+!? (Ivanchuk's creative sacrifice has infused new life into this line) 14...fxe6 15.Nxe6 and then:

(b21) 15...Qe5 16.Nxg7+ Kf8 17.Ne6+ Kf7 18.Rhe1 Bxf2! (the stem game continued 18...Qxe1? 19.Nxc5+ Kg6 20.Rxe1 Kxg5 21.Nxb7 with a decisive advantage for White, Ivanchuk-Kariakin, Amber Rapid, Nice 2008) 19.Rxe5 Nxe5 20.Nd8++ Kg6 21.Bxf6 Kxf6 22.Nxb7 Rhf8 23.Ne4+ Kg7 24.Kb1!? with a sharp but roughly balanced position.

(b22) 15...Qe7! 16.Rhe1 ( 16.Nd5!? Nxd5 17.Bxe7 Ncxe7 18.Nxc5 O-O-O 19.Rhe1 a5!? with a slight advantage for Black as his extra knight is stronger than the two white pawns) 16...Qxe6 17.Rxe6+ Ne7 (D).

18.Bxf6 ( 18.Rde1 is another sharp line analysed by Morgado and Alvarez in New In Chess Yearbook 87) 18...gxf6 19.Bd5 ( 19.Rde1? Kd7 favours Black, T.Andrews-Gopal, Philadelphia 2008) 19...Bxd5 ( 19...Kf7!? is also complex) 20.Nxd5 Rd8 21.Nxe7 Kf7 22.Rxa6 Rxd1+ 23.Kxd1 Kxe7 "and only Black has winning chances" according to John Nunn in New In Chess Magazine 2008/3. I expect further developments in this line.

8...Be7 (D)

9.Qf3

White threatens to create havoc on the long diagonal with e5, but this threat is easily parried and the queen is actually angling for the g3-square to exert pressure on Black's kingside. The queen manoeuvre marks the beginning of a piece-based attack, and this aggressive idea has grown in popularity over the past two decades in comparison to the older pawn-based attack beginning with 9.f4 . Another idea behind deferring the f-pawn advance is to retain the option of developing the dark-squared bishop to g5 or h6.

9...Qb6

Alternatives:

(a) 9...Bb7? (this careless move is a typical club-level mistake in the Najdorf; Black must always be alert to the possibility of a piece sacrifice on the e6-square, particularly if he has already played ...Be7 as this leaves the g7-pawn unguarded) 10.Bxe6! fxe6 11.Nxe6 with a dangerous attack.

(b) 9...Qc7 (this is a major alternative) 10.Qg3 Nc6 11.Nxc6 Qxc6 12.Re1 ( 12.Qxg7?! Rg8 13.Qh6 Nxe4 14.Nxe4 Qxe4 15.f3 Qf5 is comfortable for Black) 12...Bb7 and now:

(b1) One possibility is 13.Qxg7!? Rg8 14.Qh6 O-O-O with sharp play, though Black has scored well from this position in practice thanks to his strong counterplay along the half-open g-file.

(b2) 13.a3 was played in Rublevsky-Kariakin, Russian Team Ch, Dagomys 2008, and the next few moves featured an instructive battle over a tempo: White would like to delay moving his dark-squared bishop until Black has castled kingside so that he can play Bh6 in one move. Note that Black was not threatening to play ...b4 immediately because of the reply Ba4, pinning and winning the queen, but sooner or later Black will castle and threaten to drive the c3-knight away from the centre. 13...Rd8 (two can play at this waiting game: Black counters with a useful developing move to reinforce the d6-pawn) 14.f3 O-O (Black has no more useful waiting moves, so he acquiesces to the upcoming exchange of dark-squared bishops) 15.Bh6 Ne8 16.Rad1 Kh8 17.Bg5 Bxg5 18.Qxg5 Nf6 19.Rd2 Rd7 20.Red1 Rfd8 and the chances were equal. Black has successfully completed his development and defended his d6-pawn.

We now return to 9...Qb6 (D):

10.Be3

The cheeky 10.Bg5!? should be calmly answered by 10...O-O (Black must avoid the greedy 10...Qxd4? 11.e5 Nd5 12.Nxd5 exd5 ( 12...Bxg5 13.Nc7+ Kd8 14.Nxa8 and White wins) 13.Bxe7 Kxe7 14.Bxd5 , when White has an overwhelming attack) 11.Rad1 ( 11.e5? is over-aggressive and bad: 11...dxe5 12.Nf5 ( 12.Qxa8 Bb7 traps the white queen) 12...exf5 13.Bxf6 Bb7 14.Nd5 Bxf6 15.Nxb6 Bxf3 16.gxf3 Ra7 and Black has a material advantage, H.Hagessether-Elsness, Norwegian Ch, Asker 2000) 11...Nbd7 ( 11...Bb7 is a popular alternative) 12.Qg3 Nh5!? (the idea is to defuse White's attacking possibilities by exchanging dark-squared bishops) 13.Qh4 Bxg5 14.Qxg5 Nhf6 15.Rfe1 Bb7 16.Re3 Ne5 (the knight heads to the kingside for defence) 17.Rg3 Ng6 with a solid position for Black, Shabalov-Nakamura, Miami 2007.

10...Qb7

The queen 'fianchetto' is a standard manoeuvre in the Fischer Attack: the queen exerts strong pressure on the e4-pawn and takes advantage of White's momentary coordination issues.

11.Qg3 b4! (D)

Black selects an opportune moment to divert the knight to the edge of the board before White has time to prepare any sacrifices involving Nd5.

12.Na4

White is not quite prepared for the speculative sacrifice 12.Nf5?! bxc3 ( 12...exf5? 13.Qxg7 Rf8 14.Nd5 is dangerous for Black because of his lack of development) 13.Nxg7+ and then:

(a) 13...Kd7? 14.e5 Nd5 was recommended by Ki.Georgiev and Kolev on the basis of the stem game Zapolskis-Ki.Georgiev, European Team Ch, Batumi 1999, but the black king is poorly situated on d7 and White can blast open the centre with 15.Bxd5! Qxd5 ( 15...exd5 16.bxc3 Qc6 17.Rab1 gives White a crushing attack) 16.Rad1 Qe4 17.Rd4 Qxc2 18.exd6 Bf6 ( 18...Nc6 19.dxe7+ Nxd4 20.Bxd4 and White wins) 19.Rc4 and White has a decisive attack.

(b) 13...Kd8! 14.e5 Ne4! 15.Qf4 Bg5 (this is one of the reasons why the black king is safer on d8: the f-pawn is now protected) 16.Qg4 Bxe3 17.fxe3 cxb2 and Black has successfully parried the attack, Godlauskas-Tietjen, corr. 2003. White has insufficient compensation for his material deficit.

12...Nbd7 13.f3 O-O (D)

14.Rfd1

An alternative plan is to exploit Black's lagging queenside development by opening the c-file with 14.Rac1 Rb8!? (Ponomariov introduces a new idea; the older 14...Ne5 is a solid alternative) 15.c3 bxc3 16.Rxc3 Ne5 17.Rfc1 Bd7 18.Qe1 ( 18.Rc7 Qb4 19.Ra7 Rfc8 is fine for Black) 18...Bd8!? (Ponomariov employs a finesse to drive White's queen to a less active square; the thematic 18...Rfc8 is also reasonable for Black) 19.Qd1 Ba5 20.R3c2 Rfc8 is equal as Black has neutralized White's build-up along the c-file, Rublevsky-Ponomariov, Candidates match (game 4), Elista 2007.

14...Kh8 (D)

15.Kh1!?
