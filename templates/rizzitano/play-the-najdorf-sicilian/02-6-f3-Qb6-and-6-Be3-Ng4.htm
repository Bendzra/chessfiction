2 6.f3 Qb6 and 6.Be3 Ng4

The English Attack has exploded in popularity over the past two decades as players at all levels have employed this exciting attacking scheme to combat the Najdorf Sicilian. The idea of playing Be3, f3, Qd2 and 0-0-0 followed by a kingside pawn-storm beginning with g4 and often h4 was originally employed versus openings featuring a kingside fianchetto; the Yugoslav Attack versus the Sicilian Dragon and the Saemisch Variation versus the King's Indian are two well-known examples. The 'big idea' behind the growth of the English Attack was that this set-up can also be effective versus non-fianchetto openings; another example can be found in the Richter-Rauzer Attack versus the Classical Sicilian when White retreats his dark-squared bishop from g5 to e3 and plays f3 followed by g4 to launch a kingside attack.

Our coverage of the English Attack begins with the move-order finesse 6.f3 (Game 3) and continues with the standard move-order 6.Be3 . In this chapter we shall examine the sharp lines beginning with 6.Be3 Ng4 (Games 4 and 5), by which Black seeks to disrupt White's attempt to launch the standard English Attack pawn-storm. Garry Kasparov began playing 6...Ng4 during the mid-1990s and this naturally led to an increase in the popularity of the line. After 7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 Kasparov's insight was that Black's advanced kingside pawns (Black often follows up with ...h5 in this line) control useful space and can act as a battering-ram to soften up White's kingside. Today, Kariakin and Grishchuk are two strong players who continue to employ the line and develop new ideas.


Game 3 (Ruiz-Quezada) features the modern move-order 6.f3 . White's idea is to avoid the line 6.Be3 Ng4 . Black's most common reply to 6.f3 is 6...e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.Be3 , reaching a position considered in Chapter 3, Games 7 and 8. The subject of this game is 6.f3 Qb6 ; the idea behind the early queen development is to prevent White from playing Be3 for the time being, and so force White to adopt a different set-up entirely, or else to make some concessions if he insists on an English Attack. Naturally, White can argue that the black queen's early exposure on b6 can also have its drawbacks. The 6.f3 Qb6 line is a natural complement in the repertoire of a Najdorf player who prefers to meet 6.Be3 with 6...Ng4 .

The game follows one of the main theoretical paths until Ruiz strikes out with 14.f4!? to grab the centre and control the e5-square. Quezada responds with the provocative pawn sacrifice 14...g5!? 15 fxg5 h6!? to secure control over some key central squares. White goes astray several moves later by decentralizing his queen and Black misses a chance to exploit the exposed position of White's knight on a4. The finish is a typical Sicilian back-and-forth slugfest.


In Game 4 (Iordachescu-Maksimenko), White employs the standard move-order 6.Be3 and play continues with 6...Ng4 7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 10.h3 Ne5 . Both players complete their development and the position resembles a Sicilian Dragon with Black's kingside pawns situated on g5 and h6 instead of the usual g6 and h7. Iordachescu tries to take advantage of Black's slightly exposed kingside, but Maksimenko is able to counterattack on the queenside and defuse the kingside pressure by exchanging two sets of minor pieces. The end result is a well-played draw.


Game 5 (L.Dominguez-Kariakin) also features the line 6.Be3 Ng4 7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 10.h3 , and here Black varies from Game 4 by playing 10...Nf6 , Dominguez chooses the aggressive combination of 11.Qe2!? followed several moves later by 15.g4!? with the goal of freezing Black's g-pawn and prising open the h-file. Kariakin responds too slowly and White goes astray with 22.b4? instead of the thematic developing move 22.0-0-0!. The middlegame is very exciting and White sacrifices material to coax the black king out into the centre of the board. Kariakin demonstrates that the king is safe enough and he is able to launch a decisive counterattack on Dominguez's own king. This is a typical Najdorf middlegame demonstrating the importance of maintaining the initiative.

Game 3 [B90]
Orlen Ruiz - Yuniesky Quezada
Cuba 2007

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.f3 (D)

This move-order finesse is designed to avoid 6.Be3 Ng4 ; this line is the subject of Games 4 and 5.

6...Qb6

The queen move interferes with White's development by preventing Be3. Black's most important alternatives are:

(a) 6...b5?! 7.a4!? ( 7.Be3 e6 transposes to a line of the English Attack vs the Scheveningen) 7...b4 8.Nd5 Nxd5 9.exd5 Bb7 10.Bc4 Nd7 11.a5 (Sammalvuo's 11.Nf5?! is well met by 11...g6 12.Qd4 ( 12.Nh6 e5! with the threat of ...Qh4+) 12...Ne5 13.Ne3 Bg7 14.Qf4 Nxc4 15.Qxc4 Qa5 with the bishop-pair and easy development for Black) 11...Qc7 12.Qe2 g6 13.Bg5 Qc5 14.Nc6! Ne5 15.Nxe5 dxe5 (Kritz-Ruck, Budapest 2004) 16.Qxe5! Qxc4 17.Qxh8 f6 18.Qxh7 fxg5 19.Qxg6+ Kd7 20.Qf5+ Ke8 21.Rd1 gives White a strong attack.

(b) 6...e6 7.Be3 transposes into another form of the English Attack classified as a Scheveningen Sicilian (ECO code B80).

(c) 6...e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.Be3 is equivalent to the standard move-order 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.f3 , which we examine in Games 7 and 8 in the next chapter.

We now return to 6...Qb6 (D):

7.Nb3

White should avoid the reckless pawn sacrifice 7.Be3? Qxb2 8.Na4 Qb4+ 9.c3 Qa5 10.Rb1 Nbd7 , when Black has an extra pawn, Toma - Worek, Polish Under-16 Girls Ch, Zakopane 2001.

The game continuation is also commonly reached via the move-order 7.g4 Nc6 8.Nb3 e6 .

7...e6 8.g4

Alternatives:

(a) 8.Bf4!? Nbd7 (the pawn sacrifice 8...d5?! 9.exd5 Bb4 10.dxe6 Bxe6 11.Qd4! favours White, Zontakh - Ruck, Warsaw rapid 2002) 9.g4 Be7 10.Qe2 h6 11.h4 Qc7 12.O-O-O b5 13.a3 Rb8 with equal chances, Anand - Ponomariov, Wijk aan Zee 2005.

(b) 8.Qe2 Nc6 9.Be3 Qc7 10.g4 is another move-order to reach the same position as in the main game.

8...Nc6 (D)

9.Qe2

The queen move is an acknowledgement of the disruptive effect of the black queen upon White's development; White would prefer to develop with Be3 followed by Qd2. The alternative is to advance the kingside pawns with 9.g5 Nd7 10.h4 Qc7 (Black voluntarily withdraws his queen in order to clear a path for the advance of his b-pawn; this allows White to develop his bishop directly to e3 without having to play Qe2 first, but Black is aiming to show that White's kingside pawn advance is somewhat premature because the black king is still in the centre and White cannot yet employ the typical attacking motif of playing h5 followed by g6) 11.Be3 b5 12.h5 and then:

(a) 12...Nce5 13.f4 b4! 14.Nb1 Nc4 15.Bxc4 Qxc4 16.N1d2 Qc7 17.Rc1!? with an unclear position according to Acs and Hazai in Informator 87. Then I think Black should play 17...a5 18.c3 Qb7 with a comfortable position as it will be difficult for White to generate any threats on the kingside without his light-squared bishop.

(b) 12...Bb7 13.Rh3 was played in Acs-J.Polgar, Budapest 2003. Now Black should just get on with it and play 13...b4! 14.Ne2 Nce5 with good queenside play to offset White's kingside build-up.

9...Qc7 10.Be3 b5 11.O-O-O Nd7

Black covers the sensitive b6-square in preparation for playing ...b4; he also sidesteps White's prospective g5 pawn advance. The knight move is more precise than the popular alternative 11...Bb7 because it is not yet clear that the light-squared bishop belongs on b7; Black often finds it more useful to play ...Rb8 to keep a white piece out of the b6-square.

12.Qf2 (D)

12...b4 (D)

The pawn advance is more direct than the slower 12...Rb8 13.Kb1 . Then:

(a) 13...Nb6 14.f4!? b4 15.Ne2 e5 16.fxe5!? ( 16.f5 a5 17.Ng3 a4 18.Nd2 a3 with sharp play, Anand-J.Polgar, Mainz rapid 2003) 16...Nxe5 ( 16...dxe5 17.Ng3! Bxg4 18.Be2 is a promising pawn sacrifice for White as he will be able to generate a rapid kingside attack) 17.h3 favours White as his knights have some attractive central squares.

(b) 13...Nce5!? (this was a 2004 suggestion by Sammalvuo) 14.Rg1 b4 15.Ne2 Nc4 16.Bc1 Be7 17.Ned4 Ndb6 with sharp play ahead, Anand-Grishchuk, Amber Rapid, Monte Carlo 2006.

13.Na4

The knight takes aim at the b6-square. Another idea is 13.Ne2 Bb7 14.Ned4 Nxd4 ( 14...Be7!? ) 15.Nxd4 ( 15.Bxd4 e5 16.Be3 Be7 is fine for Black as White's remaining knight is a long way from d5) 15...d5 ( 15...Be7!? 16.g5 O-O 17.h4 d5 is also comfortable for Black) 16.exd5 Bxd5 17.Kb1 Bc5 18.h4 O-O with equality, Anand-J.Polgar, Cap d'Agde rapid 2003.

13...Rb8 (D)

14.f4!?

Ruiz introduces a new move to try to exploit Black's lagging kingside development. An earlier game saw 14.Rg1 :

(a) 14...Nce5 15.f4 Nc4 16.Ba7 Rb7 17.Bd4 Qc6 18.Qe2! (the queen move is more challenging than 18.Bxc4 Qxc4 19.Nb6 Nxb6 20.Bxb6 Qc6 21.Ba5 Be7 and "Black should have no complaints" - Sammalvuo) 18...Rc7 ( 18...Na3 19.bxa3 Qxa4 20.Qc4 favours White) 19.Qxc4 Qxc4 20.Bxc4 Rxc4 21.Na5 Rc7 22.Nb6 Bb7 23.Nxd7 Kxd7 24.Nxb7 Rxb7 25.Rg3 with some pressure for White as Black must work to unravel his kingside.

(b) 14...Be7 15.Kb1 O-O ( 15...Nd8?! 16.Nd2 gives White more harmonious development, Leko-J.Polgar, Cap d'Agde rapid 2003) 16.g5 Nce5 is equal.

14...g5!?

This is a common Sicilian motif when White tries to build a broad pawn-phalanx along the fourth rank: Black diverts White's f4-pawn in order to obtain control over the e5-square for his knights. This idea is more often seen in the Browne Variation of the Main Line (Game 23), but it can also occur in the English Attack when White shifts gears with f4.

15.fxg5 (D)

15.f5 Nce5 16.fxe6 fxe6 17.Bxg5 ( 17.Nd4?! Nc5 favours Black) 17...Nf7 18.Bh4 Nde5 is comfortable for Black thanks to his control of the central squares combined with his strong knight outpost on e5. Meanwhile, White's knights are out of play on the queenside.

15...h6!?

Black continues in gambit style. Another idea is 15...Nce5 .

16.gxh6

White could consider undermining the e6-pawn via 16.g6!? fxg6 17.Bc4 Nd8 18.Nd2 Bg7 , when the chances are roughly equal.

16...Nce5 (D)

17.Ba7

White compels the black rook to move forward a square to interfere with the protection of the a6-pawn.

17...Rb7 18.Bd4 Nxg4 (D)

The attempt to trap the a4-knight by 18...Qc6? is premature in view of 19.Nac5! , with favourable complications for White.

19.Qg2?

White plans on capturing Black's a6-pawn with his bishop, but this only accelerates Black's development. White should keep his queen involved on the queenside by 19.Qe2 Nge5 20.Qxa6 Rxh6 , with equal chances.

19...Nge5 20.Bxa6 Rb8 21.Bxc8 Rxc8 (D)

Black has every reason to be satisfied with the results of the opening; although he will remain a pawn down after recovering the h6-pawn, he has a secure central knight outpost and his compact pawn-structure controls many important central squares. White's knights are particularly ineffective on the queenside because they have no targets to attack and the a4-knight is unprotected. Black's king is perfectly safe in the centre, whereas White's castled king is vulnerable along the half-open c-file.

22.Qf2 Qc6?!

Quezada misses the more precise continuation 22...Rxh6! 23.Kb1 Qc6 24.Bxe5 ( 24.Nb6 Rf6 ) 24...Nxe5 25.Nb6 Rb8 26.Nd5 exd5 27.exd5 Qb6 with a clear plus for Black because of his material advantage; White has no way to displace the powerful knight on e5.

23.Nb6 Nxb6 24.Bxb6 Qxe4 25.Kb1 Rxh6 26.Bd4 Rg6 27.Rhg1 Bg7 28.Rxg6 fxg6

Also possible is 28...Qxg6 29.Rg1 Qh7 with equal chances.

29.Re1 Qf5 30.Qe2 Ke7 (D)

The king steps up to avoid a check on the b5-square.

31.Rf1?

Ruiz blunders away a pawn and the game in time-pressure. White can hang on by 31.Qg2 Qf3 ( 31...Rc7 32.Bb6! is annoying) 32.Qg5+ Bf6 33.Qd2 Qf5 34.Qg2 , with equality.

31...Rxc2! 32.Rxf5 (D)

Or 32.Qxc2 Qxf1+ 33.Nc1 Qf5 and Black's extra pawn is decisive.

32...Rxe2 33.Rf2 Rxf2 34.Bxf2 Nf3 35.Bg3 d5 36.a3 bxa3 37.bxa3 e5

The triumph of the central pawn-majority; the black king will easily round up White's passed a-pawn, whereas White has no answer to Black's connected and passed centre pawns.

Game 4 [B90]
Viorel Iordachescu - Andrei Maksimenko
Serbian Team Ch, Zlatibor 2006

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 (D)

The move-order 6.f3 , to prevent Black's next move, was the subject of Game 3.

6...Ng4

Black's most aggressive choice; by harassing White's dark-squared bishop he gains time to fianchetto his own dark-squared bishop at the risk of weakening his kingside pawn-structure.

7.Bg5

Various transpositions are possible after 7.Bc1 ; e.g., 7...Nc6 ( 7...Nf6 8.Be3 Ng4 9.Bc1 Nf6 is a draw by repetition) 8.Be2 Nf6 9.Be3 e5 ( 9...e6 10.O-O Be7 11.f4 O-O transposes into the Classical Scheveningen - ECO code B85) 10.Nb3 Be7 11.O-O O-O 12.a4 Be6 and we have reached a position from the Classical Najdorf (note 'c' to White's 10th move in Game 12) with two extra moves for each side.

7...h6 (D)

8.Bh4

The ...h6 pawn advance has weakened the g6-square and White can try to exploit this with the provocative retreat 8.Bc1!? Nc6 9.Be2 Nf6 10.Be3 , and now:

(a) 10...e6 11.f4 Be7 12.O-O Bd7 13.Qe1 transposes into the Classical Scheveningen (ECO code B85) with the extra move ...h6 for Black. This appears to be a disadvantage for Black because in some lines the g6-square is weakened or the h6-pawn is loose.

(b) With 10...e5!? , Black establishes a pawn-structure to take advantage of his control of g5 by denying White the opportunity to increase his control over the d5-square with Bg5xf6. After 11.Nb3 Be7 12.O-O O-O (Kurnosov-Shomoev, Copenhagen 2005) 13.Qd2 Be6 we have reached a position from the Classical Najdorf (note 'a' to Black's 10th move in Game 12) with the useful extra move ...h6 for Black.

8...g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 (D)

Black has gained control of the long diagonal and marginalized White's dark-squared bishop at the cost of weakening his kingside pawn-structure. White will drive the black knight away from the kingside and redeploy his dark-squared bishop with f3 and Bf2. Black must be careful about castling kingside because White has the option of playing h4 to prise open the h-file.

10.h3

White has several important alternatives here:

(a) 10.Bc4 (this move is harmless as Black can easily defuse the pressure on the f7-pawn) 10...Nc6 11.Nxc6 bxc6 12.h4 Qa5 ( 12...Qb6 is a solid alternative) 13.Qf3 Rf8 14.Bb3 Ne5 15.Qe3 Be6 16.hxg5 hxg5 17.O-O-O Ng4 with an even game, Ni Hua-Xu Jun, HeiBei Zonal 2001.

(b) 10.Qd2 (White covers the e3-square in preparation for regrouping with f3 and Bf2) 10...Nc6 11.Nb3 Nge5 12.a4! (the pawn move restricts Black's queenside expansion; White can easily be driven back after 12.f3 b5! 13.Bf2 Rb8! 14.Nd4 b4 15.Nd1 Nxd4 16.Bxd4 d5! and Black took over the initiative in Ivanchuk-Kasparov, Linares 1999) 12...Be6 13.Nd5 Rc8 with a balanced game, Hossain-Browne, Stratton Mountain 2000.

(c) 10.Be2 h5 (D).

White has several options, of which line 'c4' is the most critical:

(c1) 11.h3? (the misguided pawn move has been played surprisingly often, so it is important to be prepared for it) 11...h4! 12.hxg4 hxg3 13.Rxh8+ Bxh8 14.fxg3 (White is saddled with the dreaded 'Irish pawn-centre', viz. tripled isolated pawns!) 14...Nc6 15.Nb3 Be5 with a clear plus for Black thanks to his powerful piece outpost on the e5-square, Shirov-Sadler, Oviedo rapid 1992.

(c2) 11.Nf5!? Bxf5 12.exf5 h4 13.Bxd6 ( 13.Bxh4 Qa5! 14.Bxg4 Bxc3+ 15.bxc3 Qxc3+ 16.Kf1 Rxh4 17.Rb1 Nd7 with a solid position for Black thanks to his control of the dark squares, Shamugia-Jobava, Georgian Junior Ch, Tbilisi 2000) 13...Nxf2 14.Kxf2 exd6 15.Nd5 Nc6 ( 15...Nd7 16.Re1 O-O 17.c3 Nf6 is equal - Sammalvuo) 16.c3 Be5 17.Re1 Ne7 is equal, Jenni-Van der Stricht, Bled Olympiad 2002.

(c3) 11.h4 (White seeks to demonstrate that the g4-knight is misplaced) 11...gxh4 12.Rxh4 ( 12.Bxh4 Nc6 13.Nb3 Be6 14.Qd2 Qb6! 15.Nd5 Bxd5 16.exd5 Nce5 was fine for Black in Morozevich-Grishchuk, Moscow blitz 2006, and many earlier games) 12...Nc6 13.Nb3 Be6 14.Qd2 Rc8!? 15.O-O-O (White should avoid the trap 15.Qg5? Bf6 16.Rxh5 Kd7! (Smikovski-Yudasin, Chigorin Memorial, St Petersburg 1997) 17.Rxh8 Qxh8 18.Qf4 Nb4 with a clear plus for Black) 15...Qb6 16.f3 Qe3! 17.Bf4 Qxd2+ 18.Rxd2 Bxc3 ( 18...Nge5 19.Be3 Bf6 20.Rh1 Nc4 21.Bxc4 was played in Morozevich-J .Polgar, FIDE World Ch, San Luis 2005, and now Morozevich recommends 21...Bxc3 22.bxc3 Bxc4 leading to roughly equal chances) 19.bxc3 Nge5 20.Be3 Ng6 21.Rh1 Nce5 22.f4 (I think 22.Bd4 could be met by 22...f6!? 23.g3 Nc4 24.Bxc4 Bxc4 25.Rdh2 h4 26.gxh4 Rh5 with reasonable compensation for the pawn) 22...Nc4 and "Black everywhere has a comfortable game, and White is fighting for a draw." according to Morozevich in his instructive New in Chess Magazine 2005/8 notes to this game.

(c4) 11.Bxg4 is the most critical response. Now:

(c41) 11...Bxg4 (the bishop recapture went out of fashion after Kasparov switched to 11...hxg4 in two games from 1999, but there are still many unanswered questions) 12.f3 Bd7 13.Bf2 Nc6 14.O-O e6 15.Nde2 Qe7 16.Na4 Ne5 17.Nb6 Rd8 18.Bd4 Bc6 19.c4 (D).

Now I think Black should keep his rook on the h-file, retaining his castling rights:

(c411) The black king runs into trouble after 19...Rg8? 20.Nc3 g4 21.f4 h4 (Blehm-Kempinski, Warsaw 2002), when White should have exploited Black's poor king position with the temporary sacrifice 22.Ncd5! exd5 23.exd5 , recovering the piece with a large advantage.

(c412) 19...g4! 20.f4 h4 21.Qc2 ( 21.Nd5 exd5 22.cxd5 h3! is fine for Black) 21...h3! 22.fxe5 hxg2 23.Rf2 dxe5 24.Be3 ( 24.Bc3 Qc5 ) 24...Rh3 25.Ng3 Qh4 26.Rxg2 Rxh2 27.Qf2 Rh3 gives Black strong pressure in return for a small material investment.

(c42) 11...hxg4 (D).

White has several options, of which line 'c423' is the most critical:

(c421) 12.Qd2 Nc6 13.Nf5 Bxf5 14.exf5 Qb6!? ( 14...Qa5 15.Nd5 Bxb2 16.Rb1 transposes to line 'c422') 15.O-O Qxb2 16.Nd5 Qd4 17.Qxd4 Nxd4 18.Rae1 ( 18.f6 Bxf6 19.Rae1 Ne6 20.Bxd6 Rd8 with a favourable endgame for Black) 18...Nxf5 19.Nc7+ Kd7 20.Nxa8 Rxa8 21.Re4 Rc8 22.Rxg4 Bf6 gives Black good compensation for his small material deficit.

(c422) 12.Nd5 Nc6 13.Nf5 Bxf5 14.exf5 Bxb2 15.Rb1 Qa5+ 16.Qd2 Bd4! 17.Qxa5 Nxa5 18.Nc7+ Kd7 19.Nxa8 Rxa8 with adequate compensation for the exchange, Anand-Topalov, Dortmund 1999.

(c423) 12.O-O Nc6 13.Nf5 Bxc3 14.bxc3 Qa5 15.Qxg4 ( 15.Ne3 Be6! 16.Rb1 b5 was fine for Black in Leko-Gelfand, Amber Rapid, Monte Carlo 2002) 15...f6 16.f4!? Bxf5 17.exf5 Qc5+!? 18.Kh1 Qc4 (another idea is 18...gxf4 19.Bxf4 O-O-O with equal chances) 19.Qf3 gxf4 20.Bxf4 O-O-O (Nyysti-Sammalvuo, Velhot rapid 2003) 21.Rfe1! Ne5 22.Re4! Qb5!? ( 22...Qf7 23.Bxe5 dxe5 24.Rb1 Rd7 25.h3!? "may be slightly better for White" according to Sammalvuo, but I think Black is fine after 25...Qd5 as White's extra pawn is useless) 23.Bxe5 dxe5 24.Rb4 Qd5 with an even game.

10...Ne5 (D)

10...Nf6 is the subject of Game 5.

11.f3

White can gain the bishop-pair by 11.Nf5 Bxf5 12.exf5 , when Black has several viable options:

(a) 12...Qa5 13.Qd5 Qb6! (the queen retreat is stronger than the more popular 13...Nbc6 14.Qxa5 Nxa5 15.Nd5 Rc8 16.O-O-O with a favourable endgame for White thanks to his centralized knight and bishop-pair) 14.O-O-O Nbd7 15.h4 g4 16.Qd4 Rc8 17.Qxb6 Nxb6 18.Ne4 Ned7 with roughly equal chances, Kariakin-Grishchuk, FIDE Grand Prix, Nalchik 2009.

(b) 12...Nbd7 13.Nd5 Nb6 14.c3 Nxd5 15.Qxd5 Qd7 16.Be2 Qc6 17.O-O-O Qxd5 18.Rxd5 Rc8 19.h4 Bf6 with an equal endgame, Amonatov-Khismatullin, Zvenigorod 2008.

(c) 12...Nec6!? (this voluntary knight retreat appears to be a loss of time, but the idea is to exploit the absence of White's dark-squared bishop from the queenside) 13.Bd3 Bxc3+ (another idea is 13...Qa5 14.O-O Bxc3 ( 14...Nd7!? looks safer) 15.bxc3 Qxc3 with sharp play, Emelin-A.Naumann, Dresden 2008) 14.bxc3 Nd7 15.O-O Nce5 16.Rb1 Qc7 led to a sharp bishops-versus-knights battle in Yarmolyuk-Rammstein, corr. 2007.

(d) 12...Nbc6 13.Nd5 e6 14.fxe6 fxe6 15.Ne3 (D).

Black has two centre pawns, but he must be careful advancing them or they will become vulnerable to attack from White's bishop-pair. Now:

(d1) 15...Qa5+ 16.c3 Nf3+ 17.Qxf3 Bxc3+ 18.Kd1 Qa4+ 19.Nc2 Bxb2 20.Rc1! (Svidler's improvement over 20.Qb3 Qxb3 21.axb3 Bxa1 22.Nxa1 Ke7 with sharp play, Svidler-Topalov, FIDE World Ch, San Luis 2005) 20...Bxc1 21.Qf6 Kd7 22.Kxc1 Qxa2 23.Bd3 ( 23.Be2 is recommended by Gershon and Nor; 23...Rac8 is an adequate reply) 23...Rhc8! (Svidler's suggested improvement over 23...Rac8?! 24.Rd1 d5 ( 24...Ne7 loses to 25.Bb5+! ) 25.Bf5 Rhe8 26.Qf7+! Kd8 27.Re1! with a large advantage for White, Svidler-Grishchuk, World Ch, Mexico City 2007) 24.Rd1 Ne7! 25.Rd2 Rc6 26.Kd1! Rac8 27.Ne3 "with initiative" according to Svidler in his Informator 101 notes.

(d2) 15...O-O 16.Be2 and then:

(d21) 16...d5 17.O-O Ng6 18.c4 d4 19.Nc2 ( 19.Ng4 Nf4 ( 19...h5!? ) 20.Bxf4 Rxf4 is equal, Carlsen-Adly, Reykjavik 2006) 19...Nf4 ( 19...Qd7!? ) 20.Ne1 with a slight advantage for White thanks to his control over the light squares, Emelin-Naumann, European Clubs Cup, Kallithea 2008.

(d22) 16...Qe7 17.O-O Rad8 18.Bh5! (the white bishop prevents the manoeuvre ...Ng6-f4) 18...Kh8 19.Re1 d5 20.a4 Qf6 ( 20...Nc4? 21.Nxc4 dxc4 22.Qg4! with a clear plus for White, Kasimdzhanov-Anand, FIDE World Ch, San Luis 2005) 21.a5 Ng6 22.Ng4 Qf5 23.Bxg6 Qxg6 24.Ra3 Rde8 25.Qd2 with a slight advantage for White according to Kasimdzhanov in Informator 94.

11...Nbc6 12.Bf2 (D)

12...Be6

Alternatives:

(a) 12...Nxd4 13.Bxd4 Be6 14.Qd2 transposes to the main game.

(b) 12...e6 13.Qd2 Ng6 14.O-O-O Be5 15.Kb1 (another idea is 15.Nxc6 bxc6 16.Bd4 Bf4 17.Be3 Be5 18.h4!? to prise open the kingside) 15...Bd7 16.Nxc6! (c) 12...Ng6 13.Qd2 Qa5 (D).

Now:

(c1) 14.Nb3?! Bxc3 15.bxc3 Qc7 favours Black thanks to his superior pawn-structure, Bakre-Visweswaran, Lucknow 2004.

(c2) 14.Nd5 Qxd2+ 15.Kxd2 Rb8 16.c3 Nxd4 17.Bxd4 Bxd4 18.cxd4 b5 leads to equality.

(c3) 14.O-O-O Be6 15.Kb1 ( 15.Nxe6 fxe6 16.Bc4 Bxc3 17.bxc3 Nce5! gives Black a promising attack) 15...Rc8 16.Nb3 Bxc3 17.Nxa5 Bxd2 18.Nxc6 Rxc6 19.Rxd2 Bc4 (this was Tiviakov's Informator 89 suggestion; less accurate is 19...f6 20.b3! , when the bishop-pair gave White something to play for in Tiviakov-L.Dominguez, Wijk aan Zee 2004) 20.Bxc4 Rxc4 21.b3 Rc8 22.h4 f6 23.Kb2 Kf7 with an equal endgame.

15.a3 O-O 16.h4 Ng6 17.hxg5 hxg5 18.b4 Qc7 19.Ne2! (D)

This knight manoeuvre maintains the tension and is more challenging than 19.Bxg7 Kxg7 20.O-O-O Rh8 21.Rxh8 Rxh8 22.Kb2 f6 with equal chances, Anand-Ponomariov, Rapid match (game 2), Mainz 2002.

19...Rfc8!

This rook move was a 2004 suggestion by Sammalvuo.

Alternatives:

(a) 19...f6?! 20.Bb2 Bf7 21.Nd4 d5 (another idea is 21...Rfd8!? 22.Nf5 d5 ) 22.exd5 Qe5+ 23.Be2 Qxd5 24.O-O-O with a slight advantage for White because of his more secure king, Akopian-Kramnik, Wijk aan Zee 2004.

(b) 19...Be5!? 20.Bxe5 dxe5 21.Qxg5 Qxc2 ( 21...Rfd8 was suggested by Akopian in Informator 89, but I think White can try to retain the extra pawn by 22.Rh6 Qc6 23.Rc1 ) 22.Rc1 Qb3 23.Rh6 Rfc8 24.Rxg6+ fxg6 25.Qxg6+ Kf8 26.Qh6+ Kf7 27.Qh5+ Kf6 28.Qh6+ with a draw by repetition - Ftacnik.

20.c3 (D)

Black has no problems after 20.Bxg7 Kxg7 21.Qxg5 Qxc2 22.Qh6+ Kg8 23.Rd1 Qb2 , as given by Sammalvuo.

20...Bc4 21.Rc1

By playing 21.Ng3 White can retain his knight for a potential kingside attack, but this allows Black to use the f4-square for his own knight: 21...Bxf1 22.Kxf1 Bxd4 ( 22...Qc4+ 23.Kg1 e5 24.Be3 Qxc3 25.Qxc3 Rxc3 26.Bxg5 f6 27.Bd2 Rd3 is unclear according to Iordachescu in Informator 97) 23.cxd4 Nf4 ( 23...Qc3 24.Qxc3 Rxc3 25.Nf5 a5 26.b5 a4 is unclear - Iordachescu) 24.Kf2 Qc2 25.Ra2 with equal chances.

21...Bxe2 22.Bxe2 Bxd4 23.Qxd4 a5!

Black liberates his a8-rook.

24.Kd2

White must retain control over the c5-square by avoiding the hasty advance 24.b5?! Qc5 25.Qxc5 Rxc5 with a favourable endgame for Black; the white bishop has no targets.

24...axb4

(b) 24...Qd8 25.g3 axb4 26.axb4 Ra3 27.Rh5 "with counterplay" according to Iordachescu; Black could then consider 27...e5 28.Qd3 Kg7 to keep the game going.

Game 5 [B90]

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 Ng4 7.Bg5 h6 8.Bh4 g5 9.Bg3 Bg7 10.h3

For comments on the moves up to here, see Game 4.

10...Nf6 (D)

Black now has the idea of ...Qb6 threatening ...Nxe4. 10...Ne5 was the subject of Game 4.

11.Qe2!?

Black has a solid position after the more popular 11.Qf3 Qb6 12.O-O-O Nc6 13.Nxc6 Qxc6 ( 13...bxc6?! 14.e5 dxe5 15.Bxe5 favours White) 14.Be2 ( 14.e5 Qxf3 15.gxf3 dxe5 16.Bxe5 Bd7! is equal, Shirov-Kasparov, Sarajevo 2000) 14...Qc5 15.Rhe1 Be6 16.e5 dxe5 17.Bd3 e4 18.Nxe4 Nxe4 19.Qxe4 O-O-O with equal chances, Lutz-J.Polgar, Budapest 2003.

11.Bc4!? is an interesting pawn sacrifice. 11...Qb6 12.O-O (after 12.Bb3 , 12...Nxe4 13.Qf3 f5 and 12...O-O 13.h4 g4 are both unclear) 12...O-O (not 12...Nxe4? 13.Nxe4 Qxd4?! 14.Nxd6+! exd6 15.Qe2+ ) 13.Nde2?! ( 13.Nb3 Nc6 14.Kh1 Ne5 15.Be2 Ng6 is unclear according to Kasparov) 13...Qxb2 14.Bb3 Qa3 15.f4 Nc6 16.Kh1 Nh5! (more precise than 16...Be6 , as played in the stem game Kasparov-J.Polgar, Wijk aan Zee 2000) 17.Qd3 Qc5 18.Nd5 Nxg3+ 19.Qxg3 Bxa1! 20.Rxa1 Kh7 21.fxg5 Rg8 and Black consolidated his material gains in Azarov-Ruck, European Clubs Cup, Saint Vincent 2005.

11...Nc6

The alternative is 11...Qb6 12.O-O-O Nc6 13.Nxc6 Qxc6 14.e5 (the central pawn-break favours White because in comparison to the 11.Qf3 line, Black is unable to relieve the pressure by exchanging queens) 14...dxe5 15.Bxe5 O-O 16.Qe3 Bf5 17.Be2 Rad8 18.Bf3 Qc8 19.g4 Bg6 20.Rd4 with a slight advantage for White.

12.Nxc6 bxc6 13.e5 dxe5 14.Bxe5 O-O (D)

15.g4!?

Dominguez introduces an interesting new idea to prevent ...Bf5 and fix Black's g-pawn. The usual line is 15.h4 g4 16.g3 (D), and now:

(a) 16...Qb6?! 17.O-O-O Be6 18.Bg2 Rfd8 19.Rhe1 h5 20.b3 favoured White in Ponomariov-J.Polgar, Benidorm rapid 2002. This was the stem game for 11.Qe2 .

(b) 16...Be6 17.Bg2 Qa5 18.O-O Rad8 was suggested by Sammalvuo. Then 19.Rfe1 favours White because of his superior pawn-structure.

(c) 16...a5 17.Bg2 Ba6 18.Qe3 Qd7 (Black must avoid 18...Rc8? 19.Rd1 Nd5? 20.Nxd5 cxd5 21.Rxd5 , which is winning for White, Rowson-de Firmian, Selfoss 2003) 19.Qd4 ( 19.Rd1 Qf5 20.Rd2!? was suggested by Rowson in Informator 89; 20...Rfd8 is an adequate reply as White's king is stuck in the centre) 19...Qxd4 20.Bxd4 e5 (Hoffmann-Kulaots, Budapest 2004) 21.Bb6 Rfb8 22.Bxc6 Bb7 23.Bxb7 Rxb7 24.Na4 Rc8 25.Bxa5 Ra7 26.b4 Rxc2 27.O-O favours White as he has retained his extra pawn.

(d) 16...Bf5 17.Bg2 Qd7 18.O-O Rfd8 19.Rfe1 with a slight advantage for White thanks to his superior pawn-structure, Bacrot-Kariakin, FIDE Grand Prix, Nalchik 2009.

15...a5? (D)

The queenside pawn advance is too slow because it does not address White's threat to prise open the h-file. Black should complete his development by 15...Be6 16.Bg2 ( 16.h4 Qa5!? ) 16...Qa5 , with sharp play.

16.h4!

White sacrifices a pawn to open lines on the kingside. Instead, 16.Bg2 Ba6 17.Qe3 Qd7 18.Qd4 Qxd4 19.Bxd4 e5 20.Bb6 Rfb8 21.Bxc6 Bb7 22.Bxb7 Rxb7 23.Na4 Rc8 24.Bxa5 Ra7 25.b4 Rxc2 26.O-O Nd5 gives Black good compensation for the pawn because of the weakness of the f4-square. This line should be compared to note 'c' to White's 15th move: the structure with pawns on h3 and g4 is less favourable for White than with them on h4 and g3 because here Black has the possibility of ...Nf4 targeting White's h-pawn.

16...Bxg4 17.f3 Bf5 18.hxg5 hxg5 19.Qe3 a4

Kariakin rushes to create queenside counter-play. Instead, 19...e6 20.Qxg5 Ne8 ( 20...Bg6 21.Qg3! intending Bd3 favours White) 21.Qg1 a4 22.Rd1 Qe7 23.Bd3 gives White a powerful attack.

20.Qxg5 Bg6 21.Bd3 a3 (D)

21...Ra5?! 22.O-O-O! Qb6 23.Bxg6 fxg6 24.f4 gives White a decisive advantage as Black cannot defend his kingside.

22.b4?

When in doubt, develop another piece! White should complete the mobilization of his army with 22.O-O-O! , when Black has no adequate defence:

(a) 22...Qb6 23.b3 Nh7?! 24.Rxh7 Kxh7 25.Rh1+ Kg8 26.Bxg7 Kxg7 27.Ne4 is winning for White.

(b) 22...Qa5 23.Bc4! axb2+ 24.Kb1 (not 24.Kxb2?? Rfb8+ 25.Kc1 ( 25.Bb3 Nh7 wins for Black) 25...Ng4 26.fxg4 Qxe5 27.Qxe5 (White's threats peter out after 27.Qxg6 Qf4+ 28.Rd2 Qxc4 29.Qh7+ Kf8 and Black wins) 27...Bxe5 28.Ne2 Rb4 and Black wins material) 24...e6 25.f4! Nh7 26.Rxh7! Bxe5 27.f5 Kxh7 28.Rh1+ Kg8 ( 28...Kg7 29.Ne4 mates) 29.fxg6 Bg7 30.Qh4 and White has a decisive attack.

22...Qb6 23.Rh4 Nh7! (D)

24.Rxh7?

White should settle for 24.Qf4 Bxe5 25.Qxe5 Qg1+ 26.Bf1 Rad8 27.Ne2 with equal chances.

24...Kxh7 25.O-O-O Qxb4 26.Rh1+ Kg8 27.Bxg7?!

Relatively best is 27.Bxg6 fxg6 28.Qxg6 Rf6! 29.Qd3 ( 29.Bxf6? loses to 29...Qf4+ ) 29...Kf7 , although Black has a clear plus because White's attack has run out of firepower.

27...Kxg7 28.Qh6+ (D)

White's attack peters out after 28.Qe5+ f6 29.Qg3 f5 30.Ne2 Ra4 and Black has a decisive material advantage.

28...Kf6 29.Ne4+ Ke6 30.Rd1 Qb2+ 31.Kd2 Kd7 32.Qf4

White was in extreme time-pressure here but the end result is the same after 37.Rh4 R5d7 38.Qg3 Qa1 : Black wins.
