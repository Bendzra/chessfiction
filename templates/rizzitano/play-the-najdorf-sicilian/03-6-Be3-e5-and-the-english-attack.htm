3 6.Be3 e5 and the English Attack

The most popular response to 6.Be3 is the traditional Najdorf move 6...e5 , by which Black establishes a central strongpoint and stakes a claim to the important d4- and f4-squares at the cost of a slight weakening of the d5-square. The e5-pawn stabilizes the centre and restricts White's ability to attack the black king through the centre; the lines commencing with 7.Nb3 typically feature the delayed development of White's light-squared bishop in comparison to other Najdorf lines. The strategic complexity in the middlegame is attractive to players at all levels seeking to outplay their opponents; many of these lines are on the cutting edge of opening theory.

The English Attack with 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 has been played with both colours by most of the world's top players over the past decade: Kasparov, Kramnik, Anand, Topalov, Leko, Shirov, Gelfand, Svidler, J.Polgar, Morozevich, Grishchuk, Carlsen, Kariakin and many other great players have all enriched the line with exciting games and interesting novelties. I anticipate steady growth in the popularity of these lines as many creative new ideas continue to be developed and refined for both sides.


Game 6 (Ivanchuk-Topalov) features the positional line 6.Be3 e5 7.Nf3 , which has quite different themes from the English Attack. The kingside retreat applies pressure on the centre and discourages Black from liberating his position with the ...d5 pawn-break, but the f3-knight interferes with the launch of a kingside pawn-storm involving f3 and g4. Ivanchuk introduces a concrete idea to exploit the weakness of Black's b6-square and his creativity is rewarded when Topalov develops his queen's knight to the wrong square. Ivanchuk's middlegame build-up is quite impressive as he is able to create pressure in the centre by doubling rooks along the d-file and tying Black's rooks down to the defence of his backward d-pawn. White then opens a second front on the queenside with a4. Topalov responds by sacrificing a pawn for piece activity, but it is not enough as Ivanchuk carefully consolidates his pawn advantage. Then a rare tactical oversight by Topalov quickly ends the game.


In Game 7 (Caruana-Vallejo Pons), White plays the traditional English Attack line 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 ; after 7...Be6 8.f3 Be7 9.Qd2 , Black opts for the hypermodern idea 9...h5 to inhibit White from expanding on the kingside with g4. Caruana completes his development and builds up in the centre; after Vallejo Pons castles kingside, White shifts gears and expends a tempo to open the g-file for attack. Black manages to withstand the first assault, but after a series of inaccuracies by both sides, White is able to sacrifice the exchange to generate a kingside attack. Vallejo Pons's king is forced out into the open and quickly perishes from overexposure. This game is a classic example demonstrating the strength of a knight outpost on the f5-square versus a passive black rook.


Game 8 (Inarkiev-Siugirov) is another traditional English Attack featuring the popular line 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.f3 Be7 9.Qd2 , and here Black varies from Game 7 by playing 9...O-O . The theory of this variation has been worked out in depth; many of the lines feature classic attacks and counterattacks as the opposing kings are tucked away on opposite sides of the board. White chooses the ambitious line 14.f4 to challenge Black's e6-bishop; the aggressive pseudo-sacrifice 16.Nbd4 is met several moves later by 19...Bb3 20 axb3 axb3, opening the a-file against the white king. Black sacrifices the exchange to keep his pieces active and after a further skirmish the game appears headed for a draw. Inarkiev avoids an opportunity to steer the game toward a perpetual check; Siugirov never looks back as he wins two pieces for a rook and simplifies into a winning rook endgame. This game is a great example of the importance of maintaining the initiative: every tempo is critical as the players strive to attack the opposing king.

Game 6 [B90]
Vasily Ivanchuk - Veselin Topalov
Morelia/Linares 2007

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 (D)

6...e5

6...Ng4 was covered in Games 4 and 5.

7.Nf3

White returns the knight to the kingside; this gives him attacking possibilities later on involving Nh4 or Ng5, but the downside is that the f3-knight is a long way from controlling the d5-square. The main drawback to having the knight on f3 is that it prevents White from generating a kingside pawn-storm with f3 and g4.

7.Nb3 is White's most aggressive and probably strongest continuation. This move is the subject of Games 7 and 8.

The retreat 7.Nde2 is a speciality of Romanian GM Liviu-Dieter Nisipeanu; White avoids blocking his f-pawn at the cost of temporarily blocking his light-squared bishop. 7...Be6 8.f4 Nbd7 (Black can retain light-squared bishops by 8...g6 9.h3 h5 10.fxe5 dxe5 11.Qxd8+ Kxd8 12.O-O-O+ Nbd7 with a balanced game, Nisipeanu-Topalov, Sofia 2007) 9.f5 Bc4 10.Nc1 (D) and then:

(a) 10...Qc7 11.Bxc4 Qxc4 12.Qd3 Rc8 13.Nb3 ( 13.N1e2 looks safer) 13...Qxd3 14.cxd3 d5 1/2-1/2 Fedorov-Gashimov, Abu Dhabi 2006. A possible continuation is 15.exd5 Bb4 16.Bd2 O-O 17.O-O Rfd8 with an even game.

(b) 10...d5 11.Bxc4 ( 11.exd5 Bxf1 12.Rxf1 , Nisipeanu-Kempinski, European Ch, Dresden 2007, 12...Bb4! 13.N1e2 Nb6 14.Qd3 Nbxd5 15.O-O-O Nxc3 16.Nxc3 Qxd3 17.Rxd3 Rd8 is equal according to Nisipeanu in Informator 99) 11...dxc4 12.Qf3 b5 ( 12...Bc5 13.N1e2 O-O also equalizes) 13.N1e2 b4 14.Nd5 Nxd5 15.exd5 Nf6 16.O-O-O Bd6 with sharp play ahead, Nisipeanu-Topalov, Vitoria Gasteiz 2007.

We now return to 7.Nf3 (D):

7...Be7

This is the most popular choice: Black develops a piece and prepares kingside castling. Alternatives:

(a) 7...Nc6 8.Bc4 Be7 9.O-O O-O transposes to the note to Black's 9th move.

(b) 7...b5?! is premature, since White can undermine the b-pawn with 8.a4! b4 9.Nd5 Nbd7 10.Bc4! Nxe4 11.a5 Rb8 12.Bb6 Nxb6 13.axb6 Rxb6 14.Qe2 Nc5 15.Nxb6 Qxb6 16.Ng5 , when he has a strong attack, Lauk-S.Spasov, ICCF corr. 2006.

(c) 7...h6?! (Black prevents a white piece from occupying g5 at the cost of weakening g6 and f5) 8.Bc4 Be7 9.O-O O-O 10.Nh4! Be6 11.Bxe6 (exchanging bishops is the logical follow-up so that White can occupy the g6-square; less effective is 11.Bb3 Nc6 with equal chances, Medvegy-Bischoff, Bundesliga 2004/5) 11...fxe6 12.Ng6 Rf7 13.Qf3 ( 13.Qe2!? ) 13...Nc6 14.Qh3 Qd7 15.Rad1 with slight pressure for White.

(d) 7...Be6 (Black prevents White from playing Bc4; the bishop development has a poor reputation because White can gain the bishop-pair, but in return Black obtains control over d5) 8.Ng5 (D) and now:

(d1) 8...Qc7?! is probably too committal: 9.Nxe6 fxe6 10.a4 ( 10.g4!? also looks promising for White, Werner-Zuse, 2nd Bundesliga 1996/7) 10...Nbd7 11.g3 Qc6 12.Bg2 Nc5?! (Black is not threatening to capture the e-pawn so 12...Be7 13.O-O Nb6 looks like a safer plan) 13.O-O Be7 14.Qe2 with an edge for White thanks to his bishop-pair and prospects for opening the position with a timely f4 pawn advance, Hellers-de Firmian, Wijk aan Zee 1986.

(d2) 8...Nc6 (the knight move is more flexible) 9.Nxe6 fxe6 10.Bc4 Qd7 ( 10...d5?! 11.exd5 Na5 12.Be2 exd5 13.Bg5 favours White) 11.a4 ( 11.O-O Be7 12.Bb3!? ) 11...Be7 12.O-O Rc8 13.Qe2 O-O with a solid position for Black, Kariakin-Anand, Amber Blindfold, Nice 2008. This line needs more tests.

(e) 7...Qc7 (White is prevented from playing Bc4, but it may be premature to commit the queen this early) 8.a4 Be7 9.Be2 (the bishop development appears to be more dangerous than the popular 9.a5 ) 9...O-O 10.O-O b6 and now 11.Qd3! (D) is an idea of English GM Nigel Short:

(e1) 11...Bb7 12.Nh4 g6 13.Bh6 Rd8 14.Nf5! Bf8 (Wojtaszek-Lanin, World Under-16 Ch, Kallithea 2003) 15.Bg5 Nbd7 16.Ne3 (White has a slight advantage according to Short in Informator 67) 16...Rdc8 17.f3 Qc5 18.Rfd1 h6 19.Bh4 d5!? 20.exd5 Bxd5 21.Ncxd5 ( 21.Bf2 Be6 ) 21...Nxd5 22.Bf2 Nxe3 23.Rd2! favours White's bishop-pair on an open board.

(e2) 11...Nbd7 12.Qc4! Qd8 ( 12...Qxc4? 13.Bxc4 Bb7 14.Nd2 favoured White thanks to his control over d5 in Short-Gelfand, Novgorod 1996 and several later games) 13.Rfd1 Bb7 14.Nd2 Rc8 15.Qa2!? Nc5 16.f3 Ne6 with complex play.

8.Bc4 (D)

The bishop takes control of the critical d5-square along with the important a2-g8 diagonal. White has a couple of harmless alternatives:

(a) 8.h3 O-O 9.Bc4 Nc6 10.O-O Na5 11.Bd3 b5 is equal, Arencibia-Abreu, Santa Clara 2008.

(b) 8.a4 O-O 9.a5 Nc6 10.Bb6 Qe8 11.Bc4 Be6 12.Bd5 Rc8 with a comfortable position for Black as White lacks any effective pawn-breaks, Meijers-L'Ami, Dieren 2001.

8...O-O

Black's most popular alternative is the immediate 8...Be6 9.Bxe6 fxe6 10.Ng5 Qd7 11.Qf3 , and here:

(a) 11...Nc6 12.Qh3! Nd8 ( 12...d5 13.Na4! with strong pressure, Lederer-Lederman, Beersheba 1998) 13.Bb6 h6! 14.O-O-O ( 14.Bxd8 Bxd8 15.Qxe6+ ( 15.Nxe6 Ba5! "with good compensation" - Sammalvuo) 15...Qxe6 16.Nxe6 Ba5 is fine for Black) 14...O-O 15.Bxd8 hxg5 16.Bxe7 Qxe7 17.Qg3 favours White thanks to his superior pawn-structure.

(b) 11...d5 12.exd5 exd5 13.O-O-O d4 14.Nce4 O-O 15.Bd2 Nxe4! ( 15...Nc6?! 16.Nxf6+ Bxf6 17.Qb3+ Kh8 18.h4 b5 19.Rh3 (Veroci-Savova, Women's Zonal, Brno 1989) 19...Rae8 20.f3 favours White because of his knight outpost on e4) 16.Qb3+ ( 16.Qxe4 Bxg5 17.Bxg5 Rxf2 18.Qxe5 (Rakowiecki-Jaworski, Bielsko Biala 1990) 18...Nc6 19.Qg3 Qf7 with a slight advantage to Black thanks to his more secure king position) 16...Kh8 17.Nxe4 a5! 18.f3 a4 19.Qd3 (Gipslis-Roeschlau, Frankfurt 1989) 19...a3 20.b3 Na6! with a comfortable position for Black as he can initiate counterplay along the c-file and challenge White's knight with a timely ...Nc5.

9.O-O (D)

9...Be6

Black has a reasonable alternative in 9...Nc6 (and not 9...b5?? 10.Bd5 Nxd5 11.Qxd5 1-0 Smirin-Huizer, Groningen 1996) 10.Re1 b5 ( 10...Be6 11.Nd5 Bxd5 12.exd5 b5!? 13.Bf1 Nb4 14.c4 bxc4 15.Bxc4 Qa5 16.Bd2 Qc5 17.Rc1 Nbxd5 18.Bg5 Qa5 with sharp play, Gao Rui-Negi, Subic Bay 2009) 11.Bf1 Rb8!? 12.Bg5 (the idea behind Black's previous move is that 12.Nd5? Nxe4 costs White a pawn since Black controls the b6-square) 12...Ng4! (Black offers to exchange dark-squared bishops; he retains the knight in order to contest the d5-square) 13.Bd2 ( 13.Bxe7 Nxe7 14.a3 Bb7 is equal, Short-Ehlvest, Horgen 1995; this was the stem game for 11...Rb8!? ) 13...Be6 14.Nd5 h6 15.h3 Nf6 16.c4 Qd7 17.b3 Bd8! (Topalov prepares to activate his dark-squared bishop) 18.Rc1 Bxd5 19.cxd5 Ne7 20.Bd3 Bb6 with equality, Anand-Topalov, Morelia/Linares 2007. Topalov was well-prepared for this 8th-round game; our main game was contested in the 2nd round.

We now return to 9...Be6 (D):

10.Bxe6!?

Prior to this game, the exchange of bishops on e6 was considered harmless because it strengthens Black's centre and gives him control over d5. Ivanchuk's fresh insight was that Black has some tactical problems due to the weakness of the b6-square. Alternatives:

(a) 10.Bd5 Bxd5 11.Nxd5 Nbd7 is fine for Black as he has retained a knight to control d5, Martinsons-Neborak, corr. 2000.

(b) 10.Qe2 ( 10.Qd3 Bxc4 11.Qxc4 is equivalent) 10...Bxc4 11.Qxc4 b5 12.Qd3 b4! 13.Nd5 Nxd5 14.Qxd5 Nd7 15.Qd3 ( 15.a3 Qb8 is fine for Black) 15...Qc7 16.Rac1 ( 16.a3 Rfb8 is equal - Ki.Georgiev and Kolev) 16...Qb7 with equal chances, Vachier-Lagrave - Guidarelli, French Ch, Aix-les-Bains 2007.

(c) 10.Bb3 Nc6 11.Bg5 Nd7! (a similar idea was seen in the note to Black's 9th move (12...Ng4!): Black is willing to exchange dark-squared bishops, but he does not want to allow White to exchange his dark-squared bishop for one of the black knights as this would enable White to increase his control over d5) 12.Bxe7 Qxe7 13.Nd5 Qd8 14.Qe2 Na5 15.Rad1 Rc8 with an even game, Leko-Van Wely, Amber Rapid, Nice 2008.

10...fxe6 11.Na4 (D)

11...Ng4

Black attempts to eliminate White's dark-squared bishop. Alternatives:

(a) 11...Nbd7?! (Black drops the e6-pawn but this move is much better than its reputation) 12.Ng5 Qa5 13.Nc3 ( 13.b3 d5!? 14.Nxe6 Rfc8 is unclear) 13...Rfc8 ( 13...d5!? 14.Nxe6 d4 15.Nxf8 Rxf8 16.Bxd4 exd4 17.Qxd4 Bc5 18.Qc4+ Kh8 19.h3 favours White as he should be able, with care, to mobilize his kingside pawns) 14.Nxe6 Qb4 15.Nd5!? ( 15.Rb1 Qc4 was fine for Black as she recovered her pawn in Houska-Ding Yixin, UK-China match, Liverpool 2007) 15...Nxd5 16.exd5 ( 16.Qxd5 Nf6 ) 16...Qe4 ( 16...Qxb2? loses to 17.Rb1 ) 17.c3 Nf6 18.Bg5 Qxd5 19.Bxf6 Qxd1 ( 19...Qxe6 20.Bxe7 Qxe7 21.Qd5+ Kh8 22.Rad1 Rd8 23.Rd3 favours White because of his superior pawn-structure) 20.Raxd1 gxf6 21.f4 Kf7 22.f5 with just a slight advantage to White thanks to his control over the d5-square.

(b) 11...Nfd7 covers b6 and avoids the issues associated with allowing White to play Ng5 as in line 'a' above. Now:

(b1) 12.Qd3 b5 13.Qb3 (or 13.Nc3 Nf6 14.a4 b4 15.Ne2 Nc6 with equal chances, Lorand-Humphrey, Budapest 2008) 13...bxa4 14.Qb7 Rxf3! 15.gxf3 ( 15.Qxa8?? Rxe3 16.fxe3 Qc7 , with the idea of ...Nb6, traps the white queen) 15...Bg5 (D).

White must choose carefully:

(b11) 16.Qxa8? Bxe3 17.Qb7 ( 17.fxe3 Qc7 again traps the queen) 17...Bf4 with a clear plus for Black.

(b12) 16.f4? exf4 17.Bd4 f3 18.Kh1 Nc6! (the knight move is clearer than 18...e5?! 19.Qxa8 exd4 20.Qd5+ Kh8 21.Qxd6 Bf6 with "a highly unbalanced position, but I prefer the three black pieces against the two white rooks" according to Kolev in his New in Chess Yearbook 84 article on this line; however, I think White can disrupt the coordination between Black's minor pieces by 22.Rad1 Qe8 23.e5!? Bxe5 24.Rfe1 a3 25.Qxa3 Qh5 26.Rxe5 Qxe5 27.Qxf3 h6 28.Qf7 Qe4+ 29.f3 Qxc2 30.Rg1 Qh7 31.Rd1 Qc2 32.Rg1 with a draw by repetition) 19.Qxc6 Bf4 20.Rg1 Ne5 21.Qb7 g6 with an advantage for Black because the white king is vulnerable.

(b13) 16.Bxg5? Qxg5+ 17.Kh1 Qd8! 18.Rad1 ( 18.Rg1? Qb6 19.Qxb6 Nxb6 20.Rad1 d5 with a decisive advantage for Black, Gao Rui-Sandipan, Subic 2009) 18...Nb6 19.Rfe1 ( 19.c4? loses to 19...N8d7 20.Rxd6 Qf8 , while 19.Rg1 N8d7 20.Rxd6 Rb8 21.Qa7 (Kislik-Denisov, Budapest 2009) 21...Qc8! 22.Rgd1 Ra8 23.Rxd7 Rxa7 24.Rxa7 h6 gives Black a decisive material advantage) 19...N8d7 20.Rxd6 Rb8 21.Qxa6 Qf8 favours Black as the f3-pawn falls.

(b14) 16.Rfe1! (White waits for Black to exchange bishops so that his rook defends the f3-pawn) 16...Bxe3 17.Rxe3 Nb6 18.Rd1 N8d7 19.Rxd6 Rb8 20.Qxa6 Qc7 21.Rd1 Qxc2 22.Rxd7 Nxd7 23.Qxe6+ Kf8 24.Qxd7 Rxb2 25.Qd8+ Kf7 26.Qd5+ Kf8 27.Qd8+ with a draw by repetition.

(b2) 12.c4 Nc6 (D) and here:

(b21) 13.Rc1 Rc8 ( 13...Nc5 14.Nxc5 1/2-1/2 Bindrich-Brkic, European Team Ch, Dresden 2007; after the recapture 14...dxc5 15.Ne1!? Nd4 16.Nd3 Qd6 the chances are equal) 14.c5!? dxc5 15.Qb3 Nd4 16.Bxd4 exd4 17.Qxb7 Rxf3!? 18.gxf3 Bd6 19.Rfd1 Rb8 20.Qxa6 Qf8 21.Kf1 Qf4 gave Black sufficient compensation for the exchange in Das-Gharamian, Calvia 2007.

(b22) 13.b4 Nxb4 14.Rb1 Nc6 15.Rxb7 Na5 16.Rxd7 Qxd7 17.Nb6 Qc6 18.Nxa8 Rxa8 ( 18...Nxc4!? ) 19.c5 1/2-1/2 Fedorchuk-Borovikov, Ukrainian Games, Evpatoria 2007.

(b23) 13.Qb3 Na5 14.Qd3 Qe8!? ( 14...Qc7 15.Rac1 Rac8 ( 15...Nc5 16.Qa3 enabled White to develop some queenside pressure in E.Berg-O.Moor, Politiken Cup, Elsinore 2008) 16.c5!? dxc5 17.b4 c4 18.Qc3 b5 19.bxa5 bxa4 with rough equality, Kolev-Alsina Leal, Navalmoral 2007) 15.Rad1 ( 15.a3!? ) 15...Qg6 with equal chances. I anticipate further developments in these lines.

12.Qd3 Nxe3

A relatively new idea for Black is 12...Nc6 13.Qb3 Rf6 14.Bg5 ( 14.Bb6!? ) 14...Na5 15.Qd3 ( 15.Qb6 Rf7 16.Bxe7 Rxe7 is equal, Inarkiev-Morozevich, Russian Ch, Moscow 2008) 15...Rg6!? 16.Bxe7 Qxe7 17.h3 Nh6 18.c4 Nf7 with a balanced position.

13.Qxe3 b5 14.Nb6 (D)

14...Ra7

Black hits tactical problems after 14...Rxf3? 15.gxf3 Bg5 (Offenborn-P.Schuster, corr. 2000) 16.f4! Bxf4 (after 16...exf4 17.Qh3 Qxb6 18.Qxe6+ Kf8 19.Qd5 White wins a piece) 17.Qh3 Qxb6 18.Qxe6+ Kf8 19.Rfd1 Bxh2+ 20.Kg2 with a decisive advantage for White according to Ivanchuk in Informator 99.

15.Nd5 Rb7

Black defends the sensitive b6-square. Alternatives:

(a) 15...Rd7? 16.Nxe7+ Qxe7 17.Rad1 Nc6 18.Qb6 Rc7 19.c3 favours White, A.Sokolov-Z.Stankovic, Basle 2009. Black's queenside pawns are weak and White can also increase the pressure by doubling rooks along the d-file.

(b) 15...Nc6 16.Qd3 Qe8 17.Nxe7+ Qxe7 18.Rfd1 Rd8 19.a4 ( 19.c4 , with a slight advantage for White, was suggested by Ivanchuk) 19...Rb7 20.axb5 axb5 21.Qb3 with an edge for White, V.Spasov-Hernandez Leon, La Laguna 2007. White followed up with Ra6 to increase the pressure.

16.Qd2! (D)

Ivanchuk selects the correct square for his queen; it is important to stay beyond the range of the black knight, as demonstrated in line 'b' below. Less precise are:

(a) 16.Nxe7+ Qxe7 17.Rfd1 Rc8 ( 17...Nc6? 18.Qa3! highlights the weakness of Black's pawns, Fedorchuk-Dydyshko, Ustron 2007) 18.c3 Nd7 with equal chances.

(b) 16.Qd3 Nd7 17.Nxe7+ ( 17.Rad1 Nc5! 18.Nxe7+ Rxe7 19.Qe3 (after 19.Qxd6? Rd7 Black wins the exchange) 19...Rd7 is comfortable for Black as he has defended his vulnerable pawns) 17...Qxe7 18.Rfd1 Rb6 with a balanced game, Khairullin-Mozharov, Moscow 2008.

16...Nc6?!

Topalov elects to complete his development, but c6 is not a stable post for the knight. Black should prefer one of these alternatives:

(a) 16...Nd7 17.Rad1 Nc5 18.Nxe7+ Rxe7 19.Nxe5 Nxe4 20.Qe3 Qa8 21.Rfe1!? (the rook move is more precise than 21.f3 dxe5 22.fxe4 , when White keeps a small advantage according to Leon Hoyos in New In Chess Magazine 2007/3, but I think Black can equalize after 22...Rxf1+ 23.Rxf1 ( 23.Kxf1 Rf7+ 24.Kg1 h6 is equal) 23...Qd8 24.c3 Rd7 with the idea of infiltrating along the d-file) 21...d5 22.f3 Nf6 23.Qd4 with a slight advantage for White because he can double rooks along the e-file to apply pressure to Black's backward e6-pawn.

(b) 16...Bh4 17.Nxh4 Qxh4 18.Ne3 Rd8! (J.Hernandez-Macayo, ICCF e-mail 2007; Black leaves the rook on b7 to defend the queenside) 19.Qd3 d5 20.exd5 exd5 21.Qb3 Qa4! is equal.

(c) 16...Qd7 17.Nxe7+ Qxe7 18.Rfd1 Rb6 19.a4 ( 19.Qe3 Rc6 is similar to line 'd' below) 19...bxa4 20.Rxa4 Rxb2 21.Rb4 Rxb4 22.Qxb4 Nc6! 23.Qb6 ( 23.Qxd6?? loses to 23...Rd8 ) 23...Qd7 24.Qxa6 1/2-1/2 Strautins-Karkuth, ICCF e-mail 2007.

(d) 16...Kh8 17.Nxe7 Qxe7 18.Rfd1 Rb6 19.Qe3 Rc6 20.c3 Nd7 21.a4 Nc5 22.axb5 axb5 23.Ra5 Rb8 24.Rda1 h6 (after 24...b4? 25.Rb5! White wins a pawn) 25.Nd2 ( 25.Qe2 b4 26.Rb5?! Rcb6 27.Rxb6 1/2-1/2 D.Popovic-P.Popovic, Zlatibor 2007; after 27...Rxb6 28.Nd2 bxc3 29.bxc3 Rb2 30.Qe3 Qb7 Black has a slight advantage as he has saddled White with a weak c-pawn) 25...b4 26.c4 Kh7 with equal chances.

17.Rad1 Rd7 18.Qc3! Nb8

Black's knight quietly returns home, but 18...exd5 19.Qxc6 d4 20.c3 dxc3 21.Qxc3 favours White because of his superior minor piece.

19.Nxe7+ Qxe7 (D)

20.Rd3

More precise is 20.Rd2! Rfd8 21.Qa3 Rc8 22.Rfd1 Rc4 23.Rxd6 Rxc2 24.h4 (the idea behind the pawn move is to avoid any possible back-rank issues) 24...Rxd6 25.Qxd6 Qxd6 26.Rxd6 Nc6 27.b3 Kf8 28.Rxe6 Nb4 29.Rxe5 Rxa2 30.Ng5 with a clear plus for White according to Ivanchuk.

20...h6 21.Rfd1 Rfd8 22.h4

White creates luft for his king. The immediate 22.a4!? , to weaken Black's queenside pawns, is also strong.

22...Kh7 23.R1d2 Qf8 24.Qb3 Qe8 25.a4 Qg6!?

Topalov plays in accordance with his energetic style by offering a pawn to activate his knight. 25...bxa4 26.Qxa4 leaves Black in a passive position, where White can advance his queenside pawns and carefully prepare the b5 or c5 pawn-break.

26.axb5 axb5 27.Re3 Na6

The knight re-emerges but it is too late to defend Black's queenside.

28.Qxb5 Nc5 29.Qc4

Another way is 29.Qc6! Ra7 30.Rxd6 Rxd6 31.Qxd6 Nxe4 32.Qxe5 Ra1+ 33.Re1 with a decisive advantage for White according to Ivanchuk.

29...Ra7 30.Re1

Also strong is 30.b4 Ra1+ 31.Ne1 Na6 32.c3 , when Black's knight is hopelessly out of play.

30...Qe8 31.b4 Na4 32.Qb3 Nb6 33.Red1 Rad7 34.Qd3 Rc8 35.c3 Ra7 36.Qe3 Ra6 37.Qe2 Nc4 38.Ra2 Rac6 39.Ra7 R6c7 40.Rda1 Qf7??

Black overlooks a tactical idea.

41.Qxc4 1-0

After 41...Rxa7 42.Rxa7 White wins a piece.

A smooth win for Ivanchuk but a tough loss for Topalov as he was unable to demonstrate his trademark dynamic playing style.

Game 7 [B90]
Fabiano Caruana - Francisco Vallejo Pons
Pamplona 2008

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e5 7.Nb3 Be6 (D)

8.f3

This is the traditional English Attack move: White bolsters his centre and at the same time prepares to attack on the kingside with g4. Alternatives:

(a) 8.Qd2 Be7 9.f3 is another route to the position encountered in the main game.

(b) 8.Be2 Be7 9.O-O O-O transposes to a line of the Classical Najdorf (Chapter 5); this position is the subject of Game 12.
