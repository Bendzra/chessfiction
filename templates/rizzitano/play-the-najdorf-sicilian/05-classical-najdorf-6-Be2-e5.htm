5 Classical Najdorf: 6.Be2 e5

The Classical 6.Be2 is a popular choice versus the Najdorf Sicilian: White develops his light-squared bishop to the e2-square and prepares to castle kingside. This relatively modest bishop development should not be underestimated; although White rarely develops quick pressure against e6 and f7 in comparison to more overtly aggressive lines, the bishop on e2 can sometimes emerge with a vengeance on g4 or h5 in support of a kingside attack.

Let's examine some of the important issues to be aware of in the Classical in comparison to other Najdorf lines:

• White's light-squared bishop is not a target for Black's normal plan of queenside expansion involving ...b5. In fact, White can often respond to this thrust by playing a4 to undermine the b5-pawn and seek to gain control of c4.

• A bishop is very effective at restricting a knight posted three squares vertically or horizontally away from it. I call this fanning a knight as the bishop's scope resembles the shape of a hand fan. In some lines of the Classical Najdorf, the white bishop on e2 fans a black knight posted on e5: the bishop controls d3, c4, f3 and g4, squares that are all within range of the e5-knight.

• The bishop is able to assist in the defence of White's e4-pawn by shifting to f3 if needed.


The Classical Najdorf has been closely identified with two great players over the years: world championship candidate Efim Geller and the 12th World Champion Anatoly Karpov. Geller played the Classical for more than 40 years, racking up a tremendous plus score against the strongest opposition. Karpov infused the line with many new ideas beginning with his 1974 Candidates match victory over Lev Polugaevsky; Karpov scored 3 wins and 1 draw with the Classical. The first two Karpov-Kasparov World Championship matches also featured a number of games with 6.Be2 , but Kasparov always responded with 6...e6 and headed for the Scheveningen Variation. The Classical Najdorf has been played at least once by the last ten World Champions, and today leading players including Leko and Shirov are among its adherents.


Game 11 (Arencibia-Quezada) features the line 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.Kh1 . White's precautionary king move is a modern refinement to see Black's reply before determining how to proceed. Quezada plays one of the main lines featuring 9...Nc6 and follows a theoretical line where Black exchanges a pair of knights on the queenside to free his position. White pursues a gradual build-up on the kingside and Black counters by maintaining a strongpoint on e5. Black eventually goes astray by stranding his dark-squared bishop on the h4-square; Arencibia is able to exploit the loose piece by transforming the pawn-structure to obtain a powerful passed d-pawn. Black still has some opportunities for resistance, but a further error enables White to force home the passed pawn. The middlegame is a good demonstration of how one inaccuracy by the defender can allow the aggressor to transform the position quickly to his advantage.


In Game 12 (Naiditsch-Gelfand), White employs the Karpov plan 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.Be3 Be6 10.Qd2 , followed by the traditional pawn advance a4-a5 to restrict Black's counterplay. Black counters with a timely ...Nc5 and after an exchange of knights on this square, the players battle for control of key squares on the queenside. At one point the game appears headed for a threefold repetition, but Naiditsch gamely varies and Gelfand responds in kind by expanding aggressively on the kingside. The game is very deceptive because it begins quietly but then erupts into sharp tactics thanks to the adventurous play by the combatants.

Game 11 [B92]
Walter Arencibia - Yuniesky Quezada
Santa Clara 2007

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 (D)

The characteristic Najdorf move. Black's other options include:

(a) 6...g6 can lead to positions more akin to the Dragon; for example, 7.O-O Bg7 8.Be3 O-O 9.f4 Nbd7 10.a4 Qc7 11.Kh1 Nb6 and the game is just beginning, Galkin-Timofeev, European Ch, Dresden 2007.

(b) 6...e6 7.O-O Be7 transposes to a line of the Scheveningen Variation (ECO code B85).

7.Nb3

White has two harmless alternatives:

(a) 7.Nf5?! (this move would be strategically desirable for White if only he had time to clamp down on the d5-square with Ne3, but there is a tactical flaw) 7...d5! (the freeing pawn advance is the usual response to an early Nf5) 8.Bg5 d4 9.Bxf6 ( 9.Nd5?? Bxf5 costs White a piece) 9...Qxf6 (Black could consider the adventurous 9...gxf6!? 10.Nb1 Qb6 11.Nd2 Qxb2 12.O-O Qa3 13.c3!? dxc3 14.Nc4 Qc5 15.Rc1 Bxf5 16.exf5 Nc6 17.Rxc3 Rd8 with an advantage for Black as White has inadequate compensation for the missing pawn) 10.Nd5 Qd8 11.c4 Nc6 with a comfortable position for Black thanks to his bishop-pair and passed d-pawn, King-A.Petrosian, Belgium 1991.

(b) 7.Nf3?! (the reader should compare this position to the line 6.Be3 e5 7.Nf3 , which we examined in Game 6; here the knight retreat is ineffective since White's light-squared bishop has already been developed and White will lose a tempo moving the piece to the more desirable c4-square) 7...h6 (the idea is to prevent White from increasing his control over d5 with the standard manoeuvre Bg5 followed by Bxf6) 8.O-O Be6 9.Re1 (White's f3-knight inhibits his natural kingside pawn-break with f4, so he initiates a manoeuvre to protect his e4-pawn in preparation for Nd5) 9...Be7 10.Bf1 O-O 11.h3 Nbd7 (Black's queen's knight is usually more effective here than on c6 because he now has the option of playing ...Nc5 to increase the pressure on White's e4-pawn) 12.Nd5 Nxd5 (this capture would not be possible with the knight on c6 as White's recapture would fork two pieces) 13.exd5 Bf5 14.c4 a5 is equal, Drenchev-Clemens, Groningen 2007. Black has smoothly completed his development and secured the c5-square for his knight.

7...Be7 (D)

8.O-O

White's main alternative is to fight for control over the d5-square with 8.Bg5 . Then:

(a) 8...Nbd7?! is inaccurate on account of 9.a4! b6 10.Bc4 Bb7 11.Qe2 O-O 12.O-O Qc7 13.Rfd1 with a slight advantage to White because of his firm grip on d5; an instructive example is Kramnik-Gelfand, Cap d'Agde rapid 2003.

This position has occurred more than 30 times in my database and practice has shown that Black has difficulty generating counterplay.

(b) 8...Be6 9.Bxf6 Bxf6 10.Qd3 Nc6 11.O-O-O Qb6!? (Black offers a temporary pawn sacrifice to activate his pieces; I expect this idea to gain in popularity in comparison to the more popular alternatives 11...Nd4 and 11...Be7 ) 12.Qxd6 Be7 13.Nd5 Bxd5 14.Qxd5 O-O (or 14...Rd8 15.Qc4 Rxd1+ 16.Rxd1 Qxf2 with equality, Stellwagen-L' Ami, Dutch Ch, Hilversum 2008) 15.Qd2 Qxf2 is equal, Anand-Topalov. Morelia/Linares 2008. Black has recovered the sacrificed pawn and the game was soon drawn.

8...O-O (D)

9.Kh1

White plays a useful waiting move to see what set-up Black adopts before deciding how to continue.

Alternatives:

(a) 9.Be3 is covered in Game 12.

(b) 9.f4?! b5! (Black immediately targets White's e4-pawn) 10.a3 Bb7 11.Bf3 Nbd7 12.Qe1 Rc8 13.Qg3 Rxc3!? (the thematic Najdorf exchange sacrifice to destroy White's centre) 14.bxc3 Nxe4 15.Qe1 f5 gave Black good play for her small material deficit in Nimmy-Karavade, Indian Women's Ch, Pune 2007.

(c) 9.Bg5 Be6 10.Bxf6 ( 10.f4 exf4 11.Bxf4 transposes to note 'b1' to White's 10th move in Game 12) 10...Bxf6 11.a4 (White hopes to utilize his control over d5 to create a queenside bind) 11...Nc6 12.Nd5 Bg5 13.a5 Rc8 14.Bg4 Nb8!? 15.Bxe6 fxe6 16.Nb6 Rc6 17.c3 Nd7 is equal, Areshchenko-Kariakin, Russian Team Ch, Sochi 2006.

(d) 9.a4 Be6 (this is a straightforward way for Black to equalize; a reasonable alternative is 9...Nc6 , transposing to Game 25) 10.f4 exf4 11.Bxf4 Nc6 12.Kh1 d5! (this central break usually equalizes for Black; White's a4-pawn can sometimes become a target in the resulting endgames) 13.e5 Ne4 (after 13...Nd7 14.Nxd5 Ndxe5 15.Nxe7+ Qxe7 16.Qe1 , as in Mestel-Nunn, Zonal Playoff, Leiden 1982, Nunn's suggestion of 16...f6 is fine for Black thanks to his powerful knight outpost on the e5-square) 14.Bd3 f5 15.exf6 Bxf6 16.Nxe4 dxe4 17.Bxe4 Qxd1 18.Raxd1 Bc4 1/2-1/2 Belikov-Kovaliov, Alushta 2008.

We now return to 9.Kh1 (D):

9...Nc6

Black has a wide choice here so I shall focus on two of the alternatives:

(a) 9...b5 was Black's original choice, but attention soon turned toward other moves as practice showed that White was able to generate some pressure by coaxing Black's queenside pawns forward with 10.a4 :

(a1) 10...Bb7 11.Nd5 Nbd7?! ( 11...b4 is line 'a2') 12.Nxe7+ Qxe7 13.f3 d5 14.exd5 Bxd5 15.axb5 axb5 16.Rxa8 Bxa8 17.Bxb5 gave White a large advantage in Stellwagen-Bu Xiangzhi, Wijk aan Zee 2007. Black has insufficient compensation for the lost pawn.

(a2) 10...b4 11.Nd5 Bb7 12.Nxe7+ Qxe7 13.f3 (White reinforces the e4-pawn with the intention of slowly opening the position for his bishop-pair, so Black opts to crack open the centre) 13...d5 14.exd5 Bxd5 15.Bg5 Rd8 16.Qe1 Nc6 17.Qf2 h6 18.Be3 Bxb3 19.cxb3 Nd4 20.Bc4 with just a slight advantage for White, Yakovenko-Nayer, Russian Ch, Krasnoiarsk 2003.

(b) 9...b6!? (D).

Black leaves his pawn back one square so as to deploy his light-squared bishop on the long diagonal without allowing his queenside pawns to be undermined; once he is better developed, the b-pawn often advances another square. Boris Gelfand popularized this idea by successfully employing it in several top-level games. Now:

(b1) 10.Bg5 Nbd7 11.Nd5 Nxd5 12.Qxd5 Rb8 13.Bxe7 Qxe7 14.Rad1 Nf6 15.Qxd6 Qxd6 16.Rxd6 Nxe4 17.Rd5 f6! 18.f3 Ng5 19.Rfd1 Nf7 leads to an equal endgame, Sutovsky-Gelfand, Israeli Team Ch, Tel-Aviv 1999. Black's knight manoeuvre has defended the critical d6-square and prevented the white rooks from infiltrating along the d-file.

(b2) 10.Be3 Bb7 11.f3 ( 11.Nd5 Nxd5 12.exd5 Bg5 13.Qd2 Bxe3 14.fxe3 Qh4 is equal, J.Polgar-Gelfand, Dortmund 1997) 11...b5 12.a4 b4 13.Nd5 Nxd5 14.exd5 Nd7 15.c3 bxc3 16.bxc3 Bg5 17.Bf2 Qc7 18.c4 Rab8 19.a5 Ba8! (this move was a 2004 recommendation by Arizmendi and Moreno) 20.Bd3 (a draw was agreed here in El Taher-Ghaem Maghami, Dubai 2006; instead 20.Ra4?! Qb7 gave Black a slight pull thanks to his pressure along the b-file in Nijboer - Vachier-Lagrave, Wijk aan Zee 2007) 20...f5 ( 20...Rb4!? ) 21.Qc2 g6 with a balanced game. I expect further developments in this line.

10.f4 b5 11.Bf3 Na5 12.Nxa5 Qxa5 (D)

13.Qe1

Other moves have not caused any difficulty for Black; let's examine why:

(a) 13.Nd5 Nxd5 14.exd5 exf4 15.Bxf4 Bf5 with a balanced game, Kramnik-Topalov, Wijk aan Zee 2004.

(b) 13.fxe5 dxe5 14.Nd5 ( 14.Bg5 Bb7 (another reasonable idea is 14...Be6!? 15.Nd5 Bxd5 16.exd5 Rad8 with equal chances - Atalik) 15.a3 Qd8! 16.Bxf6 Bxf6 17.Qe1 Rc8 with a slight pull for Black because of his bishop-pair and pressure along the half-open c-file, Dervishi-Atalik, Bratto 2005) 14...Nxd5 15.Qxd5 ( 15.exd5 Bd6 blockades the passed d-pawn and prepares ...f5) 15...Qc7 16.Qxa8 Bb7 17.Qxf8+ Kxf8 ( 17...Bxf8 18.Bg5 , Sutovsky-Colovic, World Under-18 Ch, Szeged 1994, and now 18...f6 19.Be3 Qc4 is comfortable for Black as the white rooks are bottled up) 18.Be3 (Ganguly-Feldman, Mumbai 2004) and now 18...Qc4! 19.b3 Qc3 with counterplay was suggested by Ki.Georgiev and Kolev. A possible continuation is 20.Rae1 Qb2 21.Rd1 Bc6 with a slight advantage for Black as his queen is causing trouble on the queenside and the white rooks do not have any entry-points.

13...Qc7 14.a3 Bb7 (D)

15.Qg3

Arencibia continues his kingside build-up. The main alternative is 15.f5 a5!? ( 15...h6! (to prevent Bg5) 16.Qg3 Kh8 was suggested by Adams in Informator 72; a possible continuation is 17.Rd1 Qc4 18.Qe1 Rfc8 with a comfortable position for Black as White's kingside play has been stymied and Black is developing pressure along the half-open c-file) 16.Nxb5 Qxc2 17.Nc3 Qb3 18.Bg5 h6 19.Bh4 Rfd8 with equal chances, Adams-Svidler, Madrid 1998. White's slight kingside pressure is offset by the need to defend his b2-pawn.

15...Rfe8

Quezada introduces a new idea. Previously seen was 15...Kh8 16.Be3 Rac8 17.Rae1 Qb8 intending ...a5 with equal chances, Kariakin-Kovchan, Ukrainian Junior Ch, Kramatorsk 2002.

16.Be3 (D)

White could also play the immediate capture 16.fxe5 dxe5 17.Bh6 Bf8 (Black could play on with 17...g6!? ) 18.Bg5 Be7 ( 18...Re6!? is another way to avoid a repetition) 19.Rae1 ( 19.Bh6 repeats the position) 19...Qc5 with a balanced game. A common theme in these lines is that Black's e5-pawn is very effective at limiting the scope of White's light-squared bishop; White usually needs to activate this piece to generate any kingside threats.

16...Qc4

Black creates additional pressure on the e4-pawn; now White is forced to clarify the central tension to avoid material loss.

17.fxe5 dxe5 (D)

The exchange of the white f-pawn for the black d-pawn has resulted in a typical Najdorf pawn-structure; this configuration is generally satisfactory for Black because he has only two pawn-islands in contrast to White's three. White must take care to guard his isolated e4-pawn and seek counterplay along the half-open f-file.

18.Bh6 Bf8 19.Rae1 Qc5

Black can also maintain the balance with 19...Re6 or 19...Kh8 .

20.Bg5 Be7 21.Bd1

Another idea is 21.Bxf6 Bxf6 22.Nd5 Bxd5 23.exd5 g6 , with equal chances.

21...Qd6 22.Re3 Rac8 23.Rd3 Qb6 24.Bxf6

White concedes the bishop-pair in order to activate his light-squared bishop.

24...Bxf6 25.Bg4 Rc7 26.Rfd1 Qc5 27.Rf1 (D)

27...Qe7

Black can safely exchange a pair of rooks by 27...Rd8 28.Be6!? ( 28.Rxd8+ Bxd8 29.Rd1 Bf6 is fine for Black) 28...Rxd3 29.cxd3 Kf8 30.Bd5 Bc8 with equal chances.

28.Rdf3 (D)

28...Bh4?

Only bad things can happen when you have a dangling piece. Black can maintain the balance with 28...Kh8 .

29.Qh3 Qd8 30.Rd3 Qe7 31.Nd5 Bxd5 32.exd5!

White unbalances the position to exploit Black's loose bishop. The alternative recapture 32.Rxd5?! Bg5 is level.

32...Rxc2 33.d6 Qd8 34.Rdf3 Rf8 35.Be6 (D)

35...Rd2?

The rook move turns out to be a wasted move because of White's back-rank mating threats. Relatively best is 35...Kh8 36.g3 Qxd6 37.gxh4 Qc6 38.Qf5 ( 38.Bd7?! Qb7 is unclear) 38...Rxb2 39.Bxf7 with an advantage for White as Black's two pawns are insufficient compensation for White's extra bishop.

36.Bxf7+ Kh8 37.Be6 Rxf3

37...Rf4 38.Rxf4 exf4 39.d7 Rxb2 40.Qd3 is also crushing for White thanks to his advanced d-pawn.

38.Qxf3 g6 39.d7 Rd6 40.Qf7 Bg5 41.h4 1-0

The passed d-pawn decides the game after 41...Bxh4 42.Rc1 , with the idea of Rc8, winning.

Game 12 [B92]
Arkadij Naiditsch - Boris Gelfand
Dortmund 2007

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be2 e5 7.Nb3 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.Be3

9.Kh1 was covered in Game 11, along with White's other 9th-move alternatives.

9...Be6 (D)

10.Qd2

This is the first phase of the Karpov plan: White develops his queen and connects his rooks. He will follow up with a4 and a5 to restrict Black's queenside play and then he will deploy his king's rook to either c1 or d1. The second phase involves improving the position of the b3-knight by playing Nc1 followed by Na2-b4 or Nd3-b4, angling for occupation of the d5-square. Depending upon Black's response, White will sometimes change plans and play an early Nd5; after an exchange of minor pieces on d5 followed by a recapture with White's e4-pawn, White will then mobilize his queenside pawn-majority to exploit Black's lack of manoeuvring space on the queenside. Black will often respond with ...f5 to obtain counterplay on the kingside.

There are some alternative approaches:

(a) 10.Nd5 Nbd7 (the exchange sacrifice 10...Nxe4?! 11.Bb6 Qd7 12.Nc7 Nc6 13.Nxa8 Rxa8 14.c4 favours White) and now:

(a1) 11.Nxe7+ Qxe7 12.Bf3 (preventing Black from playing the liberating ...d5 pawn advance) 12...Rac8 13.Qd2 Rc6 is equal, Ascic-Ruck, Bosnian Team Ch, Neum 2003.

(a2) 11.Qd3 Bxd5 12.exd5 Nc5 13.Nxc5 dxc5 14.c4 e4 15.Qc3 Qc7 with equal chances, Faragua-Bu Xiangzhi, Beijing rapid 2008.

(b) 10.f4 exf4 and then:

(b1) 11.Bxf4 (note that White has lost a tempo because his dark-squared bishop has moved twice in comparison to the line 9.a4 Be6 10.f4 exf4 11.Bxf4 ; this should be compared to note 'c' to White's 9th move in Game 11) 11...Nc6 12.Kh1 d5! 13.e5 Nd7 14.Nxd5 Ndxe5 15.c4 ( 15.Nxe7+ Qxe7 16.Qe1 (Marjanovic-Taijan, Malta Olympiad 1980) 16...f6 with a solid position for Black) 15...Bg5 16.Nc5 Bxf4 17.Nxe6 fxe6 18.Nxf4 Qxd1 19.Raxd1 Rf6 is equal, Psakhis-I.Gurevich, Reshevsky Memorial, New York 1992.

(b2) 11.Rxf4 Nc6 ( 11...Nbd7!? , as recommended by Arizmendi and Moreno, is a reasonable alternative) 12.Nd5 Bxd5 13.exd5 Ne5 (Black has a firm grip on the e5-square to offset White's bishop-pair) 14.Rb4 Qc7 15.a4 Nfd7 16.a5 Rfe8 17.c3 Bf6 18.Bf2 Bg5 with equality, Mortensen-Karpov, European Team Ch, Plovdiv 1983.

(c) 10.a4 Nc6 ( 10...Nbd7 11.Qd2 transposes to the main game) 11.Qd2 Rc8 (a safe alternative is 11...d5 12.exd5 Nxd5 13.Nxd5 Bxd5 14.Rfd1 Bxb3 15.cxb3 Qxd2 16.Rxd2 Rfd8 with equal chances, Huebner-Anand, Wijk aan Zee 1996) 12.Bf3 Nb4 13.Rfd1 Ng4 14.Bxg4 Bxg4 15.f3 Be6 is equal, Stefansson-Renet, Reykjavik 1992.

We now return to 10.Qd2 (D):

10...Nbd7

Black has two major alternatives:

(a) 10...Nc6 11.Rfd1 a5 12.a3 a4 13.Nc1 Qa5 14.f3 Rfc8 15.Kh1! (White avoids any potential issues along the g1-a7 diagonal; this is an improvement over 15.Bf1 Bd8! intending ...Bb6 with a balanced game, Ehlvest-T.Georgadze, Moscow Spartakiad 1983) 15...Bd8 16.Qxd6 Nd4?! 17.Bxd4 exd4 18.Qxd4 Bb6 19.Qd2 and White has won two pawns, Tseshkovsky-Zaichik, Moscow Spartakiad 1983.

(b) 10...b5!? (this advance has fallen out of favour because Black concedes the bishop-pair, but it may appeal to players seeking to sharpen the position) 11.a4 b4 12.Nd5 Nxe4 13.Nxe7+ Qxe7 14.Qxb4 f5 15.Na5 Rc8 (D) leads to an unbalanced position where White's bishop-pair is offset by Black's mobile central pawns.

Now:

(b1) 16.Rfd1 (Hamed-Magerramov, Dubai 2001) 16...Nc6 17.Nxc6 Rxc6 is equal.

(b2) 16.Bf3 Nd7 17.Bxe4 fxe4 18.Rad1 ( 18.Rfd1 Nc5 19.Nc4 (Yermolinsky-Dzhandzhgava, World Open, Philadelphia 1990) 19...a5!? 20.Nxa5 Qc7 with a sharp but balanced position) 18...Nc5 19.Bxc5 Rxc5 with equality, Babaev-Magerramov, Abu Dhabi 2000.

11.a4 (D)

11...Rc8

Black completes his development by occupying the half-open c-file. Confusingly, several of these lines can lead to the same position, so let's examine the various transpositions:

(a) 11...Qc7 12.a5 Nc5 ( 12...Rac8 transposes to the main game) 13.Nxc5 dxc5 14.Rfd1 Rfd8 15.Qe1 Rxd1 16.Qxd1 Rd8 with equality, Voitsekhovsky-Kokarev, Vladimir 2008.

(b) 11...Nb6 12.a5 (White kicks the knight to c4 so that he can exchange it before Black has the chance to play ...Rc8 and recapture with the rook; see the note to White's 12th move in the main game for an example) 12...Nc4 13.Bxc4 Bxc4 14.Rfd1 Rc8 (D)

and now:

(b1) 15.Nc1 d5! 16.Bb6 ( 16.exd5 Bb4 is fine for Black) 16...Qe8! 17.exd5 Bb4 18.d6 Qd7 and Black was able to maintain the balance in Leko-Shirov, Dortmund 2002.

(b2) 15.f3 Rc6 16.Bb6 Qc8 (the exchange sacrifice 16...Rxb6?! 17.axb6 Bxb3 18.cxb3 Qxb6+ 19.Kh1 Qxb3 20.Rac1 led to material equality in Leko-Shirov, Tal Memorial, Moscow 2008, but White was able to grind out an endgame win thanks to his grip on d5) 17.Qe1 ( 17.Nc1 allows the freeing 17...d5! 18.exd5 Nxd5 19.Nxd5 Bxd5 with equal chances; the tactical point is that 20.Qxd5? Rxb6 favours Black) 17...Bd8 18.Bf2 b5 with a balanced game, Motylev-Nepomniashchy, Russian Ch, Novokuznetsk 2008.

(c) 11...Nc5 (this was Tony Kosten's 1999 recommendation; one of the ideas is to clarify the central pawn-structure before committing Black's queen's rook to the c-file) 12.Nxc5 dxc5 13.Qxd8 ( 13.Rfd1 Qc7 14.a5 transposes to line 'a' above) 13...Rfxd8 14.f3 c4 15.Kf2 Rac8 16.a5 Kf8 17.Na4 Nd7 18.Rfd1 and then:

(c1) 18...Rc6 19.Nc3 Nf6 is equal, Leko-Topalov, Wijk aan Zee 2004.

(c2) 18...Bb4!? 19.Nc3 Bc5 20.Ra4!? was played in Leko-Anand, World Blitz Ch, Moscow 2007, and now 20...Ke7 would have led to a balanced game.

12.a5

White clamps down on the queenside and controls the b6-square. The move-order is critical here and it is important to avoid the careless 12.Rfd1?! Nb6! 13.a5 Nc4 14.Bxc4 Rxc4 (the black rook is well-placed on c4 because it simultaneously exerts pressure along the half-open c-file, attacks White's e4-pawn and prevents White from activating his queen's rook via a4) 15.f3 Qc7 16.Bb6 Qc6 17.Rac1 Nd7 18.Be3 f5 19.exf5 Rxf5 with a comfortable position for Black, Kalashian-Papadopoulos, World Junior Ch, Gaziantep 2008.

12...Qc7 (D)

13.Rfd1

Karpov's original idea of 13.Rfc1 has been neutralized by both of the following ideas:

(a) 13...Qc6 14.Bf3 Bc4! (the bishop interferes with White's plans to advance his c-pawn; the bishop is surprisingly difficult to dislodge from this active post) 15.Qd1 ( 15.Nd5? Nxd5 16.exd5 Qb5! favours Black; 15.Ra4 Rfe8 16.Rb4 Qc7 17.Nd5 Nxd5 18.exd5 Nf6 with equal chances, as first played in Lukin-Hjartarson, Leningrad 1984) 15...Rfe8 16.Nd2 Be6 17.Nf1 Bf8 is equal, Leko-Gelfand, Amber Rapid, Monte Carlo 2003. Note that in both of the game examples quoted here, Black developed his king's rook to e8 rather than to d8; this is the optimal rook placement in this particular line as Black avoids any possible tactics by White involving a timely Bb6.

(b) 13...Nc5 14.Nxc5 dxc5 (this line also leaves the rook looking misplaced on c1) 15.f3 c4 16.Na4 Nd7 17.Bf1 Rfd8 18.Qe1 Qc6 with a solid position for Black as he has prevented White from establishing a queenside bind, Ivanchuk-Kramnik, Amber Rapid, Monte Carlo 2004.

13...Nc5

The knight leap was popularized by Joe Gallagher. Boris Gelfand has also played it a couple of times in addition to this game. The line is closely related to the 11...Nc5 variation because Black seeks to clarify the central pawn-structure before commencing further operations; the difference is that here Black has committed his queen's rook to c8, while White has advanced his pawn to a5. White is hoping to prove that the a5-pawn cramps Black's queenside, whereas Black will seek to demonstrate that the advanced pawn is a weakness requiring defence. Black has other reasonable moves including 13...Rfd8 , 13...Qc6 and 13...Rfe8 , but I shall not be covering these alternatives here.

14.Nxc5 dxc5 (D)

15.f3

Alternatives:

(a) 15.Nd5 Bxd5 16.exd5 Bd6 17.c4 Nd7 is fine for Black as he is poised to expand on the kingside with ...f5.

(b) 15.Bf3 Rfd8 16.Qe1 Rxd1 17.Qxd1 c4 18.Bb6 Qd6 is equal, Lautier-Gelfand, Tilburg 1996.

(c) 15.Qe1 Rfd8 ( 15...c4 16.Nd5 Bxd5 17.Bb6 Qd7 18.exd5 Bc5 19.Bxc5 Rxc5 ) 16.Rxd8+ Rxd8 17.Kh1 Qc6 18.f3 c4 with equality, Ivanchuk-Van Wely, European Clubs Cup, Kallithea 2008.

15...Rfd8 16.Qe1 Rxd1

Similar positions arise after 16...c4 17.Rxd8+ Rxd8 18.Na4 Nd7 with an even game, Petrik-Fercec, Pula 2007.

17.Bxd1

The alternative recaptures are also harmless for Black:

(a) 17.Qxd1 c4 18.Na4 Bb4 19.Bb6 Qc6 20.c3 Be7 with a balanced game, Brunner-Gallagher, Biel 1990.

(b) 17.Nxd1 c4 18.Rb1 h6 19.b4 cxb3 20.cxb3 Qc2 21.Rb2 Qc6 with a comfortable position for Black thanks to his control of the open c-file, Leosson-Gallagher, Reykjavik 1998.

17...c4

Black gains some space on the queenside and creates the possibility of ...Bb4, harassing White's a5-pawn. Now both players undertake a series of tidying-up moves to improve their positions.

18.Na4 Nd7 19.Be2 Qc6

Naiditsch had previous experience facing 19...h6 : 20.g3 ( 20.Kh1 Qc6 would transpose back into the main game; 20.Kf1 Kh8 21.Nb6 Nxb6 22.Bxb6 Qc6 with a solid position for Black, Jansa-Steingrimsson, Differdange 2007) 20...Qc6 21.Kg2 Qb5 with equal chances, Naiditsch-Hou Yifan, Moscow 2007.

20.Kh1 (D)

The white king steps aside to avoid the trap 20.Nb6? Bc5! 21.Bxc5 Qxc5+ 22.Qf2 Nxb6 23.axb6 Qxf2+ 24.Kxf2 Rc6 , when Black wins a pawn.

20...h6

Black could consider provoking queenside weaknesses with 20...Qb5!? 21.Bd2 ( 21.Qc1 Bb4 22.Nc3 Qc6 is equal, Misiunas-Avotins, Baltic Club corr. Ch 2005) 21...Bd6 22.b4 Qc6 23.c3 f5 24.exf5 Bxf5 25.Nb2 Be6 with a balanced game, Asrian-Voitsekhovsky, Minsk 1998.

21.h3 Kh7 22.Bd2 Qd6

Another idea is 22...f5!? 23.exf5 Bxf5 , to generate some play in the centre.

23.Bc3 (D)

23...Kg8

Now 23...Rd8!? looks reasonable, to grab the open d-file.

24.Bf1 Kh7 25.Qd1 Qc6 26.Qe1 Qd6 27.Kh2

27.Qd1 repeats the position.

27...Kg8 28.g3 g5!?

Gelfand tries to exploit White's offside knight by opening lines on the kingside.

29.Bg2 f6 30.h4 Kh7 31.h5!?

This is a double-edged idea as the pawn could become exposed here. A safer plan is to exchange light-squared bishops with 31.hxg5 hxg5 32.Bh3 Bxh3 33.Kxh3 Kg6 34.Kg2 with an even game.

31...g4 32.Qe3 Rg8 33.fxg4 Bxg4 34.Bf3 Qc6 35.Bd2?! f5! 36.Bxg4 Rxg4 (D)

37.Qf3!

White concedes a pawn because the obvious capture 37.exf5? runs into the surprising counter 37...Nf6!! 38.Qe2 ( 38.Qxh6+ Kg8 39.Bg5 Rh4+! is winning for Black) 38...Bd6 with a decisive advantage for Black as White's king-side pawns are vulnerable and his king is exposed.

37...Qxe4 38.Qxe4 Rxe4 39.Nc3 Rg4 40.Rf1 f4

Black can preserve some slight winning chances with 40...Bg5!? 41.Be1 Rd4 (not 41...f4? 42.Kh3 Nf6 43.Ne4! and White wins material) 42.Rxf5 Kg8 .

41.gxf4 Rh4+ 42.Kg2 Rxh5 43.Nd5 Bd8 44.Ne3 1/2-1/2

The position peters out after 44...exf4 45.Rxf4 Ne5 46.Nxc4 Nxc4 47.Rxc4 Bxa5 48.Bxa5 Rxa5 49.Rc7+ Kg6 50.Rxb7 Rc5 with a drawn rook endgame.
