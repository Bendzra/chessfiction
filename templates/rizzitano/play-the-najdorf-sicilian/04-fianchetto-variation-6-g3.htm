4 Fianchetto Variation: 6.g3

The Fianchetto Variation is one of White's most underestimated weapons versus the Najdorf Sicilian. White's light-squared bishop supports the e4-pawn and the d5-square from a safe position on g2. In many lines, the fianchetto also inhibits Black's traditional Najdorf counterplay involving an early ...b5 and quick pressure against White's e4-pawn. White expends two tempi to fianchetto his bishop, but in comparison to the Fischer Variation, the bishop is not subject to attack by an early ...b5 and the piece cannot easily be exchanged.

White has two major ideas in the Fianchetto Variation after 6.g3 e5 : he can focus on the queenside and aim for control of the d5-square beginning with 7.Nb3 (Game 9), or he can play the traditional move 7.Nde2 (Game 10) with the intention of expanding on the kingside with h3, g4 and Ng3. The Fianchetto Variation has never been as popular as some of White's more direct attacking systems, but the line is sometimes trotted out at the highest levels as a surprise weapon. Many strong grandmasters, including Alekseev, Movsesian, Al.Ivanov, Kudrin and Popovic, have shown a preference for this set-up.

The Fianchetto Variation can be a very effective repertoire choice at club level because many Najdorf players have not fully worked out a defence to it. Another potential advantage is that the characteristic positions arising from the Fianchetto are fundamentally different from more popular lines such as the English Attack and are therefore less familiar to Najdorf players.


Game 9 (Alekseev-Arizmendi) features the line 6.g3 e5 7.Nb3 . White restrains Black's queenside expansion and plays for control of the centre with the modern plan of Re1 followed by Nd2-f1, angling for a timely Ne3 with an iron grip on the d5-square. Black counters with the standard manoeuvre ...Rc8-c5 followed by ...Qa8 to exert pressure on White's e-pawn. Alekseev plays 14.Bg5 with the intention of exchanging the dark-squared bishop for one of the black knights; this is a typical method of increasing control over the d5-square. Arizmendi counters with an ambitious pawn sacrifice to increase the scope of his dark-squared bishop. The middlegame features forceful kingside play by Alekseev to exploit Black's offside queen.


In Game 10 (Alekseev-L.Dominguez), White plays the more popular 6.g3 e5 7.Nde2 , followed by the traditional kingside build-up with h3 and g4. Black leaves his king in the centre and introduces the new move 13...h5!? to cut across White's plans and open the h-file for the black rook. Black has had a lot of success delaying castling and advancing his h-pawn in several Najdorf lines; in this particular game his king is relatively safe in the centre of the board because the d-file is closed. White counters by obtaining a visually attractive knight outpost on c6, but Black is able to manoeuvre around the far-advanced steed to target White's weakened kingside pawn-structure. The game turns into a classic race between White's advancing queenside pawns and Black's kingside attack.

Game 9 [B91]
Evgeny Alekseev - Julen Arizmendi
Biel 2006

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.g3 (D)

White's light-squared bishop stakes out a claim to the long diagonal; from g2 it will defend the e4-pawn and support a potential knight outpost on d5. The Fianchetto Variation is deceptively quiet in comparison to most other Najdorf lines; play tends to develop slowly at first but then things often heat up quickly in the early middlegame. Black must play precisely to ensure he obtains active play.

6...e5

The traditional Najdorf reply seems especially logical here: Black closes the long diagonal for White's bishop, albeit at the cost of a slight weakening of the d5-square. Black has several options here and White must be prepared to meet these alternatives:

(a) 6...b5?! (this thematic advance is premature here because White can quickly generate threats along the e-file) 7.Bg2 Bb7 8.O-O e6 9.Re1 and then:

(a1) 9...Qc7? walks into 10.Bg5 Nbd7 11.a4 b4 12.Nd5! exd5 13.exd5+ Ne5 ( 13...Kd8 14.Nc6+ Bxc6 15.dxc6 Nc5 16.Bxf6+ gxf6 17.Qd4 gives White a strong attack for a small material investment) 14.f4 and White recovers his piece with a crushing initiative, Shabalov-Benjamin, US Open Ch, Philadelphia 1993.

(a2) 9...Qc8 10.a4! bxa4 11.Nxa4 Nbd7 (Y.Gruenfeld - Har-Zvi, Biel open 1993) 12.Ra3! intending Rc3 with a clear plus for White.

(b) 6...g6 leads to Dragon-type positions after 7.Bg2 Bg7 8.O-O O-O 9.h3 Nc6 10.Be3 Bd7 and the game is just beginning, Alekseev-Istratescu, Poikovsky 2007.

(c) 6...e6 7.Bg2 Qc7 8.O-O Be7 transposes to a line of the Scheveningen Variation (ECO code B80).

(d) 6...Nc6 7.Bg2 Bd7 8.O-O e6 is another transposition to the Scheveningen.

(e) 6...Bg4 7.f3 Bd7 can lead to other Sicilian lines. For example, 8.Be3 Nc6 9.g4 e6 also transposes to a Scheveningen Variation, this time an English Attack.

7.Nb3

Alternatives:

(a) 7.Nf5? , with the idea of clamping down on the d5-square with Ne3, is well-intentioned but runs into 7...d5! 8.Bg2 d4 9.Ne2 g6 with a very comfortable position for Black.

(b) 7.Nde2 is covered in Game 10.

7...Be7 8.Bg2 Nbd7 (D)

The development of the queen's knight to d7 instead of c6 enables Black to meet White's plan of Bg5 and Bxf6 with ...Nxf6 to retain control over the d5-square.

9.a4 b6 10.O-O Bb7 11.Re1

White's moves appear innocent enough, and it is easy for Najdorf players to be caught off guard; White's last move vacates the f1-square in preparation for the strategic manoeuvre Nd2-f1-e3 to control the d5-square, so Black must counter this threat.

11...Rc8

Black develops the rook to the c-file to retain the option of playing the thematic exchange sacrifice ...Rxc3; this idea is very effective if Black can follow up by capturing White's e-pawn.

12.Nd2 Rc5!?

Black clears a path to the corner for his queen to increase the pressure on White's e4-pawn while retaining pressure along the c-file.

13.Nf1 Qa8 (D)

14.Bg5!?

This is the latest try, but White has a wide range of alternatives:

(a) 14.g4 (White seeks to drive away one of the defenders of the d5-square, while clearing the g3-square for his knight) 14...Rxc3! (Black responds with a thematic exchange sacrifice; the idea is to shatter White's pawn-structure and snuff out any initiative on the kingside) 15.bxc3 Nxe4 16.Ng3 Nxg3 17.Bxb7 Qxb7 18.hxg3 O-O gives Black adequate compensation for his slight material deficit, Stoica-Stefanov, Predeal 1988.

(b) 14.f3 (the drawback to this move is that it reduces White's control over the d5-square) 14...d5! 15.exd5 Nxd5 16.Ne4 Rc7 is equal.

(c) 14.Be3 Rxc3! 15.bxc3 Nxe4 16.c4 O-O 17.Nd2?! (playing with fire; however, 17.Bc1 Ndc5 is fine for Black) 17...Nxf2! (this 2004 suggestion by Arizmendi and Moreno is an improvement over 17...Nxd2 18.Bxb7 Qxb7 19.Qxd2 Rc8 "with compensation" according to Stoica in Informator 46, but I prefer White after 20.a5! as he is opening lines on the queenside for his rooks) 18.Bxb7 Nxd1 19.Bxa8 Nxe3 with an edge for Black - Arizmendi and Moreno.

(d) 14.Bd2 O-O 15.Ne3 Rfc8 16.Nf5 Bf8 is equal, Al.Ivanov-Sarkar, Mashantucket 2007.

(e) 14.Ra3!? (the idea here is to reinforce the c3-knight) 14...O-O 15.Ne3 is another interesting try, Steel-Blaauw, Cape Town 2007.

(f) 14.Ne3!? Nxe4 and then:

(f1) 15.Nf5 Nxc3 16.Bxb7 Qxb7 17.bxc3 g6 18.Nxe7 Kxe7 19.Ba3 Rd5!? ( 19...Rhc8 20.Bxc5 Rxc5 was a promising exchange sacrifice in P.Popovic-King, Palma de Mallorca 1989) 20.Qe2 Qc6 with a comfortable position for Black.

(f2) 15.Nxe4! (this is a significant improvement) 15...Bxe4 16.Bxe4 ( 16.f3 Bg6 is fine for Black) 16...Qxe4 17.b3! Qg6 18.Ba3 Rc8 19.Nd5 gives White good compensation for the pawn in view of his powerful knight outpost, Berelovich-Henrichs, 2nd Bundesliga 2006/7. This line deserves further practical tests.

14...h6 15.Bxf6 Nxf6 16.Ne3!? (D)

Alekseev introduces a new idea rather than pause to defend his e4-pawn; previously seen was 16.Qd3 O-O 17.Ne3 Rfc8 (Tyomkin-Quan Zhe, Guelph 2004) 18.Nf5 Bf8 19.Qf3 Kh7 20.Rad1 Qb8 with a balanced game.

16...g6

Black prevents the threatened Nf5. The alternative is 16...Nxe4 17.Qf3 d5 (the exchange sacrifice 17...Rxc3 18.bxc3 Nc5 19.Qg4 g6 20.Bxb7 Qxb7 21.Rab1 Qc6 22.Qc4 Bd8 23.Rb4 favours White because of his control over the d5-square) 18.Ncxd5 and now:

(a) 18...Rxd5 19.Nxd5 Bxd5 20.Qf5 Nf6 21.Qxe5 O-O ( 21...Qb7 22.Bxd5 Nxd5 23.Qxg7 Rf8 24.Rad1 is winning for White because the black king is marooned in the centre) 22.Bxd5 Nxd5 23.c4 Nb4 24.Qxe7 Nc2 25.Rad1 Nxe1 26.Rxe1 with a clear extra pawn for White in the major-piece endgame.

(b) 18...Bxd5 19.Nxd5 Qxd5 20.Rad1 Qe6 21.Qxe4 O-O 22.c3 and despite the opposite-coloured bishops, White has a slight advantage thanks to his control of the d-file and the potential vulnerability of Black's queenside pawns.

17.Qd3 O-O 18.f4 exf4 19.gxf4 Rh5

Black also has a satisfactory position after 19...Qc8 , bringing the queen back into the game.

20.Rf1 d5 (D)

Arizmendi offers a thematic pawn sacrifice to open a diagonal for his dark-squared bishop and take aim at White's kingside. A reasonable, and possibly superior, alternative is 20...Qc8 intending ...Re8 or ...Qc5 with a balanced game.

21.Nexd5

Alekseev avoids the sharp 21.e5!? . Then:

(a) 21...Ne4 22.Ncxd5 Bxd5 23.Nxd5 Bc5+ 24.Kh1 Qxd5 25.Bxe4 ( 25.Qxd5?? Ng3# ) 25...Qxd3 26.Bxd3 favours White as he has retained the extra pawn.

(b) 21...Bc5!? 22.exf6 d4 23.Bxb7 ( 23.b4?? loses to 23...dxe3! ) 23...Qxb7 24.b4! Bxb4 25.Qxd4 Bc5 26.Qd2 g5!? with tremendous complications.

21...Nxd5 22.exd5 (D)

White elects to retain his knight for attacking purposes. The alternative is 22.Nxd5 Bxd5 23.exd5 Bd6 and Black has lost a pawn, but the opposite-coloured bishops and White's slightly exposed king assure him of reasonable counterplay.

22...Bf6?

The long diagonal looks attractive, but the bishop only becomes a target on the kingside. Black should improve the position of his queen by 22...Qc8 23.Rae1 Qc7 , with compensation for the pawn in the form of the bishop-pair and White's loose kingside pawn-structure.

23.Rad1 Bg7 24.f5

Another strong idea is 24.Rf2 , preparing the advance of the extra d-pawn.

24...Be5 25.h3 Rg5 (D)

26.Ne4!

Alekseev alertly exploits Black's offside queen to blast open the kingside.

26...Rxf5 27.Rxf5 gxf5 28.Ng3 f4 (D)

Arizmendi retains this pawn to deny White access to g3. 28...Qd8 29.Nxf5 Qg5 30.Rf1 also favours White.

29.Nf5 Qd8 30.Nxh6+ Kg7

White's pieces infiltrate the kingside after 30...Kh8 31.Qf5 Qc7 ( 31...Qf6 32.Qh5 Kg7 33.Ng4 Bd4+ 34.Kh2 and White wins a piece) 32.Be4 f6 33.Qh5 with a mating attack.

31.Nf5+ Kh8

Losing material, but even after 31...Kf6 32.Nd4 Kg7 33.c3 White has a material advantage and the black king is hopelessly exposed.

32.Qe2

The double attack on the e5-bishop and the h5-square is decisive.

32...Qf6 33.Qh5+ Kg8 34.Nh6+ 1-0

Black runs out of squares after 34...Kg7 35.Ng4 Bd4+ 36.Kf1 , winning a piece.

Game 10 [B91]
Evgeny Alekseev - Leinier Dominguez Perez
Biel 2008

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.g3 e5 7.Nde2 (D)

This is White's most popular move. 7.Nb3 was covered in Game 9.

7...Be7

The immediate bishop development is one of several options for Black.

Alternatives:

(a) 7...b5 8.Nd5 (Alexander Ivanov's preference 8.Bg5 is a sharp alternative) 8...Nbd7 9.Nec3 Bb7 10.Bg2 Nxd5 11.Nxd5 Nb6 ( 11...Nf6 12.Bg5!? Bxd5 13.exd5 Be7 "with chances for both sides" was recommended by Arizmendi and Moreno; this idea awaits a practical trial) 12.Bg5! Qxg5 (the power of White's attack is seen after the long but pleasing variation 12...f6? 13.Bxf6! gxf6 14.Qh5+ Kd7 15.Qf7+ Kc6 16.Nb4+ Kc5 17.Qxb7 Kxb4 18.a3+ Ka5 ( 18...Kc5 19.b4+ mates) 19.Bf3! Qc8 ( 19...b4 20.axb4++ Kxb4 21.O-O and White wins) 20.c3 Qxb7 21.b4+ Ka4 22.Bd1# ) 13.Nxb6 Rb8 14.Nd5 gives White a slight advantage due to his control over d5, Alekseev-Kariakin, Russian Team Ch, Dagomys 2008.

(b) 7...Nbd7 (this is Black's most popular choice) 8.a4 b6 9.Bg2 Bb7 10.h3 Rc8 11.g4 Nc5 12.Ng3 g6 (D) and now:

(b1) 13.g5 Nfd7 14.h4 Ne6 15.Nd5 Bg7 16.Be3 Nf4! 17.Nxf4 exf4 18.Bxf4 Bxb2 is equal, Golinka-Deriabin, Mariupol 2003.

(b2) 13.O-O Ne6 ( 13...h5!? ) 14.Nd5 Bg7 15.c3 O-O with equal chances, Kudrin-Peters, USA Ch, Berkeley 1984.

(b3) 13.h4!? (this is Popovic's latest idea) 13...Ne6 14.h5 Nf4 15.Bxf4 exf4 16.Nge2 with sharp play, P.Popovic - Vachier-Lagrave, Paris 2006.

8.Bg2 b5 (D)

9.h3 Nbd7 10.g4

This is a typical plan in the Fianchetto Variation: White advances on the kingside to gain space for his pieces and clear the g3-square for his knight.

10...b4!

This logical advance was suggested by Patrick Wolff in 1993, but another seven years passed until the idea received a practical test.

The alternative is 10...O-O 11.Ng3 Nc5 12.O-O Bb7 13.Be3 Rc8 with equal chances, Kamsky-Wolff, USA Ch, Long Beach 1993.

11.Nd5 Nxd5 12.exd5

The white queen becomes a target for Black's minor pieces after 12.Qxd5 Rb8 13.Ng3 O-O 14.Be3 (Himanshu-Ramesh, New Delhi 2007) 14...Nc5 15.Nf5 Bb7 16.Qc4 g6 17.Nxe7+ ( 17.Bxc5 gxf5! 18.Bxb4 fxe4 19.O-O d5 20.Bxe7 Qxe7 favours Black thanks to his clump of mobile centre pawns) 17...Qxe7 18.O-O a5 with a solid position for Black as he has completed his development and the white queen is exposed on the c4-square.

12...a5 (D)

13.O-O

Alternatives:

(a) 13.Ng3 Ba6 14.h4 O-O 15.Be3 ( 15.Nf5 Re8 with a slight advantage for Black according to Gelfand) 15...Qc7 16.Rc1 Nc5 17.b3 a4 18.Qd2 Rab8 with an edge for Black as his light-squared bishop has cut a swath through the centre of the board, Ye Jiangchuan-Gelfand, FIDE Knockout, Shenyang 2000.

(b) 13.a3 Ba6 (this is one of the benefits of delaying ...Bb7: Black is able to develop the bishop to its optimal square in one move) 14.O-O O-O 15.axb4 axb4 with a comfortable position for Black as he has smoothly completed his development, Al Modiahki-Sedlak, Biel 2003.

13...h5!?

Dominguez introduces a sharp new idea. Black can also play the solid 13...Ba6 14.Re1 O-O 15.Ng3 g6 16.Bh6 Re8 with equal chances, Livshits-Smirin, Ashdod 2006.

14.gxh5 Rxh5 (D)

15.Nd4?!

Alekseev exploits Black's undefended rook to plant his knight on c6, but it is better to open the long diagonal with 15.Nf4!? Rh4 16.Ne6 ( 16.Nh5 g6 is fine for Black) 16...fxe6 17.dxe6 Rb8 (the speculative exchange sacrifice 17...Nb6!? 18.Bc6+ Kf8 19.Bxa8 Nxa8 20.Qf3+ Bf6 21.Qxa8 Qe8 is roughly equal as White is weak on the light squares and his king is vulnerable) 18.Qd3!? Kf8 (Black must return the piece as after 18...Nf8? 19.Bc6+ White wins) 19.exd7 Bxd7 20.f4 e4!? ( 20...Kg8 is a safe alternative) 21.Qxe4 Bf6 and the looseness of White's king position gives Black solid compensation for the pawn.

15...Nf6 16.Nc6 Qc7

In the introductory chapter Typical Najdorf Themes', we discussed in detail why Black is OK here despite White possessing an advanced knight outpost.

17.Qf3?! (D)

White should immediately crack open the queenside with 17.a3 .

17...Bf5

Black begins implementing the straightforward but effective plan of piling up on White's h3-pawn.

18.Re1 Qd7 19.a3 b3!? (D)

Dominguez offers a pawn to keep lines closed on the queenside. His play in this game is a model of consistency because he keeps his focus on exploiting the weak squares on the king-side.

20.cxb3 Bxh3 21.Bd2 Bxg2 22.Kxg2 Rf5 23.Qd3 Nh5

Now the focus shifts to the f4-square: if Black can exchange dark-squared bishops, his remaining pieces will have free rein to attack the white king.

24.Rh1 g6 25.b4 Bg5 26.b5!?

The pawn capture 26.bxa5 looks more natural, but Alekseev prefers to have the passed pawn on the b-file because his knight controls the queening square.

26...Bxd2 27.Qxd2 Rf4

The rook move clears the way for the black queen to infiltrate White's kingside. Black must avoid 27...Nf4+?? 28.Qxf4 , when White is winning because of the back-rank mating threat.

28.Rh3 Kf8

The black king heads for the g7-square to avoid any potential back-rank issues.

29.Rah1 Qg4+ (D)

30.Kf1

Alekseev concedes the loss of the exchange and pins his hopes on the advanced b-pawn. His king would quickly perish after 30.Kh2 Kg7 31.Rg1 Qf5 32.Rg2 Ng3!! 33.Qc3 (White is mated after 33.fxg3 Qxh3+! 34.Kxh3 Rh8# , 33.Kxg3 Rf3+ or 33.Rhxg3 Rh8+ ) 33...Ne4 34.Qd3 ( 34.Ne7 Qd7 35.Qc6 Qxh3+! 36.Kxh3 Rh8# ) 34...Qf6 35.Qe3 ( 35.f3 Ng5 ) 35...Nxf2 with crushing threats.

30...Ng3+ 31.Rxg3 Qxg3 (D)

32.b6

White has no time for the materialistic 32.Rh8+ Kg7 33.Rxa8 Qh3+ 34.Ke1 ( 34.Ke2 Qf3+ ) 34...Qh1+ 35.Ke2 Qf3+ 36.Ke1 ( 36.Kf1 Rh4 mates) 36...Re4+ 37.Qe3 ( 37.Kf1 Qh1# ) 37...Rxe3+ 38.fxe3 Qxe3+ 39.Kd1 Qd3+ 40.Kc1 e4 and Black wins.

32...Qf3 33.Rh2 (D)

33...Kg8?!

The shortest path to victory is 33...Qb3! 34.Rh8+ Kg7 35.Rxa8 Qh3+ 36.Ke1 ( 36.Ke2 Qf3+ ) 36...Qh1+ 37.Ke2 Qf3+ 38.Ke1 ( 38.Kf1 Rh4 mates) 38...Re4+ 39.Qe3 ( 39.Kf1 Qh1# ) 39...Rxe3+ 40.fxe3 Qxe3+ and Black wins.

34.b7 Re8 35.b4

White's knight is tied down defending the d4-square; for example, 35.b8Q Rxb8 36.Nxb8 Rd4 and Black wins because of the back-rank threats.

35...axb4 36.axb4 (D)

36...Qg3

Black can wrap things up by placing White in zugzwang with 36...Rg4! :

(a) 37.Rh8+ Kg7 (not 37...Kxh8?? 38.Qh6+ Kg8 39.Ne7+ mating) 38.Qh6+ ( 38.Rxe8 Rh4 with a mating-net) 38...Kf6 and Black wins.

(b) 37.b5 Ra4 38.Qe1 Qd3+ 39.Kg1 Rg4+ 40.Rg2 Rxg2+ 41.Kxg2 Qxd5+ 42.Kh2 Qxb5 43.b8Q Rxb8 44.Nxb8 Qxb8 and Black has a decisive material advantage.

37.Rh1

Relatively best is 37.Rg2! Qh3 38.Kg1 ( 38.Qe2 Kg7 39.b8Q Rxb8 40.Nxb8 Rxb4 is winning for Black) 38...Rh4 39.f3 Qxf3 40.b8Q Rxb8 41.Nxb8 Qa3 42.Nc6 Qa1+ 43.Kf2 Rf4+ 44.Kg3 f5 45.Ne7+ Kf7 46.Nxg6 Kxg6 47.Qe2 Kf6 with a decisive advantage for Black as the white king is too exposed.

37...Qf3 38.Rh2 Qg3 39.Rh1 Qb3! (D)

Finally on the right track!

40.b8Q Rxb8 41.Nxb8 Rxb4

Slightly quicker is 41...Qf3! 42.Rg1 Rd4 , when White's back-rank weakness is fatal.

42.Nd7 Qc4+ 43.Kg2 Qg4+ 44.Kh2 Qh5+ 45.Kg2 Rg4+ 0-1

The finish would be 46.Kf3 Rf4++ 47.Kg2 Qf3+ 48.Kg1 Rg4+ and Black wins.
