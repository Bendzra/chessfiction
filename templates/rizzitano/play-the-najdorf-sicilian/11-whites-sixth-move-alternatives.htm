11 White's Sixth-Move Alternatives

White has several sixth-move choices aimed at navigating along the road less travelled. The main idea behind these lines is to surprise and confuse the Najdorf player. However, although these alternatives may have proportionally less opening theory than the mainstream choices, they should all be treated with respect. The main motivation to play one of these lesser-known variations is the desire to take the Najdorf player out of his comfort zone and force him to think on his own at an earlier stage of the game.

One of the best ways to prepare for these lines is carefully to weigh the advantages and disadvantages of a particular variation: think about what strategic goals these moves support or neglect, and how this can be exploited. Let's consider some examples:

• 6.h3 supports kingside expansion but neglects the centre. The logical response is for Black to counter in the centre with 6...e6 7.g4 d5 .

• 6.a4 is a cunning semi-waiting move: White gains space on the queenside and prevents Black from playing ...b5, but the pawn move weakens the b4-square and commits White to kingside castling. My recommendation is to play 6...Nc6 , waiting for White to develop one of his bishops; after 7.Be2 e5 White has the choice between exchanging knights with 8.Nxc6 , or heading back toward the Classical Najdorf with 8.Nb3 .

• 6.Qf3 is an aggressive attacking move, but it commits the queen at an early stage; our choice is the flexible 6...e6 .

• 6.Bd3 develops a piece and supports the e4-pawn, but the bishop blocks the half-open d-file; the response 6...Nc6! forces White to make a concession by either exchanging or retreating his d4-knight.

• 6.Rg1 supports the advance of the g-pawn, but the rook move neglects the centre and commits White to queenside castling. Our response is to counter in the centre with 6...e5 7.Nb3 Be6 8.g4 d5! .

• 6.a3 is a generally useful move which may transpose into other lines; Black can play 6...e5 7.Nf3 Be7 and face the future with confidence.

Black can obtain equality versus these less popular lines by employing some common sense and playing logical moves. The Najdorf player should always remain flexible concerning the pawn-structure; note that the traditional Najdorf reply 6...e5 is only recommended against two of the above lines.


Game 24 (Satyapragyan-Negi) covers the deceptively modest pawn move 6.h3 . White prepares an extended fianchetto on the kingside, but the idea does have the drawback of neglecting the centre and Black responds with the popular line 6...e6 7.g4 d5 . Satyapragyan introduces a new opening idea to maintain his centralized knight; Negi reacts calmly and chases the white knight away from the centre. The critical moment in the battle occurs early in the middlegame when White has the opportunity to win a pawn at the cost of losing the ability to castle; he wisely rejects the pawn-grab and instead offers a sacrifice of his own to lure Black's queen offside. Black accepts the bait but quickly returns the material in order to secure safe passage home for his queen. The position becomes simplified and the result is a well-played draw. The notes to this game also cover the minor lines 6.Qf3 , 6.Bd3 , 6.Rg1 and 6.a3


In Game 25 (Adams-Kariakin), White plays the popular restraining move 6.a4 . One of the ideas behind the queenside pawn advance is to inhibit Black's expansion involving ...b5, but a more subtle point is to see which defensive structure Black will adopt before White commits either of his bishops. Kariakin responds with the flexible 6...Nc6 , targeting the potentially vulnerable b4-square, and after 7.Be2 e5 8.Nb3 Be7 9.O-O O-O the game has transposed into a line of the Classical Najdorf with White having played an early a4 pawn advance. Adams plays 10.Bg5 (the alternatives 10.f4 and 10.Be3 are covered in Chapter 5), aiming to increase his control over the d5-square by exchanging his dark-squared bishop for the black knight. Kariakin introduces an opening novelty designed to help loosen White's queenside bind. Adams goes astray in the complications and we are treated to an instructive endgame example demonstrating the superiority of a bishop over a knight on an open board.

Game 24 [B90]
Swayangsu Satyapragyan - Parimarjan Negi
Indian Ch, Mangalore 2008

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.h3

Let's briefly survey some of White's minor alternatives:

(a) 6.a4 is the subject of Game 25.

(b) 6.a3 (White anticipates ...b5 and prepares a possible retreat for his light-squared bishop) 6...e5 (viable alternatives include 6...g6 aiming for a Dragon, or 6...e6 7.f4 ( 7.Bc4 is covered in note 'a' to White's 7th move in Game 1) 7...Be7 heading for a Scheveningen) 7.Nf3 Be7 (D) and then:

(b1) 8.Bc4 Be6 9.Bxe6 fxe6 is fine for Black.

(b2) 8.Bg5 Be6 9.Bxf6 Bxf6 10.Nd5 Nd7 11.Bc4 Nb6! (Black challenges the d5-knight; this position with White's pawn on a4 is known to be satisfactory for Black) 12.Ba2 ( 12.Nxf6+ Qxf6 13.Bxe6 Qxe6 is equal) 12...Nxd5 13.Bxd5 Qa5+ 14.c3 ( 14.Qd2 Qxd2+ 15.Nxd2 Bxd5 16.exd5 Rc8 is comfortable for Black) 14...Bxd5 15.Qxd5 Qxd5 16.exd5 Rc8 with a dynamically balanced endgame.

(c) 6.Qf3 (the aggressive queen development may transpose into more mainstream lines, but there are some independent ideas) 6...e6 (D) (Black should avoid the mechanical response 6...e5?! because 7.Nf5 gives White good prospects for exploiting the d5-square; however, 6...Qb6 and 6...g6 are reasonable alternatives).

Now White has several options:

(c1) 7.Bg5 is covered via 6.Bg5 e6 7.Qf3 in note 'b' to White's 7th move in Game 15.

(c2) 7.Be3 Nbd7 8.Be2 h5!? prevents g4 and gives Black reasonable play.

(c3) 7.g4!? Nc6 8.Nxc6 ( 8.Nb3 Ne5 9.Qg3 Nexg4 10.Be2!? Ne5 ( 10...h5!? ) 11.f4 Ng6 12.Be3 was suggested by Palliser; now 12...b5 looks like an adequate reply) 8...bxc6 9.g5 Nd7 10.h4 d5 ( 10...Qb6!? ) 11.h5 Rb8 12.a3 Bd6 with sharp play, Kogan-Zambrana, Sort 2006.

(c4) 7.b3?! (the American master Andrew Karklins and the English player Charles Kennaugh are both faithful to this line, but the queenside fianchetto is too slow to cause Black any issues) 7...Qb6! (an important finesse to drive the white knight away from the centre) 8.Nde2 Qc7 ( 8...Nc6!? 9.Bb2 Ne5 10.Qg3 Bd7 is also pleasant for Black) 9.Bb2 b5 10.a3 Bb7 (D).

Now:

(c41) 11.O-O-O?! Nbd7 12.g4? ( 12.Qe3 Rc8 is favourable for Black) 12...Rc8 13.Rd2?! Ne5 14.Qg2?! Nxe4! (a nice combination to exploit White's dormant kingside) 15.Nxe4 Nd3+ 16.Kb1 Ne1 17.Qg3 Bxe4 and Black has a decisive material advantage, Karklins-Wojtkiewicz, World Open, Philadelphia 1995.

(c42) 11.g4 (this was Karklins's improvement over his earlier game featured in line 'c41') 11...Nbd7! (this is the most accurate as White is unable to contest the critical e5-square; also reasonable is 11...d5 12.exd5 Nxd5 13.Bg2 Nd7 with just a slight advantage for Black, Karklins-Svidler, World Open, Philadelphia 1995, although Karklins prevailed in a major upset) 12.Bg2 Ne5 13.Qg3 Nexg4 with an edge for Black as White has insufficient compensation for the pawn.

(d) 6.Bd3 provides additional support for the e4-pawn and prepares to castle. The drawback of this developing move is that the bishop has limited scope and blocks the half-open d-file. Then:

(d1) One option is the traditional 6...e5 7.Nde2 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.f4 Nbd7 10.Kh1 Nc5 11.Ng3 b5 with easy development for Black in Motylev-Balcerak, Swidnica 1999, and several later games.

(d2) 6...Nc6! (D).

The knight development is the most precise way to exploit the momentary instability of the d4-knight. Now White must make a concession by either retreating or exchanging his centralized knight:

(d21) 7.Be3 Ng4 8.Nxc6 bxc6 9.Bc1 g6 is fine for Black.

(d22) 7.Nb3 g6 8.O-O Bg7 9.Bg5 h6 10.Bh4 O-O gives Black a comfortable Dragon-type position, Vozovic-Savchenko, Istanbul 2007.

(d23) 7.Nxc6 bxc6 (this knight exchange is well-known from certain lines of the Taimanov Sicilian: White has exchanged a piece which has moved three times for a piece which has only moved once, but the move ...a6 is of limited use to Black in this new pawn-structure) 8.O-O g6 9.f4 Bg7 10.Kh1 Nd7 11.Qe1 a5! (Black prepares to exchange White's potentially dangerous light-squared bishop) 12.Be3 a4 13.Rb1 O-O 14.Nd1 Ba6 with equality, Campora-Ehlvest, San Roque 1996.

(e) With 6.Rg1 , White supports the advance of his g-pawn; the downside is that he neglects his development, negates the possibility of king-side castling, and in some lines the rook would be better off on its original square. Despite these drawbacks, the rook move is surprisingly popular as White obtains considerable benefit from playing g4-g5 to drive the f6-knight away from the critical e4- and d5-squares as we have seen in many English Attack lines. 6...e5 (Black has a wide range of reasonable alternatives, including 6...g6 , 6...e6 and 6...Nc6 ) 7.Nb3 Be6 8.g4 d5! (D).

Black strikes back in the centre before White can implement his plan. Now:

(e1) 9.g5?! is a reckless thrust: 9...Nxe4 10.Nxe4 dxe4 11.Qxd8+ Kxd8 12.Be3 Kc7 13.O-O-O Nc6 14.Bg2 (Gorovykh-Utegaliev, St Petersburg 2007) 14...h6! (Black cracks open the kingside) 15.gxh6 g6 16.Bxe4 Bxb3 17.axb3 Bxh6 with an edge for Black thanks to his superior pawn-structure.

(e2) 9.exd5 Nxd5 10.Nxd5 Qxd5 11.Be3 Qxd1+ (the popular alternative 11...Nc6 12.Qxd5 Bxd5 13.O-O-O O-O-O is also sufficient to maintain the balance) 12.Rxd1 Nd7 13.Bg2 O-O-O and now:

(e21) 14.Bd5 Nc5 15.Bxe6+ Nxe6 16.Ke2 Be7 with an equal game, L.Dominguez-Naiditsch, Wijk aan Zee 2004.

(e22) 14.a3!? b6 15.Bd5 Bxd5 16.Rxd5 Be7 is equal, L.Dominguez-Delgado Ramirez, Capablanca Memorial, Havana 2004.

We return to 6.h3 . This move was employed by Fischer several times during the early 1960s; the modest pawn advance enabled him to avoid revealing any ideas in the main lines of his favourite opening and forced his opponents to think on their own.

6...e6 (D)

Black has a wide range of reasonable moves here, including 6...Nc6 heading for a Scheveningen, and 6...g6 aiming for a Dragon. Black's most popular alternative is the immediate 6...e5 , but after 7.Nde2 White often plays g4 followed by Bg2 and he has gained a tempo over the related Fianchetto Variation line - see Game 10.

7.g4

ECO classifies this position as a Scheveningen (code B81) via the Keres Attack move-order 5...e6 (instead of 5...a6 ) 6.g4 a6 7.h3 . However, this line is more likely to arise via the Najdorf move-order as the stronger alternatives 7.Be3 and 7.g5 are more challenging for Black.

7...d5 (D)

Negi responds in accordance with classical development principles by countering the wing advance with action in the centre of the board. The alternatives 7...Nc6 and 7...b5 are fully playable, although I shall not be examining these lines here.

8.exd5

White opens the long diagonal for his light-squared bishop. Other moves are harmless for Black:

(a) 8.e5?! Nfd7 gives Black a favourable French-type structure because White's extended kingside fianchetto does not enhance his prospects.

(b) 8.Nde2 Nxe4 9.Nxe4 dxe4 10.Qxd8+ Kxd8 11.Nc3 Bd7 with equal chances in the endgame, Romero-Ubilava, Subijana de Alava 2001.

(c) 8.Bg2 Nxe4 9.Nxe4 dxe4 10.c3 ( 10.O-O h5! ruffles White's exposed kingside pawns) 10...Nd7!? (Black keeps the queens on the board; also satisfactory is 10...e5 11.Nc2 Qxd1+ 12.Kxd1 Bd7 13.Bxe4 Bc6 with an equal endgame) 11.Bxe4 Qc7 12.O-O Bd6 with chances for both sides - Arizmendi and Moreno.

8...Nxd5 (D)

9.Bd2

This is White's most ambitious line because he maintains his centralized d4-knight. The alternative is 9.Nde2 Bb4 , and now:

(a) 10.Bg2 O-O (D) and then:

(a1) 11.Bd2 Nb6! (this is an important motif in several Sicilian lines: Black avoids the exchange of knights as this would assist White in freeing his position, and instead leaves White slightly bottled up with the 'superfluous knight'; this is more dynamic than an earlier game in the same tournament which continued 11...Nxc3 12.Nxc3 Nc6 13.a3 Be7 14.O-O Qc7 with equal chances, Kurajica-Mecking, Hastings 1971/2) 12.Ne4 Be7 13.O-O Nc6 14.b3 f5! with a kingside initiative for Black, Kurajica-Najdorf, Hastings 1971/2.

(a2) 11.O-O Nxc3 12.Nxc3 Qc7 (Black should avoid 12...Bxc3?! 13.Qxd8 Rxd8 14.bxc3 Nc6 15.Rb1 with a slight pull for White thanks to his queenside pressure, Zherebukh-Van Wely, Moscow 2009) 13.Qe2 (Gabdrakhmanov-Kengis, USSR Junior Ch, Riga 1977) 13...Bd7! with equal chances as Black will contest the long diagonal with ...Bc6.

(b) 10.Bd2 Nxc3 11.Nxc3 Bd7! (Geller prepares to contest the long diagonal) 12.Qe2 Bc6 13.Rg1 Qc7 14.O-O-O Nd7 is equal, Gipslis-Geller, Moscow 1967.

9...Bb4 (D)

10.Qf3!?

Satyapragyan introduces a new move to lend support to the c3-knight.

Alternatives:

(a) 10.Nde2 transposes to note 'b' to White's 9th move above.

(b) 10.Ne4 O-O 11.c4 Bxd2+ (another idea is 11...Qb6!? 12.Bxb4 Nxb4 13.c5 Qa5 14.Nb3 Qc7 with equality) 12.Qxd2 Nf6!? 13.Bg2 Nxe4 14.Bxe4 Nd7 15.O-O-O Qc7 and the chances are balanced - Arizmendi and Moreno.

(c) 10.Nxd5!? Bxd2+! (this is safer than 10...Qxd5 11.Bxb4 Qxh1 12.Qd3 Qd5 13.Qe3 Nc6 14.Nxc6 Qxc6 15.O-O-O and White has a strong attack in return for the sacrificed exchange) 11.Qxd2 Qxd5 12.O-O-O!? ( 12.Rg1 Bd7 13.Bg2 ( 13.O-O-O!? ) 13...Qe5+ 14.Qe3 Qxe3+ 15.fxe3 Bc6 is equal) 12...Bd7 13.Be2 Qxa2 ("Black is much better" according to Arizmendi and Moreno, but I think White can mix things up here) 14.Qc3! Qa4 ( 14...e5 15.Nf3 gives White an attack) 15.Bc4 Nc6 16.Nf5 ( 16.Nb3!? ) 16...exf5 17.Bxf7+! (D) and now:

(c1) 17...Kxf7? 18.Rxd7+ Ne7 19.Rxe7+ Kxe7 20.Qxg7+ Kd6 21.Rd1+ Kc5 22.b4+! Qxb4 ( 22...Kxb4 23.Qe7+ Kc4 24.Qe6+ Kb4 25.Qd6+ mates) 23.Qc7+ Kb5 24.Qxb7+ Ka4 25.Qc6+ Ka5 ( 25...Qb5 26.Rd4+ mates) 26.Qc7+ Ka4 27.Qd7+ Ka3 ( 27...Ka5 28.Rd5+ Qb5 29.Rxf5 is winning for White) 28.Rd3+ Ka2 29.Qd5+ Ka1 30.Qd4+ Qxd4 31.Ra3# .

(c2) 17...Kd8 18.Be6 ( 18.Qxg7?! Qa1+ 19.Kd2 Qa5+ 20.Kc1 Qe5 defends) 18...Qa1+ 19.Kd2 Qa4 20.Kc1 Qa1+ 21.Kd2 Qa4 22.Kc1 with a draw by repetition.

10...Nxc3 11.bxc3

White must avoid 11.Bxc3?? Qxd4 and Black wins.

11...Be7

Another reasonable idea is 11...Bd6!? 12.Bd3 Nd7 13.O-O O-O with a solid position for Black.

12.Bg2 O-O 13.Rb1 e5 (D)

14.Nf5

White wisely refrains from 14.Rxb7?! exd4 (this is more ambitious than 14...Bxb7 15.Qxb7 exd4 16.Qxa8 dxc3 17.Bxc3 Qc7 with roughly equal chances) 15.Rb1 Ra7 16.Rxb8 Bd6 17.Rb1 Re8+ 18.Kf1 dxc3 19.Bxc3 Rd7 and Black has strong pressure in exchange for the missing pawn - White's king is misplaced and his king's rook is out of play.

14...Bc5 15.Be3

The exchange of dark-squared bishops favours White because it increases his chances of infiltrating on b6 or d6.

15...Bxe3 16.Nxe3 Qa5 17.O-O

White has a slight lead in development to offset his crippled pawn-structure.

17...Nc6 18.Nc4 Qxa2 19.Qe2 (D)

White defends his c-pawn and threatens to trap the black queen with Ra1, so Black is compelled to return the extra pawn in order to extract his queen from its precarious post.

19...Be6 20.Nxe5 Nxe5 21.Qxe5 Rae8 22.Qg3 b5 23.Ra1 Qxc2 24.Rfc1!

White must remain alert as the careless capture 24.Rxa6? allows 24...Bc4 25.Rfa1 b4! 26.R6a4 b3! and Black wins material.

24...Qb3 25.Rcb1 Qc4

Black has no good way to avoid the simplification as 25...Qc2 26.Rc1 maintains the balance.

26.Rxa6 Rc8 27.Rb6 Qxc3 28.Qxc3 1/2-1/2

Game 25 [B90]
Michael Adams - Sergei Kariakin
European Clubs Cup, Kemer 2007

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.a4 (D)

White plays a generally useful move before developing either of his bishops. However, the a-pawn's advance does have some drawbacks:

• White is committed to lines involving king-side castling as the a4-pawn would be an attractive target with the white king on the queenside.

• The b4-square has been weakened; Black can often exploit this with the manoeuvre ...Nc6-b4 to put pressure on the c2-pawn and cover the d5-square.

This line is a form of the Classical Najdorf covered in Chapter 5, but White has some independent ideas below which are only available to him via the 6.a4 move-order.

6...Nc6 (D)

The idea behind the knight development is to wait for White to commit one of his bishops before playing ...e5. Black has several reasonable alternatives here, including 6...g6 heading for a Dragon and 6...e6 aiming for a Scheveningen. Black's most popular alternative is the immediate 6...e5 7.Nf3 ( 7.Nb3 Be7 8.Be2 Nc6 transposes to the main game) 7...Be7 ( 7...h6 is a popular alternative) 8.Bc4 (note how White is able to develop the bishop to c4 in a single move) 8...Be6! (it is important for Black to challenge White's light-squared bishop and contend for control of d5) 9.Qe2 O-O 10.O-O Qc7 11.Bxe6 ( 11.Ba2 Nc6!? ( 11...Rc8!? with the idea of ...Nbd7 also looks reasonable, as suggested by Arizmendi and Moreno) 12.Rd1 Nb4 with equal chances in Maijanovic-Chandler, Nis 1983; Black has exploited the weakness of the b4-square to cover d5) 11...fxe6 12.a5 h6 with equality, W.Wittmann-Nguyen Huynh Minh, Budapest 2008. Black's last move prepares ...Nbd7 without allowing Ng5 in response.

7.Be2

White has several harmless alternatives:

(a) 7.f4 Bg4!? 8.Nf3 g6 ( 8...Rc8!? ) 9.h3 Bxf3 10.Qxf3 Bg7 11.Bd3 O-O 12.O-O Nd7 (Pirc Defence players will recognize this structure from the Austrian Attack; the addition of the moves a4 and ...a6 favours Black because his pieces can utilize the b4-square) 13.Kh1 Rc8 14.Rb1 Nb4 with a slight advantage for Black, Ye Jiangchuan-Gelfand, Erevan Olympiad 1996.

(b) 7.Nb3 b6!? 8.Be2 Bb7 9.Be3 e6 10.O-O Rc8 11.f3 Be7 12.Qd2 O-O with a solid position for Black, Mestel-Sunye Neto, Las Palmas Interzonal 1982.

(c) 7.Bg5 (the bishop development does not harmonize well with a4 because in the Richter-Rauzer Variation White normally castles queenside) 7...e6 8.Be2 Be7 9.O-O O-O 10.Qd2 Qc7 11.Kh1 Rd8 with a solid position for Black, Rios Escobar-Vasquez, Santiago 2005.

7...e5 (D)

8.Nb3

White has two alternatives:

(a) 8.Nxc6 bxc6 9.f4 a5!? 10.Be3 ( 10.fxe5 dxe5 11.Qxd8+ Kxd8 was fine for Black in King-Rashkovsky, Cappelle la Grande 1990 and several subsequent games; Black has good control over the important central squares) 10...Be7 ( 10...Nd7 11.O-O Be7 is equal - I.Gurevich) 11.fxe5 dxe5 12.Qxd8+ Bxd8 13.O-O-O Be7 (this is more accurate than 13...Be6?! 14.Rd6 Bd7 15.Bc4 with a slight advantage for White, I.Gurevich-Palac, Kecskemet 1991) 14.Bb6 Be6 15.Bc7 Nd7 with equality, Zinchenko-Tukhaev, Ukrainian Junior Ch, Dnepropetrovsk 2005.

(b) 8.Nf3 h6 ( 8...Be7 is also quite playable; the idea behind the pawn move is to keep more pieces on the board by preventing White from increasing his control over the d5-square with the standard manoeuvre Bg5 followed by Bxf6; the position is actually a form of the Classical Najdorf where White has played an early a4 and Black has responded with ...Nc6 targeting the b4-square) 9.O-O Be7 10.Re1 O-O 11.Bf1 Bg4 with equality, Faibisovich-Vaulin, Pardubice 1997.

8...Be7 9.O-O O-O (D)

10.Bg5

White develops his dark-squared bishop with the intention of exchanging it for the black knight; this is a typical manoeuvre employed by White to increase his control over d5. Two alternatives transpose to lines of the Classical Najdorf:

(a) 10.f4 exf4 11.Bxf4 Be6 transposes to note 'c' to White's 9th move in Game 11.

(b) 10.Be3 Be6 is note 'c' to White's 10th move in Game 12.

10...Be6 11.Bxf6 Bxf6 12.Nd5 (D)

12...Rc8

Black completes his development and seizes the half-open c-file; 12...Bg5 13.a5 Rc8 14.c3 ( 14.Bg4 Nb8!? ) reaches the same position.

Alternatives:

(a) 12...Bxd5?! (Black should generally avoid this exchange as it cedes White control over d5) 13.Qxd5 Qc7 14.c3 Ne7 15.Qc4 with a slight plus for White as he can exert pressure along the d-file, Kostro-O.Jakobsen, Arhus 1971.

(b) 12...Nd4 13.Nxf6+ Qxf6 14.Nxd4 exd4 15.Bd3 (more challenging is 15.a5!? intending Ra4) 15...Rac8 enabled Black to equalize in Short-Lautier, Wijk aan Zee 1997.

13.c3

A useful move to counter Black's pressure along the half-open c-file; 13.a5 Bg5 14.c3 is another move-order to reach the game continuation.

Alternatives:

(a) 13.Nxf6+?! Qxf6 14.Qxd6 Rfd8 15.Qa3 Bxb3 16.cxb3 (after 16.Qxb3? Nd4 Black wins material) 16...Nd4 with an edge for Black thanks to his dominant knight, Marcotulli-Borst, IECG e-mail 2001.

(b) 13.Bg4 Nb8!? (Black prepares to redeploy his knight to the kingside; this is safer than 13...Nd4?! 14.Bxe6 fxe6 15.Nxf6+ Qxf6 16.c3 Nxb3 17.Qxb3 with a slight pull for White because of his superior pawn-structure, Adams-Edouard, European Union Ch, Liverpool 2008) 14.Bxe6 fxe6 (Black does not object to the exchange of light-squared bishops on e6 because the pawn recapture enables him to retain control over d5; this position should be compared to note 'a' to Black's 12th move) 15.Nxf6+ Qxf6 16.c3 Qe7 17.Qe2 Nd7 18.Rfd1 Nf6 19.a5 1/2-1/2 Zinchenko-Borovikov, Pardubice 2006.

13...Bg5 (D)

14.a5

This is the logical follow-up to White's previous play as he seizes control of the b6-square. A less critical line is 14.Nd2 Ne7 15.c4 Bxd2 ( 15...f5!? also looks comfortable for Black) 16.Qxd2 Nc6 with a solid position for Black as he has a strong knight outpost on d4, Vogt-Ftacnik, Czechoslovak Ch, Trencianske Teplice 1979.

14...Nb8!?

Kariakin introduces a novelty. The knight redeployment to d7 enables Black to control the sensitive b6-square; sometimes the knight may move to c5 or f6 to exert pressure on White's e4-pawn. This manoeuvre is well-known from

several Sicilian lines; Black can afford the loss of time because he has completed his development and the pawn-structure is stable. The alternative is 14...Ne7 15.Nb6 Rc6 (another idea is 15...Rc7 16.Bg4 f5 17.exf5 Nxf5 with a balanced game, Abreu-Bruzon, Santa Clara 2000) 16.Bg4 Nc8 17.Nd5 Qd7 18.h3 Bd8 19.Ra4 Ne7 with equal chances, Yudasin-A.Shneider, European Clubs Cup, Lyons 1994. Black has covered the sensitive b6-square.

15.Ra4!

Adams utilizes the fourth rank to activate his rook and increase the pressure on Black's queenside.

15...Nd7 16.Rb4 Rb8 17.Bc4 b5!?

Kariakin opts to activate his queenside pieces at the cost of incurring an isolated a6-pawn.

18.axb6 Nxb6 (D)

19.Nxb6!

Alternatives:

(a) 19.Bxa6?! Nxd5 20.Rxb8 Qxb8 21.exd5 Bd7 22.Bd3 Qb6 gives Black compensation for the pawn according to Kariakin in Informator 101. A possible continuation is 23.Nd2 Qxb2 24.Nc4 Qxc3 25.Nxd6 f5 with good play for Black thanks to his bishop-pair.

(b) 19.Na5 Bxd5! 20.Bxd5 Nxd5 21.Nc6 Nxb4 22.Nxd8 Rfxd8 23.cxb4 Rxb4 is equal - Kariakin.

19...Rxb6 20.Bxe6 fxe6 (D)

21.Na5?!

White should take advantage of the momentary looseness of the black kingside with 21.Qg4! Re8 22.Rxb6 Qxb6 23.Qh5! (the idea is to coax Black into weakening his kingside pawn-cover before exchanging minor pieces; this is more challenging than 23.Qxg5 Qxb3 24.Qd2 d5 with equal chances) 23...g6 24.Qxg5 Qxb3 25.Qd2 (White has a slight advantage according to Kariakin) 25...Qb6 (the point of White's 23rd move is revealed after 25...d5 26.h4! with an edge as White is threatening to crack open the kingside) 26.b4 with a slight structural advantage for White.

21...Rxb4 22.Nc6?!
