9 Poisoned Pawn and Polugaevsky Variations

The Poisoned Pawn Variation, 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Qb6 , has a well-deserved reputation as one of Black's most uncompromising opening choices. Black seeks to exploit the absence of White's dark-squared bishop from the queenside by launching an immediate counterattack on White's b-pawn. Many of Black's manoeuvres in this line do not appear to conform to generally accepted chess principles; for example, Black often makes many moves with his queen while lagging in development. The justification for this extreme queen activity is that the typical exchange of White's dark-squared bishop for Black's king's knight enables the black queen to operate virtually unopposed on the dark squares; the queen plays the role of 'disrupter' by constantly pecking away at White's queenside. White's dark-square colour-complex problems also appear in several sacrificial lines: White often appears to be closing in for the kill only to come up short as the black king scoots away along the dark squares.

Former World Champions Bobby Fischer and Garry Kasparov scored heavily with the Poisoned Pawn; the opening is well-suited to players who have the capacity for hard work, enjoy independent research and trust their ability to deal with unusual material imbalances. Many leading players employ the Poisoned Pawn and it is on the cutting edge of opening theory; the line is popular in correspondence chess and at lower levels too. The Poisoned Pawn requires a relatively large amount of initial effort to learn, but it can be a very effective weapon at club level because White often responds passively to avoid opening theory and a well-prepared player is able to secure easy equality as Black.

The Polugaevsky Variation, 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 b5 , is among Black's most provocative opening lines. He allows White to attack the pinned f6-knight with 8.e5 because he can save the piece with the tactical idea 8...dxe5 9.fxe5 Qc7 . White has several sharp attacking ideas and Black must play with great precision to survive the tactical onslaught. Many players consider the variation to be too high risk/low reward to employ as a primary defensive weapon, but several of the lines have been rather neglected recently and there is plenty of opportunity for independent research. The Polugaevsky Variation has been out of favour for many years since the passing of its namesake exponent, but perhaps the line will experience a revival if a leading player takes up the gauntlet.


Game 19 (Kasimdzhanov-Sadvakasov) highlights (after 7...Qb6 8.Qd2 Qxb2 ) the Poisoned Pawn knight retreat 9.Nb3 . Black responds with 9...Qa3 to remove his queen from the danger zone. Sadvakasov plays 13...Rc8 and invites Kasimdzhanov to occupy the kingside and put pressure on the f7-pawn with 14.Bh5?!; the bishop looks attractive here, but recent experience has shown that the bishop can in fact interfere with White's kingside attack. Black manoeuvres his knight to the queenside with 16...Na5, to which White responds by 17.f5, increasing the pressure on the kingside. The black knight returns to the centre and takes control of the e5-square at the cost of two tempi. Kasimdzhanov sacrifices the exchange to try to exploit the far-away position of the black queen, but Sadvakasov calmly refutes the sacrifice and exploits his material advantage. This game is more evidence that it may not be necessary for Black to play ...h5 to prevent White from occupying the h5-square with his light-squared bishop in these lines.


In Game 20 (Shirov-Wang Hao), White chooses the main line of the Poisoned Pawn with 9.Rb1 Qa3 10.e5 , and Black responds with the latest idea, 15...Bc5 to try to keep lines closed. Shirov circles the black kingside with 19.Rd6! and after 19...Qxe5 20.Bc4 Wang Hao continues developing with 20...Nc6?! (20...Qh5!? may be necessary here). Black goes astray with 22...Nde5?, allowing the spectacular sacrifice 23.Rxh6!!, leading to the win of Black's queen. This is currently the most topical line of the Poisoned Pawn and Shirov has put the onus upon Black to demonstrate a clear path to equality. The main theme of this game is the importance of piece activity: White focused on completing his development and he was able to generate irresistible threats on the kingside.


Game 21 (Leko-Ghaem Maghami) is a Polugaevsky Variation featuring the sharp line 10.exf6 . Black responds with 12...Ra7 and the players follow established theory for several more moves. Leko's new idea of 19.Nfg5! followed by 20.Rxb4!! places enormous pressure on Black's position; Ghaem Maghami goes down quickly after 22...Rg8?! (22...Bc3 is more resilient, but then 23 Rc1!! is strong) and White was able to finish off the game with an attractive temporary queen sacrifice. This was a relatively one-sided game, but it serves to illustrate the difficulties Black has defending when he is behind in development and his king is stuck in the centre of the board.

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Qb6 (D)

This is the characteristic move of the Poisoned Pawn Variation, by which Black seeks to exploit the absence of White's dark-squared bishop from the queenside.

8.Qd2

White offers the b-pawn in exchange for a lead in development and the possibility of gaining time by attacking the black queen. The alternatives are harmless for Black:

(a) 8.Qd3 (the white queen is somewhat vulnerable on the d3-square, but after an eventual ...Qa3 Black must be wary of tactics involving White playing Nd5) 8...Qxb2 9.Rb1 Qa3 10.f5 Be7 11.Be2 Nc6 12.fxe6 fxe6 13.Nxc6 bxc6 (D) and now:

(a1) 14.Bh5+!? Nxh5 ( 14...g6? 15.e5! gave White a strong attack in Mamedov-Kokarev, European Ch, Plovdiv 2008) 15.Bxe7 Nf4 16.Qd4 e5 17.Qxe5 dxe5 18.Bxa3 Nxg2+ 19.Kf2 Nf4 20.Bd6 Ng6 and the complications led to an equal endgame in Huschenbeth-Bracker, Hamburg Ch 2008.

(a2) 14.O-O Qa5 15.Bxf6 gxf6 16.Bg4 O-O (D) and then:

(a21) 17.Qc4 d5 18.Qxc6 Qc5+ 19.Qxc5 Bxc5+ 20.Kh1 dxe4 21.Nxe4 Be7 (Areshchenko evaluated this as unclear in Informator 86) 22.Be2 f5 23.Ng3 Ra7 with a comfortable position for Black thanks to his bishop-pair.

(a22) 17.Ne2 Qg5 18.Bh3 d5 19.Nf4 dxe4 20.Qxe4 Qc5+ 21.Kh1 f5 22.Qe2 Rf6 is equal, Solodovnichenko-Zakhartsov, Cappelle la Grande 2009.

(b) 8.a3 (the pawn move sets a cheap trap but does not substantially enhance White's position) 8...Nc6 ( 8...Qxb2?? 9.Na4 traps the queen) 9.Nb3 Be7 10.Qf3 Qc7 11.O-O-O Bd7 (note that Black leaves his b7-pawn at home so as to provide pawn-cover for queenside castling) 12.g4 h6 13.Bxf6 Bxf6 14.h4 O-O-O 15.Bg2 Kb8 16.g5 Be7 with a balanced game, Luther-L.Dominguez, Capablanca Memorial, Havana 2001.

(c) 8.Nb3 (the knight retreat is harmless but fairly popular as it avoids sharp theory) 8...Be7 ( 8...Qe3+ may not be to the taste of most Najdorf players, but it forces an exchange of queens that leaves White no more than marginally better) 9.Qf3 Nbd7 10.O-O-O Qc7 11.Bd3 b5 12.Rhe1 b4 (another solid line is 12...Bb7 ; for example, 13.Qh3?! ( 13.Kb1 is safer) 13...b4 14.Nb1 e5! 15.N1d2 a5 with a queenside initiative for Black, Kamsky-Topalov, Sofia 2006) 13.Ne2 ( 13.Nb1?! Bb7 14.Qh3 is a position we saw in the previous bracket) 13...Bb7 (D) and now:

(c1) 14.Kb1 a5! (the knight on b3 is an attractive target for Black's a-pawn) 15.Ned4 a4 16.Nb5 Qb6 17.Nd2 O-O 18.Qg3 Ba6 19.Nxd6!? Bxd6 20.Bxf6 Nxf6 21.e5 Nh5 22.Qf3 (Pegoraro-Mietzner, Arco 2000) 22...Bxd3 23.cxd3 Nxf4 24.Qxf4 Bc5 25.d4 Be7 with an edge for Black as his queenside attack is in progress and the bishop is more effective than the knight.

(c2) 14.Ned4 Nc5 15.Kb1 O-O 16.g4 Rac8 17.Bxf6 ( 17.Qh3?! Nxd3 18.cxd3 a5! gave Black a strong initiative on the queenside in Kamsky-Vallejo Pons, FIDE World Cup, Khanty-Mansiisk 2005) 17...Bxf6 18.g5 Bxd4 ( 18...Be7 19.h4 a5 with an unclear position - Ki.Georgiev and Kolev; a possible continuation is 20.f5!? d5 21.Nxc5 Bxc5 22.Nb3 Ba7 with roughly equal chances) 19.Nxd4 Rfd8 20.Rf1 e5 21.Nb3 Ne6 with a comfortable position for Black as his minor pieces are more effective than their white counterparts.

We now return to 8.Qd2 (D):

8...Qxb2

Black has the option of declining the gambit pawn with 8...Nc6 . This knight move has been played over 400 times, but it is not even mentioned in ECO. This is a good secondary or surprise weapon for Poisoned Pawn players in case they run into trouble in the main lines with 8...Qxb2 during a tournament. But note that the characteristic positions are more akin to the Richter-Rauzer Attack ( 5...Nc6 6.Bg5 ), which will also be in the repertoire of many 6.Bg5 players. Let's examine White's options:

(a) 9.O-O-O Qxd4 10.Qxd4 Nxd4 11.Rxd4 Be7 (D) and then:

(a1) 12.Na4 Bd7 13.Nb6 Rd8 14.Nxd7 Nxd7 ( 14...Rxd7 15.Be2 h6 16.Bh4 Nh5 17.Bxh5 Bxh4 is equal, Kramnik-Svidler, Wijk aan Zee 2004) 15.Bxe7 Kxe7 16.Be2 e5 with equal chances - Ki.Georgiev and Kolev.

(a2) 12.Be2 Bd7 13.Rhd1 Bc6 14.Bf3 h6 15.Bxf6 gxf6 16.f5 h5 with a balanced endgame, Walek-Shtyrenkov, Pardubice 1998.

(b) 9.Nb3 Ng4! 10.Bd3 h6 11.Bh4 g5! is an idea that is well known from the Main Line (Chapter 10): Black obtains control over the e5-square. Then 12.fxg5 Be7 13.O-O-O Qe3 (the exchange of queens eliminates White's attacking ambitions) 14.Be2 Nce5 15.Bxg4 Nxg4 16.Rhe1 Qxd2+ 17.Nxd2 hxg5 18.Bg3 Ne5 led to equality in Short-Naiditsch, Baku 2007.

(c) 9.Bxf6 (White exchanges his bishop for the black knight to weaken Black's kingside pawn-structure and to avoid the ...Ng4 idea in line 'b') 9...gxf6 10.Nb3 Bd7 11.O-O-O O-O-O 12.Kb1 Kb8 13.Be2 h5 (D) and here:

(c1) 14.h4!? (the idea behind this move is to fix the h5-pawn and gang up on it later) 14...Na5 15.Rh3 ( 15.Nxa5 Qxa5 16.Bf3 f5 17.exf5 Qxf5 is equal, Luther-Quezada, Capablanca Memorial, Havana 2004) 15...Rc8 (another idea is 15...Nxb3!? 16.axb3 Bc6 with a reasonable position for Black) 16.Nxa5 Qxa5 17.Rd3 (Sulskis-Loginov, Vilnius 1997) 17...Bc6 with a balanced game.

(c2) 14.Rhf1 Be7 15.Rf3 Rdg8 ( 15...h4 16.Bf1 (Spraggett-Quezada, Buenos Aires 2005) and now 16...Rh5 intending ...f5 is a suggestion by Ki.Georgiev and Kolev) 16.Bf1 h4 17.Qe1 Rg4 with equality, Kamsky-Ponomariov, FIDE World Cup, Khanty-Mansiisk 2007.

White's most popular choice, 9.Rb1 , is covered in Game 20.

The knight retreat takes away some squares from the black queen at the cost of removing the white knight from the centre.

9...Qa3

Black has only two playable alternatives:

(a) 9...Nbd7 10.Bxf6 gxf6 ( 10...Nxf6?? 11.a4! with the idea of 12.Ra2 traps the black queen) 11.Be2 and then:

(a1) 11...Qa3 transposes to the note to Black's 11th move below.

(a2) 11...b5 12.O-O Bb7 13.Rab1 Qa3 14.f5 Rc8 with sharp play, Spraggett-Komljenovic, Seville 2007.

(a3) 11...Nc5 12.O-O Bd7 13.Rab1 Qa3 (Black must avoid 13...Nxb3?? 14.axb3 Qa3 15.b4 Rc8 16.Rb3 1-0 Luther-Kersten, Bad Zwesten 2002) 14.f5 Rc8 15.Kh1 Nxb3 16.Rxb3 (Kurnosov-Kokarev, Serpukhov 2002) 16...Qa5! also leads to a sharp position.

(b) 9...Nc6 (this move has declined in popularity as the main line leaves Black defending a slightly inferior endgame) 10.Bxf6 ( 10.a3?? loses to 10...Na5 ) 10...gxf6 11.Na4 Qa3 12.Nb6 Rb8 13.Nc4 Qa4 14.a3 (White restricts the mobility of the black queen; instead, 14.Be2 allows Black to force a more favourable exchange of queens by 14...b5! 15.Nxd6+ Bxd6 16.Qxd6 Qb4+ with equal chances) 14...b5 15.Nxd6+ Bxd6 16.Qxd6 Qxe4+ 17.Be2 (D).

Now:

(b1) 17...Bb7?! 18.Nc5 Qd4 19.Rd1 Qc3+?! 20.Kf2 Rd8 21.Nd7 Rg8 (Vouldis-Paunovic, Kavala 1997) and now Nunn recommends 22.Rd3! Rxg2+ 23.Kf1! Rg8 24.Rg1 Rxg1+ 25.Kxg1 Qe1+ 26.Bf1 , when Black cannot defend against the mating threats on f8 and f6.

(b2) 17...Qd5 18.Qxd5 exd5 (Black is slightly worse because of his inferior pawn-structure) 19.O-O-O Ne7 20.Bf3 Be6 21.Rhe1 O-O 22.g4 f5 23.gxf5 Nxf5 and here White can choose between 24.Bxd5 (Adams-Gelfand, Candidates match (game 3), Wijk aan Zee 1994) or 24.Nc5 , as in Nunn-Stohl, Bundesliga 1995/6. This game is annotated by John Nunn in Grandmaster Chess Move by Move.

We now return to 9...Qa3 (D):

10.Bxf6

White exchanges his bishop for the black knight in order to weaken Black's pawn-structure and discourage kingside castling.

Black has no problems after 10.Bd3 Be7 11.O-O h6 12.Bxf6 ( 12.Bh4 Nxe4! 13.Nxe4 Bxh4 14.f5 exf5! 15.Bb5+ axb5 16.Nxd6+ Kf8 17.Nxc8 Nc6! with a clear plus for Black in Spassky-Fischer, World Ch match (game 7), Reykjavik 1972; several later games have confirmed this evaluation) 12...Bxf6 13.e5 dxe5 14.Ne4 Nd7 15.f5 Qe7 16.fxe6 Qxe6 17.Bc4 Qb6+ 18.Kh1 O-O 19.Rf3 Qc7 20.Bd5 Be7 ( 20...a5!? ) 21.Raf1 Nb6 favours Black, Mukhin-G.Sorokin, USSR 1973.

The alternative is 11...Nd7 ( 11...h5 12.O-O Nd7 is equivalent) 12.O-O ( 12.Bh5? Nc5 13.f5 ( 13.O-O?? Nxb3 14.axb3 Qc5+ costs White a piece) 13...Rg8 favours Black) 12...h5 13.Kh1 h4! 14.h3 Be7 15.Rad1 b5! and then:

(a) 16.Bxb5? axb5 17.Nxb5 Qa4 18.Nc7+ Kf8 19.Nxa8 Qxa8 20.Rde1 Bb7 with a clear plus for Black thanks to his pressure along the h1-a8 diagonal, Delavekouras-Lakatos, ICCF e-mail 2003.

(b) 16.Nb1?! Qb2!? 14.Bh5?!

The bishop excursion to the kingside is White's most popular choice here, but it is difficult to see how to develop the attack.

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Qb6 8.Qd2 Qxb2

For comments on the moves up to here, see Game 19.

9.Rb1

The rook move is the most popular choice: White occupies the half-open b-file and gains time by attacking the black queen. 9.Nb3 was covered in Game 19. Black has only one possible reply.

9...Qa3 (D)

10.e5

The merits of this central pawn advance have been hotly debated for many years. As recently as in his 2003 book My Great Predecessors, Part II, Kasparov adorned 10.e5 with the 'dubious move' symbol and called it "An unprepared attack." The move is currently back in fashion and White has recently scored several impressive attacking victories, but the only thing certain is that the pendulum is likely to swing back and forth again in the coming years as improvements are developed for both sides.

Let's briefly examine White's tenth-move alternatives:

(a) 10.Bxf6 (White exchanges his dark-squared bishop for the knight in order to break up Black's kingside pawn-structure) 10...gxf6 11.Be2 Nc6 12.Nxc6 bxc6 13.O-O Qa5 (this is a typical manoeuvre in the Poisoned Pawn: Black improves the position of his queen and pins the c3-knight) 14.Kh1 Be7 15.f5 h5! (if White reacts passively, Black will follow up with ...h4, meeting h3 with ...Qe5, ...d5 and ...Bd6) 16.Bf3 Kf8! 17.fxe6 Bxe6! 18.Rb7 Re8 gives Black a comfortable position thanks to his control of the central squares, Geissler-Verhoef, ICCF e-mail World Ch 2002.

(b) 10.Be2 (White completes his development before committing to a central pawn advance) 10...Be7 11.O-O h6 12.Bh4 Nbd7 13.Kh1 Qa5 14.f5 Ne5 15.fxe6 fxe6 16.Bh5+ (D) and now:

(b1) 16...Nxh5 17.Bxe7 Qc7 ( 17...Kxe7?? loses to 18.Nd5+ ) 18.Ndb5 axb5 19.Nxb5 Qxe7 20.Nxd6+ Kd8 ( 20...Kd7?? loses to 21.Nf5+ ) 21.Nf7++ Ke8 and here White could play on with 22.Nxe5!? (rather than 22.Nd6+ Kd8 1/2-1/2 D.Fischer-Firsching, corr. 1999) 22...Rf8 23.Ng6 Rxf1+ 24.Rxf1 Qc5 25.e5!? Rxa2 26.Qd1 with a dangerous attack.

(b2) 16...Nf7 17.Bxf7+ ( 17.e5 dxe5 18.Bxf6 gxf6! 19.Nde2 f5 favoured Black in Leisebein-Schlupeck, corr. 1987) 17...Kxf7 18.Qd3 Rd8 19.Nce2 Qh5 with equality, Behling-Geryk, ICCF e-mail 2002.

(c) 10.f5 (the idea here is to home in on the e6-square; this line was popular before the resurgence of the 10.e5 variation) 10...Nc6 11.fxe6 fxe6 12.Nxc6 bxc6 13.e5 (White sacrifices a second pawn to free the e4-square for his knight; 13.Be2 is another major line) 13...dxe5 14.Bxf6 gxf6 15.Ne4 . The knight controls the important c5- and d6-squares and limits the mobility of the black queen. Now:

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 b5 (D)

Black expands on the queenside and seeks to demonstrate that White's intended pawn advance e5 is not actually a threat. Grandmaster Lev Polugaevsky almost single-handedly transformed this provocative move into an extremely dangerous weapon: he spent a lifetime analysing and publishing analysis of this sharp line. The following quote from his classic book Grandmaster Preparation illustrates his passion for analysing The Variation': "... every day for roughly six months(!) I spent hours at the board studying positions from the variation, and even went to sleep and dreamed about it."

The Polugaevsky Variation has fallen out of favour since the passing of its namesake exponent; such razor-sharp lines are dangerous to repeat at the highest levels because the player is susceptible to being surprised by a crushing novelty. The critical positions lend themselves to careful preparation with analytical chess engines and Black is under some pressure in the main lines due to his lagging development and vulnerable king. However, White has not so far been able to deliver a knockout blow to the line. As things stand, the Polugaevsky Variation is certainly playable, and at club level it has proven to be an excellent choice for those seeking to unbalance the position at any cost. White can obtain dangerous attacking chances for a small material investment, and it takes strong nerves and confidence in one's tactical abilities to defend such positions in an over-the-board game played at a fast time-limit.

8.e5

White takes up the challenge, because a more standard plan of development allows Black to begin his queenside play more quickly than in normal lines:

(a) 8.a3 Bb7 9.Bd3 ( 9.e5 dxe5 10.fxe5 Qc7 saves the piece, as in the main line) 9...Nbd7 10.Qe2 and now 10...Qc7 11.O-O-O Be7 12.Rhe1 O-O was equal in Jonasson-Wojtkiewicz, Reykjavik 1994, but 10...Qb6 is the more thematic Polugaevsky move.

(b) 8.Bd3 Nbd7 9.Qe2 Qb6 (Black saves a tempo in comparison to some lines of the Gelfand Variation because he is able to play ...Qb6 in one move without having played ...Qc7) 10.Nf3 Bb7 followed by ...Rc8 was comfortable for Black in Metz-P.Schlosser, Baden 2000.

(c) 8.Qf3 Bb7 9.Bd3 Nbd7 10.O-O-O Be7 11.Rhe1 Qb6 (again we see this key motivation of the Polugaevsky Variation) 12.Qe3 h6 13.Bh4 b4 14.Nb1 O-O gives Black easy play on the queenside, W.Schmidt-Taufratshofer, corr. 1994.
