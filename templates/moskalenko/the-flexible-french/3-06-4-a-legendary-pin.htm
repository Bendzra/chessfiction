After 7.Ne2 c5! 8.dxc5 (if 8.a3 cxd4! 9.Bxd4 Ba5 10.b4 Nxc3!? 11.Nxc3 Bb6 ↔) 8...Nc6 ( 8...Qa5!? ) 9.a3 Bxc3+ 10.Nxc3 Nxc3 11.bxc3 Nxe5 ↔ Black is OK.

Black basically has three ways of answering the text move.

7...g6!

I recommend this defence. The game will soon acquire a more dynamic character and the possibility of castling queenside is an important resource.

(A) 7...c5? is very risky: 8.Qxg7 Rf8 ( 8...Ke7!? ) 9.Bxh6 cxd4 ( 9...Nd7 10.a3 Ba5 ) 10.a3! +/- Magem-Moskalenko, Catalunya tt 2005;

(B) 7...g5?! , from Reggio-Albin, Monte Carlo 1902, one of the first games with the move 6.Be3 , is the option recommended by many authors, but it is also dubious:

(B1) 8.Ne2! h5 ( 8...c5 9.O-O-O!? ) 9.Qf3 f5 ( 9...c5 10.O-O-O ) 10.h4! . By fixing Black's structure, White achieves a clear advantage.

10...g4 (if 10...f4 11.Bc1 g4 12.Qxf4 Rf8 13.Qh6 +/-) 11.Qf4 +/- Black has no serious counterplay, Aroshidze-Moskalenko, Banyoles 2007;

(B2) 8.h4 is also interesting, with the attractive idea of sacrificing the queen.

Possibly the modern line 6.Be3 is the future of the MacCutcheon Variation. The possibilities are very rich and there are many resources for both sides.

The option 9/10...c5 is always more active than 9/10...Nxc3, but the latter leads to more complicated play.
