Chapter 14 - The Anti-Winawer

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.Ne2

The Two Knights Variation with 4.Ne2 is one of many anti-Winawer lines, which is quite fashionable these days. It offers an escape from the main lines and interesting play. Objectively it gives an equal game. However, the final outcome usually depends on the two players' knowledge of the line. So when Marc Narciso Dublan steered towards a French game against my usual 1.d4 , I decided to give it a try.

GAME 73
Viktor Moskalenko - Marc Narciso Dublan
Catalunya tt 2005 (4)

1.d4

My opponent in this game now carelessly played the move

1...e6

forgetting that I am a specialist in the French Defence.

2.e4

I decided to test my knowledge and also my opponent's in this opening.

2...d5 3.Nc3

This move has been the most popular in recent years.

3...Bb4 4.Ne2 Nf6

This looks very logical: development and pressure on the centre, but we are not in a genuine French!

The most straightforward way to equalise is 4...dxe4!? 5.a3 Bxc3+ ( 5...Be7 6.Nxe4 Nf6 =) 6.Nxc3 Nc6 7.d5 ( 7.Bb5?! Ne7 8.Nxe4 O-O 9.Be3 f5 10.Ng5 f4 11.Bd2 Qd5 -/+ Thorhallsson-Moskalenko, Copenhagen 1995) 7...exd5 8.Qxd5 =.

analysis diagram

White has no advantage at all. For in stance: 8...Be6!? 9.Qxe4 ( 9.Qxd8+? Rxd8 10.Nxe4 Nd4 11.Bd3 Bc4! 12.O-O Bxd3 13.cxd3 =/+ Landenbergue-Rozentalis, Chiasso 1991) 9...Nf6 10.Qh4 Qe7 11.Be2 O-O-O 12.Be3 Nd5 13.Qxe7 Ndxe7 ↔ Gaponenko-Kononenko, Antalya rapid 2002.

Nimzowitsch's move 4...Nc6 contains some interesting ideas as well. However, it would lead to the same paths after 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.Nxc3 dxe4 etc.

5.e5

5...Ne4

Imitating the MacCutcheon Variation, but there are certain differences. A hybrid of the Classical Winawer and the 'Pawn Wedge' would arise after 5...Nfd7!?

(A) 6.f4 O-O ( 6...b6?! 7.a3 Be7? 8.f5! +/- Kalod-Moskalenko, Catalunya tt 2007) 7.a3 Be7!

analysis diagram

with a position quite similar to the lines we have seen before. 8.g3 c5 9.Be3 Nc6 10.Bg2 b6!? 11.O-O Asis-Moskalenko, Montcada 2006;

(B) 6.Qd3 is another sharp line: 6...O-O 7.a3 Be7 8.Qg3 Kh8 9.Be3 c5 10.O-O-O Nc6 11.f4 b5!? 12.dxc5 b4 13.axb4 Nxb4 14.f5 Bxc5 15.fxe6 fxe6 16.Nd4 Bxd4 17.Bxd4 Qa5 → 0-1 De la Riva Aguado-Moskalenko, Sitges 2006.

6.Qd3

Intending 7.a3 , with the idea of not allowing the pawns to be doubled. 6.a3!? can also be played right away: 6...Nxc3 7.Nxc3 Bxc3+ 8.bxc3 O-O 9.Bd3 f5! and the game is more or less equal.

6...O-O

Slightly slow. The most energetic reply is 6...c5!? 7.a3 c4 8.Qe3 Ba5 9.b4 cxb3 10.cxb3 Nxc3 11.Nxc3 Bd7 12.b4 Bb6 13.Bd3 Nc6 14.Ne2 f6 15.Qg3 O-O ↔ Alsina Leal-Moskalenko, Barcelona Sants 2005.

7.a3 Bxc3+

8.bxc3!

I preferred to keep more pieces on the board, intending to play 9.f3 . On 8.Nxc3 , 8...f5! is a very strong reply.

8...f6

Virtually the only defence, but it is adequate.

9.f3!

We must attack the pieces that are too active.

9...Ng5 10.Bxg5

Eliminating the poor knight, but this might not be necessary. 10.exf6!? was more interesting, and after 10...Qxf6 11.h4 Nf7 12.Bg5! White has the initiative.

10...fxg5 11.h4!

Everybody on the attack!

11...g4!

This is a strong reply, allowing Black to fight for the initiative. 11...gxh4?! 12.g3! ↑ Pablo Marin-De la Villa Garcia, Ciudad Real tt 2004.

12.fxg4

12.f4!? .

12...c5!

Activating the queenside.

13.g3

An attempt at a fianchetto.

13...Qa5 14.Bg2 Nc6

15.Nf4!

Castling is essential! With my king in the centre I would not have survived for too long. I did not like 15.Qd2? because of 15...cxd4 16.cxd4 Qxd2+ 17.Kxd2 Rf2 and Black is doing very well here.

15...cxd4 16.O-O

Now I could breathe much more easily.

16...dxc3

Eating so many pawns can be bad for your stomach! If 16...Qxc3 17.Qxc3 dxc3 18.Nxd5! ↔.

17.Kh2!

The last prophylactic move. Narciso has played well up to this point. 17.Nxd5? does not work yet because of 17...Rd8! .

17...Rd8?

A serious mistake which hands White the initiative. Better possibilities were 17...Bd7 18.Nxd5!? , or else 17...Nxe5 18.Qe2 ∞, or perhaps 17...Qc5!? , with a complicated game in all cases.

18...Nxe5 19.Qe2 ↑.

Driving a wedge into Blacks defensive lines.

19...h6 20.Rae1 Qd4 21.Qe2 Rf8?

This allows a strong blow. However, in the event of 21...Bd7 White can also play 22.Nxd5! .

22.Nxd5! Bd7 23.Nf4!

Holding on to the advantage.

Threatening 26.Rxd7 .

25...Rae8 26.Rd6 Bc8 27.Qe3 Qc7 28.Qxc3

I like eating pawns too! 28.Nxe6!? .

28...Kh8 29.Bg2 Bd7 30.Rfd1+-
