GAME 29
Zaven Andriasian - Ian Nepomniachtchi
Kirishi 2007 (6)

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nd2 Be7!? 4.Ngf3

A natural and therefore popular development of the knight, but it does not grant any advantage.

The talented Ian Nepomniachtchi (born 1990) is a new generator of ideas, for and against the French Defence. His score with black in the 3...Be7 line is 60%

4...Nf6!?

Black does not have a great deal of choice, since, for example, 4...Nc6 5.c3!? dxe4 6.Nxe4 Nf6 7.Bd3 +/=, and also 4...c5?! 5.exd5 exd5 6.dxc5 Nf6 7.Nb3 O-O 8.Be3 +/= are both better for White.

5.e5

White closes the centre. An alternative to keep the position open would be 5.Bd3!?

analysis diagram

(A) 5...b6?! is too hasty: 6.e5 Nfd7 7.c4 c5 8.cxd5 exd5 9.O-O Ba6 10.Bxa6 Nxa6 11.Nb1! Nc7 12.Nc3 Nf8 13.Be3 Nfe6 14.Qc2 O-O 15.Rad1 c4 16.Ne1 f5 17.exf6 Rxf6 18.f4 Qd6? 19.f5 +/- Adams-Rivas Pastor, Dos Hermanas 1993. But there is always a way out, such as 5...dxe4!? ;

(B) 5...c5 6.exd5 (Black seems OK after 6.dxc5 dxe4 (simplest) 7.Nxe4 Nxe4 8.Bxe4 Qxd1+ 9.Kxd1 Bxc5 10.Ke2 Nd7 11.Rd1 Nf6 12.Bd3 O-O 13.Ne5 b6 = Kobalia-Morozevich, Elista Olympiad 1998; 6.e5 Nfd7 transposes into the main game) and now:

(B1) 6...Qxd5

analysis diagram

Activating the queen as in the 'Scandinavian Hybrid'. This is also a position from a main C07 line, this time with the inclusion of the moves Bd3 and ...Be7. This difference also seems to slightly favour Black: 7.dxc5 Nbd7 8.b4 (trying to get something out of nothing. The usual variation is 8.O-O!? Nxc5 9.Bc4 Qd6!? 10.Nb3 with equal chances. 6.Bd3

(A) 6.c4?! is rather ambitious: 6...dxc4 ( 6...c5!? 7.cxd5 exd5 8.Bd3 Nc6 9.O-O cxd4 10.Re1 Nc5 11.Nb3 (Godena-Caruana, Cremona ch-ITA play-off 2006) 11...Nxd3 12.Qxd3 Nb4 13.Qd1 d3 ↔) 7.Nxc4 ( 7.Bxc4 Nb6 8.Bd3 Bd7 9.O-O Bc6 10.Re1 Bd5! 11.Bc2 Nc6 = Mahjoob-Lputian, Yerevan Wch-tt 2001) 7...Nb6 8.Nxb6 axb6 9.Bd3 Bd7 10.O-O Bc6 11.Re1 Bd5! 12.Be3 Nc6 13.a3 O-O 14.Nd2 f5! = and Black is quite comfortable in this completely blocked position, Svidler-Visser, Groningen 1990;

(B) 6.c3 c5 7.Be2!? is quite a rare continuation compared with Bd3.

analysis diagram

White's main idea is to keep a stable centre, protecting the d4 pawn with Nb3. In this case, Black cannot punish White with ...c5-c4.

7...Nc6 8.O-O O-O 9.Re1 ( 9.Nb3 c4 10.Nbd2 b5 11.a3 a5 12.Re1 Nb6 13.Nf1 Bd7 14.Bf4 b4 15.Qc1 bxa3 16.bxa3 a4 17.Bg5 Na5 18.Ra2 Rb8 19.Ng3 Na8 20.Nh5 Kh8 21.Bxe7 Qxe7 22.Bd1 Rb3 ↔ Erenburg-Kuraszkiewicz, Warsaw Ech 2005) 9...f6 ( 9...a5!? ) 10.exf6 Nxf6 11.Nf1 (transferring the knight to the kingside; 11.Bd3 Qb6!? ) 11...cxd4!? (after White has moved the knight from the centre to f1, there is no point in Black delaying the capture on d4) 12.cxd4 =/+ (Erenburg-Timman, Reykjavik 2006) and now 12...Qb6!? 13.Ng3 Bd7 and Black's prospects are better.

8.O-O

The only move to take advantage of White's temporary lead in development is 8.Ne4!? .

0-1

A very instructive game by the young and talented Ian Nepomniachtchi.

Summary of 4.Ngf3 : This is the normal continuation. White is not trying to refute Black's system, and his only aim is to gain a slight space advantage and play on from there. Although natural, this does not seem dangerous for Black.
