Chapter 12 - The Kung Fu Fight

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.exd5 exd5 4.Nf3 Nc6

The symmetry with 3.exd5 exd5 never guarantees White a quiet life, but this is only possible if you learn to play in 'kung fu' fashion with black. After 4.Nf3 , it is possible to immediately break the symmetry with 4...Nc6!? , unbalancing the game. This move promises Black rich piece play.

GAME 71
Fernando Braga - Viktor Moskalenko
Barbera 2001 (7)

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.exd5 exd5

As the legendary Bobby Fischer used to say: 'In symmetrical positions the player who makes the first mistake loses the game.' If this is the case, we just have to wait!

4.Nf3

(A) It is not too late to revert to aggressive mode: 4.c4 Nf6 5.Nc3 Be7!? 6.Nf3 O-O 7.Be3 c6 ( 7...Be6!? 8.cxd5 Nxd5 ↔ Plaskett-Gallagher, Hastings 1989/90) 8.Bd3 dxc4 9.Bxc4 Nbd7 10.O-O Nb6 11.Bb3 Nbd5 12.Ne5 Be6 13.Bg5 Re8 14.Re1 Qa5 15.Qf3 Rad8 16.Rad1 Bb4 17.Nxd5 Bxd5 18.Bxd5 Qxd5 19.Bxf6?? 4...Nc6!?

This symmetrical knights' 'Kung fu' allows Black to unbalance the game.

5.Bd3

5.Bb5 would lead to a Ruy Lopez hybrid: 5...Ne7 ( 5...Bd6?! 6.c4! ↑) and now:

(A) 6.O-O a6 7.Bd3?! ( 7.Ba4 g6!? 8.Re1 b5 9.Bb3 Bg7 =) 7...Bf5 8.Bf4 Qd7 = Yakovenko-Moskalenko, play-chess, com 2006;

(B) White's bishops met a horrible fate after 6.Bg5?! f6 7.Bf4 a6 8.Be2 g5 9.Bc1 Bf5 10.O-O Qd7 11.a3 h5 12.c4 dxc4 13.Bxc4 O-O-O -/+ Narciso Dublan-Moskalenko, Barcelona 2001;

(C) 6.Ne5 a6 7.Qh5?! Be6 8.Nxc6 Nxc6 9.Bxc6+ (this move leads to a 'double' Exchange Variation (French/Ruy Lopez), but here it favours Black) 9...bxc6 10.O-O Qf6 11.Re1 Bd6 =/+ Kruppa-Moskalenko, Sverdlovsk 1984.
