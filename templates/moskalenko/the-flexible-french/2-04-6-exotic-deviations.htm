11.Nxd4

The main idea for White after the queen exchange is to reach a better ending: three pawns against two on the queenside and more active minor pieces. However, historically and technically this rule only prevailed in Capablanca's day.

11...Bd7

With an equal position. 11...Bc5!? is another developing strategy: 12.Nb3 ( 12.Nb5?! Ke7! 13.Bf4 a6!? 14.Nc3 b5 ↔) 12...Be7 13.Bf4 Bd7 14.Be2 Nd5 15.Bg3 h5!? 16.h3 h4 17.Bh2 O-O-O 18.Rfd1 Nb6 19.Na5 Bb4 20.Nc4 Nxc4 21.Bxc4 = Akopian-Shirov, Merida 2000.

If 12.Nb5?! Rc8! . Or, for instance, 12.Bb5?! Rd8 ( 12...Bc5!? ) 13.c4 Bxb5 14.Nxb5 a6 15.Nc3 =/+. Here Black has many moves to gain a slight advantage, Rios Parra-Moskalenko, Barbera 2003.
