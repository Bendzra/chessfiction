This blockading move brought Tigran Petrosian, the seventh World Champion, several outstanding strategic victories, and it certainly suited his style.

GAME 10
Sergei Movsesian - Teimour Radjabov
Sarajevo 2002 (6)

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3 Qb6 6.a3 c4 7.Nbd2

7.g3 Bd7 8.Bh3 Be7 9.O-O h5 10.Qe2 Na5 11.Bg5 Nb3 =/+ Hermann-Van der Wiel, Bochum 1981.

7...Na5 8.g3

8...Bd7!

In many variations of the French Defence the most natural plan for Black is to complete the development of his queenside, while White castles king-side.

8...f5 is too hasty Portisch waited until move 10 before playing this advance - see the next game. 9.Ng5!? gives White a slight initiative.

However, 8...h6 is possible: 9.Nh4 Bd7 10.Ng2 O-O-O 11.Ne3?! Malaniuk-G.Kuzmin, Nikolaev Zonal 1995, and now, the best move is 11...f6! 12.f4 g5! ↔.

9.Bh3

This way of developing the bishop, employed by several Armenian masters, is quite odd and could be called the 'Armenian Fianchetto'. The most natural option is to play 9.h4 first. This we will call the 'Austrian Fianchetto' and it will feature in Games 11 and 12.

9...Be7!

The most efficient defence, preventing Ng5 and preparing ...g5 and ...h5.

(A) Petrosian's old way of playing in typical 'Labyrinth fashion' would be 9...Ne7 10.O-O g6!? ( 10...h6!? 11.Rb1 Nec6 12.Ne1 Be7 with scope for creative play, Pinter-Farago, Budapest ch-HUN 1976) 11.Rb1 h6 12.Nh4 O-O-O 13.Ng2 Kb8 14.Ne3 Rc8 15.Ng4 Bg7 16.Bg2 1/2-1/2 I.Zaitsev-T.V.Petrosian, Sochi 1977;

(B) Things do not change much after 9...h6 10.O-O Ne7 11.Re1 ( 11.Ne1 O-O-O 12.Ng2 Kb8 13.Rb1 = Lobron-Hjartarson, Manila izt 1990; 11.Nh4 Nec6 12.f4 O-O-O ↔ Psakhis-Farago, Banja Luka 1985) 11...O-O-O 12.Rb1 Kb8 =, when Black can be happy with the outcome of the opening;

(C) The most complicated move order is 9...O-O-O 10.O-O h6 11.Ne1 h5! 12.Ndf3 Nb3 13.Rb1 Nxc1 ( 13...f6!? ↔) 14.Qxc1 Ba4 15.Ng5 Nh6 16.f4 g6 ( 16...h4! ↑) 17.Ng2 Be7 with equal chances, Motylev-Balashov, Ekaterinburg tt 2002;

(D) Again, 9...f5?! is too hasty: 10.exf6 gxf6 11.O-O ( 11.Ne5 fxe5 12.Qh5+ Ke7 13.Qg5+ Kf7 =) 11...O-O-O 12.Rb1 Bg7 13.Re1 Nh6 14.b3! cxb3 15.Nxb3 Rde8 16.Bxh6 Bxh6 17.Nc5 Qc6 18.c4! → Sveshnikov-Simon Padros, Calvia 2007.

10.O-O

Castling followed by Ne1 looks the most solid. Another Armenian grandmaster unsuccessfully tried 10.Rb1 h5! 11.O-O ( 11.Bg2 h4 12.h3 hxg3 13.fxg3 Nh6 14.g4 O-O-O -/+ Etchegaray-Apicella, Nantes ch-FRA 1993) 11...Bb5!? .

Now Psakhis comments: 'Black wants to stop b2-b3 while preparing the typical manoeuvre Qc6 and Ba4. In anticipation of the coming Ne1 Black is ready to answer ...Nb3! although maybe 11...g5!? would be good enough; 12.Ne1 O-O-O 13.Bg2 Nh6 14.Qe2 Ba4! 15.b4 cxb3 16.Nd3 g4 17.Re1 Kb8 -+ Stilling-Van Manen, corr 1990. 12.a4?! stops Black's plan, but the a4-pawn will become a real target: 12...Bc6 =/+ 13.Re1 g5!? 14.Bf1 ( 14.g4? does not work due to 14...Nh6 ) 14...g4 15.Nh4 Bxh4 16.gxh4 Ne7 . There is a strong threat: Ne7-f5-h4, Art. Minasian-Kortchnoi, Ohrid Ech 2001, but I prefer 16...Qd8!? =/+ winning the h4-pawn at once.'

10...h5!?

This advance reveals the disadvantages of the plan with Bh3 without the preliminary h2-h4. Now Black threatens ...h4 or ...g5-g4.

11.Qe2

Or 11.Ne1 g5! ∆ 12.f4? g4 13.Bg2 Nh6 -/+.

11...O-O-O =/+

Or 11...g5!? .

12.Ne1

This is a key moment in the opening.

12...g6?!

This weak move allows White to block the kingside. 12...g5! was much stronger, with a clear initiative for Black.

13.f4

Now the position is balanced.

13...Nh6 14.Ndf3 Nb3 15.Rb1 Nxc1 16.Rxc1 Rdg8 17.Ng5 Qd8 18.Qd2 b5 19.Nc2 Kb8 20.Rb1 Qc7 21.Nb4 Ka8 22.Ra1 a5 23.Nc2 Rb8 24.Rfb1 Rb6 25.Bg2 Kb8 26.Bf3

1/2-1/2

Summary: The 'Armenian fianchetto' with Bh3 and without h2-h4 must be incorrect. According to strategic rules, the bishop is better placed behind its pawns.

Theory giant grandmaster Lajos Portisch (1937) always demonstrated a fine knowledge of the French Defence.

GAME 11
Josef Klinger - Lajos Portisch
Dubai ol 1986 (2)

1.e4 c5 2.c3 e6 3.d4 d5 4.e5 Nc6 5.Nf3 Qb6 6.a3 c4

In the 1950s Portisch often used the idea of 6...Nh6 (see Chapter 1).

7.g3 Bd7 8.Nbd2 Na5 9.h4

Perhaps, this preparatory advance is more effective than the direct 9.Bh3 .

9...O-O-O!?

Now queenside castling is logical. White has spent two tempi on g3 and h4. For continuations such as 9...f6 see Game 12. Another original manoeuvre is 9...Qc6 with the idea ...Qa4, see the comments to Game 15. If immediately 9...f5?! , 10.Ng5! will cause trouble.

10.Bh3

This looks natural, but it is still difficult to assess whether this is the best square for the bishop. 10.h5?! is an absurd move. We already know that the march of the h-pawn only weakens White's position: 10...Nh6!? 11.Bh3 f6 ( 11...f5!? ) 12.Qe2 Nf7 13.O-O f5! -/+. In the game Maslak-Asrian, Moscow 2007, after constructing the 'Portisch-Stonewair (see the main game), Black won by preparing a typical attack with ...g7-g6-g5.

10...f5!?

This is Portisch's plan: an aggressive break in the spirit of the Dutch Defence. The bishop on h3 will face a 'stonewall'! More common was 10...Ne7 11.O-O h6 12.Rb1 ( 12.Qe2 Nec6 13.h5 Be7 14.Re1 Rdg8 15.Nf1 Nb3 16.Rb1 g6! 17.hxg6 Rxg6 18.Kh2 h5 with the initiative for Black on the kingside, Flugzeug-CapNemo, playchess.com 2007) 12...Kb8 ∞ with a satisfactory position, Sax-Knaak, Szirak 1985.

11.O-O

The critical moment in this line. Logically, the bishop on h3 should aid White in the fight against f6 or f5, by attacking the h3-c8 diagonal. But bad now is 11.exf6?! gxf6! 12.O-O Nh6 13.Re1 e5! (for 13...Bg7 , see Game 13)

analysis diagram

with a strong initiative for Black, who controls the greater part of the board: after 14.Bxd7+ Rxd7 15.Re2 Nf5 16.Qc2 e4 17.Ne1 Rg8 18.Ng2 Bd6 he has a great advantage, Jonkman-Lemos, Pinamar 2006.

11...Nh6!

Preparing ...Nf7 and ...g7-g5.

12.Ne1?!

A dubious manoeuvre. A more tense continuation for both sides is 12.Ng5 Be7 13.Rb1 Nb3!? 14.Nxb3 Ba4 ↔ with excellent possibilities for Black.

12...Nf7 13.Ng2 g5! →

After only a few moves Black has achieved a very promising position. As in Game 8, the f- and g-pawns start an assault on the white king's fortress.

14.hxg5 Nxg5 15.Nf4 Nxh3+!

Exchanging White's best piece.

16.Nxh3 Be7 17.Rb1

White's counterplay is clearly too slow.

17...Rdg8!

The second phase of the game begins: converting the advantage.

18.Nf3 h6!

Controlling the g5-point.

19.Be3 Nb3

At the moment the knight on b3 blocks the queenside, but later it will have to return through the 'labyrinth': ...Nb3-a5-c6-d8-f7-g5.

19...Qd8!? -/+.

20.Kg2 Qd8!

The black queen helps her musketeers! As in several other games in this chapter, Black prepares a decisive attack on the kingside.

21.Rh1 h5! 22.Qe2 h4-+

The rest is a matter of style and taste.

23.Nf4 Qe8 24.Rh3 hxg3 25.fxg3 Rxh3 26.Nxh3 Qg6 27.Bf4 Na5 28.Rh1 Nc6 29.Qe3 Nd8 30.Bg5 Qg7 31.Qf4 Ba4!

Portisch releases the genie from the bottle!

This is a classic example of a successful game, with the Bc8 traversing the route c8-d7-a4-c2-e4.

32.Bxe7 Qxe7 33.Qh6 Bc2! 34.Nf4 Be4 35.Nh5 Nf7 36.Qf6 Qd7 37.Nf4 Ng5 38.Rh8 Bxf3+ 39.Kf1 Rxh8 40.Qxh8+ Qd8

0-1

A model game by the Hungarian grandmaster. White's strategy (the fianchetto g3-h4-Bh3) was refuted with great elegance. The key moves of the 'Portisch Stonewall' are 9...O-O-O , 10...f5! and then an attack with the kingside pawns. Perhaps in this case the best square for the f1-bishop is e2 or g2.

GAME 12

Monaco blind 2003 (11)

1.e4 e6

An old favourite of Evgeny Bareev, who by this time had already switched to the Caro-Kann, with which he is now so familiar.

2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3 Qb6 6.a3 c4 7.Nbd2 Na5 8.g3

Black has the good reply 11...Nh6!? , and now 12.Qh5+ does not work because of 12...g6! 13.Qxh6? Bf8 , when the white queen has no retreat) 11...gxf6 12.O-O

9.h4

White has spent two tempi on the advance of his g- and h-pawns.

9...Ne7!?

This is a typical, flexible manoeuvre in these positions, keeping four possible jumps for the knight: ...Nc6/c8/g6/f5. In the previous game Portisch played 9...O-O-O! .

The other logical option is to counterattack in the centre immediately: 9...f6!? 10.Bh3 (with 10.exf6 White can force a draw: 10...gxf6 11.Ne5!? fxe5 12.Qh5+ Ke7 13.Qg5+ Kf7 =) 10...Nh6 11.O-O Nf7 12.Re1 O-O-O 13.Nf1?! fxe5 14.dxe5 ( 14.Nxe5 Nxe5 15.Rxe5 Bd6 16.Re2 Rde8 =/+) 14...Nb3 15.Rb1 ( 15.Be3 Bc5 =/+) 15...Bc5 16.Qe2 Nxc1 17.Rbxc1 g5! ↑

A slow attempt to improve the knight's position. But the popular alternatives on the Internet don't offer too much:

( 12...Qc7 13.Nh2 O-O-O 14.f4 f5 15.Nhf3 h6 16.g4?! fxg4 17.Bxg4 g6 18.Rf2 Rdf8 -/+ Stevic-Moskalenko, playchess.com 2006) 13.b4? ( 13.Nh2 f6 14.exf6 Bxf6 15.Ng4 Be7 ↔ Stevic-Moskalenko, playchess.com 2006) 13...cxb3 14.Nxb3 Nxb3 15.Rxb3 Qc7 -/+ Bregadze-Moskalenko, playchess.com 2006;

(B) 10.Be2 Nec6 11.O-O Be7 12.h5 h6 13.Kg2 O-O-O 14.Nh2 f6 15.f4 Rdg8 ↑ Panarin-Moskalenko, playchess.com 2006;

(C) 10.Bh3 Nec6 ( 10...h6!? 11.Rb1 Nec6 12.Nf1 Be7 13.h5 Nb3 14.Bf4 O-O-O 15.Ne3 Nba5 16.O-O Rdg8 17.Nh2 Qd8 18.Qf3 g5 19.hxg6 fxg6 20.Ng2 g5 21.Be3 h5 22.g4 Rf8 23.Qe2 Qe8 24.Ne1 Qg6 25.Kg2 Qxb1 0-1 BenoitP08-CapNemo, playchess.com) 11.O-O Be7 12.Nh2 O-O-O 13.Re1 h6 14.h5 Rdg8 15.Rb1 ( 15.Ng4?!
