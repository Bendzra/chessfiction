Chapter 10 - Kortchnoi's Wedge

1.e4 e6 2.Nf3 d5 3.e5 c5 4.b4 d4

Before we learn an opening or a certain line properly, we should check the games of the experts and, after that, try to find our own directions.

In the gambit 1.e4 e6 2.Nf3 d5 3.e5 c5 4.b4 , which used to bother me, the idea of 4...d4!? was invented by the specialist Viktor Kortchnoi.

Black uses his d-pawn as a 'wedge' on d4. I like this concept, where Black fights for space in the centre.

Stefaan Deman - Danny Haberkorn
Belgium tt 1999/00 (3)

1.e4 e6 2.Nf3 d5 3.e5 c5 4.b4 d4!?

(A) In the event of 4...cxb4 White sacrifices a pawn by 5.a3 , with exactly the same idea as in the Benko Gambit, although in this case the centre is closed. For instance: 5...Nc6 6.axb4 Bxb4 7.c3 Ba5 (or else 7...Be7 8.d4 ‰) 8.d4 Nge7 9.Bd3 h6 10.O-O ‰ with a typical position in this gambit: Black is slightly passive and he does not have any counterplay, Westerinen-Brinck Claussen, Esbjerg 1978;

(B) I once tried another blockade with 4...c4!?

analysis diagram

(B1) 5.c3 a5!? ( 5...Ne7 6.d3 cxd3 7.Bxd3 Ng6 8.Qe2 Nd7 9.O-O Be7 10.Na3 O-O 11.Bd2 +/= Vafiadis-Moskalenko, Kavala 1998) 6.b5 Nd7 7.d4 cxd3 8.Bxd3 f6! ↔ ( 8...Qc7!? 9.Qe2 Ne7 10.O-O Ng6 11.Re1 Be7 ∞) 9.Qc2? ( 9.Qe2 fxe5 ↔) 9...Nxe5 10.Nxe5 fxe5 11.Bxh7 Bd6 -/+ Naer-Moskalenko, Moscow 1995;

(B2) Or, for instance, 5.a3 a5!? ( 5...b5?! 6.a4 Bxb4 7.axb5 Qb6 8.Ba3 Bxa3 9.Nxa3 Nh6 10.Bxc4! dxc4 11.Nxc4 Qc7 12.Nd6+ Ke7 13.O-O ↑ Stripunsky-Moskalenko, Simferopol ch-UKR 1990) 6.b5 Nd7 7.d3 cxd3 8.Bxd3 f6 ↔.

5.bxc5

If 5.Bb2 cxb4!? .

5...Bxc5 6.Ba3!?

White carries on with his main idea.

6.Bd3 does not offer anything special: 6...Nc6 7.Qe2 Nge7 ( 7...f5!? ) 8.O-O Ng6 9.g3 O-O ↔.

6...Qa5!

The best option. If 6...Qe7 7.c3!? Bxa3 8.Qa4+ Nc6 9.Qxa3 +/=.

7.Bxc5 Qxc5 8.c3 Nc6 9.cxd4 Nxd4 10.Qa4+

Only seven games have been played with this line, but in one of them Black found a very attractive possibility:

10...Bd7!!

This knight sacrifice is an unexpected resource. 10...Nc6 has mainly been played. This reply seemed to be forced, but after 11.Nc3 White gains the initiative, threatening Ne4-d6+: 11...Qa5 12.Qxa5 (or else 12.Qg4!? Kf8 13.Bb5 ↑) 12...Nxa5 13.Nb5 Ke7 14.Nc7 Rb8 15.d4 with a favourable endgame, Boersma-Kortchnoi, Leeuwarden ch-NED 1977.

11.Qxd4 Qc1+ 12.Ke2 Bb5+

A spectacular check. This is the key position for this new idea. If the opponent is not ready for this surprise, finding the right response over the board is a tough task.

13.Ke3

The alternative is 13.d3 Rd8! (in Yearbook 50 and Secrets of Opening Surprises 1, Jeroen Bosch gives 13...Bxd3+ 14.Qxd3 Qb2+ ) 14.Nbd2 □ 14...Qa3! ( 14...Bxd3+? is a mistake in view of 15.Qxd3 Qxa1 16.Qc2 ) 15.Qf4 (not 15.Nc4? Bxc4 16.Qxc4 Qb2+ , winning the rook on a1) 15...Qb2 16.Rd1 Bxd3+ 17.Ke1 Bc2 and the position remains complicated.

13...Bxf1

Another key moment.

14.Qc3?

White makes too many defensive mistakes. The best ideas are 14.Qa4+ Kf8 15.Qb5 and now, for instance, 15...Qxd2+!? , winning a pawn; or else 14.Qd3 Qc5+! 15.Qd4 Qxd4+!? ( 15...Qc1 = repeats moves) 16.Nxd4 Bxg2 17.Rg1 Bc6 18.Rxg7 Ne7 with interesting counterplay for Black.

14...Qd1! ↑

Threatening 15...Qe2+ and 16...Bxg2 .

15.Rxf1?

15.Qd3 was the only defence, for example: 15...Qxf3+!? 16.gxf3 Bxd3 17.Kxd3 O-O-O+ ↑ with the better chances.

15...Qxf1 16.Na3 Qa6!

And Black did not have any trouble in converting his material advantage.

17.Qc5 Ne7 18.Nb5 O-O 19.Nc7 Nf5+ 20.Kf4 Qa4+ 21.d4 Rac8 22.Rc1 Rfd8 23.Rc4 Qd7 24.Qa5 Ne7 25.Qc3 Rxc7 0-1

It is obvious that the Kortchnoi 'wedge' 4.b4 d4!? , reinforced with the fantastic resource 10...Bd7! , can be a nasty surprise for b4-gambit lovers — incidentally, a line which is especially popular on the Internet.
