GAME 57
Garry Kasparov - Vasily Ivanchuk
Horgen 1995 (6)

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 b6 5.a3!?

A logical and automatic move to clarify the two sides' strategies.

5...Bf8!?

Vasily Ivanchuk, one of the greatest champions of modern chess, sets his opponent tough tasks in all kinds of games. 5...Bxc3+!? remains a popular alternative: 6.bxc3 Ne7 7.Qg4 Ng6 8.h4 h5 9.Qg3 Ba6 10.Ne2 ( 10.Bxa6 Nxa6 11.Ne2 c5 ↔ Retinsky-Moskalenko, Alushta 1995) 10...Kf8 11.a4 Nc6!? 12.Nf4 Bxf1! 13.Nxg6+ fxg6 14.Kxf1 Qe8 ∞ 15.Qd3 Kg8 16.c4 dxc4 17.Qxc4 Rd8 18.c3 Kh7 19.Rh3 Rf8 20.Bg5 Rd5 21.Kg1 Na5 22.Qa6 1/2-1/2 Leko-Moskalenko, Copenhagen 1995.

6.Nf3

A very natural developing move. The following examples from my own games may also be instructive:

(A) 6.h4 c5!? 7.Nf3 Ba6 8.Bxa6 Nxa6 9.Qd3 Nc7 10.h5 h6 11.Ne2 Ne7 12.c3 Nc6 13.b4 c4! 14.Qe3 a5 15.Rb1 axb4 16.axb4 Nb5 17.Bd2 Ra2 18.O-O Be7 19.Nh2 Qa8 20.f4 Qa3 21.Nc1 Rxd2! 22.Qxd2 Nxc3 -+ Baches Garcia-Moskalenko, Catalonia-ch 2003;

(B) 6.Be3 Ne7 7.Bd3 Ba6 8.Nge2 Qd7 9.Ng3 Bxd3 10.Qxd3 Nbc6 11.b4 ∞ Colovic-Moskalenko, Montcada Open 2003;

(C) 6.Bb5+ (avoiding the exchange of the light-squared bishops) 6...c6 7.Ba4 Bd7 8.Nce2 c5 9.Bb3 Bb5 10.c3 Nc6 11.Nf3 Nge7 12.O-O Nf5 = Korneev-Moskalenko, Montcada Open 2003;

(D) Or else 6.b4 c5!? 7.Nf3 Nc6!? 8.Bd3 c4 9.Bf1 b5! .

analysis diagram

In Nimzowitsch's 'System' style. 10.g3 ( 10.Nxb5 a5 11.Bg5 Qb6 ↔) 10...a5 11.Nxb5 axb4 12.a4 Ba6 13.Bd2 Qa5 14.Rb1 Bxb5 15.axb5 Qxb5 -/+ Baron Rodriguez-Moskalenko, Mataro 2004/05.

6...Ne7!?

The right manoeuvre, waiting for one more tempo. We will examine the risky advance 6...c5!? in the next game. The strategic idea 6...Ba6 could be too hasty, but it has its good points: 7.Bxa6 Nxa6 8.Ne2!? (the knight heads for f4. Or, for instance, 8.h4 Ne7 9.h5 h6 10.Qd3 Nb8 11.Ne2 c5 12.dxc5 bxc5 13.c4 Nbc6 14.O-O g5!

analysis diagram

15.hxg6 Nxg6 16.cxd5 Qxd5 17.Qb5 Qd7 ↔ threatening ...Ncxe5, Berzinsh-Moskalenko, Solsona 2003) 8...Qd7 9.O-O c6 10.Nf4 Be7 11.Qd3 Nc7 12.Bd2 h5 13.Rac1 O-O-O 14.c4 Kb7 ( 14...g5!? (Black Jet) 15.Ne2 g4 ↔) 15.Rc3 ↑ preparing Rfc1, increasing the pressure along the c-file, Leko-Vaganian, Yerevan 2001.

7.h4

However, we all know that Kasparov will never wait for you! After 7.Bd3 a possible answer is 7...Nbc6!? (Petrosian's favourite plan. Or: 7...Ba6 8.Bxa6 Nxa6 9.O-O c5?! 10.Ne2 Nc6 11.Nf4 ∞ Lopez Martinez-Moskalenko, Salou 2006) 8.O-O Bb7 9.Bd2 Qd7 10.b4 Ng6 11.Re1 1/2-1/2 Nedeljkovic-Petrosian, Vienna Ech-tt 1957.

7...h6!?

This move seems to be useful, since it defends the g5-square. On the other hand, after 7...h5 8.Bg5! White gained a slight initiative in A.Sokolov-Portisch, Moscow GMA 1990.

8.h5 a5!?

As usual, Ivanchuk finds his own path instead of choosing between ...Ba6 or ...Nc6/...Bb7.

9.Bb5+

A thematic check, avoiding the exchange of the light-squared bishops.

9...c6 10.Ba4 Nd7 11.Ne2 b5 12.Bb3 c5 13.c3 Nc6 14.O-O Qc7 15.Re1 c4 16.Bc2

With a position very similar to the Labyrinths seen in Part One, Chapter 2. White will have a hard time trying to find an effective plan.

16...Nb6 17.Bf4 Be7 18.Bg3 Rb8!

Everything is ready for the 'barricades' attack ...b5-b4.

19.Nh2 Qd8?!

Lacking confidence. 19...b4! was a good move.

20.Ng4 b4!

21.axb4 axb4 22.cxb4?

After this unfortunate exchange Black gains the initiative on the queenside.

22...Nxb4 23.Bb1 Bd7 24.b3 Ra8 25.Rxa8 Qxa8 26.bxc4 Nxc4 27.Nc1?

The white knights' manoeuvres have not been very successful in this game.

27...Ba4 28.Qe2 Qa7!

Winning the d4 pawn.

29.Ne3 Qxd4 30.Nxc4 dxc4 31.Qf1 O-O 0-1

This was one of Kasparov's last losses with white.

GAME 58
Viktor Moskalenko - Jorge Gonzalez Rodriguez
Montcada Open 2006 (5)

'Barricades always have two sides.'

1.e4!

I usually play 1.d4 , but I took this decision because I knew that against 1.e4 my opponent always chooses the French Defence, which is also part of my repertoire.

1...e6

Now I do not care about the colour of my pieces!

2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 b6 5.a3 Bf8 6.Nf3 c5!?

An almost unknown move invented by Kortchnoi back in 1976. Black changes his plan and goes back to the usual French paths by attacking the centre.

7.Bg5!?

In another game against 6...c5 White continued 7.Bd3 Nc6 8.O-O Nge7 9.Nb5 Ng6 10.Ng5?! (too ambitious) 10...a6 11.Nd6+ Bxd6 12.exd6 c4 13.Bxc4 Golubev-Nosenko, Klaipeda 1985. And here 13...f6!? -/+ could have refuted White's play entirely.

7...Qd7

A novelty. My opponent in this game is a very creative player who always finds his own paths, even if they are (too) risky. I have also played this line with the black pieces, but I preferred 7...Be7!? 8.Bxe7 ( 8.h4!? , threatening Nb5, was somewhat better for White in Kurajica-Kortchnoi, Amsterdam IBM 1976) 8...Nxe7 9.Nb5 O-O! = Fluvia Poyatos-Moskalenko, Badalona 2005.

8.dxc5!?

From this point onwards I felt that White should open up the position as much as possible, in order to take advantage of his better development. One of my games with Black continued 8.Bb5 Nc6 9.Ba4 a6 10.dxc5 b5 11.Bb3 Bxc5 12.Qe2 h6 ( 12...Nge7!? ) 13.Bf4 Nge7 14.O-O-O Qa7 ∞ Lopez Martinez-Moskalenko, Sants Open 2006.

8...bxc5 9.Bb5 Nc6 10.O-O

Black's centre is strong, but he is under-developed, and so White has many tactical chances.

10...a6?!

The unnecessary weakening of the b6-square will cost Black dearly. Better was 10...Rb8!? 11.Qe2 +/=, or perhaps the immediate 10...h6!? .

11.Ba4 h6?

The white bishops prevent Black from developing comfortably Defending is not easy here.

12.Nxd5!!

I had foreseen this blow and now the moment has arrived. White sacrifices his minor pieces in order to penetrate into the opponent's fortress.

12...hxg5

The main point of White's combination is 12...exd5? 13.e6! fxe6 ( 13...Qxe6 14.Re1 ) 14.Bxc6 Qxc6 15.Ne5 +- with the threats 16.Nxc6 and 16.Qh5+ .

13.Nb6 Qb7 14.Nxa8

This seems natural. Also good was Fritz's 14.Nxg5!? Rh4 ( 14...Qxb6? 15.Qf3 with decisive threats) 15.b4! with an almost winning advantage.

14...Be7

Black is ready to capture the a8 knight, but...

15.Nxg5!

15...Bd7

There is no time for 15...Bxg5 16.Qd6 Bd7 17.Nc7+ Kd8 18.Rfd1 Qxc7 19.Qf8 mate, or 15...Qxa8 16.Qf3! .

16.Nxf7!

Another kamikaze knight!

16...Kxf7 17.Nb6! Qxb6

In the event of 17...Be8 18.Qf3+ Kg6 , simply 19.Nc4 wins.

18.Qxd7 Nxe5 19.Qe8+ Kf6

It looks as though the storm has blown over and Black can begin to breathe, but now the second part of the attack begins, this time with the heavy pieces.

20.Rae1!

This game follows strategic concepts. The ideas are activating the rook and preparing Bb3 and f2-f4, with an irresistible attack.

20...Nf3+!?

Attack is the best form of defence! This resource seems to be very strong, since it opens up the white king's position. We now arrive at the key moment in the game.

After 20...Qd6 White has 21.Re3! , threatening 22.Rfe1! and parrying the threat of 21...Nf3+ , because now the knight will be captured with check. The most tenacious was probably 20...Qd8 21.Qxd8 Bxd8 22.f4 Ng6 23.Bb3 with a big advantage for White.

21.gxf3

21.Kh1?? Rxh2+ mate.

21...Qd6

Threatening mate in one! A counterattacking attempt that fails.

22.h3!

This quiet move solves the problem.

22...Rxh3 23.Rxe6+!!

The final hardhitter in this game.

23...Qxe6

Or 23...Kxe6 24.Bd7+ ( 24.Qg6+!? wins as well) 24...Qxd7 25.Re1+ winning the queen.

24.Bd7 1-0

Black resigned, since he loses his rook on h3 without compensation. I greatly enjoyed this dynamic game, both while playing and while analysing the lines more calmly at home.

Epilogue

The defensive system with 4...b6 and 5...Bf8 allows you to:

1) either fight dogmas or follow them (and even beating Garry Kasparov!);

2) avoid long theoretical lines;

3) go straight into the middlegame;

4) defend against the dangerous attack with Qg4;

5) spend some time playing purely strategic chess...

What else can we ask for? Oh yes, STATISTICS! However, in these lines there are only attackers, defenders and eventually, just ruins, so statistics simply do not exist. What you must keep in mind is: barricades always have two sides!
