GAME 39
Dmitry Yakovenko - Alexander Morozevich
Moscow ch-RUS 2007 (1)

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.e5 Nfd7 5.f4 c5 6.Nf3 Nc6 7.Be3 cxd4

After this exchange Black wants to exert pressure on d4 with his pieces.

(A) 7...Qb6 is an almost forgotten idea: 8.Qd2?! (the main line is 8.Na4 Qa5+ 9.c3 cxd4 10.b4 Nxb4 11.cxb4 Bxb4+ 12.Bd2 Bxd2+ 13.Nxd2 +/= and Black does not get enough compensation for the piece) 8...Qxb2! 9.Rb1 Qa3 10.Be2 a6 11.f5 cxd4 12.fxe6? dxe3 winning too much material, Fluvia Poyatos-Vaganian, Barcelona 2007;

(B) 7...Be7!? is another old, natural method, recently popular: 8.Qd2!? ( 8.Be2 O-O 9.Qd2 b6!? Nijboer-Narciso Dublan, Barcelona 2007) 8...O-O 9.g3 ( 9.a3 b6!? 10.Bd3 f6 11.exf6 Nxf6 12.Bb5 Qc7 13.Bxc6 Qxc6 14.Ne5 Qe8 ↔ Macieja-Morozevich, St Petersburg 1997) 9...Rb8 10.a3 b6!?

This strategic concept, strengthening the centre, is the key to Black's defence.

(B) 11.Nce2 White's idea is to exchange the dark-squared bishops, reducing Black's attacking potential on the queenside: 11...Qe7 12.Nb3 Bxe3 13.Qxe3 f6! (starting counterplay in the centre) 14.exf6 Nxf6 15.h3 (White is forced to waste a tempo since ...Ng4 is not so easy to meet, for example: 15.Ned4 Ng4 16.Qf3 Nxd4 17.Nxd4 e5! ↔) 15...a5! 16.a3 b5 17.g4 ↔ with a double-edged position, Svidler-Morozevich, Moscow ch-RUS Superfmal 2005.

11...Qe7!?

An important improvement: the queen is much better placed on e7.

(A) 11...Nxd4 is rather different: 12.Bxd4 Qc7?! 13.Bd3 b5 14.Qh4! (this is White's idea: to create a couple of weaknesses on the kingside and to push his pawns to open up the position of the black king) 14...h6 ( 14...g6!? 15.Ne2 Bb7 16.Kb1 +/=) 15.Ne2 f6 ( 15...Bb7 16.g4! ) 16.Qg4 +/= Kramnik has won the theoretical discussion. White has developed a dangerous initiative on the kingside, while Black is deprived of any counterplay, Kramnik-Radjabov, Linares 2003;

(B) Or 11...Bxd4 12.Bxd4 b5 13.Be3! ( 13.Bd3 b4 14.Ne2 a5 ↔ Kharitonov-Iliushin, Krasnoyarsk ch-RUS 2003) 13...b4!? (considerably better than 13...Bb7 14.Bd3 f6 ( 14...Nb4 15.Bd4 Rc8 16.Kb1 Qa5 17.a3 Nc6 18.f5 Ndxe5?? 19.Bb6 1-0 Kramnik-Shirov, Monaco blind 2003) 15.exf6 Qxf6 16.Qg3 +/= Dominguez Perez-Narciso Dublan, Barcelona 2007) 14.Na4! ( 14.Ne2 a5 15.Nd4 Nxd4 16.Bxd4 Ba6 =. After exchanging the light-squared bishops Black has no problems at all, Shirov-Radjabov, Leon 2004) 14...a5!? 15.h4 Ba6 16.Bxa6 ( 16.h5 Qc7 17.h6 g6 18.Kb1 Bxf1 19.Rhxf1 f5 ∞ Ragger-Stellwagen, Yerevan Wch-jr 2007) 16...Rxa6 17.Qe2 Ra8 18.h5 Qc7 19.h6 g6 20.Kb1 Na7 21.Bxa7 Rxa7 22.Qf2 Ra6 23.Rhe1 Rc6 ↔ Yakovenko-Ni Hua, Taiyuan 2006.

37...Qf2?

The game becomes uncontrollable. As we can see, on many occasions, Morozevich lacks resources to finish a game properly. There was an easy win by 37...Qf7+ 38.Rc4 ( 38.Qc4 Qf2 39.Rb1 Rc8 ) 38...Nb6 or else 37...Qf6! with zugzwang.

59...Rfa7 60.Rd5 Ra4 61.h5 Qxd6 62.Rxd6 Rxc4 63.Rd5 Kh7 64.Rd7 Rg4 65.Rc7 Ra5 66.Nc5 Ra8 67.Nd7 Rf4 68.Nb6 Ra5 69.Nc4 Rxh5 1/2-1/2

Summary of 7...cxd4 : After castling queenside White controls the centre and the kingside, while his opponent must find some counterplay on the queenside. Sometimes Black plays ...f7-f6!? in order to open up the centre. The positions arising are very similar to the sharper lines of the Sicilian Defence, but with a closed centre.
