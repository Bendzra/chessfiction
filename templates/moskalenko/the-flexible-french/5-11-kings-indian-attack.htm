Chapter 11 - King's Indian Attack

1.e4 e6 2.d3 d5

In the King's Indian Attack 1.e4 e6 2.d3 , the following game presents you with two fighting possibilities for Black in two main lines:

A) In the Barcza System with 3.Qe2 , Black can take his chance with the central knight jump 10...Nd4!;

B) In the main line with 3.Nd2 , where play develops slowly, Black can increase the pressure on the e5 pawn by first fianchettoing Bg7 and Bb7 and then launching the surprising 'Black Jet' 11...g5!?, a radical concept that can lead to very sharp play.

1.e4 e6 2.d3 d5 3.Nd2

The main move for White. However, 3.Qe2 , known as the Barcza System, is also possible: 3...Nf6 (a natural move) 4.Nf3 Be7 5.g3 c5 ( 5...dxe4 6.dxe4 Nc6 =) 6.Bg2 Nc6 7.O-O O-O!? (in my opinion, the best plan consists in bringing the king to a safe place first and then trying to get some activity) 8.e5! (White's plan is based on an attack on the kingside, but he needs to close the centre first) 8...Nd7 9.c4 (with the idea of bringing the knight into the game via c3) 9...dxc4 ( 9...d4? would be a strategic mistake, since the blocked centre offers Black no counterplay) 10.dxc4 .

This is the key moment. Black must look for some chances as quickly as possible: 10...Nd4! .

3...c5 4.Ngf3 Nc6 5.g3 g6!?

A fianchetto against a fianchetto. 5...Nf6 is the classical response.

6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O Nge7 8.Re1

8...b6

Including the move 8...h6!? is quite useful: 9.h4 b6 10.a3 a5 11.exd5 exd5! 12.d4?! cxd4 13.Nb3 Bg4! and now White cannot play moves like h2-h3 or Bg5: 14.Qd3 Bxf3 ( 14...Bf5!? 15.Qd1 d3 ↔) 15.Bxf3 ( 15.Qxf3 a4 16.Qxd5 Qxd5 17.Bxd5 O-O 18.Bxc6 Nxc6 19.Nd2 Rac8 -/+) 15...a4! 16.Nd2 O-O 17.Bg2 Ne5 -/+ and Black won easily, Kogan-Moskalenko, Barcelona 2000.

9.c3

The most popular move.

(A) 9.exd5!? has recendy come into fashion: 9...Nxd5 (or else 9...exd5!? 10.d4!? cxd4 11.Nb3 Bg4 ↔) 10.d4!? ( 10.Nc4 has been played several times; 10...O-O 11.Nfe5 Bb7 12.Nxc6 Bxc6 13.Ne5 Bxe5 14.Rxe5 Qd6 15.Re1 e5 =) 10...cxd4 11.Nb3 O-O!? ( 11...Bb7 12.Nfxd4 Nxd4? is weaker: 13.Nxd4 Rc8 14.Rxe6+! fxe6? 15.Nxe6 ↑ Komliakov-Moskalenko, Noyabrsk 1995) 12.Nbxd4 Nxd4 13.Nxd4 Ba6!= 14.c3 1/2-1/2 Psakhis-Dokhoian, Sochi 1988;

(B) 9.e5?! is premature, in view of the weakness of the e5 pawn: 9...Qc7 10.Qe2 h6 11.h4 g5! with active counterplay: 12.hxg5 hxg5 13.Nxg5 Qxe5! 14.Qxe5 Nxe5 =/+ Yandemirov-Moskalenko, Baku 1984.

9...O-O

We have arrived at the key position in this line with two fianchettos. The alternative 9...h6!? is still playable: 10.h4 ( 10.exd5 Nxd5!? 11.Nc4 O-O 12.Nfe5 Bb7 13.Nxc6 Bxc6 14.Ne5 Bxe5 15.Rxe5 Qd6 16.Re1 Kg7 =) 10...Bb7!? (another idea is 10...dxe4 11.dxe4 Ba6 12.Qa4 Bd3 ∞ Varavin-Moskalenko, Alushta 1994) 11.exd5 Nxd5 12.Nc4 O-O 13.Nfe5 Nxe5 14.Nxe5 Qc7 15.Qe2 Rad8 = Zolnierowicz-Moskalenko, Orange 1990.

10.e5 Qc7 11.Qe2 g5!

A radical concept, inviting the exchange of White's central pawn. 11...Ba6!? is the usual move.

12.Nxg5

12.Bh3?! seems to be bad: 12...h6 13.Bg4 Ng6 14.d4 cxd4 15.cxd4 Nb4! -/+ T.Gustafsson-Murugan, Gausdal 1991.

If 12.h3 Ng6 13.Nf1 Ncxe5 14.Nxg5 (Fernandez Aguado-De la Riva Aguado, Barcelona 1993/94) 14...Ba6! ↑.

12...Qxe5!

The moment has come to take some tough decisions.

13.Nde4!

A creative reply, but also a forced move, which leads to a sharp fight.

If 13.f4? Qxe2 14.Rxe2 Ba6 -+ Hohn-S.Pedersen, Duisburg 1992; or 13.Qxe5 Nxe5 14.Bf1 Ba6 15.d4 Bxf1 -/+.

13...Ng6?

This move allows White to initiate a strong attack.

(A) If Black accepts the material with 13...dxe4!? , then:

(A1) 14.Qh5? is weak in view of 14...h6 15.Bf4 Qf6 16.Bxe4 e5! 17.Nh7 Qd6 18.Bg5 Rd8 19.Bxe7 Qxe7 20.Bxc6 Bb7 21.Bxb7 Qxb7 -/+ and the white knight remains in a tight spot on h7, Berry-Gershon, Dresden 2003;

(A2) Fritz 11 gives the wonderful variation 14.Bf4!?

14...Qf6 15.Bxe4 ( 15.Nxh7 Kxh7 16.Bxe4+ Kg8 17.Qh5 Ng6! ↔) 15...e5 16.Bxh7+ Kh8 17.Qh5 ( 17.Ne4? Qe6 18.Qh5 Qg4! ) 17...Bg4! 18.Qh4! Bh6! 19.Rxe5! Nxe5 20.Bxe5 Qxe5 21.Qxh6 Qg7 22.Qh4 f6 and White has a perpetual.

(B) 13...Qc7!? is the best try to keep on playing: 14.Nxh7 dxe4 15.Nxf8 exd3 ∞.

14.Nxh7?

Another incorrect reply. The game has gone berserk and there are many tactical possibilities. The right move was 14.f4! Qc7 15.Qh5 → (also 15.f5!? exf5 16.Qh5 h6 17.Nf6+ Bxf6 18.Bxd5 +/-) 15...h6 16.f5 dxe4 17.fxg6 fxg6 18.Qxg6 hxg5 19.Bxe4 , winning; 14.Qh5!? leads to the same line.

14...Kxh7!

≤ 14...dxe4? 15.Nxf8 Kxf8 16.h4!? ↑.

15.f4! Qc7

15...Qf5? loses the queen after 16.Bh3! Qxh3 17.Ng5+ .

16.Ng5+ Kg8 17.Qh5 Rd8 18.Qh7+ Kf8 19.h4

Trying to complicate with h5-h6.

19...Nge7! 20.Qh5

20.h5? does not work because of 20...Ng8! .

20...Nf5 21.g4 Ng3! 22.Qh7 Ne7 23.f5

There is no time to play 23.Kh2? Ng8! 24.Kxg3 Nf6 and White loses his queen.

23...exf5 24.h5?

A 'ping-pong' reply would be 24.Nxf7!? Kxf7 25.Bh6 Rg8 26.Rxe7+ Qxe7 27.Bxd5+ Be6 28.Re1 Rae8! -/+.

24...Ng8!

In order to defend against h5-h6, but also to trap the queen on h7 with ...Nf6!

25.h6 Nxh6 26.Ne6+ fxe6?

Victory would have been Blacks reward after 26...Bxe6! 27.Bxh6 Bxh6 28.Qxh6+ ( 28.Rxe6 Bg7 ) 28...Ke7 -/+.

27.Bxh6 Qf7 28.Bxg7+ Qxg7 29.Qh4 Bd7 30.Qxg3 Qxg4 31.Qd6+ Kf7 32.Re3 Qg6 33.Rg3 Be8 34.Qf4 Qf6 35.Re1 Rac8! -/+ 36.Rg5 Rd7 T 1/2-1/2

A game full of tactics, odd and beautiful, mixing bits of everything. However, the ambitious 'exchanging' plan with 9...O-O and 11...g5 can be a dangerous surprise in your repertoire.
