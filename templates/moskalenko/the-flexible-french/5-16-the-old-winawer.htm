Chapter 16 - The Old Winawer

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 c5 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Qc7

This is a method of playing the Winawer which was preferred by Mikhail Botvinnik The manoeuvre of the black queen is 'multi-geometric', since it protects the seventh rank against Qg4 (with ...f7-f5) and usually counterattacks tactically along the c-file. This 'old Winawer' contains many resources - and it is always possible to revert to the modern lines.

GAME 75
Jordi Fluvia Poyatos - Viktor Moskalenko
Catalunya ch 2005 (7)

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 c5 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Qc7!?

Another old/new surprise weapon can be 6...Ne7 7.Qg4 cxd4!? (peculiarly, the most fashionable defences are: 7...Qc7 (which does not seem to have much logic independently after ...Ne7 as then the queen cannot protect g7, but it could transpose into the main line after 8.Qxg7 Rg8 9.Qxh7 cxd4 etc.) and 7...O-O . I have never used kingside castling in this Winawer 'pseudo-main' line, since White has many possibilities of launching pretty mating attacks. But 7...Kf8!? is possibly more suitable) , with the follow-up 8.Qxg7 ( 8.cxd4 Qc7 ↔ Ragozin-Botvinnik, Moscow 1935) 8...Rg8 9.Qxh7 Qc7 ( 9...Qa5?! 10.Rb1 Qxc3+ 11.Bd2 Qc7 12.f4 +/- Alexander-Botvinnik, England-Soviet Union radio match 1946) 10.Ne2 Nbc6 11.f4 Bd7 12.Qd3 dxc3

analysis diagram

(A) In the old days white players responded classically with 13.Be3 d4!? 14.Bf2 O-O-O 15.Nxd4 Nxd4 16.Qxd4 b6 17.Bh4 Bb5 ↔ Spassky-Kortchnoi, 2nd match game, Belgrade 1977;

(B) 13.Rg1 is a modern manoeuvre, preferred by Svidler and Shirov: 13...O-O-O 14.g4 d4 15.h4 ( 15.Rb1 Be8 16.Rg3 ∞ Svidler-Berg, Heraklion Ech-tt 2007) 15...Be8 16.h5 f6!? 17.exf6 Nd5 18.Bh3 Kb8 19.g5 Bxh5 20.Bxe6 ∞ Shirov-Shulman, Khanty-Mansiysk 2007;

(C) 13.Nxc3 a6 ( 13...Na5!? ) 14.Rb1 Na5 ( 14...O-O-O?? 15.Qxa6! ) 15.h4?! Nf5 16.Rh3 O-O-O ↔ occurred in Cheparinov-Grischuk, Baku 2008.

7.Qg4

Logical and strong. The most conventional play occurs after 7.Nf3 b6!? 8.Bb5+ Bd7 9.Bd3 Ba4 10.O-O c4 11.Be2 Nc6 12.Ng5 (another option is 12.Ne1 Nge7 13.f4 O-O-O 14.Be3 f6 15.Bf2 Qd7 16.g4 h5! 17.exf6 gxf6 18.g5 Nf5 -/+ Felgaer-Moskalenko, Barcelona 2004) 12...h6 13.Nh3 Nge7 (or 13...O-O-O 14.f4 f5 15.exf6 Nxf6 16.Nf2 g5! ↑ Slaby-Moskalenko, Salou 2005) 14.f4 ( 14.Nf4!? ) 14...O-O-O 15.g4 f5 16.g5 Rdf8 17.Rf2 Nd8 18.Rg2 hxg5 19.Nxg5 Rh6 20.Be3 Nf7 21.Nf3 Ng6 22.Qd2 Rg8 23.Bf2 Qe7!? =/+ Jimenez Albadalejo-Moskalenko, Barcelona Sants 2001.

10.cxd4 Nge7 11.Bd2

If 11.Qxg7?! Rg8 12.Qxh7 Nxd4! ↔. An interesting line is 11.Be2!? O-O 12.Bh6 Qa5+! 13.Bd2 Qa4 14.c3 Na5 ↔ Korneev-Moskalenko, Naval-moral 2005.

11...O-O

A natural move. More risky is 11...Rg8!? 12.h4 Bd7 13.Bd3 h6 14.O-O ∞ Iglesias-Moskalenko, Canovelles 2001.

12.Bd3 Bd7

The key moment in this line.

13.h4?!

Players with an attacking style prefer this move, but Black will get there first. It was better to continue developing with 13.O-O Be8!? 14.Rab1 Bg6 (or 14...Qd7 15.Ng5 Bg6 16.c3 f4!? ↔ Handke-Moskalenko, Sants 2005) 15.Qf4 h6 ↔ Alsina Leal-Moskalenko, Salou 2006.

13...f4!

With many tactical tricks for Black.

14.Qg4?

Allowing another strong blow.

(A) The main trick is revealed after 14.Bxf4 Nxd4! 15.Nxd4 (if 15.Bxh7+? Kxh7 16.Nxd4 Rxf4! ; or 15.O-O Nef5 16.Qg4 Nxf3+ 17.Qxf3 Nxh4 =/+ Dolce-CapNemo, playchess.com 2006) 15...Qc3+ 16.Ke2 Qxd4 ↑ Lacasa-Moskalenko, Barcelona Sants 2003;

(B) 14.Qg5 does not work either. After 14...h6! 15.Qg4 Nxd4! 16.Nxd4 Qxe5+ 17.Kf1 Qxd4 Black won easily in the blitz games Barreiro-CapNemo and High_me-CapNemo, playchess.com 2007.

14...Nxd4!

The point of Black's counterplay.
