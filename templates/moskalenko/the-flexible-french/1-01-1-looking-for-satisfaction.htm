Chapter 1 - Looking for Satisfaction

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3

5...Qb6 6.a3 Nh6

or deviations with 5...Nh6

History

The author of the present book defeated Sveshnikov with the black pieces in his favourite Advance Variation of the French in 1987. In that game I prepared a totally new and very creative counter-plan and I obtained a very good position. But I finally won only after a tense and spectacular fight. I felt as though I had killed a monster!

I would like to thank grandmaster Sveshnikov for his combative play in both of the games annotated in this chapter. It was a real pleasure to play against him!

The Russian grandmaster Evgeny Sveshnikov was born in 1950. To the present day, he has tried to master the Advance Variation from the white side and he recently published a book on this variation.

Introduction

In this chapter I shall retrace the most important steps in my study of the Advance Variation.

Of all the many possibilities in the opening I generally tend to choose the most aggressive plans with Black. With set-ups like 5...Nh6 , Black prepares a direct attack against the d4 pawn, not allowing White time to castle and forcing him to defend his centre first. Therefore the logical reaction is the move 6.a3!? , preparing 7.b4 and then Bb2, which seems to be the best way for White to fight for an advantage.

This tense situation forces Black to look for satisfaction by entering into some quite forcing lines, where pieces will be fighting against pawns.

On a historical note, this plan was suggested long ago by the well-known German master Louis Paulsen, and introduced by him in 1879. Curiously, about 100 years later, another master, of Moldavian origin, found a similar opening idea for Black in another opening: ...d5-c6-a6-b5 in the Slav. See Viktor Bologan's book The Chebanenko Slav According to Bologan (New In Chess, 2008).

Directions

(A) 5...Qb6 6.a3 Nh6

7.b4 White gains even more space and tries to control the entire board. After 7...cxd4 8.cxd4 Nf5 9.Bb2 we reach the key position of this line.

The first question is: which bishop should be developed first? And the second is: what should Black do next? The answers can be found in Game 1: Sveshnikov-Moskalenko (1987) and also in the comments to the important Game 2: Shirov-Gurevich.

(B) 5...Nh6

This move, bringing the knight straight into play, is more flexible than the immediate 5...Qb6 , and gives several plans to choose from in this complex position - see Game 3: Sveshnikov-Moskalenko (1993).

Looking for Satisfaction - Games

GAME 1
Evgeny Sveshnikov - Viktor Moskalenko
Norilsk 1987 (7)

This was a very hard test for me, since I was facing a great master of the Advance Variation. However, I had prepared a 'surprise bombshell' at home.

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 4.c3 Nc6

4...Qb6!? is even playable right now. This move order avoids 5.Be3 , another popular plan for White. The stem game went as follows: 4...cxd4?! 5.cxd4 Bb4+ 6.Nc3 Bxc3+? 7.bxc3 Nc6 8.Bd3 ( 8.Qg4!? ) 8...Nge7 9.f4 Nf5 10.Nf3 O-O 11.g4 Nh4 12.O-O Nxf3+ 13.Qxf3 Bd7 14.Qh3! g6 15.f5! (already winning) 15...exf5 16.gxf5 gxf5 17.Rxf5 Bxf5 18.Bxf5 1-0 Greco-NN, Europe 1620.

5.Nf3

With 5.Be3 an interesting miniature went 5...Qb6 6.Qd2 Bd7 7.Nf3 cxd4 8.cxd4 Rc8 9.Nc3 Na5!? 10.Rc1 Bb4 11.Bd3 Nc4 12.Qe2?? ( 12.Bxc4 □ 12...Rxc4 =) 12...Nxb2! 13.Bd2 Na4! 0-1 Romero Ruscalleda-Pomes Marcet, Catalunya tt 2005.

5...Qb6!

The pressure on d4 does not allow White to continue calmly.

6.a3!?

This is the first key position in this line. White already has a space advantage in the centre and on the kingside because of his e5 pawn, and he also wants to gain more space on the queenside by playing b2-b4. I am sure that both known developments of the kings bishop are weaker:

(A) 6.Be2 Nh6! (this is better than 6...Nge7 ) 7.b3 (after 7.Na3? cxd4 8.cxd4 Bxa3! 9.bxa3 Nf5 10.Be3 Qa5+ Black has the initiative. Cubles-Moskalenko, Canovelles 2000; or 7.Bxh6 gxh6 ( 7...Qxb2? 8.Qd2 Qxa1 9.O-O is better for White) 8.Qd2 Bg7 9.Na3 cxd4 10.cxd4 O-O 11.O-O f6 12.exf6 Rxf6 13.Nc2 Bd7 14.b4 Be8 15.b5 Ne7 16.Ne5 Ng6! 17.Ng4 Rf8 18.g3 h5 ↔ Bakre-T.L.Petrosian, Dubai 2007) 7...cxd4 8.cxd4 Bb4+!

analysis diagram

9.Kf1 □ 9...Nf5 ( 9...O-O!? ↔) 10.Bb2 Be7 11.Nc3 O-O 12.Na4 Qd8 13.g3 f6! 14.exf6 Bxf6 15.Kg2 Qd6 16.Re1 b6 17.Bb5 Bb7 and Black has the better chances, Kupreichik-Piskov, Germany Bundesliga B 1998/99;

(B) There is also the gambit line with 6.Bd3 , but fortunately Black is not forced to accept the material: 6...Nh6!? ( 6...cxd4 7.cxd4 Bd7 8.O-O Nxd4 9.Nbd2 ∞) 7.O-O cxd4 8.cxd4 Nf5

During the 1980s and 1990s theory favoured White after 9.Bxf5 exf5 10.Nc3 Be6 , but, thinking that the books might be wrong, I studied the position more closely and eventually managed to achieve outstanding results with Black from here. For instance: 11.Ne2 h6! (this avoids the simplification with Bg5, and prepares ...g7-g5!?) 12.Qa4 Be7 13.a3 O-O 14.b4 Rfc8 and Black is dominating every aspect of the game: 15.Bd2 a6 16.Rfc1 g5! 17.Qb3 Na5!? 18.Qd3 Nc4 19.Ne1 f4 20.g3 fxg3 21.hxg3 Bg4 22.Nc3 Qe6 23.Be3 b5 24.Qf1 Ra7 25.Qg2 Nxe3 26.fxe3 Rac7 winning easily, Schenk-Moskalenko, Palma de Mallorca 2000.

6...Nh6!

This is a classic French manoeuvre, intending to attack the d4 pawn. Until 2005 the set-up ...Qb6-Nh6 was my main plan against the Advance Variation. The play is completely different after 6...c4!? , which we will study in Chapter 2.

I have never liked the line 6...Bd7 7.b4 cxd4 8.cxd4 Rc8 9.Bb2 , when White threatens Nc3-a4 with the initiative, and now 9...Na5 10.Nbd2 Nc4 11.Bxc4 dxc4 12.Rc1 Qa6 13.O-O .

analysis diagram

This is also better for White, because of his better development and the threat of d4-d5.

WEAPON: If 13...Ba4 14.Qe2 Bb5 15.Rfe1 c3? 16.Qe3 cxd2 17.Rxc8+ Kd7 18.Rxf8 , White wins.

7.b4

Planning to conquer the board with his pawns. 7.Bxh6? Qxb2! 8.Bc1 Qxa1 9.Qc2 Bd7 must be better for Black.

7...cxd4 8.cxd4

8.Bxh6?! is still weak. White takes the chance to exchange the knight before it reaches f5, giving Black a bad pawn structure. But on the other hand White gives up the bishop pair. In my opinion that is more than enough compensation for Black's structural weaknesses: 8...gxh6 9.cxd4 Bg7 10.Nc3 O-O ( 10...f6!? ) 11.Bb5 a5 ↔ Rozentalis-Berg, Stockholm 2005/06.

8...Nf5

Black's pieces will fight against the wall created by the white pawns.

9.Bb2

9.Be3 can be met with:

(A) 9...Bd7!?

WEAPON: 10.Nc3? Nxe3 11.fxe3 Nxb4! 12.axb4 Bxb4 13.Rc1 Rc8 14.Qb3 Qa5 15.Kd2 O-O ‰ 16.Bd3 f6!? and Black has the initiative.

10.Bd3 Nxe3 ( 10...Rc8!? Scavo-Moskalenko, Badalona 2002) 11.fxe3 Rc8 12.O-O Be7 13.Nbd2 Nd8!? 14.Qe2 Rc3 ↔ Baklan-Moskalenko, Donetsk Zonal 1998.

(B) Interesting is 9...f6!? ↔, a move very well explained in the Survey 'Fighting for Equality' by grandmaster Carlos Matamoros in Yearbook 72:

(B1) 10.b5?! Nxe5! 11.dxe5 Nxe3 12.fxe3 Qxe3+ 13.Qe2 Qc1+ 14.Qd1 1/2-1/2 Romanishin-Lputian, Yerevan 1988 and Movsesian-Lputian, New York Open 1998;

(B2) 10.exf6 gxf6 11.Bd3 Nxe3 12.fxe3 Bh6 13.Qe2 Bd7 14.Nc3 Ne7 15.O-O O-O 16.Kh1 Rac8 17.Nd1 Bg7 18.Nf2 e5 19.dxe5 fxe5 20.e4 d4 21.Rac1 Ng6 22.Rxc8 Bxc8 23.g3 Bh6 and Black was clearly better in Morozevich-Bareev, Monaco blind 2002.

This is another key moment in the opening. Black faces a dilemma: which bishop should be developed first?

9...Be7

Obviously a good move: now Black is able to castle and he also maintains his knight on f5 by the possibility of 10.g4 Nh4 . However, nowadays the most fashionable move is 9...Bd7!? , thanks to several famous victories for Black in 2005. See Game 2.

10.Bd3!

The d4 pawn is protected by tactical means. 10.h4!? (comfortably preparing 11.g4 ) is an idea of the Leningrad grandmaster Mark Tseitlin: 10...Bd7 ( 10...h5?! 11.Bd3 +/=; 10...O-O!? ) 11.g4 Nh6 12.Rg1 f5!? N ( 12...O-O is also playable: 13.Nc3 Kh8!? ↔ ∆ ...Ng8; 13...f6?! 14.Na4 Qd8 15.exf6 Rxf6 16.g5 Rxf3 17.Qxf3 Nf5 ∞ Grischuk-Johannessen, Reykjavik 2000) 13.exf6 ( 13.g5 Ng4! ) 13...gxf6 14.Nc3 Nf7 with a complicated game, Lopez Martinez-Moskalenko, Catalunya tt 2004.

10...a5!?

My counterattack begins. The natural 10...O-O!? ∞ has not yet been properly explored, although Black looks very solid here. Black still has time to go for complications with 10...Bd7 11.O-O g6 12.Nc3!? ( 12.Bxf5 exf5 13.Nc3 Be6 14.Na4 Qd8 15.Nc5 b6 16.Qa4 Rc8 17.Nxe6 fxe6 18.Rac1 Qd7 19.Rc3 Nd8 = Erenburg-Moskalenko, Villa Salou 2003) 12...Nfxd4 13.Nxd4! (less good is 13.Nxd5? exd5 14.e6? (better 14.Nxd4 Nxd4 15.e6 Nxe6 16.Bxh8 f6! =/+) 14...fxe6 15.Nxd4 Bf6! -+ Saldano-Moskalenko, Roquetas de Mar 2004), when White gets the better chances: 13...Nxd4 (Black can consider 13...Qxd4!? 14.Qe2 ∞) 14.Qg4 Nc6? ( 14...Nb3!? ) 15.Rfe1 Qd8 16.b5 Na5 17.Nxd5! ↑ (Jonkman-Moskalenko, Banyoles 2005).

A recent game with high-ranked players continued 10...a6 11.O-O O-O 12.Qd2 h6 13.Bc2 Bd7 14.Bxf5 exf5 15.Nc3 Be6 16.Na4 Qb5 17.Nc3 Qb6 18.Na4 Qb5 19.Nc3 1/2-1/2 Khairullin-Volkov, Sochi tt 2007.

11.Bxf5

I think that this typical exchange favours Black in almost every case. It should be borne in mind that the king's bishop is one of White's most important pieces in the Advance Variation. Later another move for White was found: 11.Qa4!? O-O 12.b5 Nh4 13.Nxh4 Bxh4 ∞ Kiik-Kortchnoi, Stockholm 2003/04.

Or 11.b5 Ncxd4 12.Nxd4 Nxd4 with the point 13.Qg4 Nb3! 14.Qxg7 Rf8 15.Ra2 Bd7 ↔ and Black is OK.

11...exf5 12.Nc3 Be6

My own experience tells me that this bishop is not as bad as theory books say.

13.b5 a4! ↔

This unexpected resource is the main idea of my plan: sacrificing the a-pawn will give Black the chance to improve his knight's position with the manoeuvre ...Na5-c4. He gains the initiative on the queenside.

14.Qd3

Later, opponents tried new moves against me:

(A) 14.O-O Na5 15.Bc1 Nc4 16.Nxa4 Qxb5 17.Nc3 Qc6 18.Rb1 Bxa3 ( 18...h6 ) 19.Bxa3 Nxa3 20.Rb3 O-O -/+ Vasiukov-Moskalenko, Belgorod 1990;

(B) 14.Rb1 Na5 15.Qxa4?! O-O ↑ 16.Qd1? Nc4 17.a4? Nxb2 18.Rxb2 Qa5 -+ Garrido Fernandez-Moskalenko, Barbera del Valles 2003.

14...Nb8

This might be a psychological mistake. Now the knight must spend more tempi to get to c4, but I did not want to sacrifice material against a grandmaster! The most active idea was to pursue my initial plan with 14...Na5! 15.Nxa4 Qa7 , with full compensation for the pawn.

15.Bc1 h6!

A very useful move in this position. The white queen's bishop is kept out of play.

16.Ng1

White is also looking for the best squares for his pieces.

16...Nd7 17.Nge2 Qc7 18.Bd2 Nb6 19.Rc1?!

19...Qd7

19...Nc4!? .

20.Na2 O-O 21.Bb4

White has found a way to exchange his passive bishop, but the knight on c4 will be another reason for concern.

21...Rfc8 22.Rxc8+ Rxc8 23.O-O Bg5!? 24.f4

Now the knight will never get to f4.

24...Be7

24...Bd8!? was a more ambitious move.

25.Bxe7 Qxe7 26.Nb4 Qd7 27.Rc1?

Now White loses his b5 pawn. Better was 27.Nc3 Nc4 ↔.

27...Rxc1+! 28.Nxc1 Nc4

Winning material. Black keeps a clear advantage.

29.Ne2 Qxb5 30.Ng3 g6 31.Kf2 Qb6 32.Nf1?

This allows a cool sacrifice which will conquer all White's pawns in return. If 32.Ne2 Qd8 or 32...f6!? , with advantage to Black.

32...Nxa3!-+

A combination resembling draughts: you sacrifice a piece and in return you get to capture three or four pawns.

33.Qxa3 Qxd4+ 34.Ke1 Qe4+! 35.Kd1 Qxf4 36.Nd2 Qxh2 37.Qxa4 Qxg2

A lone knight is protecting the 'naked' white king. The game is over.

In this - for the author - unforgettable game, grandmaster Sveshnikov did not manage to demonstrate a single effective idea in his favourite variation against my counterattack 10...a5 and 13...a4! . While fighting for the initiative, he forgot to defend his advanced pawns.

As a summary of the main plan with 5...Qb6 , 6...Nh6 and 9...Be7 , I can assert that Black gains counterplay in almost all lines. Still, I was not completely satisfied and I thought that White could improve his play at certain points.

GAME 2
Alexei Shirov - Mikhail Gurevich
Khanty-Mansiysk 2005 (3)

In my opinion, this was an important game for the Advance Variation, which increased the popularity of 9...Bd7. I recommend the reader to make a deep study of the secondary lines, since the alternatives given there are equally important as the game moves.

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.e5 c5 4.c3 Nc6 5.Nf3 Qb6 6.a3 Nh6 7.b4 cxd4 8.cxd4 Nf5 9.Bb2 Bd7!?

A creative continuation, allowing the advance g2-g4. Now the natural moves 10.Bd3 , 10.Nc3 or 10.Nbd2 would simply lose the d4 pawn.

A logical retreat, although the most dynamic option seems to be 10...Nh6!? , preparing a classical counterattack against the centre, and now:

11.Nc3

11...Na5!?

A well-known resource in these structures. Grandmaster Psakhis wrote in The Complete French: 'White got a few weaknesses on the queenside and Black tries to exploit them. This move has become quite popular in the last couple of years'.

An equally good alternative is the jump with the other knight: 11...Ng6!? and now:

(A) 12.h4 h5! 13.g5 Na5 14.Na4 Qc6 15.Nc5 Nc4 is favourable for Black, Sveshnikov-Ostenstad, Oslo tt 2008 (1);

(B) 12.Na4 Qd8 13.Nc5 ( 13.h4 h5! 14.g5 b6 15.Bd3 Nf4 16.Ba6 Ne7 17.Nc3 Nf5 ↔ Nevednichy-Lputian, Ohrid Ech 2001) 13...Bxc5 14.dxc5 Qc7 15.Qe2 (Vysochin-Prezerakos, Korinthos 2004) and now the best solution for Black is 15...O-O!? intending 16.h4 d4! with satisfactory counter-play;

(C) 12.Rc1 Be7 13.h4 Qd8 ( 13...h5! ) 14.Nb5 a6 15.h5? ( 15.Nd6+ Bxd6 16.exd6 Qb8 17.h5 Nf4 18.Qd2 Qxd6 19.Ne5 g5 20.hxg6 Nxg6 is unclear) 15...axb5 16.hxg6 fxg6 and Black is better, Sveshnikov-Agrest, Oslo tt 2008 (3).
