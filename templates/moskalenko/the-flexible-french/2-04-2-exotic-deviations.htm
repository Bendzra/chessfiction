GAME 28
Alexander Onischuk - Alexander Morozevich
Germany Bundesliga 1998/99 (8)

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nd2 Be7 4.c3

A modest try for technical players. The present game is the most interesting and attractive played in this line.

4...c5!?

4...dxe4 (analogous to the Rubinstein Variation) is a good way to equalise: 5.Nxe4 Nf6 ( 5...Nc6 ) 6.Nxf6+ Bxf6 7.Nf3 Nd7 8.Bf4 O-O 9.Be2 b6 10.O-O Bb7 11.Qd2 c5 12.dxc5 Nxc5 13.Qe3 Qd5 14.Rfd1 Qe4 15.Qxe4 Nxe4 16.Be5 Rfd8 17.Bxf6 Nxf6 18.Ne5 Kf8 19.f3 Nd5 20.Kf2 f6 21.Ng4 Ke7 1/2-1/2 A.Sokolov-Kortchnoi, Madrid 1988.

5.dxc5

5.e5?! leads to the line with 4.e5 c5 5.c3?! , see the comments to the previous game.

The other option is 5.exd5 :

(A) 5...exd5!? 6.dxc5 Bxc5 7.Ngf3 Nf6 8.Bb5+ Nc6 ( 8...Bd7 =) 9.O-O O-O 10.Nb3 Bb6 ( 10...Bd6 =) 11.Bg5 a6 12.Ba4 Bc7 13.Nbd4 Qd6 14.Bxf6 Qxf6 15.Qd3 Bg4 16.Bc2 g6 17.h3 Bd7 Black's counterplay compensates for his isolated pawn on d5, Drabke-Fluvia Poyatos, Oropesa del Mar jr 2000;

(B) After 5...Qxd5 we have actually reached a position from one of the main C07 lines (with 3...c5 4.exd5 Qxd5 ), with the inclusion of the moves c2-c3 and ...Bf8-e7. This difference seems to slightly favour Black, so he should not have any problems in equalising: 6.Ngf3 ( 6.dxc5 Qxc5 7.Ne4 Qc6 8.Bd3 Nd7 9.Nf3 Ngf6 10.Qe2 Nxe4 11.Bxe4 Qc7 12.O-O O-O 13.a4 Nf6 14.Bc2 b6 15.Bg5 Bb7 = Dvoiris-Morozevich, Samara 1998) 6...cxd4 7.Bc4 Qh5 8.Nxd4 Qxd1+ 9.Kxd1 a6 10.Re1 Nf6 11.a4 b6 12.Be2 Bb7 13.Bf3 Bxf3+ 14.N2xf3 O-O 15.Kc2 Nbd7 16.Nc6 Bc5 17.Be3 Rfc8 18.Bxc5 Rxc6 19.Bd4 Nd5 20.g3 Rac8 21.Kb3 f6 22.Rad1 Kf7 23.Be3 Rc4 24.Bf4 Rxc3+ 25.bxc3 Rxc3+ 26.Kb2 Rxf3 -/+ Khamrakulov-Caruana, Dos Hermanas 2006.

(the bishop was on d3 earlier — see the line 4.Bd3 ) 9...Bd7 10.Bxc6 bxc6 11.O-O Ne7 12.Be3 O-O (Black's position is very comfortable) 13.Re1 Ng6 14.Nc5 (Adams-Morozevich, Cannes rapid 2001) and now 14...fxe5 15.Nxd7 Qxd7 16.Bxb6 e4! 17.Rxe4 axb6 =.

6...Nf6

7.e5!?

This advance always leads to sharp and double-edged positions. 7.Bd3 dxe4 ( 7...Nc6!? ) 8.Nxe4 Nxe4 9.Qa4+! Bd7 10.Qxe4 Bc6 11.Qe2 Nd7 12.O-O a5 13.Rd1 Qc7 14.b4 Be7 15.Bb2 = Svidler-Shipov, Moscow blitz 2006.

7...Qb6! 8.Nd4 Nfd7 9.Qg4

This is a very provocative manoeuvre. After 9.N2b3 Nxe5 Black wins a pawn;

9...O-O!

Thanks to his lead in development, Black soon obtains a dangerous initiative after preparing an exchange sacrifice.

The attacking machine is turned on, but tactical mistakes are also about to appear.

17.Ng5

17.Nxc6 bxc6 18.Rd2 Bh6 19.Ng5 Kg7! -/+.

17...h5! 18.Ndf3 Bf5?

Too optimistic. 18...Nxf2? was weak in view of 19.Rxd5! Nxh1 20.Qc4! ↑. But 18...Ne7! , defending and attacking at the same time, and intending ...Nf5→Qh4, would have won tactically: 19.Bd3 Bh6! 20.Kb1 Bf5 -+.

19.Rxd5

Suddenly White is back in the game.

19...Nb4 20.Bc4! Kg7??

Another serious mistake. Black should have played 20...Nxd5 , with a complicated position.

≥ 24.Bb3! Nxf2! ( 24...Nxe5? 25.Rxe5 Bf6 26.Rxf5! +-) 25.Qf4 Qa6 26.c4! ∞ - 26.Ngf7 is parried by 26...Qe2! (not 26...Qf1+? 27.Rd1 Qxg2 28.Qh6+ Kf6 29.Nd7+! Bxd7 30.Qf4+ Kg7 31.Rxd7 +-) 27.Qh6+ Kf6 28.Nd7+! Bxd7 29.Qf4+ Kg7 30.Qh6+ Kf6 with a perpetual.

24...Qxf2??

24...Ba3 25.Bxf5 Rxc3+ or 25.bxa3 Rxc3+ both lead to perpetual check; 24...Rd8! -/+.

25.Ne6+ Kh6?

And Black resigned without waiting for 26.Nxg4+ Bxg4 27.Qxf2 , winning the queen.

25...Bxe6 26.Qxe7+ Bf7 27.Qxf7+ Qxf7 28.Nxf7 Kxf7 29.Rd7+ Ke6 30.Rxb7 is not really saving Black either.

1-0

Another sharp and dramatic game by Alexander Morozevich.

Summary of 4.c3 :
This move is not especially useful in these lines. The game is still very balanced. If White plays the advance e4-e5, the arising positions are very similar to those of the Advance Variation with 4.e5 , but the extra move Nd2 is not very appropriate here.
