Chapter 9 - Black Queen Blues: Winawer Variation

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 c5 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Qa5 7.Bd2 Qa4

'Queens never make bargains' — Lewis Carroll, Through the Looking Glass

History

In the 1940s a new concept in the Winawer Variation was devised: the sharp queen move 6...Qa5!? . Soon the idea was developed by several leading players, such as Mikhail Botvinnik and David Bronstein. And later on, in 1956, Black found out that the queen was useful not only for launching a bold attack, but also for establishing a powerful blockade with 7...Qa4!? . And this is the key move of our second handling of the Winawer Variation; see also Game 59, Zsedenyi-Portisch.

The idea of ...Qa5 and then ...Qa4 has survived, and with the years it has come back into use as an exotic variation. Black fights for the initiative and starts a direct counterattack, making use of the great capabilities of the black queen.

Plans

First, let us look at the possibilities and resources offered by the ...Qa5-a4 manoeuvre:

* The black queen attacks the pawn on c3 and prevents White from developing comfortably;

* After the black queen goes to a4, either the squares d4 and c2 will be attacked, or the queenside will be blocked with the advance ...c5-c4. After this White's two bishops will be temporarily out of play;

* Black has the option of castling queenside and counterattacking in the centre and on the kingside;

* Another possible plan is ...b6 and ...Ba6 — the exchange of the light-squared bishops reduces White's initiative and increases the weaknesses in White's pawn structure;

* The classical break ...f6!? will always help Black to achieve counterplay in the centre; a blockade with ...f7-f5 is also possible;

* Another important strategic resource for Black is an attack with his queenside pawns by ...b5/...a5/...b4;

* If the queens are exchanged, Black retains good counterplay on all sides of the board;

* From the start White has two different strategies at his disposal. He may choose closed and slow play, trying to dominate the entire board, or he can look for sharp lines, trying to open up the position right from the very first moves. However, both sides can change their strategy suddenly, at any point, and this is the most interesting and amusing aspect in the games presented.

Here's an example from my own practice with a typical Winawer ending (see the notes to Game 62)..

After checking everything, my opponent played 53.Rg7, thinking that he was playing only for two results; a draw or a win.

However, after 53...Rxg7 ( 53...Ke6? 54.Rxh7! ) 54.hxg7 Bg8 55.Kh6?? Kg4!! , this evaluation changed dramatically. Later, with 66...a1Q, Black's queen was resurrected and he ended up with a full point (Corrales-Moskalenko, Sabadell 2008).

Directions

After 6...Qa5 we have the first critical position. It looks as though White doesn't achieve anything with the gambit 7.Nf3?! because of 7...cxd4! , with sufficient counterplay for Black.

Therefore, if White wants to fight for the advantage he must choose between:

1) a solid game with 7.Qd2 , in which the chances are equal;

2) ambitious play with 7.Bd2! , which is White's main move. But here Black continues with his queen manoeuvre; 7...Qa4!? , blocking the a-pawn and attacking the d4- and c2-squares.

This is the starting position of our study. It is very complicated, and it contains many possibilities for both sides.

White has four natural reactions: two queen moves: A) 8.Qb1 , B) 8.Qg4 , or rapid development with the aim of attacking with C) 8.Nf3 or D) 8.h4 .

In MegaBase there are many games with this line. But here we will analyse some illustrative games which have been played recently, with typical play for each side in every line:

A) 'Anti-blues': 8.Qb1 - the threat of 9.Bb5+ prevents the classical plan with ...b6 and ...Ba6 (Games 60-62).

In this case Black blocks the centre with 8...c4 , forcing the white queen to waste time getting reactivated. The alternative is 8...a6 , which allows the exchange of queens after 9.Qb3!? Qxb3 10.cxb3 , with a comfortable ending for White.

A1) 8...c4 9.Nf3 and now:

A11) 9...f6 , the classic break in the French Defence (Game 60);

A12) 9...Nc6 is a natural reply and a standard plan which prepares queenside castling (Game 61).

A2) 8...c4 9.h4 . Just as in 9.Nh3 , there are two main plans: 9...Nc6/f6; here we find several examples with very complicated play (Game 62).

B) Super blues: 8.Qg4 — this is the most aggressive move in all variations of the French Defence, creating weaknesses in Black's position (Games 63, 64).

The pawn on g7 is under attack and there are two ways of defending it:

B1) 8...g6 is the classical 'Winawer-Nimzowitsch' defence, with a sharp fight between the queens after, for instance, 9.Qd1 b6 10.h4 Ba6 11.Bxa6 Nxa6 12.h5 cxd4 (Game 63);

B2) 8...Kf8 is a modern idea in the French Defence; the king defends the dark squares! After 9.Qd1 b6!? 10.c4/h4/dxc5 White tries to become active in the centre and/or attacks on the kingside (Game 64).

C) 8.Nf3 — White prefers quick development (Games 65-67)

C1) 8...b6!? with the idea ...Ba6, reducing White's initiative. At this point White can either open the game with 9.Qb1 or continue developing with 9.Bd3 (Game 65);

C2) 8...c4 with ideas of blockading and/or attacking on the wings (Game 66)

C3) 8...Nc6 , developing and looking for pressure in the centre (Game 67).

D) 8.h4 - on the attack! This leads to an even more creative game (Game 68).

Black Queen Blues - Games

Let us look at the stem game with the ...Qa5-a4 manoeuvre, which illustrates the character of the ensuing play.

The famous grandmaster Lajos Portisch was the first to play the 'Black Queen Blues'.

GAME 59
Bela Zsedenyi - Lajos Portisch
Budapest Szikra 1956

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 c5 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Qa5!

The black queen begins playing the 'blues'.

7.Bd2!

(A) The critical reply to the gambit 7.Nf3 is 7...cxd4! 8.Bd3 ( 8.Qxd4 Nc6 ↔) 8...Nc6 9.O-O dxc3 10.Rb1 Nge7 with two extra pawns; or else 10...f6 ∞ B.Martin-Dreyer, Otago ch-NZL 1991;

(B) The modest defence 7.Qd2 offers a solid game for both sides: 7...Nc6 8.Nf3 Nge7!? (other interesting plans are 8...f6 9.Rb1 ∞, or 8...b6 9.dxc5 bxc5 10.a4 Ba6 11.Bxa6 Qxa6 12.Ba3 Rc8 ∞ Poulton-Pert, England tt 2001/02) 9.Be2 Bd7 ( 9...b6!? 10.dxc5 Ba6! ↔) 10.a4 Rc8 11.O-O cxd4 12.cxd4 Qxd2 13.Bxd2 Nf5 14.c3 Na5 and Black managed to win this equal ending, Ragozin-Botvinnik, Moscow match 1944.

7...Qa4!?

This is the key move of Black's plan. Black immediately exerts pressure on White's weaknesses on the queenside, limiting the activity of the white pieces. The alternatives in the years 1943-46 were:

(A) In the stem game 7...c4?! was played. This is a typical blockade, but at the moment it is premature: 8.h4 ( 8.Qg4! ↑) 8...Ne7 9.f4?! Nbc6 10.h5 Bd7 ( 10...Nf5! ) 11.Nf3 h6 12.Be2 O-O-O 13.Qc1 Nf5 14.Kf2 Rdf8 ↔ preparing ...f7-f6, with a complex game, De Agustin-Alonso Leira, Madrid 1943;

(B) Also interesting is 7...Ne7!? 8.Bd3 ( 8.c4 Qc7 ↔; 8.Qg4!? ) 8...Nbc6 9.Nf3 c4! 10.Bf1 Bd7 11.g3 f6! ↔ Simagin-Bronstein, Moscow city championship 1946.

8.Qg4!

The white queen's move to g4 is the most aggressive plan in the Winawer Variation. For 8.Qb1 , see Games 60-62 and for 8.h4 , see Game 68. Another old game by the famous black player went: 8.Nf3 (see also Games 65-67) 8...Ne7 9.Qb1 (threatening Bb5+) 9...c4! .

We have one of the key positions of the 'black queen' variation. 10.g3 Nbc6 11.Bg2 Bd7 12.O-O O-O-O 13.Ng5 Rdf8 14.f4 f6! (the customary break, which ensures counterplay in the centre) 15.Nf3 Kb8 16.Qb2 Be8! (atypical manoeuvre with the French bishop) 17.Bh3 Bg6 18.Rac1 Bf5 with excellent possibilities for Black, Dely-Portisch, Balatonfured 1959.

8...g6

The modern defence is 8...Kf8!? , see Game 64.

9.Ra2?!

9.Qd1 is the main line, see Games 63 and 64.
