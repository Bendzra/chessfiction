GAME 63
Mohamad Naser Al Sayed - Bartosz Socko
Port Erin 2006 (3)

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 c5 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Qa5 7.Bd2 Qa4 8.Qg4!?

If the black queen plays the blues, then the white queen plays the super-blues here. Their fight already starts in Round 1: Q→g7, Q→c2.

8...g6

This is how black players defended the kingside in the old days. The disadvantage of this move is that the dark squares are weakened. The modern defence is 8...Kf8 , see the next game.

9.Qd1

A typical and strong manoeuvre.

9...b6!?

This is one of the most useful plans in the 'Black Queen' variation; it prepares ...Ba6. Of course there are always intermediate moves, such as:

(A) 9...Nc6 10.Rb1!? (putting a very specific question to Black: the threat is 11.Bb5! . If 10.Nf3 b6 11.h4 (Bologan-Psakhis, playchess.com 2004) 11...cxd4 12.h5 Nge7 ∞) 10...a6 11.Nf3 b5!? with a very complicated game ahead, Berg-Zhang Pengxiang, Fuegen tt 2006;

(B) 9...cxd4?! 10.Rb1 d3?! (better is 10...a6 11.cxd4 Qxd4 12.Nf3 ↑) 11.Bxd3 Qxa3 12.Nf3 Qc5 13.h4 +/- as in the game Anand-Nikolic, Groningen 1997.

10.h4

Attacking players just love to advance this pawn!

10...Ba6!?

This is a fresh idea of grandmaster Lev Psakhis. Black allows the advance h4-h5. For the moment no one has ever captured 10...cxd4 and we still don't know why.

Another typical line is the normal blockade move 10...h5!? and now:

(A) A solid possibility is 11.Nf3 Ba6 12.Bxa6 Qxa6 13.a4 Nc6 14.Qb1 Qc4 15.dxc5 Nge7! ↔ Colovic-Psakhis, Andorra 2002;

(B) Or White can fight for the initiative with 11.c4 Nc6 12.Nf3 Nge7 13.dxc5 bxc5 14.cxd5 (if 14.Be2 dxc4 15.O-O Nd5 16.Bg5 Ba6 17.Re1 c3!? and Black is in good shape, Carlsen-Agdestein, Moss ch-NOR 2006) 14...exd5 15.Be2 Bg4 16.O-O Bxf3 17.Bxf3 Qxh4 18.c4 Nxe5 19.cxd5 O-O 20.Bc3 Nxf3+ 21.Qxf3 Nf5 - this position is ideal for Black, Karjakin-Zhang Pengxiang, Kemer tt 2007;

(C) 11.Bd3 (simply developing) 11...Ba6 12.dxc5 bxc5 13.Nf3 Nd7 14.O-O Ne7 15.Bg5 Nf5 16.Re1 Bxd3 17.cxd3 Qxd1 18.Rexd1 O-O 19.Rdb1 Rfb8 with an equal ending, Bobras-Akopian, Port Erin 2006.

11.Bxa6 Nxa6

11...Qxa6!? is a different concept: 12.h5 Nc6 13.hxg6 fxg6 14.Qg4 Kd7 15.Nf3 h5!? 16.Qxg6 Nge7 17.Qd3 Qxd3 18.cxd3 cxd4 19.cxd4 Nf5 20.Bc3 Rag8 ‰ Apicella-Jacimovic, Fuegen tt 2006.

12.h5

White continues according to plan. Two other gentlemen played a solid game with 12.Ne2 h5 13.Bg5 Rc8 14.O-O Ne7 15.Bf6 Rf8 16.Qd2 Nb8 ( 16...cxd4!? ) 17.Rfb1 Nd7 18.dxc5 Rxc5 19.Rb4 Qa6 = Chandler-N. Pert, London match 2000.

12...cxd4!

At last someone decides to capture the pawn!

13.hxg6 fxg6 14.Qg4

Round 2 of the queens' confrontation (see the move 8.Qg4 ). In the stem game of 10...Ba6 the black queen had to face a white rook: 14.Rh4 Ne7 15.cxd4 Rc8 ( 15...Nc5!? ) 16.c3 Nc5! 17.Rh3 Qxd1+ 18.Rxd1 Na4 19.Ne2 h5 20.Bc1 Kd7 21.Rdd3 Nf5 and Black won by improving his position little by little, David-Psakhis, Bad Wiessee 1999.

14.cxd4 Qxd4 15.Nf3 is unclear - sorry, no games.

14...Ne7!

In this way Black retains dangerous counterplay

15.cxd4 h5!?

This was not the only possibility, but...

16.Ne2??

Leaving his queen en prise! 16.Qh4!? ∞ or 16.Qxe6 Qxd4 =/+.

16...hxg4

And the sharp duel between the two queens is decided in favour of the 'Black Queen'.

17.Rxh8+ Kd7 18.Rxa8 Nc8!-+

Winning easily.

19.f3 Qc6 20.Rxc8 Qxc8 21.c3 gxf3 22.gxf3 Qh8 23.O-O-O Qf8 24.Rg1 Qxa3+ 25.Kc2 Qa2+ 26.Kd3 Nc7 27.Ke3 a5 28.Rxg6 Qc2 29.Rg7+ Kc8 30.Rg1 a4 31.Ra1 Nb5 32.Nf4 a3 33.Nd3 Kd7 0-1

The sequence 8.Qg4 g6 9.Qd1 b6!? promises a creative game in all lines.

GAME 64
Joost Michielsen - Viktor Moskalenko
Banyoles Open 2006 (3)

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 c5 5.a3 Bxc3+ 6.bxc3 Qa5 7.Bd2 Qa4 8.Qg4 Kf8!?

A modern resource in the French Defence. The idea is to defend g7 with the king without weakening the dark squares, as is the case after 8...g6 . But now Black's plan of castling queenside has become impossible.

8...Ne7?! is very sharp but too risky: 9.Qxg7 Rg8 10.Qxh7 cxd4 11.Nf3 (or, for instance, 11.Ne2 Nbc6 12.f4 Bd7 13.Qd3 Nf5 14.g3 O-O-O ∞ Mitkov-C.Hernandez, Mexico City 2006) 11...Nbc6 12.g3 Bd7 13.Be2 O-O-O 14.O-O? ( 14.Qd3 is better) 14...dxc3 15.Bg5? Qe4! and Black won material, Mata-S.Williams, London 2001.

9.Qd1 b6!?

Preparing ...Ba6.

10.c4!?

The 'Blues Hook' 12...Ba6 after 10.c4 was developed by the number one player from the British Virgin Islands, William Hook, against none other than Bobby Fischer.

Normally with this advance White starts action in the centre. Other moves:

(A) 10.dxc5 bxc5 ( 10...Nd7!? ) 11.Nf3 Ne7 12.Bd3 Ba6 ↔ Kurnosov-Ustinov, Cheliabinsk 2005;

(B) The alternative is the flank attack 10.h4!? (starting another of White's typical plans: ∆ h5, h6, Rh4, Rf4) 10...Ba6 (the stem game saw 10...Ne7 11.h5 h6 12.Rh4 Ba6 13.Bxa6 Nxa6 ( 13...Qxa6!? ) 14.Rf4 Qd7 15.Qf3 Nc6 16.Nh3 Rc8 17.g4 ∞ Fischer-Hook, Siegen Olympiad 1970) 11.Bxa6 ( 11.h5 Bxf1 12.Kxf1 h6 13.Rh4!? Ne7 14.Rf4 Nbc6 15.Nf3 Na5 16.Kg1 Nc4 17.Ne1 Rc8 ↔ Konguvel-Vilela, Balaguer 2006 (6))

and now:

(B1) 11...Qxa6!? (this allows the knight to develop towards the centre: ...Nc6) 12.h5 h6 13.Rh4 Ne7 14.Rf4 Nbc6 and the position looks equal. The attacking and defensive resources balance each other, for example: 15.a4 Rc8 16.a5 cxd4 17.cxd4 Qc4 18.axb6 axb6 19.c3 Ke8 20.Ra4 Qb5 21.Ne2 Na5 22.Bc1 ∞ Castellanos-Vilela, Balaguer 2006 (8);

(B2) The other option is 11...Nxa6 , to activate the a8 rook and play on the c-file, although the a6 knight will be out of play, for instance: 12.h5 h6 13.Rh4 Rc8 14.Rf4 cxd4 15.cxd4 Ne7 ( 15...Rxc2? is a bad move: 16.Bc3! Qb3 17.Rf3 Ne7 and soon 1/2-1/2 in Konguvel-Moskalenko, Badalona 2006. But here 18.Ne2! gives White a clear advantage) 16.Qf3 Ke8 17.c3 Rf8 18.Nh3 Nb8 19.Qg4 Rg8 20.Qf3 1/2-1/2 Cheparinov-Nikolic, Wijk aan Zee II 2005.

10...Ne7

Solid play. 10...Ba6!? .

11.Nf3

11.cxd5 Qxd4!? 12.dxe6 Qxe5+ 13.Be2 Bb7!? 14.Bf4 Qc3+ 15.Bd2 Qe5 16.Bf4 Qc3+ 17.Bd2 1/2-1/2 Grischuk-Shulman, Khanty-Mansiysk 2005.

11...Ba6!

11...Nbc6!? 12.dxc5 dxc4 13.h4?! Ba6! ↔ Konguvel-Sengupta, AtuI ch-IND 2006;

11...dxc4!? 12.dxc5 Bb7 ∞

12.cxd5 Bxf1!

The exchange of the light-squared bishops reduces White's initiative.

13.Kxf1

The intermediate move 13.d6?! is not of much use: 13...Ba6! 14.dxe7+ Kxe7 15.Bg5+ f6! ↔.

13...Nxd5 14.dxc5 bxc5 15.g3

Fianchettoing his king.

15...Nd7

15...Nc6!? .

16.Kg2 h6! 17.h4 Ke7!

Preparing an exit: ...Rh8-d8 and ...Kf8.

18.h5 Rhd8 ↔

The position is balanced, but humans make mistakes.

19.Rh4 Qc6 20.Qe2 Kf8 21.Kh2 Ne7 22.Rg1!?

Preparing a possible attack along the g-file with g4-g5.

22...Qc7!

Whereas Black looks for counterplay in the centre.

23.Bf4 Nf5 24.Rh3 Nb6

24...Nd4!? .

25.Qe4 Nd5 26.g4 Nxf4 27.Qxf4 Nd4 28.Qe4 Qc6!?

The queen exchange will be to Blacks advantage.

29.Qxc6 Nxc6 =/+

It is still necessary to show good technique in the ending.

30...Rab8! -/+.

31.Nxd4 cxd4 32.Rd1 Rac8 33.Rd2 Rc4!? 34.Kg2 d3!? 35.Rhxd3

35.Rg3!? Re4 =/+.

35...Rxg4+ 36.Kf3 Rxd3+ 37.Rxd3 Rg5 38.Ke4 Rxh5 39.Rd8+ Ke7 40.Rb8 Rh4+ 41.Ke3 f5!? 42.Rb7+ Kf8 43.Rxa7 Re4+ 44.Kd3 Rxe5 45.a4 h5 46.a5 h4 47.a6 Ra5 48.c4 Kg8!-+

The h-pawn will arrive first.

49.c5 Rxc5 50.Re7 Ra5 51.Rxe6 h3 52.Rb6 h2 53.Rb1 Rxa6 54.Ke3 Rh6 55.Rh1 Rh3+ 56.f3 Kf7 57.Kf2 Kf6 58.Kg2 Rh8 59.Kg3 g5 60.Kg2 Ra8 61.Kg3 Ra2 62.Kh3 Ke5 63.Kg3 f4+ 64.Kh3 Rf2 0-1

Hooks defence to 8.Qg4 Kf8 will always be a good alternative.

Summary of 8.Qg4 : The early black queen sortie takes the sting out of White's play, since after 8...Kf8 or 8...g6 he must take the threat against c2 into account, and there is nothing better than to ruefully retreat with 9.Qd1 . Black can combine the development of his knight and the attack on the d4 pawn with the strategic plan ...b6/...Ba6.
