Chapter 5 - Morozevich's Resources

Practical possibilities in the classical line 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.e5 Nfd7

Directions/Resources

In the diagram position there are three main directions and some deviations. We will give a brief description of the typical games and strategic ideas for both sides.

* Apparently, Morozevich is delighted to play these positions with either colour.

* We will observe that Morozevich's typical tactical resource in many games is the 'Black Jet' advance ...g7-g5, but he prepared it specially for the 'Roulette' tournament in Monaco.

The hero of the French Defence: the Russian grandmaster Alexander Morozevich (born 1977). He often chooses less popular lines, aiming for a complicated fight.

(A) 5.Nce2

This manoeuvre maintains the possibility of the 'Pawn Wedge' seen in Part Two, Chapter 3. Transpositions lead to similar examples. I think the most interesting way for Black to create counterplay is: 5.Nce2 c5 6.c3 Nc6 7.f4 and now 7...Qb6!? .

And then he continues depending on the circumstances, for example: 8.Nf3 Be7 9.a3 or 8.Nf3 f6!? 9.g3 (Game 37).

(B) 5.f4 c5 6.Nf3 Nc6 7.Be3 a6 8.Qd2 b5

This is a solid, important plan in the Classical Variation: Black plays ...a6 and ...b5, preparing an attack similar to those in the Sicilian Defence, especially when castling takes place on opposite sides.

* One of Black's possible resources is the knight manoeuvre ...Nc5-Ne4!?.

* Common replies for White are: 9.a3/dxc5/Be2/h4 (Game 38).

(C) The popular line 7...cxd4 and the classical deviation 7...Qb6 .

After 7...cxd4 8.Nxd4 Bc5 Black will soon castle and look for counterplay on all sides (Game 39).

Morozevich's Resources - Games

This is a game I played during a private match against Alexander Morozevich. It was an agreement between us and a sponsor, and also a match intended to improve his strength before playing some important tournaments. 'Moro' is a very ambitious and aggressive player, and when playing he does not enjoy just winning the game, he also wants to destroy his opponent's mind! During play he applies a lot of pressure and looks aggressively into his opponent's eyes â€” a bad tendency that arose in the era of Tal, continued in the Fischer-Spassky match and was further developed, in my opinion, by Karpov and Kasparov. I felt very uncomfortable in the Central Chess Club of the Russian capital...

5.Nce2

Similarly to the idea of the 'Pawn Wedge' (see Game 26), White chooses a plan which strengthens his pawn structure in the centre, at the same time preparing an attack on the kingside. However, Black can profit from his lead in development by starting counterattacks all over the board. Here there are possible transpositions to the line with 5.Nce2 , for example: 5.f4 c5 6.Nf3 Nc6 7.Ne2 and now:

(A) 7...Qb6!? . I think that this move order is more interesting and dynamic, since it attacks d4, prevents Be3 and retains certain tactical chances, such as ...Bb4+/...f6/...g5.

It seems as if White has the initiative, but calm down, there are still some resources available to Black.

A very strong reply. We have counterplay! I did not like the check 28...Nc2+ because of 29.Kf2 Nxa1 30.Nxf8 Rc2+ 31.Ke3 Kxf8 32.Rxa1 +/= and the white king is more active.

30...Rf2

30...Rfc8! -+. I saw this move, but I wanted to save time on the clock!

31.Ke1 Rc2 32.Kd1 Rf2

We repeated moves in time-trouble.

33.Ke1 Rc2 34.Kd1

White couldn't claim a draw here, as on move 30 he still had the right to castle!

34...Rfc8!

Finally the winning move.

35.Nc5 Nxc5!

This exchange sacrifice is decisive.

36.Kxc2 Nxd4+ 37.Kd1 Ne4

The two black knights are very strong in the centre of the board.

38.Rg2 Nc3+ 39.Ke1 Nb3

There are no squares available for the rook.

40.Ne3 Nxa1 41.Nf5 Rc7 42.e6 g6! 0-1

My opponent resigned at last and left the room at once...

After 5/7.Nce2 White is behind in development, so in many cases knight sacrifices on e5 or d4 will favour Black. The blockade with ...f5 offers him a solid game.

I think the best move order is first ...Qb6/...f6, etc.
