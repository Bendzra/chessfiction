Chapter 15 - The Semi-Winawer

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5 c5 5.Bd2

5.Bd2 is a problematic move in the Winawer. After this Black has experienced some problems in unbalancing the game and fighting for the initiative in the opening. In this chapter we will look at a surprising bishop retreat: 6...Bc5!. Instead of getting exchanged on d2, on c5 the bishop defends d4 and at the same time controls the dangerous check on d6.

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Bb4 4.e5

The same player used to play a modest line: 4.Qd3?! Ne7!? .

analysis diagram

This development is more flexible than 4...Nf6 and it enables Black to save some tempi.

5.Bd2 O-O 6.a3 Bxc3 7.Bxc3 b6! (ensuring the typical French counterplay) 8.O-O-O Ba6 9.Qe3 Bxf1 10.Rxf1 a5! (the bishop on c3 is now a useless piece) 11.Nf3 Nbc6 12.Bd2 b5 13.Qd3 dxe4 14.Qxe4 Qd5 15.Qxd5 Nxd5 16.Rd1 Rfb8 17.b3 a4! 18.Kb2 f6! =/+ with a comfortable endgame which allowed Black to win easily, Jerez Perez-Moskalenko, Barbera 1999.

4...c5 5.Bd2 cxd4!?

Our line begins with this move. The most usual options are 5...Nh6 , 5...Nc6 and especially 5...Ne7 .

6.Nb5 Bc5!

This is the line we are going to develop. Those who play 5.Bd2 against the Winawer are used to seeing the black bishop exchanged on d2 after 6.Nb5 . After that, White tends to regain his pawn with a comfortable game.

7.Qg4!?

The most aggressive line, much in the Winawer style.

The main alternative is 7.b4 Be7!? (the usual retreat, but 7...Bf8!? 8.Nxd4 Ne7 9.Ngf3 Nbc6 ↔, and 7...Bb6!? 8.Qg4 Kf8! ∞ Ibarra Jerez-Suba, Granada 2007, also have their advantages) 8.Nxd4 Nc6 9.Nxc6 bxc6 10.Qg4 g6 11.Nf3 h5! 12.Qf4 Nh6 ↔ with a playable position.

7...g6

This defence leads to a complicated game. 7...Kf8!? is more solid, for instance: 8.Nf3 ( 8.b4 Bb6!? ∞) 8...Nc6 9.Nbxd4 Qb6!

analysis diagram

10.Bc3 f6!? ( 10...Bxd4!? 11.Bxd4 Qb4+! ↔ is an interesting alternative) 11.Qf4 (there is nothing better) 11...Bxd4! 12.Nxd4 (if 12.Bxd4?! Nxd4 =/+) 12...Nxe5 and Black has an extra pawn in exchange for an insecure position.

8.O-O-O!?

A novelty.

(A) The usual move is 8.b4 Bf8! ( 8...Be7!? ) 9.Nxd4 Bg7!? ↔ B.Muhren-Van den Berg, Hengelo 2000;

(B) In another game I faced 8.Nxd4 Nc6 9.Bc3 Nh6! 10.Qf4 Bxd4 11.Bxd4 Nf5 ↑. The knights on f5 and c6 should guarantee that Black will not have any problems here.

analysis diagram

12.Nf3? (a dubious move, since it allows a tactical sequence. 12.Ne2 was safer) 12...g5! (the white queen is overloaded) 13.Nxg5 ( 13.Qd2? g4 ) 13...Ncxd4 14.O-O-O and the position is complicated, but advantageous to Black, Ayza Ballester-Moskalenko, La Pobla de Lillet 2004. Now, 14...f6! -/+.

8...Nc6 9.Nf3 f6!?

A typical fight for the centre. 9...Qb6 deserves consideration, for instance: 10.Qf4 Bd7 ∞, or else 9...Nge7 with equal chances.

10.Nbxd4!

Starting a virtually forced sequence. If 10.exf6? Nxf6 -/+ Black develops his knight while threatening the queen.

22.Rde1 Kf7 23.Bc5 Ne4 24.Bd4 Rxf1 25.Rxf1+ Ke7 26.a4 c5 27.Be5 h5 28.h3 c4 29.b3 cxb3 30.cxb3 Nc5 31.Rf3 Nd7 32.Bc3 e5 33.Kb2 d4 34.Bd2 Ke6 35.Bg5 e4 36.Rf4 Kd5 37.Rf7 Ne5 38.Rb7 e3 0-1

The move 5.Bd2 is not especially strong (compared with 5.a3 ), but since it prevents the doubling of pawns on c3 it tends to lead to positions with a slight advantage for White without any risk. The statistics are very reasonable for White. However, the black counter-plan 5...cxd4!? 6.Nb5 Bc5! is not very well known and it has given me good practical results.
