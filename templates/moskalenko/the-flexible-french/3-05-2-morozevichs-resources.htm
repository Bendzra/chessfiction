GAME 38
Karsten Rasmussen - Viktor Moskalenko
Estartit Open 2007/08 (5)

This game is typical of the Classical French with 3.Nc3 Nf6. Apart from the opening, we should take note of the good middlegame and also of the classic endgame, which helps the chances for both sides to be understood.

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.e5 Nfd7 5.f4 c5 6.Nf3 Nc6 7.Be3 a6!?

An important idea in this system, preparing the advance ...b7-b5. I discovered this plan in the 1980s.

8.Qd2

The most flexible reply, keeping all options open.

8.Ne2 would be similar to Game 37, aiming to consolidate the d4-square at the cost of delaying queenside development: 8...Qb6 9.Qc1 g5!?

analysis diagram

A well-known resource in the French, but also in accordance with Morozevich's creative style. 10.c3 ( 10.fxg5 cxd4 11.Nexd4 Ncxe5 ↔) 10...cxd4 ( 10...g4!? 11.Nd2 f6 ↔) 11.cxd4 Bb4+ 12.Kf2 f6! ∞ Black radically solves the problems of searching for counterplay and it is difficult for White to find a continuation over the board, Topalov-Morozevich, Morelia/Linares 2007.

The typical break 9...f6! also leads to a fierce struggle for the central squares, for instance: 10.c3 Be7 11.g3 O-O 12.Bh3 cxd4 13.cxd4 fxe5 14.Bxe6+ Kh8 ↑ and the black king finds a really safe square.

8...b5

Now White can choose from several continuations.

9.a3

A matter of fashion. Those who have no time to study, and who do not have an opinion of their own, follow the fashion.

(A) In former times 9.dxc5 used to be the main reply: 9...Bxc5 10.Bxc5 Nxc5 11.Qf2

(or 15...Ke7 16.Rfb1 a3 17.c3 Qxf2+ 18.Kxf2 a5! ↔ Videki-Moskalenko, Balatonbereny 1994) 16.Rae1 ( 16.bxa4 Qxf2+ 17.Kxf2 Rb4 ↔) 16...Qxf2+ 17.Rxf2 O-O!? with an equal game, Perpinya Rofes-Moskalenko, Barbera 2004.

WEAPON: 11...Qe7?! is a weak move: 12.Bd3 b4 13.Ne2 a5 14.O-O Ba6 15.Qe3! ∆ 15...O-O?? (≥ 15...Nxd3 16.cxd3 O-O 17.Ned4 +/=) 16.Bxh7+! (a thematic sacrifice) 16...Kxh7 17.Ng5+ Kg6 18.f5+ and mate in four, Svidler-Renner, Germany Bundesliga B 2001/02.

We now reach a popular position in practice.

9...Qb6

(A) 9...Bb7!? Another positive move.

(A1) After 10.dxc5 Bxc5 11.Bxc5 Nxc5 Morozevich showed in the following game that he favours dynamic factors (rapid development) over static ones (the exchange of the dark-squared bishops): 12.Bd3 b4 ( 12...Qb6!? ) 13.Ne2 (after this voluntary retreat, all the aspects mentioned in the previous comment seem to become valid; 13.axb4 Nxb4 14.Be2 Qb6 ↔) 13...Qb6 14.Qe3?! d4!
