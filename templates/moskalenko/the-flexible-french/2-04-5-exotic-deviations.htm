7.Qe2

White prepares Nb3-Bg5 and 0-0-0. The main continuation 7.O-O Nf6 8.Nb3 will be examined in the next four Games 33-36.

A very solid concept (Black intends ...0-0), but this move is no stronger than:

(A) 9...Qb4+!? Both sides have a lot to say, though: 15.g4 Bb7 16.Rhg1 Nd5!? 17.g5 Nb4 18.Kb1 Bd5 19.a3 Nxd3 20.Qxd3 Rd8!? 21.f4 O-O 22.h4 Qb7! 23.Bf2 b4! → 24.axb4 Bxb4 25.h5 Rb8 and Black arrives first; 26.g6?? fxg6 ( 26...Be1! ) 27.hxg6 Be1! -+, winning a piece, Korneev-Matamoros, Malaga 2001.

10.O-O-O

10...e5?!

A dubious advance. During the game I thought that ...Bg4 was a good defensive resource, but unfortunately it is inadequate.

10...h6! was necessary. This move order offers different possibilities:

(A) 11.Nbxd4? is bad: 11...hxg5 12.Nxe6 Qb8! 13.Nexg5 Qf4+! 14.Kb1 O-O 15.g3 Qc7 16.Bxf7+ Rxf7 17.Qc4 Bf8 and Black gains a decisive advantage, 0-1 Tischbierek-Luther, Bremen ch-GER 1998;

(B) 11.Bh4 Qf4+!? ( 11...e5!? 12.Bg3 Bg4 13.Bb5 O-O 14.Bxc6 Qxc6 15.Bxe5 Rfe8 ∞) 12.Kb1 Qe4 13.Rhe1 Qxe2 14.Rxe2 Bd7 with a typical, quite balanced ending.

11.h3?!

Perhaps my opponent was afraid of the possibility ...Bg4, but this is a passive option.

11.Bb5?! O-O! ;

11.Rhe1! is the critical move: 11...h6 ( 11...Bg4 12.Bb5 +/=) 12.Bh4 Bg4 13.Bb5! Bxf3 14.gxf3 O-O-O 15.Bxc6 Qxc6 16.Qxe5 d3 17.c3!? and it seems that White is better here.

11...O-O

Black can be very satisfied with the opening. In the remainder of this dramatic encounter, both players made many mistakes right up to the end...
