GAME 52
Alexandra Kosteniuk - Stephen Giddins
Port Erin 1999 (6)

Here we have an interesting fight between youth and experience. Giddins tries an alternative on the 11 th move.

1.e4 e6 2.Nf3 d5 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.e5 Nfd7 5.d4 c5 6.dxc5 Nc6 7.Bf4 Bxc5 8.Bd3 f6 9.exf6 Nxf6 10.Qe2 O-O 11.O-O-O Qa5!?

In order to start hostilities on the queenside.

WEAPON: 11...Nh5? is dubious because of the typical sacrifice 12.Bxh7+! Kxh7 13.Ng5+ Kg6 14.Nxe6! with a decisive advantage for White: 14...Bxe6 ( 14...Nxf4 15.Qg4+ +-) 15.Qxe6+ Kh7

analysis diagram

16.Rxd5 ( 16.Qh3!? g6? 17.Rxd5 Qe8 18.g4 Qe6 19.Rxh5+ gxh5 20.Qxh5+ Kg7 21.Qxc5 +- Pablo Marin-Narciso Dublan, Barbera del Valles 2005) 16...Nxf4 17.Qe4+ Kh8 18.Rxd8 Raxd8 19.g3 Rde8 20.Qc4 Ne6 21.f4 Bb6 22.Ne4 Ncd4 23.c3 Nf5 24.Kb1 Ne3 25.Qb5 Nxf4 26.Nd6 Re6 27.Qd7 Ref6 28.gxf4 Rxf4 29.a3 Rg4 30.Re1 Rf3 31.Nf7+ 1-0 Kosteniuk-Stellwagen, Wijk aan Zee 2000.

12.Kb1 Nb4!?

This seems to be a logical move. In the event of 12...Bd7 the game reverts to usual Zakharov lines: 13.Ne5!? Nxe5 14.Bxe5 b5 15.g4!? b4

analysis diagram

16.g5! bxc3 17.gxf6 Rab8 18.b3! ( 18.Bxb8?? Rxb8 -+) 18...Rxf6 ( 18...g6 19.Rhg1 +-; 18...gxf6 19.Qh5 1-0 Stukopin-Stella, Sibenik jr 2007) 19.Bxf6 gxf6 20.Rhg1+ Kf8 21.Qg4 Ke8 22.Qg8+ Bf8 23.Rg7! 1-0 Alagulian-Sambuev, Moscow ch-city 1999.

Back to the game.

13.Be5 Bd7

13...d4? is an unsuccessful counterattack: 14.Nxd4 Nfd5 15.Nb3 ( 15.Bc4!? ) 15...Nxc3+ 16.Bxc3 Qxa2+ 17.Kc1 Rxf2 .

analysis diagram

And now it is White's turn: 18.Qe5 Bf8 19.Bxb4 Bxb4 20.Bxh7+ Kxh7 21.Qe4+ Kg8 22.Qxb4 +- Navarro-Stella, Fiuggi ch-ITA-jr 2007.

14.Nd4 Bxd4 15.Bxd4

This may be the critical position in the 11...Qa5 line.

15...Nc6

WEAPON: The trap 15...e5?! does not work because of 16.Qxe5! (but not 16.Bxe5 Rae8 ↔) 16...Nc6 17.Qf4 Ng4 18.Qg5 Nxd4 19.Bc4!! and White is better in all lines.

The most natural move would be 15...Rac8!? with lots of possibilities for both players.

16.Be5 Nxe5

16...b5!? was a sharper option.

17.Qxe5 Rac8

According to my engine, 17...Ng4 18.Qh5 Nf6 offers better chances of equalising.

18.f3! +/=

White has a classic strategic advantage, as Black's minor pieces are now inactive.

18...Qc7 19.Rhe1 Qxe5 20.Rxe5 Ne8

In the ending that now arises White has a clear advantage, but Kosteniuk failed to do the technical job properly.

21.Ne2

21.Bb5!? Bxb5 22.Nxb5 Rf6 23.Rde1 with pressure for White; 21.Nb5! +/=

21...Nd6 22.f4 Rce8 23.Re3 Bb5 24.Bxb5 Nxb5 25.g3 Kf7 26.b3 h6 27.Re5 Kf6 28.c4 Nc7 29.Nc3 g5! ↔

Changing the character of the game.

30.cxd5 gxf4 31.gxf4 exd5 32.Nxd5+ Nxd5 33.Rexd5 Re2 34.Rd6+ Kf5 35.Rxh6 Rc8! 36.Rf1 Rcc2=

Even though Black is two pawns down, the ending is still a draw. The active position of his rooks on the second rank compensates for the missing material.

37.Rh5+ Kf6 38.Ra5 a6 39.h4 Rh2 40.h5 Rb2+ 41.Ka1 Rbd2 42.b4 Rde2 43.Rb1 Ref2 44.b5 Rxh5 45.Ra4

Not 45.bxa6?? Rxa5 46.axb7 Raxa2 mate.

45...Rxb5 46.Rxb5 axb5 47.Rb4 Ke6 48.Rxb5 Rxf4 49.Rxb7 Kd6 50.Kb2 Kc6 51.Rb8 Kc7 1/2-1/2

Summary: After 11...Qa5 and 12...Nb4 Black was unable to create any support for his active pieces and he soon had to switch to passive defence. In any case, this was a complicated game right up to the last move.

Later, in the year 2004, Spanish youngsters improved their repertoire with this line.
