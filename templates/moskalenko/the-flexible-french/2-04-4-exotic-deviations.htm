GAME 30
Vasilios Kotronias - Ian Nepomniachtchi
Moscow 2007 (8)

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nd2 Be7 4.Bd3

Another popular set-up, which is probably more flexible and more dangerous for Black. The difference with 4.Ngf3 is that the text move allows Qe2 at a favourable moment.

4...c5!

The most dynamic response.

(A) Romanishin's original idea was 4...Nc6 , but this manoeuvre is somewhat slow: 5.Ngf3 (now 5.c3 can be met by 5...dxe4! 6.Bxe4 ( 6.Nxe4 e5! is fine for Black) 6...Nf6 7.Bf3 O-O 8.Nc4 and now interesting is 8...Re8!? 9.Ne2 e5!? 10.Bxc6 bxc6 11.Nxe5 Bd6 12.O-O Ba6 13.f4 Ng4 14.h3 Nxe5 15.fxe5 Bxe5 =) 5...Nb4!? .

Later Morozevich used this resource in a more sensible set-up. See the comments to move 7 in this game. 6.Be2 dxe4 ( 6...c5!? is slightly weird but playable, since it carries with it several structural choices, for instance: 7.exd5 Nxd5 8.Nb3 cxd4 9.O-O Ngf6 10.Nbxd4 O-O 11.c4 Nb4 12.b3 Qc7 13.Nb5 Qb8 14.Bb2 Nc6 15.Nbd4 Bd7 16.Nxc6 Bxc6 = Kotronias-Lputian, Yerevan Zonal 2000) 7.Nxe4 Nf6 8.Nxf6+ Bxf6 9.O-O O-O 10.c3 Nd5 11.Bd3 . Black's position is temporarily passive (Radulov-Romanishin, Leningrad 1977; 8 games in MegaBase). My advice here is to continue 11...Qe7!? (instead of ...b6?!), preparing ...Rd8 and ...c7-c5, with an equal game;

(B) 4...Nf6 is more appropriate in the French, but after 5.e5 ( 5.Ngf3 leads back to 4.Ngf3 ) 5...Nfd7 6.c3!? ( 6.Qg4 g6 7.h4 c5 8.c3 cxd4 9.cxd4 Nc6 10.Ne2 Qb6 11.Nf3?? 1/2-1/2 Abergel-Farago, Scanno 2005, but now 11...Qb4+! and 12...Ndxe5 gains material) we reach the usual position of Chapter 3, with the added move ...Be7 which does not seem useful here: 6...c5 7.Ne2 Nc6 8.O-O must favour White, even though this position is quite new in practice;

(C) 4...dxe4 would follow the same idea as in the 3...dxe4 Rubinstein Variation (or also the Petroff Defence); Black tries to equalise without allowing any weaknesses: 5.Nxe4 Nd7 6.Nf3 Ngf6 7.Qe2 (preparing to castle queenside) 7...c5 8.Nxf6+ Nxf6 9.Be3 O-O 10.dxc5 Nd5 11.O-O-O Qa5 12.Kb1 Nxe3 13.Qxe3 Qxc5 = Navara-Shirov, Prague blitz 2005.

5.dxc5

The logical continuation (after ...Be7) and also the main one. 5.exd5?! is nothing to worry about: 5...Qxd5! 6.Ngf3 is similar to the previous game with 4.Ngf3 , but after 6...cxd4! we arrive in a theoretical variation with the extra moves Bd3 and ...Be7. This should favour Black, since White's normal move, with the bishop on f1, would be Bc4. Now, that would mean the loss of a tempo.

5...Nf6!?

Black prefers to develop. After the solid move 5...Nd7 he has to reckon with 6.b4! (≤ 6.exd5 exd5 7.Nb3 Nxc5 8.Nf3 Nf6 9.O-O O-O 10.Be3 Nce4! ∞ 11.Be2 Bd6 12.Re1 Re8 White has no advantage at all, and he may even be slightly worse, Adams-Lputian, Manila Interzonal 1990) 6...a5 7.Bb2! ( 7.a3? is dubious: 7...axb4 8.Bb2 bxa3! 9.Bxg7 Bf6 10.Bxh8 Bxh8 11.Ra2 Nxc5 is in Black's favour, Parligras-Rocha, Calvia Olympiad 2004) 7...Bf6 8.Bxf6 Qxf6 ( 8...Ngxf6!? 9.a3 Ne5 ‰) 9.Ngf3 axb4 10.exd5 exd5 11.O-O Ne7 12.Nb3 O-O 13.Qd2 Qc3 ( 13...Nc6!? ) 14.Qg5 ( 14.Qxc3!? bxc3 15.Rfe1 Nc6 16.Bb5 Nb4 17.Nfd4 +/=) 14...Nf6 = Wolff-Lputian, Tilburg rapid 1992.

6.Qe2!?

This possibility is the difference between 4.Bd3 and 4.Ngf3 .

6...Nc6

The older but more solid theoretical line here is 6...O-O!? . Black is not in a hurry to take on c5, but he prepares to capture the pawn with his queen's knight: 7.Ngf3 a5!?

(A) The advance 8.e5?! does not seem to disturb the black army: 8...Nfd7 9.h4 h6!? 10.c3 Nxc5 11.Bc2 (Rozentalis-Vallejo Pons, Calvia Olympiad 2004) and now after 11...b6! ↑ White is in trouble, since he faces threats such as ...Ba6!;

(B) 8.a4?! Na6 9.e5 Nd7 10.Nd4 Ndxc5 11.Bxa6 Rxa6 =/+ Navara-Pelletier, Plovdiv Ech-tt 2003;

(C) 8.O-O Na6 9.e5 ( 9.exd5 exd5 10.Re1 Re8 11.Nb3 Nxc5 12.Bb5 Bd7 = Naiditsch-De la Riva Aguado, Pamplona 2004) 9...Nd7 .

This is the key position in the old line and perhaps in the entire 3...Be7 variation.

(C3) 10.c4!? Naxc5 ( 10...Ndxc5 fails to 11.Bb1! d4? (≥ 11...Nb4 ∞) 12.Rd1 d3 13.Qe3 Nb4 14.Ne4 Qc7 15.Nxc5 Bxc5 16.Qe4 Qb6 17.a3 Bxf2+ 18.Kf1 Nc6 19.Bxd3 g6 20.Bh6 f5 21.Qe2 Bc5 22.Bxf8 Bxf8 23.c5 Bxc5 24.Bc4 a4 25.Rd3 Qc7 26.Rad1 Be7 27.Rd6 Nd8 28.Nd4 Bxd6 29.exd6 Qf7 30.d7 Bxd7 31.Nxe6 +- Vachier Lagrave-Baklan, Paris 2004) 11.Bc2 b6!? 12.b3 Bb7 13.Bb2 f5 14.Nd4 Qb8 15.Rad1 Qxe5 16.Qxe5 Nxe5 17.Nxf5 (Vachier Lagrave-Sprenger, Germany Bundesliga 2007/08), and now 17...Bf6! 18.Nd6 Ba6 19.Rfe1 Ned3 20.Bxd3 Nxd3 21.Bxf6 Rxf6 ↑ with a slight initiative for Black.

It is dubious to change the move order with 6...a5 7.Ngf3 Na6?! (it is remarkable that top players sometimes play the opening so carelessly... 7...O-O! is preferable) 8.Bb5+ ( 8.exd5!? ) 8...Bd7 9.Ne5 Nxc5 10.Nxd7 Nfxd7 11.exd5 exd5 12.Qe5 with a comfortable advantage, Gelfand-Aronian, Monaco rapid 2008. Levon Aronian is a great specialist in the Ruy Lopez, but in the French he is still quite a 'green' participant! :-)

7...Nb4!?

Following in the footsteps of Romanishin and Morozevich. After one of his games Morozevich mentioned this move as being a new theoretical course. The alternatives are suffering a crisis:

Summary of 4.Bd3 and Black's counterplay:
This offers richer and more dynamic play than 4.Nf3 . Both sides can vary their opening moves. Objectively, Morozevich's plan with 6...Nc6 , 7...Nb4 is an especially tactical operation. In this line, the possibility of White castling should be constantly reckoned with ( 8.O-O!? — see next game). The classical plan of 6...O-O , 7...a5!? , 8...Na6 and recovering the pawn is much safer.

12.Bh4

The critical moment in this line. Black must find an effective defence.

12...Bd7!?

The most usual and logical move. Other ideas should be carefully analysed:

(A) Black now has the possibility of playing 12...Qb6 , without having to worry about an immediate Bg5-Be3: 13.e5! Ng8 (very striking, but also the only move) and now:

(A1) After 14.Bxe7 Black gets an easy game: 14...Nxe7 15.Nfd4 Bd7 16.Rac1 O-O 17.Qd2 Rfc8 18.Qa5 Qxa5 19.Nxa5 b6 20.Nab3 g5! .

It is clear that White has many problems, the most critical being his pawn on e5: 21.Rce1 Ng6 22.g3 a5 23.Nd2 a4 24.Re3 a3 25.b3 Rc7 26.Kg2 b5 27.b4 Ra4 28.Rb1 f6! (after this fine and unexpected stroke Black is completely winning) 29.exf6 e5 -+ Leko-Morozevich, Sarajevo 1999;

(A2) 14.Bg3! (this is the critical move, with the idea of bringing the bishop to e3 to gain some tempi. Black will have to move his queen and king's bishop to be able to develop his knight. 14...Bd8 proved catastrophic in the following game: 15.Bf4 Qb5?! 16.Rfc1 Ne7 17.Nbd4 Qa4 18.b3 Qd7 19.a4 O-O 20.Bd2 a5 21.Be3 Nc6 22.Nxc6 bxc6 23.Bc5 Re8 24.Rc3 Qb7 25.Rac1 Bd7 26.d4 Be7 27.Bxe7 Rxe7 28.Ne1 Qb4 29.Qe3 Rf8 30.Nd3 Qb6 31.Nf4 Kh7 32.Qd3+ Kh8 33.Nh5 Be8 34.Nf6 g6 35.Qd2 Kg7 36.Qf4 Qd8 37.Qxh6+! and mate in 6 moves, Brodsky-Jenetl, Krasnodar 1999.

After 14...Bd7 15.Bf4 Qa6 16.a4 Rc8 17.Nbd4 Bc5 18.Bd2 Qb6?! ( 18...Bxd4 19.Nxd4 Ne7 with an equal game) 19.Be3 ( 19.a5! +/-) 19...Qd8 20.Rfc1 White's advantage is obvious, Anisimov-Nepomniachtchi, Krasnoyarsk 2007.

(B) 12...g5 is very provocative; after 13.Bg3 things are very unclear. No games have been played in this line, perhaps for this reason;

(C) The natural continuation is 12...O-O 13.e5 Nd7 14.Bxe7! Qxe7 15.Nfd4 ( 15.Rac1!? +/=) 15...a5 16.f4 ( 16.Nb5!? ) 16...a4 ↔ Nguyen Ngoc Truongson-Nepomniachtchi, Moscow 2007;

(D) 12...dxe4!? 13.dxe4 - there are no games with this line;

(E) The most recent game with this line went: 12...a5!? 13.e5! Ng8

14.Bxe7 ( 14.Bg3 h5 15.Bf4 Nh6 16.Nbd4 +/=) 14...Nxe7 15.Rac1 O-O 16.Nbd4 Bd7 17.Qc2 and Black equalised easily after 17...Qb8 18.Qc7 Rd8 19.Qxb8 Raxb8 20.Rc5 b6 21.Rc7 Kf8 22.g3 Ke8 23.Nd2 Rdc8 24.Rfc1 Rxc7 25.Rxc7 Kd8 26.Rc3 g5!? = Renet-Vitiugov, Cappelle la Grande 2008.
