Chapter 13 - Weathering the Storm

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Bg5 Be7 5.e5 Nfd7 6.h4 O-O

In the Alekhine-Chatard Attack (C14), White aims to start a kingside storm with 6.h4 . But in an old game with Ilia Smirin the author managed to prove that Black can provocatively castle 6...O-O and weather this storm with accurate play! This forgotten game and some others in the notes cannot be found in any database, but fortunately they were written down in my old notebook, biding their time.

GAME 72
Ilia Smirin - Viktor Moskalenko
Beijing 1991

1.e4 e6 2.d4 d5 3.Nc3 Nf6 4.Bg5 Be7 5.e5 Nfd7 6.h4!?

An aggressive attack in World Champion Alexander Alekhine's style.

6...O-O!?

Castling is a healthy concept, but in this structure it is very provocative, inviting an immediate storm on the kingside!

7.Bd3

White wants to attack from a comfortable position. The most aggressive option is 7.Qg4 .

(A) Now 7...c5?! does not work because of 8.Bh6 g6 9.h5! →;

(B) But 7...f6!? can be a surprise weapon: 8.Bd3 Rf7!? 9.Nxd5 exd5 10.e6 Bb4+! ∞ Papa-Ulibin, Geneva 2004;

(C) 7...f5 and now:

(C1) 8.Bxe7 Qxe7 9.Qg5!? Qb4 10.O-O-O c5 11.Qe7 Qb6 12.Na4 Qc6 13.Nxc5 Nxc5 14.Qxc5 Qxc5 15.dxc5 Nd7 16.Nf3 Nxc5 = Hebden-M.Gurevich, England tt 1997/98;

(C2) 8.Qg3 c5 9.Bh6 Rf7 10.Nxd5 exd5 (≤ 10...cxd4 11.Nxe7+ Qxe7 12.O-O-O Nc6 13.Nf3 +/= Barrientos-Moskalenko, Badalona 2005) 11.e6 Kh8! 12.Bf4 Rf6 13.exd7 Bxd7 ↔;

(C3) 8.exf6?! Nxf6 9.Qg3 c5 ( 9...Bd6!? ) 10.dxc5 Nbd7 11.O-O-O Nxc5 12.Bd3 b5! 13.Nf3 b4 14.Bxf6 Bxf6 15.Ne2 a5 16.Kb1 Ba6 17.Ned4 Qb6 -/+ Kupreichik-Moskalenko, Pinsk 1986.

7...c5!

With this energetic reply in the centre the battle begins! The blockade 7...f5?! does not work because of the manoeuvre 8.Nh3! , threatening Nh3-f4, with the initiative, Khalifman-Ulibin, Sochi 1989.

8.Qh5!?

Bringing up all the resources against the enemy king.

WEAPON: Now Khalifman's move 8.Nh3?! prepares the thematic trick 9.Bxh7+ , but Black has a very strong defensive resource in 8...g6! (the key move. If 8...f5? 9.Nf4 +/-, or 8...cxd4? 9.Bxe7 Qxe7 10.Bxh7+! Kxh7 11.Qh5+ Kg8 12.Ng5 +-)

analysis diagram

The main defensive resource for Black is based on the exchange sacrifice after 9.Bh6?! cxd4! 10.Bxf8 Bxf8 11.Ne2 Nxe5 12.Nxd4 Qxh4 with a great advantage for Black, Hall-Moskalenko, Cambados 1992.

9.f4 cxd4!

analysis diagram

10.Nb1? (or, for instance, 10.Ne2 Qa5+ ( 10...f6! 11.exf6 Nxf6 -/+) 11.Qd2 Qxd2+ 12.Kxd2 Bb4+ 13.Kd1 f6 14.Bh6 fxe5!? ↔ Relange-M.Gurevich, Belfort 1997) 10...Nc6 11.Nd2 Nc5 ( 11...f6!? ) 12.Nf2 f6! 13.exf6 Bxf6 14.Bh6 Bg7 15.Bxg7 Kxg7 16.h5 g5! 17.h6+ Kh8 18.Qh5 gxf4 19.Nf3 e5! and the position is completely won for Black, Hector-Moskalenko, Copenhagen 1995.

(B) 8.Nf3 is not very useful, since it blocks the d1-h5 diagonal for the white queen: 8...f5 9.Qd2 Nc6 10.Bb5 cxd4 11.Nxd4 Ndxe5 =/+ winning a pawn. Dubois-Moskalenko, Val Thorens 1990. Also, 8...cxd4! is now possible, with an advantage for Black, since 9.Bxe7?! Qxe7 10.Bxh7+? loses to 10...Kxh7 11.Ng5+ Kh6! and there is no attack.

8...g6

Black builds a fortress. 8...h6? 9.Bxh6! .

9.Qh6

This was the key position of my studies in the 1990s, when I did not have a computer and I had to analyse at home over the board!

There is no way back: 9.Bxe7?! Qxe7 10.Qg5 Qxg5 11.hxg5 cxd4 =/+.

9...Nc6!

This is the continuation which allows Black to keep on fighting. If 9...cxd4!? White has 10.Nf3!? Nxe5 □ ( 10...dxc3? 11.h5! cxb2 12.Rb1 Bb4+ 13.Kd1 f6 14.hxg6 and White wins) 11.Nxe5 dxc3 12.Nxg6 (analysis will show if other moves are better) 12...fxg6 13.Bxg6 hxg6 14.Qxg6+ Kh8 and there is nothing better than a draw.

10.f4

10.Bxe7 must be analysed: 10...Qxe7 11.h5 cxd4 ( 11...Nb4 12.Nf3 Nxd3+ 13.cxd3 cxd4 14.Ne2 g5 15.Rh3 f6 16.exf6 Qxf6 17.Qxg5+ Qxg5 18.Nxg5 e5 19.Ne6 Rf6 20.Nc7 Rb8 21.Nxd5 ) 12.hxg6 fxg6 13.Bxg6 hxg6 14.Qh8+ Kf7 15.Rh7+ Ke8 16.Rxe7+ Nxe7 -/+ Bezemer-Wuestefeld, Wijkaan Zee 2005.

10...Nxd4!

The only defence (discovered in my old analysis). However, in several recent games Black did not find the right path:

10...Re8? 11.Nf3 ( 11.dxc5 Bf8?! 12.Qxf8+ Rxf8 13.Bxd8 +/= Iordachescu-Tadic, Zlatibor tt 2007) 11...Nxd4 12.Nxd4 cxd4 13.Bxe7 Qxe7 ( 13...Rxe7! and Black is OK) 14.Nb5 Nc5 15.O-O-O ↑ B.Muhren-Middelveld, Hilversum ch-NLD 2008;

WEAPON: 10...cxd4??

analysis diagram

Unfortunately, this loses due to mating threats: 11.Nf3!! dxc3 ( 11...Ndxe5 12.fxe5 dxc3 13.h5! cxb2 14.Rb1 , winning) 12.h5 cxb2 13.Rb1 ↑ and there is no defence according to my analysis back in the 1990s, and confirmed by Fritz 11.

11.Nf3

(A) 11.O-O-O!? might be more interesting for White, and here several options should be checked:

(A1) 11...f5 12.Nf3 Nxf3 13.gxf3 (Bellon Lopez-Gleizerov, Stockholm 2004/05) 13...c4!? ∞;

(A2) 11...Re8!? (a defensive resource, threatening 12...Bf8 ) 12.Bxe7!? ( 12.h5 Bf8! ) 12...Rxe7!? ( 12...Qxe7 13.h5 Nf8 14.hxg6 fxg6 15.Nh3 ∞) 13.h5 Qf8 14.hxg6 ( 14.Qg5!? f6! 15.exf6 Qxf6 ) 14...fxg6 15.Qh4 b5! ↔.

analysis diagram

(B) On 11.h5 the critical continuation is 11...Bxg5! ( 11...Nf5!? 12.Bxf5 gxf5 13.Bxe7 Qxe7 14.O-O-O f6 ↔) 12.fxg5 Qe7! , when Black seems to maintain his fortress, for instance: 13.hxg6 fxg6 14.Bxg6 hxg6 15.Qh8+ Kf7 16.Rh7+ Ke8 17.Rxe7+ Kxe7 18.Qh7+ Rf7 19.Qxg6 Nxe5 -/+, favouring Black.

11...Nxf3+ 12.gxf3 f5

12...Re8!? with the same idea 13...Bf8! .

13.h5 Bxg5 14.fxg5 Nxe5

14...Qe8!? .

15.hxg6 Qe7 16.gxh7+ Kh8 17.O-O-O Qg7

Now Smirin sacrifices two pieces, trying to put an end to the game.

18.Bxf5?

≥ 18.Qh5 ∞.

18...Qxh6 19.Rxh6 Rxf5 20.Nxd5 exd5 21.Rxd5 Nf7

21...Nc6 22.g6 Be6 -+.

22.Rxf5 Bxf5 23.Rf6 Nd6 24.Rxd6 Rf8 25.Kd2 Kxh7 26.c3 Kg7 27.b4 cxb4 28.cxb4 Re8 29.a4 Re7 1/2-1/2

The position is technically winning for Black, but the old time control did not allow me to finish the game properly

Summary of the 'storm' variation 6.h4 O-O!? in the Alekhine-Chatard-Smirin Attack: this ensures a hard fight for the initiative with bags of adrenalin and lots of enjoyable tactics. All this thanks to my old notebook, where all the details of this battle were preserved.
