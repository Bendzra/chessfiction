Chapter Three: KIA Versus the Caro-Kann

In this chapter we'll investigate the King's Indian against the Caro-Kann, beginning with the moves 1.e4 c6 2.d3 .

Though as we shall see, the typical black centre structure with pawns on c6, d5, and e5 can arise via other opening systems, including those after 1.Nf3 . In a game given here, Nakamura actually began with 1.g3 . Therefore, even if you play 1.Nf3 or have a different opening line prepared against the Caro-Kann, it would be a good idea to study the games in this chapter.

Black's choice of pawn centres

After 1.e4 c6 2.d3 Black virtually always puts a pawn on d5 or e5 at some point - often he builds a full pawn centre with both these moves. Thus in Game 14 we see the sequence 1.e4 c6 2.d3 d5 3.Nd2 e5 4.Ngf3 Bd6 5.g3 Nf6 6.Bg2 O-O 7.O-O Bg4 .

Black has borrowed an idea from the Slav ...Bg4 System of Chapter Six. His position is a tough nut to crack. His king is safe, his development is smooth, and his pawns have more than their fair share of the centre. Nonetheless, White maintains some minuscule advantages. His bishop is more comfortable on g2 than Black's bishop on g4, which might find itself shut out of the action. White has the f4-square securely defended, whilst the f5-square could be a base for his knight. Black's pawns do, indeed, have more than their share of the centre, but the pawns on both d5 and e5 are potential targets. It's easier for White to arrange an attack on them than it is for Black to organise counterplay against the e4- or d3-pawns.

Alternatively Black can combine building a big centre with the fianchetto of his bishop on g7 with the moves 1.e4 c6 2.d3 d5 3.Nd2 g6 4.g3 Bg7 5.Bg2 e5 6.Ngf3 Ne7 7.O-O O-O .

Black suffers somewhat from the fact that to keep e5 defended the knight has had to go to the inferior e7-square rather than f6. As in Game 14 the black centre is a target, and after 8.Re1 d4 White can besiege the d4 point with either 9.Nc4 and 10.c3 (Game 15) or the immediate 9.c3 (Game 16).

A third option for Black is to renounce the chance to build a full centre and settle for a solid 'Slav' triangle of pawns with 1.e4 c6 2.d3 d5 3.Nd2 Nf6 4.Ngf3 Bg4 5.h3 Bxf3 6.Qxf3 e6 .

In this sequence Black put his bishop on g4 to avoid having it shut in after ...e7-e6. Its subsequent exchange has given White the fabled advantage of the two bishops, though he will have to work hard to make them count for anything. Still, White can be pleased that he has the chance to expand with moves like f2-f4 and e4-e5 in the future, a strategy that came out tops in Game 17.

Alternatively, Black can choose a 'Philidor' centre with the moves ...e7-e5 and ...d7-d6: for example, 1.e4 c6 2.d3 e5 3.Nf3 Nf6 4.Nbd2 d6 5.g3 g6 6.Bg2 Bg7 7.O-O O-O 8.c3 .

This structure might also arise if Black plays a King's Indian Defence (KID) versus the KIA: for example, see Game 41 in Chapter Eight. Here Black is already committed to ...c7-c6, while in the KID version he might prefer ...Nc6. White has a small edge as he is moving first in a symmetrical position. It is OK for Black, but perhaps the Philidor pawn centre is a little too passive for modern taste. Tiviakov outplays his opponent in exemplary style in Game 18.

In Game 19 I've gathered together three rather sharp and offbeat approaches for Black built around 1.e4 c6 2.d3 d5 3.Nd2 e5 4.Ngf3 Bd6 5.g3 f5!? .

White has played slowly, blocking in both the bishops on f1 and c1, and spending a tempo on the leisurely g2-g3. Therefore Black feels justified in trying to overrun the centre with his pawns. This is a tricky idea for White to meet. Still, it would be strange if he was overwhelmed, as Black only has one piece developed himself.

Two other lines examined in the notes to Game 19 are the alternatives 3...dxe4 and 4...Nd7 in the above sequence.

The chapter ends with a splurge of analysis that must look rather unappealing to someone who plays the KIA looking for a quiet life on the theoretical front. Well, needs must and you'll find a critical line after 5...f5 .

Part One: Black plays ...Bd6 and builds a big pawn centre

Game 14
Nakamura,H - Navara,D [A07]
Spanish Team Championship, 2010

1.e4 c6 2.d3

White doesn't try for a space advantage. Instead he supports the e4 point in anticipation of Black's next move. The game will be decided by the quality of manoeuvring in the middlegame, not by any immediate attack.

2...d5

The most natural move as it utilizes the c6-pawn to support a gain of space in the centre.

3.Nd2

White hurries to defend the e4-pawn again as the queen exchange after 3.Nf3 dxe4 4.dxe4 Qxd1+ 5.Kxd1 gives little hope for advantage.

3...e5

Premature is 3...dxe4 – see the analysis to Game 19.

4.Ngf3 Bd6

More accurate than 4...Nd7 which, as also discussed in Game 19, gives White the chance for the initiative with 5.d4! .

5.g3

The point of White's set-up isn't to grab pawns.

White shouldn't go hunting the e5-pawn with 5.Qe2 as 5...Nf6! is a strong riposte. For example, 6.exd5 cxd5 7.Nxe5 O-O! with a horrible pin on the white knight, queen and king looming along the e-file after ...Re8. ( Even worse for White would be 6.d4 exd4 7.e5 O-O! 8.exd6 Re8 9.Ne5 Bg4! 10.f3 Bh5 when there is no defence against ...Nbd7 as 11.g4 Nbd7 12.f4 Nxg4 leads to a complete collapse ) .

However, 5.d4 is a perfectly decent way to play it as White. Then 5...dxe4 6.Nxe4 is bad for Black, so he has to enter an isolated queen's pawn (IQP) position with 5...exd4 6.exd5 Nf6 ( another IQP centre arises after 6...cxd5 7.Nxd4 Nc6 8.N2f3 Nf6 9.Be2 O-O 10.O-O , etc ) 7.dxc6 Nxc6 8.Be2 O-O 9.O-O Re8 10.Nb3 , as in N.Chadaev-V.Bologan, Astana (blitz) 2012. Of course, here we are concentrating on KIA-style responses, but it's worth knowing about this option.

5...Nf6

Here the sharp 5...f5 is the main subject of Game 19.

6.Bg2 O-O

Both players complete their development, ignoring each other for the moment.

7.O-O Bg4 8.h3 Bh5

Flank openings are rich in transpositions.

The present game actually began with the move order 1.g3 d5 2.Bg2 e5 3.d3 Nf6 4.Nf3 Bd6 5.O-O O-O 6.Nbd2 c6 7.e4 Bg4 8.h3 Bh5 . You might like to reread the comments above relating to this position. Play has transposed to a pawn structure reached in the Slav ...Bg4 System, which we'll encounter in Chapter Six. Here Black is somewhat more flexible as he isn't committed to playing ...Nbd7.

9.Qe1

QUESTION: What is the purpose of this move?

ANSWER: Nakamura breaks the pin on f3, thereby clearing the way for his next move. He also bolsters the e4-pawn, which allows the knight on d2 more freedom of action, and, finally, Black is forced to respond to a possible threat of 10.exd5 and 11.Nxe5 .

9...Re8

Navara bolsters the e5 point with the rook rather than with 9...Nbd7 as he has a more active move with his knight in mind.

10.Nh4 Na6 11.a3

EXERCISE: Why do you think White plays this feeble-looking pawn move?

ANSWER: Nakamura has to stop 11...Nb4 with the threat of 12...Nxc2 , not only winning a pawn, but also trapping the queen.

If instead he plays 11.c3 , he weakens his defence of d3; 11...Nc5 would then contain the awkward threat of 12...Nxd3 .

11...Nc5 12.b3

Finally White attends to the development of his queen's bishop. He intends to have one bishop aiming at the d5-pawn, the other at e5.

12...dxe4

If you ask David Navara why he played 12...dxe4 , he would probably just shrug and say that in his judgement it felt right to stabilize the centre so that his pieces have more freedom of action.

If we are looking for a concrete reason, we might mention that White is planning Bb2 and then Nf5. The bishop on d6 would like to retreat to f8 in response, but then the e5-pawn could be a target after e4xd5. Note also that the Czech Grandmaster plans ...Ne6. This contains ideas of ...Nd4 and is also a deterrence against White advancing f2-f4. The ...Ne6 move would, however, be problematical if White had the option of e4xd5 in conjunction with the moves Nf5 and Bb2.

13.dxe4

QUESTION: If it were White's turn to move now, can you suggest a good positional idea for him?

ANSWER: Nakamura would jump at the chance to play 14.Nc4 , activating the knight with the positional threat of 15.Nxd6 . Even if the knight was kicked back from c4 by a subsequent ...b7-b5 after Black has evaded the Nxd6 exchange, it could return to e3, a better square for the knight than d2 as it is further up the board and in contact with the f5-square.

13...b5 14.Bb2 Ne6

Black could also wait with 14...Qc7 , when after 15.Nf5 Bf8 White can play sharply with 16.f4 .

QUESTION: How to assess this position?

ANSWER: This pawn stab is a fundamental idea, suggested by both the pawn structure and more specifically White's deployment of the bishop to b2. On the other hand, it happens in the full fury of the black pieces. After 16...exf4 17.gxf4 the e4-pawn is attacked by both black knights and the rook on e8. It is a fine pawn and mobile, but if it collapses it's all over for White. After the cold-blooded 17...Rad8 , adding more dynamism to the black pieces, the exchange 18.Bxf6 gxf6 doesn't achieve anything for White. Then 19.Ng3 Bg6 20.f5 , trapping the bishop, fails to 20...Bd6! 21.fxg6 Bxg3 22.gxh7+ Kh8 ( or 21.Rf3 Bxg3 22.Rxg3 Rxd2! , overloading the white queen who has to defend g3 ) .

Nonetheless, the e4-pawn lives despite all the pressure. White can continue after 17...Rad8 with 18.e5 , for example. The position remains double-edged, but you can understand why human players at least would like to avoid this as Black. I think most (human) players would wilt in the face of ideas such as Bxf6 or the apparent trapping of their bishop, or indeed the advance 18.e5 . A computer likes Black's activity, but then they aren't programmed to feel fear.

Navara's knight move is both active minded – it aims for an invasion on d4 – and defensive minded – it prevents the f2-f4 advance.

15.Ndf3

It is White's turn to spoil the dreams of a black knight by preventing 15...Nd4 . At the same time e5 is attacked.

15...Bxf3

Black concedes the bishop-pair, but on the other hand, he is well entrenched on the dark squares.

16.Nxf3 Qc7

The idea of arranging f2-f4 is pretty much dead as Black has four pieces trained on the f4 point and besides White's knight is needed on f3 to watch over the d4-square. Nakamura therefore focuses on the queenside and the open d-file. Once lines open up in the distant future White's light-squared bishop might become a powerful piece.

17.Qe2 a6 18.Rfd1 c5

Black gains space on the queenside.

EXERCISE: The move 19...Nd4 is on the cards, when Black's knight finally gets to utilize the d4-square. What is the best way to anticipate it?
(a) Prevention with 19.c3 .
(b) Preparation of c2-c3 with Rac1.
(c) The immediate attack on b5 with 19.a4 .

19.c3

ANSWER: Nakamura chooses to keep the knight put of d4. At the same time he vacates the c2-square as a stopping off post for his knight in a manoeuvre to d5. Nevertheless, the drawback is obvious: the bishop on b2 is shut in and the pawn on b3 is left loose. As we shall see below, these factors could have been exploited by Black.

A better idea is 19.Rac1 which deters Black from playing 19...Nd4 as 20.Nxd4 cxd4 21.c3! opens up the c-file and d-file to the joy of White's rooks and the distress of the black queen and bishop. ( After the alternative recapture 20...exd4 , 21.c3! is still strong – White's bishop on b2 will have an open diagonal aiming at f6, and White's f2-pawn can become mobile with f2-f4 now that the barrier on e5 is removed ). Therefore after 19.Rac1 Black should avoid 19...Nd4 , after which it's not that exciting for White.

Finally, that brings us to 19.a4 . If then 19...b4 the d4-square is secured for the knight, but the black queenside pawns are mouldy, especially as he doesn't have a light-squared bishop to defend the hole on c4. White could reply 20.Qc4 when the queen is nicely placed – an attempt to evict her with 20...Nd7 , intending 21...Nb6 , could be met with 21.a5! .

The key variation after 19.a4 is 19...Nd4 20.Nxd4 cxd4 21.axb5 axb5 22.Rxa8 Rxa8 .

EXERCISE: It appears that White has achieved nothing, but a combination appears in this seemingly tactically dead position. Can you see it?

ANSWER: Of course, not 23.Qxb5? Qxc2 when White can resign, ( but he can play 23.Bxd4! winning a pawn, for if 23...exd4 24.e5 (uncovering an attack on a8), the pin with 24...Re8 fails to 25.exd6 attacking the black queen, when 25...Qxd6 26.Qxb5 is winning for White ) .

Therefore the best move was 19.a4 , which is both attacking and deals with the threat of 19...Nd4 .

19...Qc6 20.Ne1

20...Bc7

QUESTION: Do you think Black should have tried 20...h5 to exploit the white knight's absence from f3?

ANSWER: With the text Black plays too passively in the face of White's plan of Nc2, Ne3, and Nd5. It seems that Navara was so wrapped up in his plan of controlling the dark squares that he missed some good light-square moves. As the knight has left f3, 20...h5!? would threaten to undermine White's pawns with 21...h4 . After 21.h4 Black can push on the queenside with 21...c4! shutting in the white bishop on b2. White's pawns would have lost a lot of their dynamism on both wings. If Black didn't like 20...h5 , he should at least have played 20...c4 to frustrate White on the queenside.

21.Nc2 Ng5

Here was the last chance for 21...c4 , though now White has the option of 22.Nb4!? .

22.h4 Ne6 23.Ne3 Nf8

If 23...Nxe4? 24.Qc2 wins the knight.

24.c4! Rab8 25.Rac1

EXERCISE: In what ways has White improved his position over the last few moves?

ANSWER: Whilst Black has been floundering with his pieces, Nakamura has made great strides forward. He has brought his knight into contact with the key d5-square; his bishop on b2 has been reactivated by c3-c4; and his rook on c1 is making life uncomfortable for the pawn on c5 and the queen hiding behind it. It's no wonder that Black's position quickly fragments on the queenside.

25...Ne6 26.Nd5 Nd4

An attempt at counterplay before b2-b4 shatters the queenside.

27.Bxd4 exd4 28.b4! cxb4 29.Nxb4

Even stronger than 29.axb4 as the knight causes havoc on c6.

29...Qd6

After 29...Qb6 30.cxb5 axb5 31.Nc6 Ra8 the pawn stab 32.e5! not only hits f6, but also uncovers a defence of the knight on c6 by the bishop on g2. Then after both 32...Nd7 33.Nxd4 ( or 32...Ra6 33.Rb1 and then 34.Rxb5 White is winning ) .

30.cxb5 axb5 31.Nc6 Rbc8

32.Nxd4

As in the previous note, 32.e5 is also a killer move: for example, af 32...Qxa3 33.Nxd4 with the threats of 34.Bb7 or 34.Nxb5 wins, as 33...Rxe5 34.Qxe5! Bxe5 35.Rxc8+ is decisive.

32...Qxa3 33.Bh3!

Now 34.Bxc8 will win the exchange for nothing, so Black decided to go out in a blaze of glory:

33...Rxe4 34.Qc2 Bxg3 35.Qxc8+ Re8 36.Qf5 Bxh4 37.Qf3 Qb4 38.Qf4 1-0

Part Two: Black builds a big pawn centre with ...Bg7

Game 15
Movsesian,S - Panarin,M [B10]
Russian Team Championship, 2010

1.e4 c6 2.d3 d5 3.Nd2 g6

Black finds a different way to support his pawn centre with the bishop.

4.g3 Bg7 5.Bg2 e5 6.Ngf3 Ne7

QUESTION: Are there any drawbacks to Black's opening system?

ANSWER: You can't have everything you want in an opening, especially if you are playing Black against an alert opponent. In a perfect world Black would build a pawn centre with ...d7-d5 and ...e7-e5, get to fianchetto his bishop with ...g7-g6 and ...Bg7, and play ...Nf6 and ...Nbd7. However, one of these moves always proves impossible due to White's pressure or lack of time. Here, for example, the black knight goes to e7, so that the bishop on g7 keeps e5 defended, but this means that the 'ideal' move ...Nf6 won't be part of his deployment.

We might also add that fianchetto on g7 has removed the bishop from contact with the queenside. Therefore an attack on a black pawn on d4 with c2-c3, which results in the opening of the c-file, has more power. This is especially seen in Game 16, below, in which White's dark-squared bishop proves of far more worth than the bishop on g7 because it has influence over the queenside struggle.

QUESTION: Couldn't Black try 6...Nd7 7.O-O Ngf6 when he has his knight on the 'best' square f6?

ANSWER: Well, White can respond 8.Re1 , when we should investigate what happens if Black ignores the latent threat to e5 with 8...O-O 9.exd5 cxd5 10.Nxe5 . Every time this has been tried Black has recaptured with 9...Nxd5 , when 10.Nc4 and moves like 11.a4 , to stabilize the knight on c4 against a ...b7-b5 lunge, and 12.h4 , intending h4-h5, gives White good play.

QUESTION: But what, indeed, happens if 9...cxd5 , keeping the centre intact, when 10.Nxe5 Nxe5 11.Rxe5 Ne4 traps the white rook?

ANSWER: It is true the rook is trapped, but there is a strong exchange sacrifice with 12.Rxd5! Qxd5 13.Nxe4 Qd8 14.c3 Bf5 15.Bf4 . White has a knight and two pawns for the exchange and the chance to pressure the black queenside with Nc5 or Nd6. He can also advance the passed d-pawn in the future.

Instead 11...Bg4 12.Bf3 Qd7 gives Black some play for the pawn, ( but no longer 12...Ne4? as 13.dxe4 Bxf3 14.Nxf3 just costs material )

We should now return to the main game, where Black chose 6...Ne7 .

7.O-O O-O 8.Re1

First of all White provokes Black into advancing his d-pawn with the threat of 9.exd5 followed by 10.Nxe5 .

8...d4

It's all about the bishops.

If 8...Nd7 or 8...f6 a bishop is shut in on c8 or g7 respectively. Meanwhile 8...Bg4 9.h3 forces Black to give up the bishop-pair. Black could have stood his ground with 8...Qc7 , though. In that case a methodical build-up on the queenside followed by a break in the centre gave White the edge after 9.c3 a5 10.b3 b6 11.Qc2 Rd8 12.Bb2 Ba6 13.d4 in L.Oll-P.Wells, Antwerp 1996.

9.Nc4

The knight makes use of the c4-square to renew the attack on e5.

Also possible is the immediate 9.c3 as in Game 16.

9...Qc7

EXERCISE: What would you do after 9...b5 with the knight on c4?
(a) Take the pawn on e5.
(b) Go back to d2.
(c) Retreat to a3.

ANSWER: Black has a neat tactical possibility after 9...b5!? as 10.Ncxe5? f6 traps the knight. White could retreat the knight to a3 in the style of the game. On the other hand, Black hasn't wasted a move by defending the e5-pawn with Qc7.

He could use this tempo to strengthen his queenside pawns: for example, with 10.Na3 a6!? 11.c3 c5 12.Nc2 Nbc6 .

So after 9...b5 it looks better for White to change track with 10.Ncd2! and then continue a2-a4, Nb3, Bd2, etc, when the black queenside pawns look shaky. The white knight also spies a hole on c5. So I would give option 'b' preference. It takes advantage of the fact that pieces can go backwards, whereas pawns can't.

10.c3

Having cajoled the pawn to d4, Movsesian begins to undermine it.

10...b5

QUESTION: How should White continue if Black plays 10...c5 standing his ground in the centre?

ANSWER: If Black bolsters d4 with 10...c5 then 11.cxd4 cxd4 12.Bd2 Nbc6 13.Rc1 ( or 13.a4 give White pressure on the queenside. He will try to advance b4-b5. )

11.Na3 dxc3 12.bxc3 Rd8

Panarin plays in the style of the Grünfeld. He hopes that his piece activity will restrain White from conquering the centre with d3-d4, but Movsesian will cut across his plans with a fine exchange sacrifice.

13.Qe2 Na6 14.d4 Bg4

EXERCISE: How do you assess the position after the pawn grab 14...exd4 15.cxd4 Bxd4 16.Nxd4 Rxd4 and what would be White's best continuation?

ANSWER: This line would be suicidal for Black as the dark squares on his kingside become indefensible. White would surely win after 17.Bb2 , but the most convincing way is actually to fianchetto the queen: 17.Qb2! Rd8 ( if 17...c5 18.Nxb5 ) 18.Bh6 with bedlam on g7.

15.Nc2 exd4 16.cxd4 c5

Black has done everything he can to put pressure on the white centre, but it springs forwards after White's next two moves:

17.Bf4! Qd7 18.d5!

18...c4

EXERCISE: After 18...Bxa1 19.Rxa1 in your judgement how much compensation does White have for the exchange?

ANSWER: Black would perish sooner or later on the dark squares after 18...Bxa1 19.Rxa1 , especially as his knights aren't able to defend the dark squares in the centre. There would already be the idea of 20.Be5 , 21.Qe3 and 22.Qh6 to mate on g7. After 19...f6 , White could build up towards a centre breakthrough with e4-e5 with 20.e5 or put pressure on the queenside with 20.a4!? .

In the game keeping the bishop doesn't do him much good either as White's pawns roll forwards.

19.Qe3 Bxf3 20.Bxf3 b4 21.Rab1 Qa4 22.Nd4 Nc5 23.d6 Nd3

EXERCISE: Should White take the knight with 24.dxe7 , sacrifice the exchange with 24.e5 , or save his rook with 24.Red1 ?

ANSWER:

24.e5!

The solid 24.Red1 gives White good winning chances, though Black can fight on with 24...Bxd4 25.Qxd4 Nc6 26.Qxc4 Nce5 . Instead, Movsesian insists on sacrificing an exchange in this game, and rightly so. He shuts out the bishop on g7 whilst activating his own light-squared bishop. The black knight on e7 is now really hanging, whereas Black would suddenly be winning after the hasty 24.dxe7? Bxd4! 25.exd8Q+ Rxd8 26.Qe2 Bxf2+ .

24...Nxe1 25.Rxe1 Nf5

There's nothing to be done. Black avoids material loss, but will obliterated by the advance of White's centre pawns.

26.Nxf5 gxf5 27.Bxa8 Rxa8 28.e6 Qe8

The game soon ends after 28...fxe6 29.Qxe6+ Kh8 30.d7 , threatening 31.Qe8+ winning a rook.

29.d7 Qxe6 30.Qd2

Black can't stop the pawn queening and save his own queen.

Game 16
Andreikin,D - Lintchevski,D [B10]
Dagomys, 2010

1.e4 c6 2.d3 d5 3.Nd2 g6 4.g3 Bg7 5.Bg2 e5 6.Ngf3 Ne7 7.O-O O-O 8.Re1 d4 9.c3!?

In contrast to Game 15, White attacks d4 at once.

QUESTION: But can't Black play 9.c3 dxc3 10.bxc3 Qxd3 , winning a pawn?

ANSWER: After 9...dxc3 10.bxc3 Qxd3 White has a strong initiative with 11.Ba3! when there are three possibilities:

(a) 11...Qd8 12.Nc4! planning to invade on d6.

(b) 11...Re8 12.Qb3 with ideas of either 13.Ng5 , hitting f7, or 13.Bf1 followed by 14.Nc4 and 15.Nd6 .

(c) 11...c5 , handing back the pawn, is best for Black, though White is on top after 12.Bxc5 Nbc6 13.Qa4 .

9...c5

Black declines to capture on c3, preferring to strengthen his hold on d4.

10.cxd4 cxd4 11.b4 b5?

Evidently the aim is to stop White expanding further on the queenside with moves like a2-a4, b4-b5 and Ba3. The pawn move also keeps the white knight out of c4. But Black is breaking two important principles.

QUESTION: What are those principles?

ANSWER: Firstly, you shouldn't gratuitously move pawns on the side of the board where you are weakest. And, secondly, if you try to prevent a breakthrough that is going to happen anyway, your efforts to delay it normally only make matters worse.

12.a4!

Exactly, Now Black isn't able to maintain the queenside blockade.

12...bxa4 13.Nc4 Nd7 14.Qxa4 Nb6 15.Nxb6 Qxb6 16.Nd2!

White's queenside attack is flowing smoothly as the other knight joins in the battle.

16...Qe6

The queen runs away from being attacked by 17.Nc4 , but in fact there are even more attractive squares for the white knight on the queenside.

17.Nb3!

An outpost on c5 will be more valuable to the horse than one on c4 as it is deeper in the enemy's camp.

17...Qb6

Black tries again with his queen on b6.

18.Bd2!

EXERCISE: Why is this a good quiet move of the kind White is always looking for in the King's Indian Attack?

ANSWER: Firstly, the bishop move is good in its own right as it defends b4 against attack by ...Rb8 and deters a breakout with ...a7-a5 in the event that White has played Nc5 or otherwise weakened his grip on that square. Secondly, the bishop move improves the overall coordination of the white pieces as it clears the way for the rook on e1 to join in the queenside action down the c-file. And, finally, it has value as a waiting move – White wants to see what Black does with his bishop on c8 before committing his knight to an advanced square.

18...Bb7

QUESTION: Black has chosen b7 for his bishop. Do we want a5 or c5 as a post for our knight?

19.Na5!

ANSWER: After the routine 19.Nc5? Black can blockade the queenside after all with 19...Bc6 and 20...Bb5 . After the game move White threatens 20.Qd7 , winning a piece. Black would probably do best to go into heavy defensive mode with 19.Na5 Rfb8 , when 20.Rec1 intending 21.Rc5 keeps up White's strategic onslaught. Note how in that case both the white knight and rook would be supported by the pawn on b4 and making use of dark squares weakened by Black's thoughtless 11...b5 move. In the game Black collapses quickly:

19...Bc6? 20.Nxc6 Qxc6

After 20...Nxc6 21.Rec1 Ne7 22.Rc5 Black is completely dominated on the queenside, but he should have tried to tough it out that way.

21.Qxc6 Nxc6 22.Rec1

22...Nd8

QUESTION: What happens after 22...Rfc8 which looks very natural?

ANSWER: Bobby Fischer once write a puzzle book and I remember he kept telling the reader to "look at the whole board". After 22...Rfc8 23.Bh3! Black can resign as 23...Rc7 , ( and 23...f5 24.exf5 is hopeless as well ) 24.b5 is a fatal pin.
