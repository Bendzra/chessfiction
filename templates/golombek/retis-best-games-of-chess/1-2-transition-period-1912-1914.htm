II
TRANSITION PERIOD, 1912-1914

By now Réti had decided that his vocation was to be a chess master, and he had turned professional. Increased practice and study of the game were rapidly making him a most formidable player, and his results in international chess were much superior to those achieved in the period described in Chapter I. Indeed, when the great international tournament at Mannheim, 1914, was unhappily cut short by the outbreak of the First World War, he was well placed with 7 points out of 11, having beaten (amongst others) Duras and Tartakower.

As regards the openings, for most of this period he was still a King's Pawn player, and two international tournaments in which gambit openings had to be played allowed his combinational genius full scope. Some of the most celebrated games from these events were played by Réti, and are to be found here. Right at the very end of this period we see him switching over to the Q side openings, and these he regularly played at Mannheim in 1914.

Game No. 10 shows him beating such a great master as Duras with a bewildering succession of sound sacrifices, whilst Game No. 11 is a justly famous example of the Muzio, and is overflowing with combinative ideas. Very rare are the occasions in Spielmann's career where he has gone after a pawn at the expense of position. Game No. 12 provides one of the exceptions in which Réti gains two Bishops at the cost of a pawn, and exploits their great power in an open position to perfection.

The sweet and artful simplicity with which Réti wins Game No. 13 is strongly reminiscent of Capablanca: perhaps a study of the latter's games at San Sebastian, 1911, may have influenced him here. How best to utilise command of the open QR file, a problem that often arises in the Ruy Lopez, is shown in Game No. 14, where Réti draws the utmost profit from his command of greater space.

Three splendid games are given from the Gambit Tournament at Baden-bei-Wien, 1914. Here the rules were rather different from those at Abbazia, where a set variation of the King's Gambit was enforced. As long as the players offered a true gambit, they were at liberty to play what opening they pleased. This idea was obviously likely to be fertile in the production of varied types of games, and the three given here are amongst the finest. In Game No. 15 Réti defeats his great friend Julius Breyer by a successful defence of the Evans Gambit; whilst in the next two games he emphasises his superiority to the Swedish master Nyholm by beating him twice with equal brilliance, whether attacking with a King's Gambit or counterattacking with Black against the Danish.

After all these excitements, the change to the slower tempo of the Queen's Gambit in Game No. 18 may seem rather tame to some readers, but what is lost in violence is gained in additional subtlety, and there is, too, a charming sacrificial finish.

ABBAZIA GAMBIT TOURNAMENT, 1912
Kings Gambit Accepted
Réti — O. Duras

1.e4 e5

2.f4 exf4

These were the prescribed moves in this tournament, after which the players were allowed to select whichever variation they pleased.

3.Nf3 d5

4.exd5 Nf6

5.c4 ...

A move that has been unjustly superseded by 5.Nc3 , in modern praxis. In reality, it gives White many chances of gaining and retaining the initiative — more so, anyway, than its modern rival.

5. ... c6

An attempt to break down White's pawn mass by 5...b5 would fail after 6.Nc3 bc 7.Bxc4 Bd6 8.O-O O-O 9.d4 , when Black will find it very difficult to defend his KBP on f4.

6.d4 ...

Snatching the pawn by 6.dc , would lead to a fine game for Black after 6...Nxc6 7.d4 Bg4 .

6. ... cxd5

7.Bxf4 ...

Or White can play the line recommended by Stoltz, 7.c5 b6 8.b4 a5 9.Ne5 ab 10.Bb5+ , with great advantage. However, Black need not play like this, but should first develop his K side with 7...Be7 and 8...O-O before he attacks White's advanced Q wing.

7. ... Bb4+

8.Nb1-c3 O-O

9.Bd3 Re8+

10.Be5 Be6

This runs contrary to his general plan and brings him at once into difficulties. The right continuation was 10...Nc6 11.O-O Nxe5 12.Nxe5 (not 12.de dc! ), 12...dc 13.Bxc4 Be6 , with an approximately equal game. White lias more open lines, but his QP may become a handicap.

11.c5! Nf6-g4

Thinking that White cannot castle because of the Knight fork. In this belief he is much mistaken, and instead he should play 11...Nc6 .

12.O-O! Bxc3

13.bxc3 Ne3

On the surface Black has attained his objective; now, however, his K side is subjected to a number of shattering blows.

14.Bxh7+! Kh8

Neither 14...Kxh7 15.Qd3+ Nf5 16.g4 nor 14...Kf8 15.Qd3 Nxf1 16.Bd6+ can be played without the black King coming under a fierce and irresistible attack.

15.Qd2! ...

Another fine move. Black is forced to take the Rook and allow White's Queen to reach a mating position.

15. ... Ne3xf1

16.Qh6 f6

17.Qh5 ...

Not 17.Qh4 fe 18.Ng5 Ne3 , and a mate by White is not to be seen.

17. ... Bg4

For now if 17...fe 18.Nxe5 , and Black has to give up his Queen to prevent Ng6 mate.

18.Qxg4 Kxh7

19.Ra1xf1 Nd7

This belated development does not help at all. However, there is no saving move; if 19...fe 20.Qh5+ , followed by 21.Ng5 wins easily, and if 19...g6 both 20.Ng5+ and 20.Qh4+ , win. Finally, if 19...Qe7 (so as to control f7), then 20.Qh5+ Kg8 21.Nh4 fe 22.Ng6 Qf6 23.Rxf6 gf 24.Qh8+ Kf7 25.Qh7+ , followed by 26.Qxb7, wins easily.

20.Qh5+ Kg8

21.Nf3-g5! Nd7-f8

22.Qf7+ Kh8

23.Rf1-f4! ...

Even here White must be precise, since if 23.Rf3 Qd7 24.Rh3+ Qxh3 25.gh fe , leaves Black with some chances of resistance; now mate is not to be prevented, so Black resigns.

ABBAZIA GAMBIT TOURNAMENT, 1912
Kings Gambit Accepted, Muzio Variation
Réti — A. Flamberg

1.e4 e5

2.f4 exf4

3.Nf3 g5

4.Bc4 g4

5.O-O d5

Brentano's Defence, by which Black surrenders a pawn in the interests of quick development. For the immediate acceptance of the piece, see Game No. 6.

6.exd5 ...

Taking off with the pawn signifies that White is about to launch an attack on the K file. Capture with the Bishop leads to quite a different type of game. An example of this occurred in a later round of the same tournament, Auerbach-Spielmann, where play continued 6.Bxd5 gf 7.Qxf3 Nf6 8.Qxf4 Be7 9.Nc3 O-O 10.d3 c6 11.Bb3 Be6 12.Bd2 Bxb3 13.ab , when White still holds the advantage.

6. ... gxf3

7.Qxf3 Bd6

After 7...Nf6 8.Qxf4 Be7 9.b3 , White has a magnificent attacking position.

8.d4 ...

The older line, 8.d3 , gives White a more solid attack; if one may use such an adjective when dealing with the Muzio, e.g. 8...Ne7 9.Bxf4 Bxf4 10.Qxf4 O-O 11.Nc3 Ng6 12.Qg3 Nd7 13.Rae1 . Here clearly White's position is well worth the piece.

8. ... Qf6

Rather better is the development of a minor piece; but Black still has to struggle against a number of difficulties, as was shown in a later round of this tournament in a game, Réti-von Freymann: 8...Ne7 9.Bxf4 Bxf4 10.Qxf4 O-O 11.Nd2 Ng6 12.Qe3 Kg7 13.Rae1 Nd7 14.Ne4 f5 15.Ng3 Nb6 16.Nh5+ Kh8 17.Qh6 Rg8 18.d6 Qf8 19.Re8 , resigns.

9.Qe4+! Qe7

If 9...Ne7 10.Bxf4 Bf5 11.Bxd6 Bxe4 12.Rxf6 cd 13.Nc3 Bxc2 or 13...Bg6 14.Re1 , and Black is lost, since 14...Kd7 is met by 15.Nb5 .

10.Nb1-c3 ...

It is remarkable but true that White's attack persists even after the exchange of Queens.

10. ... Nd7

If he tries to force White to make the exchange by 10...f5 then there comes 11.Qd3 , followed by Bxf4 and Rae1. Or if 10...Nf6 11.Qxe7+ Kxe7 12.Bxf4 Bxf4 13.Rae1+ , with a crushing advantage in development.

11.Bxf4 Qxe4

12.Nc3xe4 Bxf4

13.Rf1xf4 ...

With the simple but deadly threat of 14.Re1 .

13. ... f5

This must be reckoned as Black's best chance, since otherwise he does not get the opportunity of carrying on his development. If 13...Ne7 14.d6 , and if 13...Nb6 14.Re1! Nxc4 15.Nd6+ , followed by 16.Nxf7 , with a won game.

14.Rf4xf5 Ne7

Apparently at long last White is being forced to retreat and Black can complete his development. But now comes a beautiful surprise.

15.Re1 Nb6

If 15...Nxf5 16.Nd6+ Kf8 17.Re8+ Kg7 18.Nxf5+ Kf6 19.Rxh8 Kxf5 20.Rxh7 , and White has a winning position since he not only has four pawns for the piece but still has a grip on the Black game.

16.Bb5+ Kd8?

Here Black clearly fails to find the best line. The correct method of play was worked out by that indefatigable analyst, George Marco, as follows: 16...Bd7 17.Re5 Bxb5 18.d6 O-O-O 19.de Rd5 20.Nf6 Rxe5 21.Rxe5 Be8 22.b3 Nd7 23.Re6 h5 24.Nxe8 Rxe8 25.Rh6 , and the game should be drawn.

17.Re5 Ne7-g6

There is a neat mate after 17...Nbxd5 18.Ng5 Rf8 19.Rxd5+ Nxd5 20.Nf7+ Rxf7 21.Re8# .

18.Ne4-g5! Nxe5

19.Re1xe5 Bd7

There is an echo of the combination mentioned above in 19...Rf8? 20.Nf7+ Rxf7 21.Re8# .

20.Ng5-f7+ Kc8

21.Nf7xh8 Bxb5

22.Re5-h5 Bc4

Worse is 22...Kd7 23.Rxh7+ Kd6 24.b3 , and White's QP cannot be taken.

23.Rh5xh7 Bxd5

24.h4 ...

White intends to win by means of his two united passed pawns; but there is still much interesting and difficult play in the position.

24. ... Be4

25.Rh7-g7 Bxc2

26.h5 a5

27.h6 a4

28.h7 Bxh7

29.Rg7xh7 ...

It has taken White some twenty-three moves to regain the piece sacrificed in the opening, but as interest he has acquired a distant passed pawn.

29. ... Nb6-c4

30.Nh8-f7 Ra8-a6

31.g4 Nc4xb2

If he tries to block the pawn, his Rook is in danger, e.g. 31...Rg6 32.g5 , and now both 32...Nxb2 and 32...Nd6 lose after 33.Rh8+ , followed by 34.Ne5+ .

32.Rh7-h8+ Kd7

33.Ne5+ Ke6

34.g5 Nd1

He cannot save the game. If 34...Kf5 35.g6 Kf6 36.Rf8+ Kg7 37.Rf7+ , K moves 38.Rxc7 and wins. Or if 34...c5 35.Rh6+ Kf5 36.Rxa6 ba 37.g6 Kf6 38.dc , etc.

35.Rf8 Ne3

36.Kf2 Nd5

37.g6 ...

Resigns.

This must have been a most difficult and tiring game to conduct for both players, abounding as it does in tricky and complicated variations.

TREBITSCH TOURNAMENT, VIENNA, 1913
Two Knights' Defence (by Transposition)
R. Spielmann — Réti

1.e4 e5

2.Nf3 Nc6

3.d4 exd4

4.Bc4 ...

A more peaceful temperament would have recaptured the pawn with dead equality, but Spielmann, away from the chess board the most amiable and friendly of persons, was all fire and lightning when engaged in the game.

4. ... Ng8-f6

5.O-O Nf6xe4

6.Re1 d5

7.Bc4xd5 Qd8xd5

8.Nb1-c3 Qd8

A good alternative to the more usual 8...Qa5 , whereas both 8...Qc4 9.Nd2 and 8...Qf5 9.Nxe4 Be7 10.Bg5 are distinctly in White's favour.

9.Re1xe4+ ...

Black retains his pawn with a good game after 9.Nxe4 Be7 10.Bg5 Be6 11.Bxe7 Kxe7 12.Neg5 Qd5 13.Qd2 h6 .

9. ... Be7

10.Nf3xd4 f5

Better than 10...Nxd4 11.Rxd4 Bd7 12.Bf4 Qc8 13.Nd5 , with great advantage to White.

11.Re4-f4 ...

Against Dr. Hartlaub's remarkable suggestion, 11.Bh6 , Black should play 11...fe (and not 11...O-O 12.Nxc6 bc 13.Rd4 Qe8 14.Bf4 Bf6 15.Rd3 Rf7 16.Re3 Re7 17.Nd5! , and wins, L. Schmid-D. V. Hooper, Hastings, 1951-1952) 12.Bxg7 Rf8 13.Qh5+ Rf7 14.Rd1 Nxd4 15.Rxd4 Bf5! 16.Rxd8+ Rxd8 with at least an equal game, if not more, since Ms two Bishops will become powerful eventually.

11. ... O-O

12.Be3? ...

Spielmann's pugnacity betrays him. He cannot reconcile himself to the correct continuation, 12.Nxc6 Qxd1+ 13.Nxd1 bc , with approximate equality, though Black's two Bishops still give him the better endgame chances.

Nevertheless, these two Bishops can work even more effectively in the middle game, as Spielmann soon discovers to his cost.

12. ... Bd6!

13.Nd4xc6 b7xc6

14.Ra4 ...

It is White's melancholy fortune that wherever this Rook goes it is vulnerable to attack. If 14.Rd4 c5 15.Rd2 Bb7 16.Nd5 Bxd5 17.Rxd5 Qe7 followed by 18...f4 when Black has a strong K side attack.

At least on a4 the Rook performs an attacking function.

14. ... Qd8-f6

15.f2-f4 ...

He must prevent f5-f4; whilst if he is so rash as to take the pawn, then Black gets an overwhelming attack after 15.Rxa7 Rxa7 16.Bxa7 Qh4 .

15. ... Re8

16.Qf3 ...

Now 16.Rxa7 would be met by 16...Rb8 .

16. ... Ra8-b8

17.Bd4 ...

If 17.Rb1 Rxb2! 18.Rxb2 Qxc3 , and Black wins.

17. ... Qf7

18.Ra4xa7 ...

He takes the plunge, and in so doing allows Black to break into his position. Clearly, 18.Qxc6 Bd7 loses the exchange, but after quiet moves, such as 18.Rb1 or 18.b3 , Black increases his pressure by 18...c5 and 19...Bb7 .

18. ... Rb8xb2

19.Qf3-f2? ...

Wrongly allowing the Black Queen to get to c4, where it exerts a paralysing influence on White's pieces. Better is 19.Qd3 , when, however, Black proceeds to make White's life miserable with 19...Rd8! (and not 19...Bxf4? 20.Ne2 ).

19. ... Qc4

Threatening to win a piece in two ways — by either 20...c5 or by the even more striking way that comes to pass in the game. White cannot meet the two threats.

20.a3 ...

If 20.Ra4 Rb4 21.Rxb4 Bxb4 N moves, 22...Re2 , and Black wins. The text move, by controlling b4, threatens to play this variation, but Black has a more striking line.

20. ... Rb2xc2!

Winning a piece, though White wriggles hard for another ten moves before resigning himself to the inevitable.

21.Ra7-a4 ...

Or 21.Qxc2 Qxd4+ 22.Qf2 Qxc3 , Ra1 moves or 23.Qf1 , 23...Bc5 .

21. ... Rc2xf2

22.Ra4xc4 Rf2xf4

23.Rc4-a4 ...

Meeting the threat of 23...c5 , but not dealing with

23. ... Rf4xd4

24.Ra4xd4 Bc5

25.Rd1 Ba6!

Preventing any unpinning by 26.Kf1 .

26.Kf2 Rd8

27.Ke3 Kf7

28.Na2 Ke6

29.Nb4 Bb5

30.Nc2 Ke5

Resigns

White is in zugzwang. Sooner or later the Knight must move, when f5-f4+ drives off the King and Black takes the piece at his leisure — a fine illustration of the power of two Bishops.

DEBRECEN, 1913 Ruy Lopez, Steinitz Defence
Réti — J. Székely

1.e4 e5

2.Nf3 Nc6

3.Bb5 d6

4.d4 exd4

It is unnecessary to abandon the centre so quickly. Better is 4...Bd7 .

5.Nf3xd4 ...

The Morphy move, 5.Qxd4 , has been shorn of most of its terrors by 5...Bd7 6.Bxc6 Bxc6 7.Bg5 Be7! 8.Qxg7 Bf6 9.Qxh8 Bxh8 10.Bxd8 Bxb2 11.Bxc7 Kd7 12.Ba5 Bxa1 13.Bc3 Bxc3+ 14.Nxc3 Re8 15.O-O Nf6 = (Voellmy-Henneberger, 1931).

5. ... Bd7

6.Nc3 Be7

7.Bxc6 bxc6

8.O-O Ng8-f6

9.b3 ...

The Bishop is best developed along this diagonal, since it serves little purpose when posted on the K side.

9. ... O-O

10.Bb2 Re8

11.h3 ...

So that the Knight can eventually retire to f3 without being pinned by Black's QB.

11. ... Bf8

12.Re1 c5

A plausible but incorrect advance, as White proves with his next few moves. The right plan was to redevelop his KB by g6 and Bg7. White cannot then make any vigorous thrust in the centre, for if 12...g6 13.f4 Bg7 14.e5 Nd5 , gives Black a fine game ( 15.Nxd5 cd 16.ed? c5! ).

13.Nd4-f3 Bc6

Black is quite unsuspecting of any danger, and is preparing to strengthen his position by Nd7 and g6, followed by Bg7.

14.e5! ...

A clever pawn sacrifice that cuts right across Black's plats.

14. ... Bxf3

15.Qxf3 dxe5

If 15...Rxe5 16.Rxe5 de 17.Rd1 Bd6 18.Nb5 Qf8 19.Nxd6 cd 20.Rxd6! , regaining his pawn with great advantage to White.

16.Ra1-d1 Bd6

17.Nd5 Nd7

Best. If 17...Nxd5 18.Qxd5 (threatening 19.Bxe5 ), 18...Qf6 19.f4 Qxf4 20.Rf1 Qe3+ 21.Kh1 Rf8 22.Rd3 Qe2 23.Rdf3 , with a winning position, since 23...Qxc2 loses after 24.Rxf7 Kh8 25.Bxe5 .

18.c4! ...

Blocking any hopes Black may have had of freeing himself by 18...c4 .

18. ... f6?

An error which at once loses the pawn he has been given and leaves him with much the worse game.

His position is in any case very difficult, the chief drawback being his weakness on the White squares.

If 18...g6 19.Rd2 f5 20.Nxc7 e4 (or 20...Qxc7 21.Qd5+ ) 21.Rxd6! Qxc7 22.Qc3 Ne5 23.Rd5 , and wins.

Or if he tries to cover some of the above-mentioned weakness by 18...Re6 , then White breaks through at once by 19.Nxc7! Bxc7 20.Rxd7!

The best plan is to manoeuvre his Knight round to the K side by 18...Nf8 when Réti intended to play 19.g3 , and if 19...Ng6 20.h4 , threatening to drive the N back with full compensation for his pawn sacrifice in command of open lines and the constricted position of his opponent.

19.Nd5xc7! Qxc7

20.Qd5+ Kf8

In view of the approaching endgame, it is better to centralise the King rather than put it out of play by Kh8.

21.Qxd6+ Qxd6

22.Rxd6 Ke7

23.Rd5 ...

Threatening to win the KP by f2-f4, White exploits his command of the Q file with considerable skill.

23. ... g5

24.g3 Re8-g8

Indirectly preventing (for the moment at any rate) White from carrying out his plan of f2-f4.

25.Re1-d1 Rg8-d8

He loses a pawn after 25...Rad8 26.Ba3 , whereas now this move ( 26.Ba3 ) can be met by 26...Rac8 .

26.f2-f4! ...

A very fine idea indeed. He gives up a pawn, not only to increase the scope of his Bishop, but also to bring his King quickly into the game.

26. ... g5xf4

27.gxf4 exf4

28.Kf2 Ke8

Black's powers of resistance are obviously shattered by the number of shocks White has contrived to pack into so few moves, and he is resigned to await the coming doom. Better than the text, however, is 28...Ne5 , when White's best way of pursuing his advantage is by 29.Bxe5 (not 29.Ba3 Rxd5 30.Rxd5 Rc8 31.Bxc5+? Rxc5 32.Rxc5 Nd3+ ) 29...Rxd5 30.Rxd5 (or also 30.Bxf6+ Kxf6 31.Rxd5 Rc8 32.Rd7 , though this gives Black rather more counter-play than the line outside the brackets) 30...fe 31.Rxe5+ Kd6 32.Rh5 Rh8 33.Kf3 , and White removes the KBP at his leisure with a won ending.

Black's passivity in the actual game allows White to bring it to a swift conclusion.

29.Kf3 Ke7

30.Kxf4 Ke6

31.Rd6+ Ke7

32.Kf5 ...

Resigns as Bxf6+, followed by exchange of all the pieces, is not to be averted.

BUDAPEST, 1913 Ruy Lopez, Morphy Defence
Réti — Dr. L. Asztalos

1.e4 e5

2.Nf3 Nc6

3.Bb5 a6

4.Ba4 Nf6

5.Nc3 ...

A solid continuation that was popular in the heyday of Dr. Tarrasch, largely through his influence. It should not, however, present any special difficulty to Black, and modem praxis prefers 5.O-O .

5. ... b5

The most enterprising move; a safe line, is 5...d6 .

6.Bb3 Be7

Better than 6...Bc5 7.Nxe5 , when White obtains a marked pull.

7.O-O d6

He must not neglect his development in search of pawns here, for 7...b4 8.Nd5 Nxe4 9.Qe2 Nc5 10.Nxe5 is very good indeed for White.

8.a4 ...

The most vigorous method of exploiting Black's weakened Q wing is by 8.Nd5 , for if then 8...Nxe4 9.d4 gives White a strong attack.

8. ... Rb8?

He abandons the "a" file to White, a relinquishment that proves very costly later on. Correct is 8...b4 , and if 9.Nd5 , 9...Na5 .

9.a4xb5 a6xb5

10.d3 ...

White can play to win a pawn by 10.Qe2 , but Black obtains a more than compensatory attack with 10...O-O 11.Nxb5 Bg4 .

10. ... O-O

In a game from the Trebitsch Tournament of the same year, Spielmann here developed his QB against Réti with the continuation, 10...Bg4 11.Be3 O-O 12.Nd5 Kh8 13.c3 Nxd5 14.Bxd5 Bd7 15.Ra6 Qe8 16.d4 f5 17.ef Rxf5 18.Qd3 Rf8 19.Bg5 Nd8 20.Bxe7 Qxe7 21.de de 22.Re1 , when White's advantage is manifest.

11.Nd5 Nxd5

12.Bxd5 Bd7

13.c3 Bf6

13...Kh8 followed by f7-f5 would accomplish the Spielmann manoeuvre as above with a move in hand and would be preferable to the text, after which Black is rapidly pressed to death.

14.Be3 Qe8

15.d4 Ne7

16.dxe5 dxe5

He would do better to remove one of the offending pieces by 16...Nxd5 .

17.Bc5 Rd8

18.Ra1-a7 c6

19.Bb3 ...

Threatening to win material by 20.Bb6 .

19. ... Ne7-c8

20.Ra7-c7 Bg4

21.Qa1 Ne7

Black has been on the verge of a precipice for some time; now two little pushes by White send him toppling over.

22.Nxe5 Rd8-a8

23.Nxg4! ...

White gets more than enough for his Queen.

23. ... Ra8xa1

24.Ng4xf6+ gxf6

25.Rf1xa1 Qd8

Or 25...Ng6 26.Bxf8 Qxf8 27.Rxf7 , winning.

26.Rxe7 Qd2

27.Re7-a7 Qxb2

28.Bxf8 ...

Resigns

He is mated after 28...Qxb3 29.Bh6 , or 28...Kxf8 29.Rxf7+ .

Baden-BEI-WIEN GAMBIT TOURNAMENT, 1914
Evans Gambit Accepted
J. Breyer — Réti

1.e4 e5

2.Nf3 Nc6

3.Bc4 Bc5

4.b4 ...

Though this century-old gambit is rarely played nowadays, it still has much vigour and leads to most resting combinative games.

4. ... Bxb4

5.c3 Ba5

6.d4 d6

The Lasker, and now practically standard Defence; incidentally, at the time this game was played, it was known as the Alapin Defence, since that Russian master had analysed and advocated it.

White can now regain his pawn by 7.de de 8.Qxd8+ Nxd8 9.Nxe5 , but he has an indifferent position owing to the weakness of his Q side pawns.

7.Qa4 ...

Inferior to the more usual 7.Qb3 . Also intriguing is 7.Bg5 , with which the Russians have been experimenting of late. If then 7...f6 8.Qb3 Kf8 9.Be3 , or 7...Nge7 8.Bxe7 Kxe7 (not 8...Qxe7 9.d5 , followed by Qa4+, winning a piece) 9.O-O , in both cases with advantage to White.

As will be seen, Breyer too has Bg4 in mind, but the immediate Qa4 merely exposes the Queen to attack. The way Réti demonstrates this is very clever and most instructive.

7. ... exd4

8.Bg5 Ne7

9.Nxd4 Qd7!

It is essential to unpin the KN, as White was threatening to win a piece by 10.Nxc6 .

10.Bb5 ...

10.Bxe7 looks promising, but after 10...Nxd4! 11.Qxa5 Nc6! (not 11...Nc2+ 12.Kd2 Nxa1 13.Bh4 , when Black loses two pieces for the Rook as his N cannot escape) 12.Qg5 Qxe7 13.Qxg7 Qxe4+ 14.Be2 (or 14.Kd1 Qg4+ 14...Qe5 15.Qh6 Bf5 , Black is a pawn up and has much the better game.

10. ... a6!

Forced and good. He loses a piece after 10...O-O? 11.Qxa5!

11.Bb5xc6 Nxc6

12.Nd4-f5 ...

If 12.Nxc6 , Black has the ingenious intermezzo, 12...Bb6!

12. ... f6

13.Be3 b5

14.Qd1 ...

A confession that his 7th move has turned out a failure.

14. ... Qf7

15.O-O ...

Allowing Black to take off the N. Whilst his pawn on f5 then gives him a fine outpost on e6, it is also vulnerable itself and therefore a source of weakness. Hence 15.Nd4 , was to be preferred.

15. ... Bxf5

16.exf5 O-O

17.Qc2 ...

Preparing to bring his Knight out, which Black at once prevents.

17. ... Qc4

18.a3 ...

A move of great force that at once gains the initiative.
