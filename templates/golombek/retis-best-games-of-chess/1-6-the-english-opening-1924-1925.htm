VI
THE ENGLISH OPENING, 1924-1925

Whilst not abandoning the Réti Opening, Réti felt the need for another weapon in his armoury as White, and for this purpose he chose one of the oldest openings — the English. But the spirit in which he played it and the objectives at which he aimed were far removed from the lines of a hundred years back (when this opening was last popular). He evolved a system of attack on the white squares, especially on the squares d5, c6 and b7, that was akin to one of the main ideas in the Réti, but differed in being more clear-cut and possibly more vigorous.

Practically all of the games in this chapter are of this type, and all are marked by a strategy that is worked out in a particularly neat and pleasing manner. However, the first game given here, No. 46, is not an English but a most severe Réti Opening which he won during a long stay in South America. With Game No. 47 we get the first English Opening, which is characterised by an enduring attack carried out with great precision against a good defence. The win of a pawn in the early stages in Game No. 48 is only the prelude to the major part of the game in which the ending is especially interesting and difficult.

The great tactician, F. J. Marshall, always presented special problems to Réti, and it needed supreme efforts on his part to overcome the American champion. Only a succession of brilliant and original ideas in the middle game enable him to do this in Game No. 49.

Game No. 50 is a classic that should be in every textbook on the middle game to demonstrate the correct and full exploitation of pawn weaknesses on the Q side. Game No. 51, too, is a model of its kind with control of the centre leading to a fine K side attack. We are back again in the Réti Opening domain in Game No. 52. Here Réti's opponent, a contemporary of Tarrasch and author of a great biography of the nineteenth century German champion, Anderssen, naturally enough fails to comprehend White's novel strategy and, by a series of brilliant thrusts, Réti wins with a fine K side attack.

In the last game in this chapter will be found the other side of the medal. Here Réti as Black defends against a Nimzowitschian English, quite a different type from that he himself favoured. The whole game is worthy of deep study, since, not only does it present two of the leaders of the hypermodern school in conflict, but it also shows two contrasting temperaments: that of Nimzowitsch humorously and even mysteriously imaginative, with an immense repertoire of tactical tricks; whilst Réti's is just as imaginative in a less mysterious but more elegant and pleasing style. It is perhaps too broad a generalisation to make, but by and large here we have the strategist Réti as opposed to the tactician Nimzowitsch.

BUENOS AIRES, 1924
Réti Opening
Réti R. — Grau

1.Nf3 Nf6

2.c4 d5

Of the many possible moves here, this is one of the worst; it loses time and commits Black to one line of play much too early.

3.cxd5 Qxd5?

From now on the game consists of a continuous harrying of the black Queen, so that this unfortunate piece consumes no less than 50 per cent, of Black's moves. Better was 3...Nxd5 4.e4 Nb6 5.d4 g6 .

4.g3 c6

Black conducts the defence as though it were a Centre Counter, and this move has the function of opposing the action of White's KB whilst giving the Queen a retreat on c7. However, it is still liable to attack even here, and simpler and better was 4...g6 5.Nc3 Qd8 .

5.Nc3 Qa5

6.Bg2 Bf5

By a series of subtle moves, White proves the early development of Black's QB to have been a mistake. Best was still 6...g6 .

7.d3 e6

8.O-O Nb8-d7

9.Bd2 Be7

If 9...Qc7 10.Rc1 , and the black Queen is still liable to attack by an eventual Nb5.

10.Nd4! ...

A powerful move characteristic of Réti's centralising strategy and the necessary preparation of his ensuing manoeuvre.

10. ... Bg6

The lesser evil was 10...O-O , and if 11.Nd5 Qd8 12.Nxe7+ Qxe7 13.Nxf5 ef , with admittedly much the worse position, but one that would allow chances of further resistance.

11.Nd5 Qd8

12.Nxe7 Qxe7

13.Qb3! ...

The first move the white Queen makes is also the start of the final winning combination. How is Black now to defend his QNP?

If 13...Rb8 14.Qa4 , threatening both 15.Qxa7 and 15.Bxc6 .

If 13...Nc5 14.Qa3 , threatening 15.Bb4 . Finally, if 13...Nb6 , White can play either 14.a4 , threatening a4-a5, or even 14.Nxc6 bc 15.Bxc6+ Nfd7 16.Bxa8 Nxa8 17.Rac1 , with a won game, as Black cannot castle because of 17...O-O 18.Bb4 .

13. ... Qc5

14.Be3! ...

Not 14.Qxb7 Rb8 , when White loses the Knight on d4; but now he threatens Nxe6 and at the same time protects his Knight. Black's reply is forced.

14. ... Qb6

15.Qa3 ...

Preventing Castles K and once again threatening Nxe6.

15. ... Qc7

If 15...Qd8 16.Bxc6 bc 17.Nxc6 , and Black loses his Queen.

16.Ra1-c1 ...

The chief threat now is 17.Nb5 , followed by Nd6+.

16. ... Qe5

This, as Grau himself says, is a mistake in a position where every move by Black would be a mistake.

Probably best is 16...Nd5 , allowing 17.Nb5 and 18.Nd6+ . In any event, Black would lose, but the text makes the end mercifully quick.

17.Bxc6! Resigns

For if 17...bc 18.Nxc6 , threatening both mate and the win of the Queen. Or if 17...Rb8 18.Bxb7 Rxb7 19.Nc6 . A most entertaining brevity with an instructive lesson as to the inadvisability of early Queen moves.

BADEN-BADEN, 1925
English Opening
Réti — C. Carls

1.c4 ...

There is a humorous touch in Réti's adopting this opening, in that the German master, Carls, was never known to play any other opening than the English in tournaments.

1. ... Nf6

2.Nc3 c5

Signifying his intention of playing a symmetrical defence and imitating White's moves as long as possible. This is not a good policy, and instead Black has two alternatives that ensure him a game with counter-chances. He should strike back in the centre by either 2...e5 or else 2...d5 and if 3.cd Nxd5 4.e4 Nxc3 5.bc g6 .

3.g3 g6

There was still time to play a line similar to that mentioned above by 3...d5 4.cd Nxd5 5.Bg2 Nxc3 6.bc g6 . But after the text move White has too strong a hold on d5 for Black ever to play the freeing move, d7-d5.

4.Bg2 Bg7

5.d3 ...

White delays the development of his K side in favour of his Q side pieces, since he wishes to concentrate on exchanging Black's most important minor piece, the fianchettoed KB.

5. ... Nc6

6.Bd2 d6

Black might well refrain from this flattering imitation and play 6...O-O , so as to reply to 7.Qc1 Re8 8.Bh6 Bh8 .

7.Qc1 Nd4

We have seen this manoeuvre before (Game No. 42); Bd2-h6 is temporarily prevented on account of an eventual Nc2+, winning the Rook.

8.Rb1 Rb8

9.Bh6 ...

With the Rook moved, he has no reason to fear 9...Bxh6 10.Qxh6 Nc2+ 11.Kd2 Nd4 12.e4 (to prevent Nf5), when Black will have great difficulty in completing his development, e.g. 12...Ng4 13.Qg7 Rf8 14.Nh3 Ne5 15.Ng5 .

9. ... O-O

10.Bxg7 Kxg7

11.e3 Nc6

12.Ng1-e2 ...

Black now plays most ingeniously, and almost contrives to restore the balance and secure equality, but not quite. Despite his opponent's tactical skill, Réti keeps a firm hand on the strategic rein and preserves the initiative.

12. ... Ne5

13.Qd2 Bf5

14.e4 Bh3

15.O-O Qd7

16.Nd5 ...

So as to clear the long diagonal for the Queen with an indirect attack on Black's King.

16. ... Bxg2

17.Kxg2 Nc6

18.Qc3 e5

Best; White was threatening 19.Nxf6 ef 20.Nf4 Ne7 21.h4 , with a strong K side attack.

19.Nxf6 Kxf6

20.f4 Kg7

21.f5 ...

At first glance, this blocking of the position may appear to lessen White's chances of attack considerably. But he still has other lines of attack in addition to the KB file, and meanwhile it is essential to prevent Black from creating a counter-attack by Nc6-d4, followed by f7-f5.

21. ... f6

22.Qd2 ...

With the idea of playing g3-g4-g5.

22. ... g5

23.g4 b5

The natural reaction; he plays for a counter-attack on the Q side so as to counter-balance White's pressure on the other wing.

24.h4 h6

Not 24...gh 25.Rh1 , when White's attack on the K side soon breaks through.

25.Rh1 bxc4

Curiously enough, the opening up of this line, which one might assume to be the natural continuation to gain a counter for Black, is in reality the seed of his destruction. White proceeds to pile on the pressure on the K side, and then administers a crushing blow on the Q wing whilst Black's pieces are engaged in defence on the other side.

Better, therefore, was 25...b4 .

26.dxc4 Nd4

27.Nc3 Rh8

28.Rh3 Rb8-g8

29.Rb1-h1 Qd8

30.Nd5 gxh4

If Black just holds on passively to the position, then White will win by playing his King to e3 and tripling his major pieces on the KR file. Then he has the deadly threat of h4xg5, and if h6xg5, Rh7+.

31.Rxh4 Kf7

Hoping for a formidable counter-attack after 32.Rxh6 Rxg4+ 33.Kf2 Rxh6 34.Rxh6 Qg8 .

32.Kf2 Qf8

This does not help much; but if, for instance, 32...Rg5 33.Rxh6 Rxh6 34.Rxh6 Rxg4 35.Rxf6+ , and wins. It will be observed that 32. Kf2, quiet as looks, has given White just the extra tempo he needs for fulfilling the attack.

33.Rxh6 Rxh6

34.Rxh6 Qg7

Black seems to have made all safe on the K side; but now there comes a killing blow from quite another direction.

35.Qa5! Resigns

For if 35...Qxh6 36.Qc7+ Kf8 or Ke8 37.Qe7 mate.

BADEN-BADEN, 1925
English Opening (by Transposition)
Réti — E. Grünfeld

1.g3 ...

An experiment which Réti was soon to abandon in tournament play. The idea is to strike at the white squares at once, for which purpose it is unnecessary to hinder Black from playing e5, as for instance, White does when he plays 1.Nf3 in the Réti Opening.

As a matter of fact, the game soon transposes to a quite well-known form of the English Opening.

1. ... e5

2.Bg2 ...

Or White may try a sort of Alekhine's Defence with a move in hand, as occurred in the game Réti-Alekhine from the same tournament, which went 2.Nf3 e4 3.Nd4 d5 4.d3 ed 5.Qxd3 Nf6 6.Bg2 Bb4+ 7.Bd2 Bxd2+ 8.Nxd2 O-O 9.c4 , when it will be observed that, as so often in Réti's games, White has managed to create a tense position in which he has pressure on the white squares.

2. ... Nf6

Rather timid; boldest and best was 2...d5 .

3.Nf3 Nc6

3...e4 4.Nd4 , which would transpose into the Réti-Alekhine game, would be preferable.

4.d4 exd4

If 4...e4 5.Nfd2 Nxd4 6.Nxe4 Nxe4 7.Qxd4 Nf6 8.c4 , with advantage to White.

5.Nxd4 Bc5

6.Nxc6 bxc6

7.c4 ...

We have now completely transposed to a variation of the English Opening in which Black's Q side pawns are weak. How Réti takes advantage of this is a lesson in itself. The reader should also compare Game No. 50.

7. ... O-O

8.O-O Re8

9.Nc3 Bf8

Black prepares to play an eventual c6-c5.

10.Bf4 ...

A strong move with a number of hidden points. Its immediate threat is 11.Nb5 , and it is superior to the fianchetto development by b3 and Bb2, since it allows the Queen to attack Black's pawns by Qa4.

10. ... Rb8

11.Qc2 Bb7

A modest method of developing this piece; but if 11...c5 , with the idea of exchanging White's powerful KB next move by Bg2, then 12. Nb5 is very strong.

12.Rf1-d1 a6

He still wants to achieve c6-c5, and so prevents White from replying Nb5. Another system which might give more life to the QB on another diagonal would be 12...d6 13.c5 d5 14.b4 Ba6 , but White could then quietly play a6, with the powerful threat of e2-e4 to follow.

13.Rd2 ...

Threatening quite simply to double Rooks, with further pressure on the wretched QP.

13. ... c5

14.Bxb7 Rxb7

This exchange has not helped Black much; in fact, now Black's QB is off, he will find it impossible to defend all his Q side pawns.

15.Bg5 Be7

If he protects his QP by 15...d6 , then 16.Ne4 , threatening both 17.Nxc5+ and 17.Nxf6 .

16.Qa4 ...

The long-awaited Queen sortie that now forces the win of a pawn.

16. ... h6

17.Bxf6 Bxf6

18.Rxd7 Qb8

White has won his pawn, but now there comes the usual reaction in such positions when the side that has had to surrender material is striking back along the open lines that have resulted. Here White has to concern himself with the safety of two pawns, since not only is QN2 threatened, but also K2 after the exchange of Bishop for Knight.

19.Rd2! ...

He does not mind giving back a pawn, since this will merely enable him to come down to a won ending.

19. ... Bxc3?

Not the best way of getting back the pawn, as it leads to a Rook and pawn ending of which Réti's beautiful technique makes light work. A better chance was 19...Rb4 when Réti intended 20.Qa3 , and now if (a) 20...Rxc4 21.Nd5 Bg5 22.f4 c6 (if the Bishop moves, then 23.Qxa6 ) 23.fg cd 24.gh gh 25.Qxa6 or if (b) 20...Qb7 21.Rad1 Rxc4 22.Nd5 Bd4 23.Qf3 c6 24.Ne3 Rb4 (not 24...Bxe3 25.Rd7 ) 25.b3 Re7 26.Nc4 , threatening e2-e3.

In both cases White has a marked advantage and should eventually win, but Black would have more practical chances of resistance than after the move actually played.

20.bxc3 Rb1+

21.Rxb1 Qxb1+

22.Kg2 Re6

If 22...Qe4+ 23.f3 Qe3 24.Qd7 Re7 25.Qd8+ , followed by Qd3, retaining the extra pawn.

23.Qc2 Qxc2

24.Rxc2 Re4

25.f3 ...

More incisive than 25.Kf3 , when Black can maintain his Rook on e4 by f7-f5 and it is difficult for White to make headway. Now, however, Black must take the pawn and we have a Rook and pawn ending in which White is to all intents and purposes a pawn up, as Black's doubled QB pawns are not worth more than White's single QBP.

The method by which Réti now utilises his advantage of four pawns to three on the K side is very instructive.

25. ... Rxc4

26.e4 Kf8

27.Kf2 Ke7

28.Ke3 Ra4

29.Kd3 ...

He completes the centralisation of his King before proceeding to the advance of his K side pawns.

29. ... Kd6

30.Rb2 c4+

31.Ke3 Kc5

32.f4 Ra3

33.Rc2 g6

34.Kf3 Kd6

35.Ke3 Kc5

White's last two moves have been merely to gain time on the clock. He now continues with his K side pawn advance.

36.h4 ...

Threatening 37.h5 , and so forcing Black's reply.

36. ... h5

37.f5 gxf5

Otherwise White himself plays 38.fg fg 39.Kf4 , followed by Kg5.

38.exf5 Kd6

39.Kf4 ...

Not 39.Kd4 c5+ 40.Kxc4 Ra4+ 41.Kd3 Rg4 , when the game is drawn.

39. ... f6

40.g4 hxg4

41.Kxg4 Ke7

42.h5 Kf7

43.h6 Kg8

44.Kh5 Kh7

White has made the utmost progress possible with his King and pawn, and must now utilise his Rook to get any further. This necessitates giving up the QBP, and is not without danger if the ending is not conducted with the greatest accuracy.

45.Rd2! Rxc3

46.Rd7+ Kh8

47.Kg6 ...

Not 47.Rxc7 , because of 47...Rg3 , threatening Rg5+.

47. ... Rg3+

48.Kxf6 c5

49.Rc7 c3

50.Rxc5 Kh7

What is the precise way of winning now? The forthright 51.Rc8 , hoping for 51...Kxh6 52.Rh8 mate, gives Black drawing chances after 51...Rg2 52.Rxc3 Rxa2 53.Kf7 Kxh6 54.f6 Rf2 .

51.Kf7! Kxh6

Or 51...Rg2 52.Rxc3 Rxa2 53.f6 Kxh6 54.Kf8 Rf2 55.f7 Kg6 56.Kg8 Rxf7 57.Rg3+ , winning the Rook.

52.f6 Rf3

53.a4 Rd3

54.a5 Rf3

55.Rc6 Kg5

Setting, even at this late stage, a very neat trap.

56.Kg7! ...

Not 56.Rxa6? c2 57.Rc6 Rxf6+ , and Black wins.

56.............. Resigns

As he loses his Rook after 56...c2 57.Rxc2 Rxf6? 58.Rg2+ .

BADEN-BADEN, 1925
Queen's Pawn, Queen's Indian Defence (by Transposition)
Réti — F. J. Marshall

1.c4 b6

One of the common faults in dealing with the English Opening for Black is that of committing oneself too early to a fixed line. White can then quite often transpose to a favourable variation of another opening. Here he gets a type of Queen's Indian in which White has command of the centre.

Better are both 1...Nf6 and 1...e5 .

2.d4 Bb7

3.Nc3 Nf6

4.Nf3 d5

Black's hypermodern spirit was not long-lived. He prefers immediate counter-action in the centre to the more restrained 4...e6 (which was, nevertheless, the better move).

5.cxd5 Nxd5

6.g3 Nd7

7.Bg2 e6

8.O-O Be7

Now, or on the next move, Black should have freed his game by 8...Nxc3 , followed by c7-c5.

9.Qc2 O-O

10.Rd1! ...

Directed against the freeing manoeuvre mentioned above, which now involves allowing an ugly pin on the Q file.

10. ... c5

Black is undeterred by White's last move, and proceeds in straightforward fashion right into a lost position. Better was 10...Qc8 , first, and then 11...c5 .

11.dxc5 Nxc5

Suddenly the position has become fraught with danger. If 11...Bxc5 then White obtains a won game by 12.Ng5 N7f6 13.e4 Nb4 14.Qb1 Qc8 15.e5 h6 16.ef hg 17.fg Kxg7 18.Bxb7 Qxb7 19.Bxg5 .

12.Ne1! f5

White was threatening to win a piece by 13.e4 .

13.b4! Na6

14.Nxd5 Bxd5

15.Bxd5 exd5

16.Qb3 Kh8

17.Nd3 ...

White refrains from taking the pawn here, since it would give Black distinct counter-chances after say, 17.Rxd5 Qe8 18.a3 Bf6 19.Ra2 Rc8 . Black's weakness on d5 will not run away, and meanwhile White brings another piece into excellent play.

17. ... Bf6

18.Bb2 Bxb2

19.Qxb2 Qe7

20.a3 Nc7

21.Nf4 Ra8-d8

22.Rd2 Rd6

23.Rc1! ...

Much stronger than the obvious 23.Rad1 , since not only does it prepare for action along the QB file (where Black is weakest) but it also prevents Black from doubling Rooks. For if 23...Rfd8 24.Qc2 , with a double attack on the Knight and the KBP.

23. ... Qf7

24.Qe5 Rd7

25.Rd2-c2 Re8

Hoping to gain time to play Ne6, since once the Knights are exchanged Black's position is tenable; but now comes a pretty combination.

26.Nd3! Kg8

Black loses at once if he takes the Queen: 26...Rxe5 27.Nxe5 Qe7 28.Nxd7 Ne6 (or 28...Qxd7 29.Rxc7 , winning the Queen) 29.Rc8+ Nd8 30.Ne5 Kg8 31.R1c7 Queen moves 32.Rd7 , etc.

27.g4 ...

Another brilliant and original idea. He wants to vacate the square K5 for his Knight, but would like to do this with gain of tempo. Hence the pawn sacrifice. However, the prosaic 27.Qb2 would have preserved his advantage without running the risks incurred by the text move.

27. ... fxg4?

He must now accept the sacrifice (or, rather, offer), of the Queen and play 27...Rxe5 28.Nxe5 Qe6 29.Nxd7 Ne8 30.Nb8 fg , and though White's two Rooks are here worth more than the Queen, Black has distinct counter-chances on the K side.

28.Qg3 ...

Now not only is Black's Knight en prise, but he is threatened with the loss of the exchange by Ne5. He cannot ward off both threats.

28. ... Nb5

29.Ne5 Qe6

Or 29...Qf5 30.Nxd7 Nd4 31.Qd3 Nxe2+ 32.Rxe2 Qxd3 33.Rxe8+ Kf7 34.Ne5+ .

30.Nxd7 Nd4

The best chance; he loses a piece, but gains several pawns in return.

31.Qe3! Qxe3

32.fxe3 Nxc2

33.Rxc2 Rxe3

Black has two pawns for a piece that at the moment is out of action. White has to play carefully to ensure the win; above all, he must see to it that not too many pawns are exchanged, since there is the danger of coming down to a drawn ending with Rook and Knight against Rook.

34.a4 h5

35.Rd2 g3

36.hxg3 Rxg3+

37.Kf2 Rg4

Or 37...Rg5 38.Rd4 , and White will at last be able to get his Knight into effective play by Nb8 and Nc6.

38.b5 Rf4+

39.Kg3 Rxa4

40.Rxd5 h4+

41.Kf3 h3

42.Kg3 a6

43.Nxb6 Ra3+

44.Rd3 h2

Marshall always believed in playing till the bitter end.

45.Kxh2 Rxd3

46.exd3 axb5

47.Nd5 Kf7

48.d4 Ke6

49.Nb4 Kf5

50.Kg3 Ke4

51.d5 Kf5

52.Nc6 ...

Neatly cutting off the King from the passed pawn.

52. ... Kf6

53.Kf3 g6

54.Kg4 g5

55.Kg3 Kf7

Against other King moves, the QP simply marches on to Queen.

56.Kf3! ...

A neat move that, by gaining the distant opposition, forces the win of the KNP, or else the advance of the QP to the eighth rank.

56. ... Kf6

57.Kg4 Kg6

58.d6 b4

59.d7 Resigns

MARIENBAD, 1925
English Opening
Réti — D. Przepiórka

1.c4 Nf6

2.Nc3 e5

3.Nf3 Nc6

4.d4 exd4

Or Black can play 4...e4 , but after 5.Nd2 Nxd4 6.Ndxe4 Ne6 7.Nxf6+ Qxf6 8.e3 , White has Some advantage, owing to his pressure on the white squares, whilst Black's Knight on e6 is not nearly so well placed as one might imagine from its centralised appearance.

5.Nxd4 Bc5

The normal move at the time this game was played; but the experience of the result here did much to destroy its popularity, and it became recognised as a bad move that does nothing towards relieving White's attack on the white squares.

The correct line is 5...Bb4 , and if 6.Bg5 h6 7.Bh4 Bxc3+ 8.bc d6 9.f3 O-O 10.e4 Ne5 , with about equal chances. White's two Bishops compensate for his inferior pawn structure.

6.Nxc6 bxc6

7.g3 d5

Black tries to rid himself of his pawn weakness by an immediate advance, instead of holding the pawns back and playing 7...O-O as Grünfeld did in Game No. 48 with equally fatal consequences.

8.Bg2 Be6

Black soon repents this move, which Réti shows to be a waste of time. Better, though still not calculated to cheer the spirit, was 8...Be7 .

9.O-O O-O

10.Qa4! Bd7

A reluctant necessity; if 10...Qd7 11.Rd1 Rfd8 12.Bg5 , and Black can no longer defend his vital d5 square.

11.Bg5 ...

Now stronger than Bf4, which was played in Game No. 48, since here it is essential to undermine Black's d5.

11. ... Be7

12.Rf1-d1 h6

Or 12...c5 13.Qa3 , and the QP falls.

13.Bxf6 Bxf6

14.Qa6 ...

A fine move that paralyses Black's Q wing. Premature would be 14.cd cd , since the discovered attack on the white Queen would give Black time to strengthen his centre by c7-c6.

14. ... Rb8

A laudable desire to counterattack, which, however, really shortens the game. Perhaps the best chances are afforded by a voluntary surrender of the d5 point, together with an unpinning of the black Queen by 14...Bxc3 15.bc Qf6 .

15.cxd5 ...

The timing here, as throughout the game, is perfect on White's part. He takes off at just the right moment. An attempt to increase the stress on the Q file by 15.Rd2 would be a blunder because of 15...Rxb2 16.Rxb2 Bxc3 .

15. ... cxd5

To either 15...Rxb2 or 15...Bxc3 White has the powerful reply, 16.dc!

16.Nxd5 Bxb2

17.Ra1-b1 c6

If 17...Bb5 18.Qxa7 Bxe2 19.Rd2 Bc4 20.Rdxb2 Rxb2 21.Rxb2 Bxd5 22.Bxd5 Qxd5 23.Qxc7 , with a won ending.

Position after 17...c6

18.Qxa7! ...

A beautifully logical move that shows how strong he has become on the white squares; indeed, it is remarkable how often in this game the combinations seem to dwell on squares of this colour.

18. ... cxd5

19.Rxd5 Qe8

Rather better is 19...Qf6 , though even then White would win easily enough.

20.Rxd7 Qxe2

Black may have thought that White would be content with his extra pawn and that the Bishops of opposite colour might yield him drawing chances; but the surprises are by no means at an end.

21.Rxf7! Be5

22.Rxb8 Rxb8

23.Rxg7+! ...

White has made a complete clearance of the seventh rank, and the Bishops of opposite colour greatly facilitate his attack instead of tending towards the draw.

23. ... Bxg7

24.Qxb8+ Kh7

25.Qb1+ Kh8

26.Be4! Be5

27.Qb7 Resigns

The only way of avoiding an immediate mate is by 27...Kg8 , and then comes 28.Qd5+ . A game of great elegance in which every move seems to fit to form a perfectly harmonious work of art.

MARIENBAD, 1925
English Opening
Réti — R. Spielmann

1.c4 e5

2.Nf3 d6

Not a good line against the English, as Black has little counter-chances and a passive position in the centre. Better is 2...Nc6 .

3.d4 exd4

4.Nxd4 Nf6

With the pawn on d6, the natural developing line of the KB is along the long diagonal; hence 4...g6 , followed by Bg7, was indicated here.

5.Nc3 Be7

6.g3 O-O

Natural moves are not always the best. Not only so as to develop the QB, but also to avoid the possibility of Black retaining the gambit pawn by dc and b5.

5. ... Be7

7.Nf3 Nb8-d7

This and the next move are prompted by the spirit of passivity with which Black started the game.

10.Ne5 ...

Clearing the line of action for his KB.

10. ... Nf6-d7

The Bishop is badly placed here; better is 4...d5 or, if Black wishes to avoid giving his opponent a Sicilian with a move in hand, 4...g6 5.Be2 Bg7 , with equality.

5. ... O-O

6.O-O Re8

7.a3 ...

Gaining the technical advantage of two Bishops or else forcing the Bishop to retreat; but the resulting pawn formation is not exactly a happy one, since it is very difficult to make the pawn mass dynamic. Primarily with the object of a central advance by c7-c6 and d6-d5, but also in order to hinder White's last plan, as outlined above. For if 10.d3 Ng6 11.e4 Ng4 , with advantage to Black.

So as to hinder White's d3-d4 after he himself has played d6-d5.

If White had played 12.d4 , then Réti intended to avoid the exchange of Queens by 12...Qc7 .

12. ... d5

Allowing Black full scope in the centre; but if 13.cd cd , White becomes very weak on c3.

13. ... Qc7

14.Qa4 ...

14.a6 b6 is in Black's favour.

Not 14...b6 15.ab ab 16.Qxa8 , etc. , as White's two Rooks are stronger than Black's Queen. Now, on the other hand, Black is preparing a menacing attack on the K side.

White's opening strategy has been disproved, and he has difficulty in forming any reasonable plan. 16. ... e4

This simple-looking move that seems only directed at attack has a subtle point. He intends to play Bg4 and exchange off Bishops, thereby weakening White still more on the white squares; but he waits till White has brought his Knight to g2, so that after pawns have been exchanged on f3 he must retake with Bishop rather than Knight.
