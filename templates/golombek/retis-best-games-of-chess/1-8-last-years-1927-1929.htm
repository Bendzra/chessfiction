VIII
LAST YEARS, 1927-1929

Réti's results in the previous few years had not been entirely satisfactory, but from 1927 onwards they began to follow a rising curve of success so that he scored a number of first places in 1928, the last full year of his life. His career really ends in 1928, as his last tournament was a small one in Stockholm that bridged over into the next year. He was one of those invited to play at the great Carlsbad Tournament of 1929 but fell ill and died a month before that tournament started.

Without entirely deserting the English and Réti Openings, he seemed to show a tendency in this period to go over to more orthodox lines of the Queen's Gambit, in the handling of which he was certainly most proficient. Game No. 62 is a classical miniature — again the unfortunate Grau being the victim. It demonstrates that symmetry is not the easiest way to secure equality for Black, and may even be the quickest road to defeat. The power of two Bishops is beautifully shown in Game No. 63, and there are many original touches in Game No. 64, which has a pretty final combination.

An approach towards more orthodox lines is seen in Game No. 65, a Queen's Gambit Accepted played in a style of which the classicists would have thoroughly approved. Here too there is an admirable combination to finish up with, some seven moves deep. Another anticipation of future trends can be observed in the opening of Game No. 66. In this the advantages achieved in the opening and middle game are consummated by a very finely played ending.

Subtlety and vigour are nicely blended in a Queen's Gambit in Game No. 67, and Game No. 68 resembles No. 56 both in opening and in the great vigour with which Black endues his counter-attack.

1928 was an unhappy year for Spielmann whenever he was opposed by Réti over the board. He was beaten no less than four times in resounding fashion, and the best game of these four appears here as Game No. 69. The extent to which Réti held Spielmann in thrall can be seen in the following gamelet from Dortmund, 1928: White: R. Spielmann, Black: Réti. Bishop's Opening. 1.e4 e5 2.Bc4 Nc6 3.d3 Nf6 4.Nc3 Bb4 5.Ne2 d5 6.ed Nxd5 7.Bxd5 Qxd5 8.O-O Qa5 9.a3 O-O 10.Be3 Bxc3 11.Nxc3 Nd4 12.b4 Qa6 13.f4 Qc6 14.Qd2? Qxc3

White resigns.

In the last game of this book we see Réti reverting to the King's Pawn, undoubtedly because he knew Kostic was addicted to the form of Sicilian Defence he played here and because he had worked out a better line than theory had recognised hitherto. It is interesting to note that his previous game against Kostic given in this book, Game No. 31, was also a Sicilian Defence, and that in both cases he won by exploiting pawn weaknesses and transforming these into a lost ending for his opponent by superb endgame play.

INTERNATIONAL TEAM TOURNAMENT, LONDON, 1927
Queen's Gambit Declined, Symmetrical Defence (by Transposition)
Réti (Czechoslovakia) — R. Grau (Argentina)

1.c4 c5

2.Nf3 Nf6

3.d4 d5

This is an opening of transpositions; starting off as an English, it has rapidly changed into a Queen's Gambit; or Black could now reach the Sicilian Defence by 3...cd 4.Nxd4 g6 5.Nc3 Bg7 6.e4 .

The text move is playable, but leaves Black always struggling to overtake White's one move advantage in development.

4.cxd5 cxd4

It is clear that 4...Qxd5 5.Nc3 , loses a tempo for Black, whilst if 4...Nxd5 5.e4 Nf6 6.Nc3 cd 7.Bb5+ , and again White is well ahead in development.

5.Qxd4 Qxd5

6.Nc3 Qxd4

7.Nxd4 a6

Better than 7...e5 8.Ndb5 Na6 9.g3 , with the superior game for White owing to the attacking posts occupied by his Knights.

8.g3 ...

Only by keeping the lines open for his pieces can White avoid an early draw. For instance, the game Alekhine-Grünfeld, Baden-Baden, 1925, went 8.e4 e5 9.Nf3 Nc6 10.Bc4 Bc5 11.Bg5 O-O 12.Rc1 Bb4 , and a draw was agreed.

8. ... e5

9.Nb3 Nc6?

Black treats the opening altogether too simply. He must counteract the pressure White is threatening to bring to bear on his Q side by the fianchettoed Bishop, and hence correct is 9...Bd7 10.Bg2 Bc6 .

10.Bg2 Bd7

11.O-O Be7?

The second indifferent and shortsighted move, after which Black's game is not to be saved, since, just as in the previous game, Réti gets unchallenged control of d5. 11...h6 was essential, though even then White would have the advantage by playing 12.Be3 , and occupying the c5 square.

12.Bg5 O-O

13.Rf1-d1 Rf8-d8

Réti points out that moving the other Rook here is just as bad, for after 13...Rad8 14.Bxf6 , and (a) 14...Bxf6 15.Nc5 Bc8 16.Nxa6 or (b) 14...gf 15.Nd5 Bc8 16.Rac1 , and the threat of 17.Rxc6 forces win of material.

14.Bxf6 gxf6

Or 14...Bxf6 15.Nc5 Bc8 16.Nd5 , and Black is hopelessly placed, e.g. if then 16...Kf8 17.Nb6 Rxd1+ 18.Rxd1 Rb8 19.Nd7+ , or, first, BxN, followed by Nd7+.

15.Nd5 ...

Threatening both 16.Nb6 and 16.Nxe7+ , followed by Bxb7.

15. ... Ra8-b8

Not 15...Bc8 16.Nxe7+ .

16.Nc5! ...

A powerful move that poses a number of problems for Black. It threatens either 17.Nxe7+ or 17.Nxd7 . Taking the Knight loses the exchange and a pawn after 16...Bxc5 17.Nxf6+ , King moves, 18.Nxd7 , and, finally, 16...Be6 leads to a lost ending by 17.Nxe7+ Nxe7 18.Bxb7 .

16. ... Kf8

17.Nxf6! Resigns

Again he loses the exchange and a pawn by 17...Bxc5 (f6) 18.Nxd7+ , etc. A game which, despite the early exchange of Queens and apparent easy equality for Black, demonstrates exactly how White should retain the initiative by a number of piquant points.

INTERNATIONAL TEAM TOURNAMENT, LONDON, 1927
English Opening
Réti — M. Romih (Italy)

1.c4 e5

2.Nc3 f5

He commits himself too early with this move, and it is instructive to see how, from now on, Réti plays so as to deprive Black's QB of adequate development (which inadequacy is indeed the logical consequence from Black's second move).

Better is 2...Nf6 .

3.e3 ...

He is preparing to induce Black to play e5-e4, partly in order to open up the KB file for the Rook eventually by f2-f3, and partly so as to secure the valuable square of f4 for his KN.

3. ... Nf6

4.d4 e4

Or 4...ed 5.ed Be7 6.Bd3 , with advantage to White.

5.Nh3! Bb4

A bad move which unnecessarily offers White the advantage of two Bishops. He should play 5...Be7 .

6.Qb3 Bxc3+

7.Qxc3 Nc6

This and the following are artless moves that do nothing towards solving the problem of the development of his QB. He should play 7...b6 and Bb7.

8.Be2 d6

9.O-O O-O

10.b3 ...

Along this diagonal, with its potential sweep right into Black's K side position, the Bishop is ideally developed. The only positional objective for White to achieve now is the further opening up of lines so as to get his two Bishops actively working in co-operation.

10. ... Bd7

11.Bb2 Qe7

12.Nf4 Ra8-e8

13.Ra1-c1 ...

Against 13.Nd5 , Black can play 13...Qd8 , followed by Ne7. The text prepares the move.

13. ... Bc8

14.Nd5 Qd8

15.f4 a6

A pointless move that merely gives White an extra tempo. It is true that 15...ef e.p. 16.Bxf3 gives fresh lines to White, but unless he ventures into this there is no future for Black whatsoever, and he might at any rate hope for some play on the K file in this way. It should also be observed that by leaving the pawn on f4 he greatly enhances the value of White's QB, the action of which cannot be blocked at any stage by Ne5.

16.Nxf6+ Rxf6

17.d5 Nb8

18.c5 c6

A move that Black cannot now afford to play. He should try to hold the position by 18...Qe7 .

19.Rf1-d1 Re7

Black cannot exchange a pawn, since either way he loses material eventually, and meanwhile his minor pieces are comically helpless.

20.cxd6 Qxd6

21.dxc6 Qxc6

22.Rd1-d8+ Rf8

22...Re8 loses at once because of 23.Qxc6 .

23.Qb4! ...

Fatal for Black; his Queen has no escape square that will protect the Rook other than e6, where it cannot go because of Bc4.

23. ... Rxd8

24.Qxe7! ...

And not 24.Rxc6 Nxc6 , when all is protected.

24. ... Qd7

Again the only move to save the Queen, but it does nothing for the King.

25.Bc4+ Resigns

The mate next move by 25...Kh8 26.Bxg7 is a veritable apotheosis of Bishops.

INTERNATIONAL TEAM TOURNAMENT, LONDON, 1927
Réti Opening
Réti — A. Muffang (France)

1.c4 Nf6

2.Nf3 b6

3.g3 Bb7

4.Bg2 e6

5.O-O Be7

Thus far as in Game No. 59, where Réti continued: 6.Nc3 .

6.d3 O-O

As in the above-mentioned game, Black neglects the centre and spends the rest of his time regretting it. Correct here is 6...d5 .

7.e4! ...

A vigorous move that cramps Black and so enables Réti to launch a K side attack almost at will.

7. ... d6

Black has resolved to make White's attack as difficult as possible, at any rate, as regards its success. He plans a system of blockade in which he will fortify his K side position very heavily indeed.

7...d5 is no longer good because of 8.e5 Nfd7 9.Qe2 , with a much more difficult position to defend than in the game itself.

8.Nc3 Nb8-d7

9.Ne1 ...

The typical Réti manoeuvre: the Knight is to be centralised for the attack, the KBP prepares to advance and at the same time by revealing the power of the KB, White is able to gain more control of the centre by d3-d4.

9. ... Ne8

10.d4 g6

11.Bh6 Ng7

12.Nd3 ...

Having fully developed his minor pieces, White now plans to attack by f2-f4-f5. Black therefore blunts the force of this projected advance by his next move.

12. ... e5

13.d5 ...

Exchanges by 13.de Nxe5 14.Nxe5 de 15.Nd5 c6 16.Nxe7+ Qxe7 , whilst gaining the advantage of two Bishops, would greatly relieve the congestion in the Black position.

13. ... Nc5

14.Nxc5 bxc5

15.f4 exf4

Against the more stubborn 15...f6 White would play 16.f5 g5 , and, after due preparation, h2-h4, with an attack similar to that which Réti has often brought to a triumphant conclusion in the games of this book.

16.gxf4 Bf6

Black appears to have freed his position without incurring any weakness; but now Réti enlivens the attack by an excellent positional pawn sacrifice.

17.e5! dxe5

18.Ne4 exf4

The position is opening up with astonishing rapidity. If Black tries to hold on to his QBP by 18...Qe7 , then he loses a piece after 19.d6 cd 20.Nxf6+ .

19.Nxc5 Bc8

20.Bxf4 Nf5

20...Bxb2 would be weak because of an eventual 21.d6 by White; but now that the Knight is covering this square, Black does not fear an immediate d6; he would lose the exchange, but gain at least a pawn in return, whilst his position would take a change for the better.

21.Ne4 Bd4+

22.Kh1 Rb8

He would give White a precious extra move in the attack by 22...Bxb2 23.Rb1 Bd4 24.Bh6!

23.Bh6! ...

Still extremely strong; clearly 23...Nxh6 24.Qxd4 would leave Black hopelessly weak on the black squares, and meanwhile White threatens to win material by 24.Rxf5 and 25.Qxd4 .

23. ... Bg7

It is wrong to exchange the KB, which is the key to the defence of the K side. Correct is 23...Be5 , when White does best to play 24.Bg5 f6 25.Bf4 rather than 24.Bxf8 Qh4 , with a most formidable attack for Black.

24.Bxg7 Kxg7

25.Qd2 Qe7

26.Ra1-e1 Qb4

27.Qd3 Bd7

The Rooks must be brought into co-operation, since 27...Qxb2 would lose after 28.Rb1 .

28.b3 Rb8-e8

29.Re2 Re5

30.Bh3 Rf8-e8

For the last six moves both players have been under acute time pressure, but now the game was adjourned, and after deep reflection Réti sealed the following move.

31.Bxf5 ...

He wants to obtain the square d4 for his Queen. If, instead, 31.Rfe1 Nh4 32.Bxd7 Rxe4 , when White cannot play 33.Rxe4 because of 33...Qxe1+! 34.Rxe1 Rxe1 mate.

31. ... Bxf5

Not 31...gf 32.Rg2+ Kf8 33.Nf6 , and wins.

32.Qd4 f6

Losing quickly owing to the ensuing combination. He should have tried 32...Kf8 , getting the King off the dangerous diagonal. If then 33.Ng5 Rxe2 34.Qf6 Rxh2+ 35.Kxh2 Qd6+ 36.Qxd6+ cd , when Black is out of all his troubles, since 37.Nxh7+ Kg7 38.Ng5 Re2+ is in his favour.

However, White could play better as follows (after 32...Kf8 ): 33.Rf4 , masking the diagonal h2-b8 and so preventing the above variation. If then 33...Qe7 34.Ng5 f6 35.Rxf5 gf 36.Ne6+ Kf7 37.Qh4 Rh8 38.Qh5+ Kg8 39.Rg2+ , and mates next move.

Position after 32...f6

33.Nxf6! Kxf6

If 33...Rxe2 34.Nxe8+ Kf7 35.Nxc7 , and wins.

34.Rf1-e1 c5

35.Qh4+ g5

36.Qh6+ Bg6

37.Rxe5 Resigns

A bit prematurely, as he can still cany on for some time by 37...Qxe1+ 38.Rxe1 Rxe1+ however, the Queen, in combination with the passed QP, would eventually force the win.

Queen's Gambit Accepted (by Transposition)
Réti — W. Winter

1.Nf3 d5

2.c4 dxc4

3.e3 ...

Regaining the pawn in a different manner from that used in Game No. 61, but one that leads almost inevitably to a transposition to the Queen's Gambit Accepted.

3. ... c5

4.Bxc4 e6

5.d4 Nf6

6.Nc3 Nc6

7.O-O a6

8.Qe2 b5

After 8...cd 9.Rd1 , White regains his pawn with advantage.

9.Bd3 ...

A speculative sacrifice of a pawn which, however, Black does not accept. The more usual line is 9.Bb3 Bb7 10.Rd1 c4 .

9. ... cxd4

Black could transpose back into the normal line here by 9...Bb7 10.Rd1 c4 , but prefers to give White an isolated QP and concentrate his efforts on blockading and attacking it.

10.exd4 Bb7

It would have been interesting to see how White would have dealt with the situation if Black had taken the pawn. Grünfeld suggests 10...Nxd4 11.Nxd4 Qxd4 12.Qf3 Rb8 13.Rd1 Qg4 14.Qc6+ , with, he says, a strong attack. After 14...Nd7 , however, there seems little attack for White.

Rather better is the continuation advocated by Kmoch: 10...Nxd4 11.Nxd4 Qxd4 12.Be3 Qg4 13.f3 , but even so 13...Qh5 , threatening an exchange by 14...Bc5 , appears adequate for Black.

11.Bg5 Be7

Now 11...Nxd4 12.Nxd4 Qxd4 would be bad because of 13.Nxb5 , and if 13...ab 14.Bxb5+ Ke7 15.Rfd1 , winning the Queen.

12.Ra1-d1 Nd5

Though this is not directly bad, it is not the best, and, moreover, it is an ominous manifestation of Black's wrong intentions. He plans to indulge in much manoeuvring on the Q side before he has castled his King into safety.

Castles at once was quite all right; if he wished to start activities on the Q side, he should at least leave the KN to guard the King by playing 12...Nb4 13.Bb1 Nbd5 .

13.Bc1! ...

Much better than 13.Bxe7 Ndxe7 , which would indeed justify Black's Knight move, as White would then have little or no attack.

13. ... Nc6-b4

He still had time to castle.

14.Bb1 Rc8

14...O-O would now be dubious, as White would have a strong K side attack, commencing with 15.Ne4 . This move is just as effective in the actual game, and Black should have taken the opportunity of preventing it by playing 14...Nxc3 15.bc Nd5 , and if either 16.Bd2 or 16.Qd3 Rc8 with counter-play.

15.Ne4 Nf6

White was threatening 16.a3 Nc6 17.Nc5 , with great advantage in position, so Black hastens to give his QN a retiring square on d5.

16.Nc5! Bd5

For if 16...Bxc5 17.dc Nbd5 18.b4 , with a positionally won game. Black is now threatening to gain relief by Bc4.

17.Ne5! Qb6

If 17...Bxc5 18.dc Rxc5 19.a3 Nc6 20.Be3 , winning the exchange. The RP cannot be taken without getting the Knight trapped by either 17...Nxa2 18.Bd2 or 17...Bxa2 18.Bxa2 Nxa2 19.Bd2 .

18.a3 Nc6

19.b4 Nxe5

This loses; he should play 19...Rd8 in preparation for castles. Castles at once 19...O-O would lose the exchange by 20.Nd7 . After the text move, White's continuous threats deprive Black of any opportunity of getting his King into safety.

20.dxe5 Bc4

21.Bd3 Bxd3

22.Rxd3 Nd5

23.Qg4! ...

The start of a fine combination which Réti has calculated out to the end of the game. The immediate threat is 24.Rxd5 , and Black can no longer castle because of 24.Bh6 , winning the exchange.

23. ... Bxc5

24.Rxd5! ...

Not 24.bc Qxc5 25.Qxg7 , when Black can hold the position by 25...Qf8 .

24. ... Bxf2+

The alternative is 24...Bf8 25.Rfd1 Rxc1 26.Rxc1 ed 27.Qc8+ Qd8 28.Qb7 , winning the Queen and the game, as Black also loses his Q side pawns.

25.Kh1 Qc7

If 25...O-O 26.Bh6 , wins, and this Bishop move is also crippling after 25...g6 .

26.Qxg7 exd5

Or 26...Rf8 27.Bh6 .

27.Qxh8+ Kd7

28.Qxh7 Qxc1

29.Qxf7+ Kd8

30.Qxd5+ Resigns

White eventually gets a check on the KB file, thereby regaining the piece when he has three pawns more, apart from the acute probability of a mating attack against the exposed King.

BERLIN, 1928
Réti Opening
Réti — G. Stoltz

1.Nf3 e6

2.g3 d5

3.Bg2 c5

The perennial fault against this system: he shows his hand too soon. Why commit oneself to a single line of play when there is the good developing move 3...Nf6 to be played?

4.O-O Nc6

Again Nf6 was better; partly so as to get castled as soon as possible, partly so as to avoid White's getting a sort of Grünfeld Defence in reverse, when White's KB will become very powerful.

5.d4 ...

Réti abandons his usual 5.c4 , since there is now no necessity to tease Black's centre pawns forward, and his object can be achieved best by a direct attack.

This variation was later taken up and exploited with great effect by the Hungarian master, Barcza, after whom many text-books call the opening nowadays.

5. ... cxd4

6.Nxd4 Qb6

He does not wish to allow White to establish his pieces on the centre squares; meanwhile, however, he neglects his K side, and 6...Nf6 was imperative.

7.Nxc6 bxc6

8.c4 Ba6?

This was the last occasion on which he could and should have played 8...Nf6 . Curiously enough, the development of the Bishop on this diagonal is the worst possible way of bringing the piece out; even the modest 8...Bd7 was preferable.

9.cxd5 exd5

Unfortunately, he cannot round off his pawn structure by 9...cd , since then 10.Nc3 Nf6 11.Qa4+ Nd7 12.Be3 gives White a great lead in development.

10.Nc3 Nf6

11.Qa4 Bb5

Forced, owing to White's threat of 12.Nxd5 . If, to block the pin, he plays 11...Nd7 , then 12.Bxd5 cd 13.Nxd5 Qb7 14.Rd1 Rd8 15.Bf4 , and White wins.

12.Nxb5 cxb5

13.Qb3 ...

Black's opening tactics have been utterly refuted. He is left with an isolated QP that is difficult to defend, whilst White has retained two powerful Bishops. One must admire the tenacious ingenuity the youthful Stoltz now displays in making Réti fight every inch of the way.

13. ... Rd8

14.a4! Bc5

White's last move won a pawn, since 14...ba 15.Qxa4+ would lose the QRP for Black. The text means that at any rate this extra pawn will be one of a doubled pair.

15.axb5 ...

Threatening 16.Ra6 , followed by Rc6.

15. ... Qe6

16.Ra6 Bb6

17.Be3 O-O

17...d4 would be bad because of 18.Bc6+ .

18.Bxb6 axb6

19.Qe3! ...

Forcing the exchange of Queens, since 19...Rb8 20.Qxe6 fe 21.Bh3 Rfe8 22.Rc1 , followed by either Rc6 or Rc7, would win quickly. Though White now has two sets of doubled pawns, he still has the initiative, and the mastery with which he works out the win is worthy of careful study.

19. ... Qxe3

20.fxe3 Rf8-e8

Counter-attack is the best policy; if 20...Rd6 21.Rc1 , followed by Rc6.

21.Rxb6 Rxe3

22.Bf3 Rb3

23.Rd1 Rb4!

Not 23...Rxb2 , when 24.e4 wins the QP and the game almost at once. With the text, Black sets a subtle trap. If 24.Bxd5 Kf8! , and now White can only draw either by 25.e4 Nxe4 or 25.Bb3 Rxd1+ 26.Bxd1 Rxb2 27.Kf1 Rb1 28.Ke1 Ne4 .

24.Rd3 ...

But now that the Rook is protected by the KP, White really does threaten 25.Bxd5 .

24. ... Kf8

25.b3 Ke7

26.Rb7+ Ke6

27.Bg2 ...

Neatly forcing Black to weaken his K side pawn position. He must now advance his KNP or else lose material.

27. ... g5

28.Bh3+ g4

29.Bg2 Rc8

30.Rb6+ Ke5

31.Re3+ Ne4

If 31...Kd4 32.Re7 , winning the KBP.

32.Rc6 Rb8

32...Rxc6 33.bc Rb6 34.Bxe4 de 35.Rc3 wins quickly for White.

33.Bxe4 dxe4

34.Rc5+ Kd6

He cannot advance because of 34...Kd4 35.Rec3 , threatening 36.e3 mate and so forcing 35...e3 36.R3c4+ Rxc4 37.Rxc4+ , followed by Rxg4.

35.Rg5 ...

White has worked out the ending with great accuracy; the Q side pawns are to be surrendered for those on the K wing.

35. ... Rb8xb5

36.Rxb5 Rxb5

37.Rxe4 h5

Or 37...Rxb3 38.Rxg4 Re3 39.Kf2 Re7 40.Rg7 , and Black must either lose another pawn or exchange Rooks.

38.b4 f5

39.Rf4 Ke5

40.Kf2 Rd5

41.Rc4 ...

Threatening to exchange Rooks by 42.Rc5 .

41. ... Rd1

42.Ke3 Rh1

47.b7 Rh8

If 47...g2 48.Kf2 g3+ 49.Kg1 .

48.b8Q Rxb8

VIENNA, 1928
Queen's Gambit Declined, Orthodox Defence
Réti — A. Becker

1.d4 e6

2.c4 Nf6

3.Nf3 d5

Transposing into the Queen's Gambit rather than continuing on Indian lines by 3...b6 , though this defence is perfectly satisfactory.

4.Bg5 Nb8-d7

5.e3 Be7

6.Nc3 O-O

7.Rc1 c6

8.a3 ...

This move was tried several times in the Alekhine-Capablanca match in 1927, but always resulted in a draw. Its point is that White wishes to delay playing 8.Bd3 , which loses a tempo after 8...dc 9.Bxc4 . One might think that a6 is in any case a gift of a tempo to Black, but this is only partially true, as in some variations it can be very useful to have the retreat on a2 for the Bishop.

8. ... a6

With the idea of playing d5xc4, followed by b7-b5. Stahlberg's exchange manoeuvre by 8...Ne4 is simpler, but there is nothing wrong with the text move.

9.cxd5 ...

To avoid the variation mentioned above.

9. ... Nxd5

Best. 9...ed , though freeing a path for the QB, leaves a fundamentally weak pawn position on the Q side of which White can eventually profit by playing b2-b4, a3-a4 and b4-b5 (after placing his major pieces on the QB and QN files).

10.Bxe7 Qxe7

11.Bd3 Nxc3

The preliminary to e6-e5, which cannot be played at once since White would then exchange Knights himself and leave a seriously weak pawn on Black's d5.

12.Rxc3 e5

13.Qc2 h6

14.O-O Re8

This plausible move is shown by Réti's subtle play to be inferior.

Correct is 14...ed , and, in reply to either 15.ed or 15.Nxd4 Nf6 . The drawback of the text move is that it leaves f7 weak.

15.Bc4! ...

A fine move; if now 15...e4 16.Nd2 Nf6 17.Ba2 , preparing a K side attack by Bb1 and f2-f3. Now, too, the raison d'etre of White's 8th move becomes clearer.

15. ... exd4

Black must choose between e5-e4 and this exchange, as the black Knight must some time or other be moved away from the support of the KP in order to develop the QB.

16.exd4! ...

The most forceful; 16.Nxd4 would avoid the isolated pawn, but deprive White of play along the K file.

16. ... Qd6

17.Re3! Nf6

If 17...Rxe3 18.fe , and suddenly White has overwhelming pressure on f7. If then 18...Nf6 19.e4 , or 18...Nf8 19.Ne5 .

18.Rxe8+ Nxe8

19.Re1 Qd8?

Black has not yet realised how weak he is on f7, otherwise he would have played the better Queen move, 19...Qf8 , when Nd6 can be tried next.

Against 19...Nf6 20.Qb3 increases White's pressure, and if 19...Nc7 , White can either adopt Becker's attractive sacrificial suggestion, 20.Bxf7+ Kxf7 21.Qh7 , with 22.Ne5+ as a threat, or else play the positional 20.Qb3 Ne6 21.Bxe6 fe 22.Qb6 with great advantage.

20.Ne5 Be6

Losing a pawn; but Black only now observes that 20...Nd6 fails against 21.Nxf7 Nxf7 22.Qg6 Qf8 23.Bxf7+ Qxf7 24.Re8+ .

21.Bxe6 fxe6

The move introduced by Capablanca in Ms 1927 match against Alekhine to avoid the Cambridge Springs Defence, 6. Nf3 Qa5. Whether it is necessary to avoid the Cambridge Springs is another matter. At the time of writing, theory would seem to indicate that the defence can be viewed with equanimity by White.

As Alekhine played. Spielmann afterwards came to the conclusion that correct was 6...dc 7.Bxc4 Qa5 , since, he says, White must play BxN on account of the threat of Nf6-e4. Even so, Black's Queen is badly placed on a5, where it can eventually be attacked by b4.

7.Nf3 O-O

8.Qc2 a6?

This, and his next two moves, form a manoeuvre that is much too slow, thereby giving White considerable latitude in the creation of a K side attack. He should play 8...dc 9.Bxc4 Nd5 10.Bxe7 Qxe7 11.O-O Nxc3 12.Qxc3 b6 , after which Black will succeed in completely freeing himself by Bb7, Rfc8 and c6-c5.

A strong move that supports the centre and, by its indirect attack on the Queen, discourages c5.

9. ... Re8

10.Bd3 h6?

Spielmann was to regret this move bitterly later on. 10...dc was still the best.
