III
FULL MASTERSHIP, 1918-1921

The cessation of international chess activities during the 1914-1918 period made a gap in Réti's career, but once they started up again Réti revealed a new strength that placed him amongst the leading masters of Europe. It was as though the interval had given him time to reflect upon the basic verities of the game and develop a new and original approach. Support for this theory is gained from the fact that it was in this period that he began to write a series of articles that was to make him the acknowledged literary leader of the hypermodern school of chess.

Together with this new approach he retained to the full his native combinative gifts, and this period is studded with beautiful examples of his skill (perhaps one should say genius) in this direction.

The first international success to bring him really to the notice of the chess world was his First Prize at Kaschau (or Kassa) in Hungary in 1918. Game No. 19 from this tournament is a blend of this new subtlety with his old combinative power, and has a beautiful finish of a type that can be seen in a number of Réti's games. The reader will note that the opening was a Queen's Gambit, but Réti had not yet entirely changed over from the K side to the Q, and his games at this time show him using the one and the other, according to the way the fancy takes him. In the other game from Kaschau, Réti gets the worse of the opening and looks like losing in the middle game. Then, however, he makes a remarkable recovery, attains a drawn position and finally, by magnificent endgame play, even wins the ending.

Game No. 21 is a brief, pretty game very much in his earlier manner, whilst in Game No. 22 Réti again gives an illustration of the strength of an attack in the centre as opposed to that on the wing. Another King's Gambit, but one of rather subtler vintage than the previous examples, is Game No. 23, in which Réti finds a better line than that played some twenty-six years later, though for some mysterious reason a number of text-books quote the later form as being correct.

The match against Dr. Euwe provides two beautiful games that have appeared in many anthologies; but they are too fine and typical °f their author for me to leave them out of this collection.

Then come the first two half-open defences in this book. Game No. 26 was played in the great international tournament at Gothenburg, in which practically all of the great masters of Europe were competing (Alekhine was about the only exception) and in which Réti established a claim to be recognised as a great master himself by winning first prize. The game won against Bogoljuboff shows the first example of Réti using the French Defence, and using it in a way that could be subsequently demonstrated to be in strict accordance with the new school of chess arising in the post-war years. It can be reckoned as his first truly modern game, and is characterised by a kind of delayed counter-attack in the centre which has now become normal, but was then novel.

This period, as though to emphasise his full maturity, also sees the composition of Réti's first endgame study to be published. It will be found at the end of the chapter.

KASCHAU, 1918
Queen's Gambit Declined, Orthodox Defence
Réti — Dr. L. Asztalos

1.d4 Nf6

2.Nf3 d5

3.c4 e6

4.Nb1-c3 Be7

Instead of transposing into the Orthodox Defence, Black could have freed his game here by 4.c5 .

5.Bg5 O-O

6.e3 Nbd7

7.Ra1-c1 Re8

This, and the next move, form too passive a defence, and it is instructive to observe how quickly White obtains a winning advantage as a result of Black's rather colourless play. Best is what has now become the normal move in this line, viz. 7...c6 .

8.Qc2 ...

Partly so as to increase pressure along the "c" file and partly in order to exert the utmost possible control of e4 — always a vexed and vital point in the Queen's. Another excellent method of attack is the Alekhine line, 8.Bd3 c6 9.O-O dc 10.Bxc4 Nd5 11.Ne4 .

8. ... Nd7-f8

A defensive move, lacking any savour of counter-attack. Other lines are, however, also unsatisfactory for Black, e.g. 8...c6 9.Bd3 dc 10.Bxc4 Nd5 11.Bxe7 Qxe7 12.Ne4 , again leading to the line of attack practised and advocated by Alekhine, or 8...dc 9.Bxc4 c5 10.O-O cd 11.Nxd4 a6 12.Rfd1 Qa5 13.Bh4 Ne5 14.Be2 Ng6 15.Bg3 e5 16.Nb3 Qc7 17.Qb1 , with much the better game (Rubinstein - Maroczy, Gothenburg, 1920).

9.Bd3 d5xc4

10.Bxc4 c5

Black is content with a humble development of his QB; if, instead, he tries for a more ambitious method by 10...a6 11.O-O b5 12.Be2 c5 13.a4 b4 14.Nb1 cd 15.Nxd4 , then he finds himself forced to exchange it off after 15...Bb7 16.Nc6 , and is then left fatally weak on the white squares.

11.O-O c5xd4

12.Nf3xd4 Bd7

13.Qb3! ...

A fine attacking move that not only threatens the b7 pawn, but envisages a concentrated onslaught, on the K side.

13. ... Ra8-c8

14.f4 ...

Threatening f4-f5.

14. ... Nf6-g4

15.Ne4! ...

A centralisation that achieves an enormous amount; by protecting the KB, he releases the Queen to menace Qxb7 after h2-h3, and he also threatens Nd6.

15. ... Qa5

Exchange of Queens looks plausible, but leads to a lost ending after 15...Qb6 16.Qxb6 ab 17.Bxe7 Rxe7 18.Nd6 Rc7 19.Be2 Rxc1 20.Rxc1 Nxe3 21.Rc7 Nd5 22.Rxb7 Nxf4 23.Bf3 , when, as White threatens to win the exchange by Nc6, Black must allow him to take off the b pawn. Then White will have two united passed pawns and much the better piece position.

16.Bxe7 Re8xe7

17.h3 Ng4-h6

The simplest continuation which allows White to win a pawn whilst retaining his hold on the position. The other possibilities were (a) 17...e5 18.hg ed 19.Nd6 , threatening both the Rook on c8 and the f7 pawn; (b) 17...Ba4 18.Qa3 Rec7 19.Nd6 Rd8 20.b3 Qb6 21.hg winning a piece; (c) 17...Nf6 18.Nxf6+ gf 19.f5 , with a strong attack.

18.Qxb7 ...

If, instead, 18.f5 , Black can counter-attack by 18...Qe5 .

18. ... Rc8-c7

19.Qa6 Qb4

20.a3! ...

A subtle move that forces Black back on the defensive. 20.Qd6 allows Black to sacrifice the exchange by 20...Rxc4 21.Rxc4 Qxc4 22.Qxe7 Qd3 23.Nf2 Qxe3 24.Nb3 Nf5 (not 24...Qxf4 25.Ng4 , and wins) 25.Qb4 Ng3 followed by Ne2+ with some drawing chances.

20. ... Qb8

Not 20...Qxb2 21.Rb1 , winning the Queen.

21.Qd6 Be8

22.Bb5 ...

The most vigorous move, which soon brings about the finish.

22. ... Bxb5

23.Rc1xc7 Nh6-f5

24.Nd4xf5 Re7xc7

25.Ne7+ Kh8

26.Ne4-g5! Be8

Or 26...h6 27.Nxf7+ Kh7 28.Qb4 Qe8 29.Nd6 Qxe7 30.Qe4+ Kh8 31.Nxb5 , with an easy win for White.

27.Nd5! ...

Resigns

The theme of this pretty finish — the discovered threat of mate on the back rank — is recurrent in Réti's chess career.

KASCHAU, 1918
Queen's Pawn Opening
Dr. M. Vidmar — Réti

1.d4 Nf6

2.Nf3 d5

3.Bf4 ...

Not a promising line for White, since, through failure to exert pressure on d5 by c2-c4, he allows Black to gain the initiative by himself attacking White's d4.

3. ... c5

4.e3 Qb6

A move that is not so effective as it appears; simply 4...e6 was best.

5.Nb1-c3 ...

For now if 5...Qxb2 6.Nb5 Na6 7.Rb1 , and White can if he likes enforce a draw by perpetual attack on the Queen or else play for an attack by continuing his development with Be7 and 0-0.

5. ... c4

By cutting off the action of White's KB, this does now threaten a capture of the b2 pawn. Nevertheless, it is mistaken strategy to release the pressure on White's d4, and better would be both 6...e6 and 6...Nc6 .

6.Rab1 Bf5

Part of Black's faulty plan. There was still time to obtain good counter-play by 6...e6 , followed by Bb4.

7.Ne5 e6

8.f3 Nb8-c6

9.g4 Bg6

10.h4 Nc6-b4

11.e4 h6

He must give his Bishop an escape square, since if 11...de 12.h5 , wins a piece whilst, though after 11...Qxd4 12.Qxd4 Nxc2+ 13.Kf2 Nxd4 14.h5 Bxe4 15.fe , Black has obtained three pawns for the piece, the open lines mean that White has an overwhelming advantage.

But now White ruins his opponent's K side pawn structure and also gains the advantage of two Bishops. Black has some counter in so far as White has also compromised his K side pawn position, but this should not prove sufficient.

12.Ne5xg6 f7xg6

13.e5 Nd7

14.a3 Nc6

15.Ne2 Be7

16.c3 O-O-O

17.Qc2 g5

On g5 the pawn is still a fairly easy object of attack; but 17...Nf8 would mean that Black could never get his Rooks into cooperation, and as played Black does at least get some counter-attack on the K side.

18.hxg5 hxg5

19.Rh1xh8 Rd8xh8

20.Bg3 Qd8

21.Bg2 Qg8

22.Kf2 Qh7

Black's best chance of saving the game is to come down to an ending. If he does not exchange Queens, White will break open the "b" file by b2-b3 with a strong attack on the King's position.

23.Qxh7 Rh8xh7

24.Kg1 ...

Preparing a double attack on the g5 by Bg3-f2-e3 and Ne2-g1-h3. Black rightly does not attempt to stave off this attack, but strives for counter-chances on the other wing.

24. ... Nd7-f8

25.Bf2 Nf8-g6

26.Be3 Rh7-h8

27.Kf2 b7-b5

28.Kg3 a7-a5

29.Ne2-g1 Rh8-f8

30.Ng1-h3 b4

31.axb4 axb4

32.Bxg5? ...

The wrong way. He should play 32.Nxg5 , so as to retain control of f4 square when Black cannot prevent the advance of the f3-f4.

32. ... Bxg5

33.Nh3xg5 Ng6-f4

Despite the pawn minus, Black's chances are now at least as good as White's, owing to the poor scope of the Bishop.

34.Bf1 ...

Rather better than this defensive move is 34.Nh3 , when, however, Black can still nullify White's Bishop by 34...Nxh3 35.Bxh3 g5 .

34. ... b3

A strong move that encircles the Bishop with an iron hoop of pawns and also provides Black with all the aggressive chances on the Q side.

35.Rd1 Nc6-a7

The Knight is to be brought to b5, where it threatens, as the occasion arises, to sacrifice itself on c3.

36.Rd1-a1 Kb7

37.Ng5-h7 Rf8-f7

38.Nh7-g5 Rf7-f8

39.Ng5-h7 Rf8-f7

40.Ra1-a4 ...

White is not content with a draw, as half a point means that his opponent will be sure of first prize. This was a last-round game, when Réti had 9 points, Vidmar 8 and Breyer and Schlechter (who by this time had already completed their last-round game) 71/2 each.

40. ... Na7-b5

41.Nh7-g5 Rf7-f8

42.Ra4-a1 Kb6

43.Kf2 Kb7

44.Ke3 Kb6

45.Rd1 Kc6

46.Ra1 Kb7

47.Rd1 Kc6

48.Rd2 ...

With the idea of attacking the g7 pawn by Rh2-h7.

48. ... Nb5-a7

49.Rd2-h2 Na7-c8

Again Black rightly plumps for counter-attack in preference to passive defence. If now 50.Rh7 Nb6 51.Rxg7 Na4 , and White's b2 pawn falls, when Black's advanced b3 pawn cannot be stopped.

50.Kd2 Nb6

51.Kc1? ...

Obsessed with the weakness of his QN pawn, White goes over to the defensive into a lost game. Correct, as nearly always in positions where both sides are saddled with pawn weaknesses, was counterattack. Instead of the text move, White should play 51.Ke3 , and if 51...Na4 52.Nh7 Rf7 53.Ng5 , with a draw by repetition of moves.

51. ... Ra8

52.Kb1 Ra2

53.Nh3 Nxh3

54.Bxh3 Na4

55.g5 ...

Freeing his Bishop, but too late to influence the result of the game. If 55.Rg2 g5 , and White is in zugzwang, since a King move loses after Ra1+.

55. ... Nxc3+

56.Kc1 Nb5

57.Rg2 ...

If 57.Bxe6 c3 , wins at once for Black.

57. ... Nxd4

58.f4 c3!

Black plays the ending with beautiful energy.

59.b2xc3 Ne2+

60.Rxe2 ...

If 60.Kb1 Rc2 , and Black's threat of Nxc3+, followed by b3-b2+, can only be averted by giving up the exchange.

60. ... Rxe2

61.Bxe6 Kc5

62.Bc8 d4

63.cxd4+ Kxd4

64.Bf5 Kc3

65.Kd1 Rd2+

66.Ke1 b2

67.e6 Rc2

Resigns

A noteworthy and instructive ending.

BUDAPEST, 1918
Ruy Lopez, Schliemann Defence
Z. von Balla — Réti

1.e4 e5

2.Nf3 Nc6

3.Bb5 f5

Throughout his career, Réti was fond of trying experimental and risky variations against what Dr. Tartakower has so aptly termed 'the Spanish torture'. Of all the lines he essayed, this was certainly the liveliest.

4.d3 ...

A move that has been unjustly criticised. Hitherto 4.Nc3 has been recommended as much superior, but some Russian analysis has shown that Black has plenty of resources in this line, e.g. 4.Nc3 fe 5.Nxe4 d5 6.Nxe5 de 7.Nxc6 bc 8.Bxc6+ Bd7 9.Qh5+ Ke7 10.Qe5+ Be6 11.Bxa8 Qxa8 12.Qxc7+ Bd7 and chances are about even.

4. ... Ng8-f6

5.O-O Bc5

Black is almost too much inspired with the spirit of aggression, but he does not like to shut his Bishop in by 5...d6 , which is the only reasonable alternative.

6.Bc4 ...

Missing the most vigorous reply to Black's last move. He should play 6.Nxe5 Nxe5 7.d4 , and if 7...Bb6 8.de Nxe4 9.Nd2 Nxd2 10.Bxd2 , with the better game. If then, for instance, 10...O-O 11.Bc4+ Kh8 12.Bb4 Re8 13.Qh5 Rxe5 14.Bc3 , with a very strong attack in return for the pawn.

6. ... d6

7.Nf3-g5 ...

This meets with a startling reply. White soon pays dearly for his neglect of the basic rule of development. Instead, he should play 7.Bg5 , and if 7...Qe7 8.Nc3 .

7. ... f5-f4!

8.Ng5-f7 ...

If 8.Bf7+ Ke7 , and White will have to waste a move retreating the Bishop to b3, owing to the threat of 9...h6 .

8. ... Qe7

9.Nf7xh8 Bg4

White is temporarily a Rook up, but all Black's minor pieces are developed, whereas White has not yet started to get his Q side pieces into action. The manner in which Réti uses his advance in development is extremely entertaining.

10.Qd2 Nd4

Threatening 11...Nf3+ 12.gf Bxf3 13.h3 (otherwise Black plays Qd7 and Qh3) 13...Ng4 14.hg Qh4 , with mate to follow.

11.Kh1 ...

Preparing to safeguard his position by f2-f3, which he could not do before owing to the discovered check.

11. ... Nd4-f3!

12.Qa5 ...

He is mated after 12.gf Bxf3+ 13.Kg1 Nxe4 14.de Qg5# .

12. ... Nf6xe4!

13.g3 ...

If 13.de Qh4 14.h3 Bxh3 , and again mate cannot be averted.

13. ... Ne4xf2+

14.Rf1xf2 Bxf2

15.Kg2 ...

An attractive picture (from the Black point of view) is given by the mate with two Bishops after 15.Nd2 Nxd2 16.Bxd2 Bf3# .

15. ... f4xg3

16.h2xg3 Bxg3!

The death blow; White's King is now wide open to attack from the assembled black pieces whilst his own forces remain neatly tucked away at home on the Queen side.

17.Qb5+ c6

18.Qb4 Qh4

19.Bf7+ Ke7

20.Qxb7+ Kf6

Resigns

A charming miniature and a triumph of mind over matter.

STOCKHOLM, 1919
Petroff Defence
Réti — R. Spielmann

1.e4 e5

2.Nf3 Nf6

3.d4 ...

A pleasant change from the more usual 3.Nxe5 .

3. ... Nxe4

4.Bd3 d5

5.Nxe5 Bd6

If 5...Be7 6.O-O O-O 7.c4 Nf6 8.Nc3 c6 9.Qb3 , and White has the better game.

6.O-O O-O

7.Nc3 ...

More likely to retain the initiative is 7.c4 , and if 7...c6 8.Nc3 .

7. ... Bxe5

Best; bad for Black is 7...Nc6 8.Nxe4 de 9.Bxe4 Nxe5 10.de Bxe5 11.Bxh7+ Kxh7 12.Qh5+ , when White wins a pawn; and though after 7...Nxc3 8.bc Bxe5 9.de , White's pawns are disrupted on the Q side, he has ample compensation in the possession of two Bishops in such an open position.

As played, White still obtains two Bishops, but Black has a firm hold on the centre.

8.d4xe5 f5

9.Qh5 Be6

10.Rf1-d1 ...

Threatening to win a pawn by 11.Bxe4 fe 12.Nxe4 .

10. ... Qe8

11.Qh4 c6

Black would do better to attack White's centre pawn at once by 11...Nc6 and if 12.Bb5 a6 or if 12.Bf4 Ne7 , threatening 13...Ng6 .

12.Be3 Nd7

13.f4 Qf7

14.Ne2 Kh8

Black has formed the unhappy plan of a counter-attack on the K side by a regrouping of his major pieces, together with an eventual advance of the KN pawn (g5-g5). But all this takes up much time, and meanwhile White gets ahead with an attack down the central lines.

Black's best counter-chance lies in an advance of the centre pawns by 14...c5 , and if 15.Bb5 Nb6 .

15.Rd1-f1 Rf8-g8

16.Bxe4 d5xe4

If 16...fe 17.f5 Bxf5 18.g4 , winning a piece. But now White can occupy the "d" file with a crippling effect on Black's game.

17.b3 ...

A wise precaution; otherwise Black plays Bc4, exchanging off a Bishop that has little scope owing to the nature of the pawn structure. In addition, he prepares to take away the square d5 from Black by c2-c4.

17. ... c5

18.Ra1-d1 b6

19.Rd6 Nf8

20.Rf1-d1 Ng6

21.Qe1 Ne7

22.c4 Ra8-e8

23.Nc3 g5

Achieving his much-blunted purpose: it is White rather than Black who profits from this opening up of lines.

24.fxg5 Ng6

25.Bc1! ...

A strong move; the Bishop is switched over to the long diagonal so as to take advantage of Black's self-weakening g5.

25. ... Nxe5

26.Ne2 Bc8

If 26...Nd3 27.R1xd3 ed 28.Bb2+ Rg7 29.Nf4 Bc8 30.Bxg7+ Kxg7 31.Qc3+ Kg8 32.Nd5 , with the deadly threat of Nf6+.

27.h4 Rg6

28.Bb2 Kg8

Black's desire to escape from the pin as soon as possible is easy to understand; nevertheless, he would do better to exchange off at least one pair of Rooks by 28...Rxd6 .

29.Bxe5 Rxe5

30.Rd8+ Re8

31.Nf4 ...

For now White can utilise the awkward position of Black's Rook on g6 to gain an all-important tempo for bringing the Knight to d5.

31. ... Rc6

32.Nd5! ...

Conclusive; once the Knight gets to f6, Black can resign.

32. ... Rxd8

33.Nf6+ Kg7

34.Rxd8 Qe6

35.Rg8+ Qxg8

Or 35...Kf7 36.Qd1 , threatening both Qh5+ and Qd8.

36.Nxg8 Kxg8

37.Qd2 ...

Resigns White threatens to win a piece by 38.Qd5+ Re6 39.Qd8+ , whilst if 37...Kg7 38.Qd5 Rg6 39.h5 Rxg5 40.Qd8 , and again Black loses a piece.

ROTTERDAM, 1919
Kings Gambit Declined
Réti — Speyer

1.e4 e5

2.f4 Bc5

3.Nf3 d6

4.c3 Nc6

See Game No. 9 for 4...Bg4 . But Speyer's move is still not the best. Correct is 4...Nf6 , since as played White soon gains complete control of the centre.

5.Bb5! ...

A pin with uncomfortable consequences for Black, since he must also take into account an eventual d2-d4-d5 by White.

5. ... Bd7

6.d4 exd4

7.cxd4 Bb6

Checking with the Bishop brings only momentary alleviation, e.g. 7...Bb4+ 8.Nc3 Nf6 9.e5 Ne4 10.Qc2 d5 11.O-O Bf5 12.Bxc6+ bc 13.Qa4 , and Black is already in a bad way.

8.Nb1-c3 Ng8-e7

9.f4-f5 ...

Subtle play; he foresees that Black's best counter would be an eventual f7-f5, and hastens to eliminate the possibility. At the same time a line is opened for his Q Bishop that is to prove extremely useful as the game proceeds.

Many years later (in the U. S. A. Championship Tournament between Santasiere and Reshevsky, 1946), the same line was played up to this point; but now White played the simpler and more obvious 9.O-O O-O 10.Kh1 Bg4 11.d5 Nd4 12.Be2 Nxe2 13.Qxe2 f5 , and the game was agreed a draw on the 22nd move. The contrast between this humdrum handling and Réti's superb dash is very marked.

9. ... Nc6-a5

Trying, by a manoeuvre reminiscent of the normal defence to the Ruy Lopez, to free his game by exchanges. But the sole result is to leave his Knight high and dry on the edge of the board and, though not entirely satisfactory, 9...O-O must be played.

10.Bd3 ...

He is not going to be so obliging as to fall in with Black's wishes with 10.Bxd7+ .

10. ... c5

11.O-O cxd4

12.Nd5 ...

Threatening 13.f6 .

12. ... f6

For if 12...Nxd5 13.ed , and Black cannot save his other Knight in view of the threat of b5. White now destroys his opponent by a couple of hammer-blows.

13.Nf3-g5 O-O

If 13...fg 14.Bxg5 Nac6 15.f6 , and wins.

14.Qh5! fxg5

Nor does refusal of the sacrifice help Black, for if 14...h6 15.Ne6 Bxe6 16.fe Qe8 17.Qg4 , with an attack that cannot be stemmed.

15.Bxg5 Rf8-f7

16.Nxe7+ Rxe7

17.Qh4 Nc6

18.Bc4+ Kh8

19.f6 gxf6

20.Bxf6+ ...

Resigns

After 20...Rg7 Black is mated in six moves.

AMSTERDAM, 1920
Two Knights' Defence
DR. M. Euwe — Réti

1.e4 e5

2.Nf3 Nc6

3.Bc4 Nf6

An excellent defence that, with its immediate counter-attack, was well suited to Réti's enterprising style.

4.d4 ...

4.Ng5 d5 , despite some recent attempts at improving and rehabilitating the line for White, is still recognised as being in Black's favour, since the latter soon holds the initiative.

4. ... exd4

Very bad for Black is 4...Nxd4 5.Bxf7+ Kxf7 6.Nxe5+ , etc., and White also has the better game after 4...Nxe4 5.de .

5.O-O ...

Against either 5.Ng5 or 5.e5 Black has the good and vigorous reply: 5...d5!

5. ... Nf6xe4

6.Re1 d5

7.Bxd5 ...

If 7.Nc3 , not 7...dxc4 8.Rxe4+ , when White has the attack; but 7...Be6 and if 8.Nxe4 dc 9.Neg5 Be7 10.Nxe6 fe 11.Rxe6 O-O , with advantage to Black.

7. ... Qxd5

8.Nc3 Qa5

Best; though, as Game No. 12 has shown, 8...Qd8 is also quite playable.

9.Nxd4 ...

After this inferior move, Black soon obtains the upper hand through a few powerful strokes. Correct is 9.Nxe4 Be6 10.Neg5 O-O-O 11.Nxe6 fe 12.Rxe6 Bd6 , and though Black still has the preferable game, White is not without counter-chances and his position is, at any rate, much better than that he gets after the move played in the game.

9. ... Nxd4

10.Qxd4 f5

11.Bg5 ...

With the quite natural assumption that 12.Qd8+ is a formidable continuation; but the course of the game shows the weakness of this threat, and White should have contented himself with 11.Bd2 Qb6 12.Qxb6 ab 13.f3 Bc5+ 14.Kh1 Be6 15.fe O-O-O 16.Bg5 Rde8 , when Black's two Bishops give him the better chances, but White has drawing possibilities.

11. ... Qc5!

12.Qd8+ Kf7

13.Nxe4 fxe4

14.Ra1-d1 ...

If 14.Rxe4 Bf5 exchanges Rooks with a simple win for Black.

14. ... Bd6!

The celerity with which Black manages to bring both his Bishops into action is quite remarkable. With the text move he sacrifices a Rook, but by getting White's Bishop in return remains with material as well as positional superiority.

15.Qxh8 Qxg5

16.f4 ...

After 16.Qxh7 Bf5 17.h4 Qg4 White loses his Queen.

16. ... Qh4

Not 16...Qxf4 17.Rf1 nor 16...Bxf4 17.Rd8 , in both cases with a win for White.

17.Rxe4 ...

Probably White was expecting here 17...Bf5 , on a parallel with the continuation given above after White's 14th move; instead of which there comes a most beautiful and crushing blow. However, if White tries to strengthen his K side by 17.g3 , Black wins by 17...Bc5+ 18.Kh1 Bg4 19.Rf1 Qh5 .

17. ... Bh3!

A fine combination that forces mate.

18.Qxa8 Bc5+

19.Kh1 Bxg2+!

Black must operate incessantly with check as otherwise White himself has a mating series of checks, starting with Qe8+.

20.Kxg2 Qg4+

21.Kf1 Qf3+

22.Ke1 Qf2#

mate

AMSTERDAM, 1920
Queen's Pawn, Dutch Defence, Staunton Gambit
Réti — Dr. M. Euwe

1.d4 f5

2.e4 ...

A gambit that gives rise to lively play in which White has adequate compensation for the pawn sacrifice in the open diagonals for his Bishops.

2. ... f5xe4

3.Nc3 Nf6

4.Bg5 ...

The best continuation of the attack here is the immediate advance of the KBP, e.g. 4.f3 ef 5.Nxf3 d5 6.Ne5 , with an excellent aggressive position for White, or 4.f3 d5 5.Bg5 Bf5 6.fe de 7.Bc4 , and again White has fine attacking chances.

4. ... g6

Defence of the KP by 4...d5 is bad because of 5.Bxf6 ef 6.Qh5+ g6 7.Qxd5 , when White must win a pawn. But the best move here is 4...Nc6 and if 5.d5 Ne5 6.Qd4 Nf7 , and Black has a good game. Or if 5.f3 e5 6.d5 Nd4 7.fe Be7 8.Bc4 d6 9.Nge2 Ng4 10.Nxd4 Bxg5 , and Black has a strong counter-attack (Barda-Rossolimo, Hastings, 1949-1950).

5.f2-f3 e4xf3

Forced; if 5...d5 6.fe de 7.Bc4 Nc6 8.Nge2 , with advantage to White.

6.Ng1xf3 Bf8-g7

7.Bf1-d3 c7-c5?

Black has conceived the unhappy idea of making a sortie with his Queen so as to attack White's b2 pawn. Far preferable is 7...d5 , and if 8.Qd2 Nc6 9.O-O-O Nb4 , with considerable counter-play for Black.

8.d4-d5 Qd8-b6

He should have remembered that neglect of development is fatal in an open position (White has all his minor pieces in play, whilst Black's Q side pieces are unmoved) and played here 8...d6 .

9.Qd1-d2 Qb6xb2?

Relying (wrongly) on his next move to interrupt the smooth working of White's pieces. 9...d6 was still the best move.

10.Ra1-b1 Nf6xd5

Position after 10...Nxd5

After 10...Qa3 11.Nb5 Qxa2 12.O-O Na6 White wins by 13.Ra1 and if 13...Qxd5 14.Rxa6 , but the text move, which reveals the action of Black's strong KB, appears to be more than adequate — were it not for a combination that Réti must have had in mind for some time.

11.Nc3xd5! ...

A double Rook sacrifice that makes this game a perfect counterpart of the other match game given above. 11.Rxb2 Bxc3 would in fact lose for White.

11. ... Qb2xb1+

12.Ke1-f2 Qb1xh1

Not much better is 12...Qxa2 13.Bxe7 Qxd5 14.Bxg6+ hg 15.Qxd5 Kxe7 16.Re1+ , when, though Black is nominally material to the good, he has a lost game, as he will be unable to develop his Q side pieces before his King succumbs to the attack of White's three pieces.

13.Bg5xe7 d7-d6

White was threatening 14.Qe2 , followed by a fatal discovered check.

14.Be7xd6 Nb8-c6

15.Bd3-b5 ...

White's handling of the final attack is very instructive; he removes the only piece with which Black can make some attempt to defend himself.

15. ... Bc8-d7

Réti — A. Selesniev

1.e4 c6

Réti was himself much addicted to this defence, and it is interesting to see how he deals with one of his own favourite lines.

2.d4 d5

3.Nc3 dxe4

4.Nxe4 Nf6

Preferable is the other main line here, 4...Bf5 .

5.Ng3 ...

For now White could have forced on Black the choice between spoiling his pawn formation after 5.Nxf6+ by 5...gf or else being saddled with a position that would eventually lead to an inferior ending after 5...ef .

5. ... e6

Black should not voluntarily shut in his QB, one of the important differences between this and other defences being precisely the opportunity for developing this piece.

Quite a good line for Black (and one that has the advantage of having been rarely tried here) is 5...Qc7 or he may prefer the rather dull equality that results from 5...e5 6.Nf3 ed 7.Nxd4 Be7 8.Be2 O-O 9.O-O Bc5 10.Ndf5 Qxd1 , etc.

6.Nf3 Bd6

The start of a bad plan that soon compromises his game. Correct was 6...c5 .

7.Bd3 Nb8-d7

8.Qe2 Qe7

The black Queen's best place in the Caro-Kann is on QB2; on K2 it serves no useful purpose and indeed is liable to attack by White's Knight (after he has played Re8 and so pinned the KP). To play 8...Qc7 at once would also be bad on account of 9.Nf5 , and this threat probably induced him to make the text move. However, simply 8...O-O was best.

9.O-O O-O

10.Ne4! Nxe4

Another disadvantage of having the Queen on K2 now becomes apparent, as the natural defensive move, 10...Be7, is impossible.

11.Qxe4 f5?

A serious weakening of his K3, of which Réti takes exemplary advantage. Preferable was 11...g6 , though White would still have a fine game after 12.Bg5 .

12.Qe2 c5

He cannot get rid of his backward pawn by 12...e5 , on account of 13.Bc4+ , followed by 14.Ng5 with the threat of 15.Qh5 .

13.Re1 Nb6

So as to prevent Bc5.

14.d4xc5 Bxc5

15.a3 ...

15. ... Qf6

16.c4 Bd7

17.Bg5 Qg6

18.b4 Bd4

Losing a pawn; but other moves cost a piece.

20.Nxe6 Bxe6

21.Qxe6+ Kh8

22.c5 Nb6-c8

KIEL, 1921
French Defence
E. D. Bogoljuboff — Réti

1.e4 e6

2.d4 d5

3.Nc3 Nf6

4.Bg5 Bb4

The MacCutcheon Variation, at the moment out of favour, as most players prefer to play Bb4 a move earlier for Black. Nevertheless, it is a good line offering excellent prospects to a vigorous player and will certainly come back into fashion some time or other.

5.e5 h6

6.Bd2 Bxc3

White gets a strong attack after 6...Nfd7 7.Qg4 , whilst 6...Ne4 loses a piece by 7.Nxe4 .

7.Bxc3 ...

Bogoljuboff tries a variation from the normal 7.bc Ne4 8.Qg4 in the hope of unsettling bis opponent by taking him away from the 'book'; vain hope, as Réti now proceeds to find the very best move time after time.

7. ... Ne4

8.Bb4 ...

If 8.Bd3 Nxc3 9.bc c5 , or if 8.Bd2 c5 9.Be3 Nc6 10.c3 Qb6 (Duras-Prokes, Vienna, 1907), in both cases with a good game for Black.

8. ... c5!

9.Bxc5 ...

Not 9.dc Nxf2! 10.Kxf2 Qh4+ .

9. ... Ne4xc5

Best. Four years later (at Baden-Baden, 1925) Réti was to try against the same opponent 9...Nc6 10.Ba3 Qa5+ , but after 11.c3 Nxc3 12.Qd2 Na4 13.Qxa5 Nxa5 14.b3 Nc3 15.Nf3 , White's strong Bishops gave him a clear advantage.

10.d4xc5 Qa5+

Black also stands well after 10...Nd7 11.Qd4 Qc7 12.Nf3 Nxc5 (Spielmann — Nimzowitsch, Gothenburg, 1920), but the text move is more ambitious, since it plans to gain eventual control of K4.

11.c2-c3 Qxc5

12.Qd4 Qc7!

The Queen exchange would give rise to a position that would be even in White's favour as he would be the first to occupy the QB file with his Rooks.

13.Ng1-f3 Nb8-c6

14.Bf1-b5 O-O

15.Bxc6 ...

White makes this exchange in the belief that (a) he can hold back Black's QBP and so make Black's Q Bishop an indifferent piece, and (b) he can maintain his hold on K5, thereby keeping the black Rooks out of the game. Both ideas are mistaken, and instead he should play 15.Qe3 .

15. ... bxc6

16.b4? ...

Whether he likes it or not, he must let Black get in c5 and Castle K at once. After Black's reply, White's King has no safe refuge.

16. ... Ba6!

17.Kd2 Ra8-c8

18.Qc5 f6!

The commencement of White's disillusionment: K5 will not be under his control for long.

19.Rh1-e1 fxe5

20.Nf3xe5 ...

Not 20.Rxe5 Rxf3 .

20. ... Rf8-f5!

21.Qe3 ...

White must abandon his other point of vantage, for if 21.Nf3 e5 , threatening 22...e4 and 23...Qf4+ .

21. ... c5

22.Ra1-c1 ...

If 22.bc Qa5 23.Nd3 Rf6 24.a4 d4 , and Black wins.

22. ... c5xb4

23.c3xb4 ...

It seems that now White has contrived to force the exchange of a pair of Rooks the fury of Black's onslaught has been stemmed; but White's position is like a house of cards that can be upset by one flip of the hand and this Réti now provides.

23. ... d4!

Note the progress of this pawn. White cannot take it because of 24.Qxd4 Rxf2+! 25.Qxf2 (or 25.Ke3 Rc2 ), 25...Qd6+ 26.Ke3 Qxe5+ 27.Kd2 Qd5+ 28.Ke3 Qd3+ 29.Kf4 Rf8+ , etc.

24.Qg3 Qd6

25.Rc1xc8+ Bxc8

26.Nd3 Qd5

27.Rc1 ...

Gaining a momentary breathing space, for now 27...Qxa2+ would lose the initiative because of 28.Rc2 .

27. ... Ba6

28.Nd3-c5 d3!

29.Nc5-b3 ...

Other Knight moves also lose, e.g. 29.Nxa6 Qxa2+ 30.Kxd3 Qxa6+ 31.Ke3 Qa3+ , or 29.Nxd3 Bxd3 30.Qxd3 Rxf2+ .

29. ... Qe4

30.Qb8+ ...

A desperate decentralisation; but he is helpless against the threats of 30...Qe2+ and 30...Qxb4+ .

30. ... Rf5-f8

31.Qxa7 Qe2+

32.Kc3 d2!

33.Rg1 Rf8-c8+

34.Nb3-c5 Qc4+

35.Kb2 ...

Or 35.Kxd2 Rd8+ , etc.
