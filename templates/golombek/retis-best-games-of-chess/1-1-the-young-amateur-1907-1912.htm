I
THE YOUNG AMATEUR, 1907-1912

The earliest years of Réti's chess career are chiefly divided between Hungarian national tournaments and events in Vienna, where he went to study at the University. He took part in three international tournaments — Vienna, 1908, Prague, 1908, and San Remo, 1911 — with poor and even, in the first case, with disastrous results. But in all these events there were signs of the great master to come. At Vienna he had a long, stoutly fought draw against Duras, one of the joint winners of the tournament; whilst at San Remo he won well against the veteran Gunsberg and also against the strong Polish master, Przepiórka.

In this earliest stage he was almost entirely a King's Pawn player as regards the openings — with the one exception of the Hungarian National Tournament at Székesfehérvár, where he played only Queen's Gambits. As White he tried Ruy Lopez and King's Gambit variations with equal impartiality, and as Black he cheerfully allowed his opponent to play any form of K side opening with a Renaissance curiosity becoming to a young player. In this department one cannot discern any real foreshadowing of the original ideas with which Réti was later to enrich opening theory.

As a player it was already clear that he possessed great combinational gifts and that no risk, however great, would deter him from embarking on any adventurous course that held out interesting prospects. He was as yet far from mature positionally, but already had that innate mastery of the endgame that infallibly marks out all great players from their very start. An instance of this is to be found in the first game given here against Barász (this last should not be confused with the later player, Baratz, at present living in Paris).

The opening of Game No. 2 seems like a struggle between two schools — that of Tarrasch for White and that of Steinitz for Black. The latter, by a Gilbertian paradox, though earlier in time, is clearly later in spirit and style. The middle game, conducted with a fine fresh vigour by the winner, shows Réti making combinational rings round his victim. Réti's incisive qualities in attack, both in the centre and on the K side are well exemplified by Game No. 3, in which Black must have been extremely puzzled to find a storm blowing up so suddenly out of a conventional opening.

It is entertaining to see Réti, in Game No. 4, playing a defence that he was later to discredit by the invention of a system that has practically banished the Tarrasch Defence from master play. Here is a case where he takes any number of risks so as to win, perhaps rather unjustifiably, a game that has some pretty combinational touches. The sudden death meted out to Black in Game No. 5, with its fine finishing combination, is an excellent illustration of the superiority of a central attack over that on the flank.

Réti's adversary in the next game, Franz Chalupetzky, was better known as a journalist and producer of hooks on chess than as a player. He was for many years joint editor of the Magyar Sakkvilag, and succeeded in making it one of the leading chess magazines of his time. The Muzio with which Réti beats him was one of Réti's favourite variations of the King's Gambit and a line which well suited his devil-may-care attitude towards the openings at this time.

We now come to the first internationally played game of this book — that against Gunsberg, Again, as in Game No. 2, it seems as though we are watching two schools clashing. Whilst Réti is still inspired by Steinitz, Gunsberg appears to have an even earlier era in mind — that of Anderssen. The admirable strategy by which Black switches his attack from one wing to the other makes the game valuable from the point of view of a consideration of Réti's progress as a player.

The two games from Temesvar confirm this advance. No. 8 has a strong swift counter-attack and No. 9 shows him exerting enduring pressure on the weak points of his opponent.

Since White's kNight is not yet on c3 (and hence White is not yet in a position to play e3-e4) this aggressive move is better than 6...Be7 .

8.e4 ...

If 8.b3 , then Black gets a good game by 8...dc 9.bc e5 .

9.Nxe4 Nxe4

10.Bxe4 f5

Black has a difficult position, but this move worsens it, resulting as it does in a backward KP.

Clearly he cannot play 10...e5 on account of 11.de Nxe5 12.Nxe5 Bxe5 13.Bxh7+ Kxh7 14.Qh5+ , winning a pawn. Both 10...Nf6 and 10...c5 are open to the objection that White replies Bc2, with the eventual threat of Qd3 always providing White with a fierce K side attack.

TREBITSCH TOURNAMENT, VIENNA, 1909
Vienna Game
J. Krejcik — Réti

1.e4 e5

2.Nc3 Nf6

3.g3 ...

A slow and ineffective form of the Vienna much favoured by Mieses.

3. ... Nc6

Not the best; Black should take advantage of White's snail-paced development to open up the game with 3...d5!

4.Bg2 d6

This gives Black a solid if somewhat cramped position. Instead, he can well play 4...Bc5 5.Nge2 d6 with an equal game and not so constricted a position as after the text.

However, it will be seen as the game goes on that it is part of Black's policy to play a la Steinitz and let White wreck himself on the sturdy rocks of his defence.

5.Nge2 Be7

The formation g7-g6 and Bg7 would be bad here, since White would eventually play f2-f4, when Black's pawn on g6 would offer White a natural target by f4-f5.

6.O-O O-O

7.d4 exd4

8.Nxd4 Nxd4

He must not allow 9.Nxc6 bc 10.e5 , whilst if 8...Bd7 9.Nd5 gains further ground for White.

9.Qxd4 Re8

10.b3 ...

An excellent manoeuvre which is no doubt a reminiscence of the Tarrasch method of dealing with the Steinitz Defence to the Ruy Lopez. The QB has no good square on the c1-h6 diagonal, whereas it is very powerful on the long diagonal.

10. ... Bf8

11.Bb2 Qe7

So as to be in command of the open file should White play 12.Nd5 Nxd5 13.ed .

12.f4 c6

A move out of its time. In these days players cheerfully create backward pawns, isolani and the very pawn formation that Black has here; but in 1909, when the classical school was supreme, such a move must have sent shudders down the spine of the theorists.

13.Ba3 ...

White comprehensibly enough at once fastens on to this weakness (as he thinks it), and intends to exploit it so as to obtain a winning attack.

Nevertheless, the best way of meeting the problem presented by Black is to leave the QB where it is, and play 13.Rae1 , followed by Nc3-d1-f2 and c2-c4.

13. ... Qc7

Meeting the tactical threat of 14.e5 .

14.Rad1 Rd8

15.e5 ...

White cheerfully presses on, quite without realising that he is giving hostages to Fortune. The best move now was to restore his B to its correct square with Bb2.

15. ... Ne8

Black recoils to great effect; he threatens 16...Bg4 and 17...de .

16.f5 ...

White is alive to the threat mentioned in the previous note, and hastens to cramp in Black's QB. If, instead, 16.Ne4 , 16...Bg4 17.ed Bxd1 , and Black wins.

Strong though the text move looks, it is in reality a mistake, and White should content himself with the simple 16.ed . For now there comes a nerve-shattering blow.

16. ... Bxf5

17.Rxf5 ...

If 17.ed Nxd6 18.Bxd6 Rxd6 and Black is a good pawn up, with the better game.

17. ... dxe5

18.Qa4 b5

19.Qa6 Bxa3

White has good fighting chances after 19...b4 20.Bxc6 bc 21.Re1 Bxa3 22.Bxa8 Bc5+ 23.Kg2 Rxa8 24.Rfxe5 .

20.Qxc6? ...

After this, which exposes the Queen to further attack, White loses rapidly. A much better fight would have been made by 20.Qxa3 Qb6+ 21.Kh1 b4 22.Qc1 bc 23.Rxe5 , though Black would still have the upper hand by 23...Nf6 , with the strong threat of Ng4.

20. ... Rxd1+

21.Nxd1 Rd8

22.Nc3 Bc5+

23.Kf1 ...

23.Kh1 , is just as bad, e.g. 23...b4 24.Qxc7 Nxc7 25.Ne4 Rd1+ 26.Bf1 Bd4 , when Black's threat of Nd5 and Ne3 cannot well be met.

23. ... b4

24.Nb5 Qe7

25.Bd5 Nf6

26.Bc4 a6!

A very pretty diversionary sacrifice in order to gain the square d7 for the Queen.

27.Qxa6 ...

He must take the pawn, since 27.Nc7 Rc8 28.Nd5 Rxc6 29.Nxe7+ Bxe7 merely loses a piece for White.

27. ... Qd7!

28.Bd3 ...

White's King is past saving after 28.Rxe5 Qh3+ 29.Ke1 Qxh2 . Now Black finishes off the game with a few incisive strokes.

28. ... e4

29.Rxc5 exd3

30.Nd6 ...

What can White do? If (a) 30.Qc6 Qh3+ 31.Qg2 Qxg2+ 32.Kxg2 d2 , making another Queen, or (b) 30.Qd6 Qh3+ winning the Queen, or (c) 30.cd Qxd3+ 31.Kg2 Qe4+ 32.Kh3 Qg4+ 33.Kg2 Rd2+ 34.Kg1 Qd1 mate.

30. ... Qh3+

31.Ke1 ...

Or, if he likes, he can get into a mating net by 31.Kg1 Ng4 .

31. ... Qe6+

Resigns

Not because of 32.Kf1 Qxd6 33.Qxd6 Rxd6 34.Rc8+ , and White mates, but because of 32.Kf1 Qe2+ 33.Kg1 Ng4 , and we are looking, from another angle, at the mating net mentioned in the note after White's 31st move.

Ruy Lopez, Berlin Defence
Réti — Dr. P. Meitner

2.Nf3 Nc6

3.Bb5 Nf6

This defence, at the cost of some insecurity in pawn position, secures Black quite a free game.

4.O-O Nxe4

5.d4 ...

Both 5.Qe2 and 5.Re1 can be met by 5...Nd6 , with a good game for Black.

5. ... Be7

Not now 5...Nd6 6.Bxc6 , and after either 6...dc or 6...bc White gets the upper hand by 7.de .

6.Qe2 ...

White's attack soon fades out after 6.d5 Nd6 7.Bxc6 dc 8.dc f6 .

6. ... Nd6

After 6...f5 7.de O-O 8.Nc3 Nxc3 9.Qc4+ Kh8 10.Qxc3 , White has the better game owing to his command of greater space.

7.Bxc6 bxc6

7...dxc6 8.de Nf5 9.Rd1 Bd7 10.e6 fe 11.Ne5 leads to a considerable advantage for White.

8.dxe5 Nb7

To the player innocent of opening theory this must seem a queer place for the N; but it is the best. If instead 8...Nf5 9.Qe4!

9.Nc3 O-O

9...Nc5 , with the threat of 10...Ba6 , looks good, but then comes 10.Nd4 Ba6 11.Qg4 Bxf1 12.Qxg7 Rf8 13.Kxf1 Ne6 14.Nxe6 fe 15.Qxh7 and White has a won game.

10.Nd4 ...

Tempting Black to advance his Q side pawns and so weaken them. Stronger still, however, is 10.Re1 , and if 10...Nc5 11.Nd4 Ne6 12.Be3 Nxd4 13.Bxd4 c5 14.Be3 d5 15.ed Bxd6 16.f4 Bf5 17.Qf2 , with lasting pressure on Black's Q side.

10. ... c5?

Just the reaction White had hoped to provoke. Now Black's Q side is most vulnerable, and it is instructive to see how White's moves flow on from this point in one clear, logical line to an overwhelming finish. Instead of the text, Black should hasten to free his position by exchange with 10...Bc5 and if 11.Rd1 Bxd4 .

11.Nf5 d6

This provides a fresh weakness along the long diagonal; but how else can Black deal with the threat of 12.Qg4 ? If 11...g6 12.Nxe7+ Qxe7 13.Nd5 Qh4 14.g3 Qh3 15.Nf4 Qf5 16.g4 Qg5 17.Ne6 , winning the exchange.

12.exd6 Bxd6

13.Nxd6 cxd6

Contrast Black's position with that attained in Game No. 2. There indeed Black appeared to have a similar weakened pawn structure; but he still retained the KB — a vital piece that made all the difference.

14.Qf3! ...

A very strong move which intends a double attack — on the weak Q side pawns, and on Black's K side where Black has no minor piece to defend his King.

14. ... Rb8

Hoping, rather faintly, for a counter-attack along the b file.

15.Bf4! Be6

16.Rad1 Qb6

17.Qg3 ...

Threatening to win the exchange by 18.Bh6 .

17. ... Kh8

18.Ne4 ...

Winning a pawn, but there is worse to follow.

18. ... Rbd8

The Rooks must avoid forking possibilities by White's Bishop.

If, instead, 18...Qxb2 19.Nxd6 Qxc2 (White threatened both 20.Be5 and 20.Rb1 ) 20.Nxf7+ , winning the exchange.

19.Nxd6 Rd7

20.Ne4 Rfd8

Natural and disastrous; he must play 20...Rxd1 21.Rxd1 f6 , with, it is true, a very bad game, but he is still alive. Now there is a quick and neat finish.

21.Rxd7 Rxd7

22.Be5! f6

23.Nxf6! Qd8

Or 23...Re7 24.Nh5 .

24.Nxd7 Qxd7

25.Re1 ...

Resigns. Black can do nothing; if 25...Bg8 26.Bxg7+ Qxg7 27.Qxg7+ Kxg7 28.Re7+ .

TREBITSCH TOURNAMENT, VIENNA, 1910
Queen's Gambit Declined, Tarrasch Defence
Dr. J. Perlis - Réti

1.d4 d5

2.Nf3 e6

3.c4 c5

4.Nc3 Nf6

5.cxd5 exd5

Up to this last move the opening was really a Semi-Tarrasch Defence, and Black could have safely recaptured with the N here, since 5...Nxd5 6.e4 Nxc3 leaves him with comfortable equality. After the text move we are back in the Tarrasch Defence proper.

6.Bf4 ...

This was early days in the history of the treatment of the Tarrasch Defence, before Réti had evolved his own characteristic method: 6.g3 Nc6 7.Bg2 Be7 8.O-O O-O 9.dc Bxc5 10.Na4 Be7 11.Be3 .

The text puts little difficulty in Black's way.

6. ... Nc6

7.e3 cxd4

8.Nfxd4 Bb4

9.Bb5 ...

More energetic than the continuation in the game, Rubinstein-Nimzovitch, Carlsbad, 1907, 9.Nxc6 bc 10.Bd3 O-O 11.O-O Bd6 12.Bg3 Bxg3 13.hg c5 14.Rc1 Be6 15.Qa4 Qb6 , when Black had an excellent position.

9. ... Qb6

10.O-O Bxc3

White was threatening 11.Qa4 Bxc3 12.Nxc6!

11.bxc3 O-O

12.Rb1 Nxd4

Reluctant as he is to round off White's pawn position, Black is forced to do so, otherwise White wins a piece by 13.Bxc6 .

13.cxd4 Ne4

Black must counter-attack or succumb; if 13...Qa5 14.Bd6 Rd8 15.Be7 , wins the exchange; whilst Black's position is unenviable after 13...Be6 14.Bd7 Qa6 15.Bxe6 fe 16.Qb3 .

14.Bd3 ...

The obvious, but not the strongest move; 14.Qe1 , preventing Black's reply and threatening Bd3, was correct.

14. ... Qa5

15.f3 ...

The commencement of some interesting complications, in which, however, White goes astray.

15. ... Nd2

Bad would be 15...Nc3 16.Qe1 b6 17.Rc1 Nxa2 18.Qh4 , with an overwhelming attack for White, the difference in this variation from the text being that White's QR enters at once into the attack.

16.Qe1 b6

17.Bd6 ...

17. ... Nxf1

18.Qh4 h6

18...f5 19.Bxf8 , would transpose into the losing variation mentioned in the bracketed note above.

19.Bxf8 Ba6!

20.Bxa6? ...

After this Black's N becomes active again and White loses a pawn. Correct is 20.Bxg7 Kxg7 (not 20...Bxd3 21.Qxh6 Bh7 22.Bf6 , followed by mate) 21.Bxa6 Qxa6 22.Rxf1 Qxa2 23.Qg4+ Kh8 or 23...Kf8 24.Qh5 Kg7 25.Qg4+ , and Black must submit to a draw, since he cannot allow his King to be chased into the open.

20. ... Nxe3!

21.Qf2 ...

If he now tries for a mating attack by 21.Bxg7 , then Black gets in first with 21...Qxa2 .

21. ... Kxf8

22.Bd3 ...

A longer resistance is put up by 22.Qxe3 , though White would still have little hope of saving the game after 22...Qxa6 23.Qe5 Qc4 . But the N on e3 has a terrifying agility, as White soon discovers.

VIENNA, 1910
Ruy Lopez, Steinitz Defence
Réti — K. Sterk

1.e4 e5

2.Nf3 Nc6

3.Bb5 Nf6

4.O-O d6

A familiar transposition from the Berlin to the Steinitz Defence.

5.d4 Bd7

6.Nc3 Be7

7.Bg5 ...

The usual move is 7.Re1 , in order to induce Black to play 7...ed and so give up some central control, the point of Re8 being that White puts an extra guard on his K4. However, the text move also has attacking possibilities.

7. ... exd4

Not wholly necessary, as Black can play 7...O-O ; and if 8.Bxc6 Bxc6 9.de de 10.Qxd8 Bxd8 11.Nxe5 Bxe4 12.Bxf6 Bxf6 13.Nd7 Bxc3 14.Nxf8 Bxb2 15.Rab1 Ba3 16.Nd7 f6 , when White's N has no escape.

8.Nxd4 O-O

9.Bxc6 bxc6

10.Qd3 Ng4

This method of freeing Black's position (up to Black's 15th move inclusive) was recommended by Em. Lasker in the book of the 1909 St. Petersburg Tournament. The present game has theoretical importance, since it disposes of Lasker's system in no uncertain fashion.

This being so, Black's best way of relieving his congestion is by 10...h6 11.Bh4 Nh7 12.Bxe7 Qxe7 .

11.Bxe7 Qxe7

12.f4 ...

It is important to control e5 square, for if 12.Rae1 Qh4 13.Nf3 Qh5 14.h3 Ne5 , and Black is out of danger (Em. Lasker-E. Cohn, St. Petersburg, 1909).

12. ... f5

13.Rae1 fxe4

13...Qf7 also leaves the Queen open to attack, as was shown in the sudden death that came to Black in the Schlechter-Havasi game from Kaschau, 1918: 14.h3 fe 15.Nxe4 Nf6 16.Ng5 Qd5 17.c4 Qa5 18.Re7 Rfd8 19.Nxh7 , resigns, since 19...Nxh7 is met by 20.Qg6 .

14.Ncxe4 Qh4

15.h3 Nf6

Thus far Lasker with the judgment that the position is equal; but White's next move throws a bright light on the weakness of Black's e6 square.

16.Ng5! d5?

This makes matters rather worse, as now Black is also weak on c6. Black cannot keep White's pieces out of the vital square e6; for if 16...Rae8 17.Nge6 Bxe6 18.Nxe6 Rf7 or 18...Re7 19.f5 . His safest move therefore is 16...Kh8 .

17.Ng5-e6 Bxe6

18.Rxe6 c5?

Black's moves with his Q side pawns are unfortunate and disastrous; no wonder that his position collapses so suddenly in view of the continual loosening process in which he has indulged. He can do little to save the game in the long run, however. The best chance is to sacrifice a pawn by 18...Ne4 but White retains his advantage even then with 19.Rxc6 Rxf4 20.Rxc7 Rxf1+ 21.Qxf1 Qf6 (White threatened 22.Rxg7+ ) 22.Qxf6 gxf 23.Nf5 , and Black loses another pawn.

19.Nf5 Qh5

20.Rxf6! Resigns

For if 20...Rxf6 21.Qxd5+ , wins the Rook; whilst if 20...gf 21.Qg3+ Kf7 22.Qg7+ Ke6 23.Qe7+ Kxf5 24.h4+ .

The loser of this game may be known to some of my English readers as the player who took part in the Premier Tournament of the London Congress in 1927, where he shared third and fourth prizes with Dr. Seitz.

A strange misdating has arisen with regard to this game in books on opening theory. Dr. Tartakower in his great work The Hyper-modern Game of Chess gives it as having been played in 1916, an error in which he has been slavishly followed by subsequent writers (e.g. Paul Keres in his 1949 book on the openings, and others). The game was actually played in the Cafe Central in Vienna on April 3rd, 1910.

BUDAPEST TOURNAMENT, 1911
King's Gambit Accepted, Muzio Variation
Réti — F. Chalupetzky

1.e4 e5

2.f4 exf4

3.Nf3 g5

4.Bc4 g4

5.O-O ...

The Muzio Gambit with which White sacrifices a whole piece in the interests of development — justifiably so if one takes into consideration how often White has been successful when Black has gone in for this line.

5. ... gxf3

For 5...d5 , see Game No.11.

If White is allowed to play Qxf4 with a triple attack on the vital f7, then clearly Black is lost and he must therefore protect his own f4 square.

First fix, then strike.

2.f4 Bc5

A good but difficult defence to the King's Gambit. It demands great precision and coolness of temperament. Those in search of a peaceful life would be well advised to play 2...ef , followed by d7-d5, whilst the man born to counter-attack can always try the Falkbeer.

3.Nf3 d6

4.c3 ...

More aggressive than 4.Nc3 , and regarded by Spielmann, that great gambit player, as the only line here that will maintain White's initiative.

4. ... Bg4

Inferior to 4...Nf6 , since now White can employ the following ingenious manoeuvre, introduced into master-play by Marshall:

5.fxe5 dxe5

6.Qa4+ Bd7

If 6...Qd7 7.Bb5 c6 8.Nxe5 , winning a pawn; whilst if 6...Nc6 , Spielmann has shown that White gets the better game by 7.Nxe5 Qh4+ 8.g3 Bf2+ 9.Kxf2 Qf6+ 10.Kg1 Qxe5 11.Bg2 .

7.Qc2 Qe7

With the idea of holding the centre and preparing for Q side castling. It is not a happy one and better is 7...Nc6 though White still has a fine game after 8.b4 Bd6 9.Bc4 Nf6 10.d3 , the game Bronstein-Panov, Moscow, 1947, now continued 10...Qe7 11.O-O O-O-O 12.a4 , with a strong attack for White.

10.Nb1-d2 Nb8-c6

Black's only possible counter.
