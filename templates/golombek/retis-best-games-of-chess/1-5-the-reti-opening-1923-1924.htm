V. THE RÉTI OPENING, 1923-1924

This period is marked by an event in the history of opening theory — the introduction of the Réti Opening, which was first played in the game Réti - Grünfeld, Margate, April 4th, 1928. The inauguration of the system was far from auspicious, as Réti lost in thirty-six moves. This was not, however, any fault of the opening, but rather due to a tendency on Réti's part to overpress his attack in the middle game, where he sacrificed a piece for two pawns and then got desperately short of time. Réti had three Whites in this tournament, and the contemporary Press, at a loss for a better name, put all three under the heading of 'Irregular'. The later two were genuine Réti Openings, and the first, against O. C. Muller, had the following strange start: 1.e4 e5 2.d3 d5 3.c3 de 4.de Qxd1+ 5.Kxd1 . It was won by Réti in thirty-five moves, but it is hardly necessary to say that the opening was not responsible, nor did it set any fashion.

During this period Réti almost invariably played his own opening, thereby setting annotators and chess journalists numerous problems. Firstly, there was the vexing question of the name. Many authorities are of the opinion that it is not decent to call an opening after a man until he is dead, so for some time the term 'Réti Opening' was frowned upon. The feebler-witted stuck to the word 'Irregular' on the grounds that it was noncommittal, unlikely to be injurious to anybody even if it were equally unhelpful to everybody, and that the adjective seemed to have something faintly disparaging about it. Those whose chess was learnt round about the 1870 period and who had acquired no new knowledge since then stuck firmly to the title 'Zukertort's Opening'. Unfortunately for them, even their historical claims proved to be incorrect, since an examination of Zukertort's games shows that he invariably used the initial move Nf6 merely as a transposition into a close form of the Queen's Gambit (by 'close' is meant an early e6 for White, shutting in his QB). Tartakower, wittiest of chess writers, avoided the difficulty by entitling it the 'Zukunftseroffnung' — the 'Opening of the future', and then playfully suggested that it should be called 'Napoleon's Opening', since a game starting 1.Nf3 was supposed to have been played by Napoleon at Malmaison in 1804 against Madame de Remusat.

Later on, by the way, it appeared that this, and other Napoleonic games, were composed many years after the Emperor's death.

A still more disputed point was the worth of the opening. Analysts nurtured in the classical school could see no point in it whatsoever, and prophesied that it would soon die a natural death. Teichmann, shocked at the twofold fianchetto, referred to it as 'the stupid, double-hole variation'. Gradually, as Réti achieved success after success with it, it began to dawn on even these partisan-minded critics that there was something in the opening after all. Other masters began to take it up, and the influence especially of Réti's own writings, supplemented by those of Tartakower and others, made itself felt, for these explained how it was observation rather than immediate control of the centre at which his opening efforts were aimed. The Black central pawns were to be teased on to commit themselves into advanced positions. Then White would use them as objects of attack, whilst one or other of his fianchettoed Bishops would become powerful along a diagonal which had been inevitably opened for it by the advance of the Black pawns. This explanation revealed that, far from being 'stupid', this opening was the subtlest on the board. Nor indeed was it dull, a prosaic exploitation of technical advantages — a charge that is sometimes levelled against it even now. Whole new vistas were opened up to the opening students — lines that could not be played by rote according to existing book theory, but that demanded native intelligence and inspiration. That it could result in beautiful attacks both on the K and Q side the games of this chapter and subsequent ones are sufficient to show.

Game No. 36 sees the opening employed against one of the greatest masters of the classical school — Rubinstein. Apart from being a fine game intrinsically, it is an excellent illustration of how an early advance in the centre by Black reacts to his disadvantage in eventually yielding White complete control there. The attack on the Q side in conjunction with the sweep of the KB along the long diagonal is well shown by Game No. 37.

Another example of Réti's fine handling of the counter-attack in the French Defence is Game No. 38. Here Réti again defeats Bogoljuboff by gaining complete command of the centre. Later on there was question of a match between Réti and Bogoljuboff in 1924. It was to have taken place in Czechoslovakia, but the occurrence of the New York tournament prevented this interesting possibility from materialising.

The curiosity about the opening of Game No. 39 is that it is Réti who is conducting the defence against his own opening. This he Naturally does to perfection, and the Rook and pawn ending is extremely fine.

A pattern that recurs throughout nearly all these games with the Réti Opening is traced by the power of the KB, a path for which has been cleared by early middle game activities. Game No. 40 shows this piece, not only attacking on the Q side, but delivering the coup de grace in co-operation with a K side attack. Both Bishops come into their own in Game No. 41, which has a very brilliant finish. For sheer excitement, Game No. 42 would be hard to beat; despite the mistakes both sides make when in time trouble, this is a fine, stirring game that has been too little noticed in the past.

The last three games are taken from the 1924 New York tournament, and are deservedly famous. To have defeated three such players as Capablanca, Alekhine and Bogoljuboff in one tournament must surely be a record, especially if one remembers that Bogoljuboff was at that time in his prime. The Bogoljuboff game is an anthology piece of which one never tires, whilst the victory over Capablanca came as a thunderclap to an astonished chess world. After his one-sided match win against Em. Lasker in 1921 and his impressive score at London, 1922, Capablanca had acquired an almost mythical reputation of invincibility. His defeat in such fine style by Réti was a resounding blow struck for the hypermodern school. The strategy by which Réti attacks and destroys Alekhine's Q side in Game No. 45 is worthy of the deepest study, and has met with insufficient and grudging praise at the hands of Alekhine himself in the tournament book.

CARLSBAD, 1923
Réti Opening
Réti — A. Rubinstein

1.Nf3 d5

This game is an interesting clash between one of the greatest leaders of the classical school — Rubinstein — and a correspondingly outstanding figure amongst the hyper-moderns. Note the contrast between the direct and clear handling of the opening by the former as opposed to the subtle lateral treatment by the latter.

2.g3 Nf6

3.Bg2 g6

4.c4 d4

Black has a difficult choice to make here. The Réti system has two main objectives — either to make Black compromise himself in the centre or else to induce him to abandon it altogether. With this pawn advance, Rubinstein commits himself too early and so allows White to obtain the first-mentioned objective. Or else he may surrender the centre and play 4...dc , when White can either reply 5.Na3 , followed by Nxc4 with a firm grip on e5, or he may play 5.Qa4+ , posing Black quite a new set of problems.

Simple development by 4...Bg7 5.cd Nxd5 6.Nc3 also brings with it heavy problems, as will be seen later on in Game No. 40. Hence the best reply is to strengthen the centre by 4...c6 .

5.d3 Bg7

6.b4 ...

Vigorous play that strictly follows the strategy Réti has had in mind from the start. The Black QP is to be deprived of its natural support by a pawn on b5 and will eventually have to be exchanged, when White will conquer the centre with a force all the greater for having been retarded.

MAHRISCH-OSTRAU, 1923
Réti Opening
Réti — A. Pokorný

1.Nf3 Nf6

2.c4 g6

3.b4 ...

An ambitious move which has an additional point to that of the enlarged Q side fianchetto. White intends also to fianchetto on the K side and will use the QNP as an extra barb to enforce the action of the KB. Later on Réti was to come to the conclusion that the text move was too risky, since it exposed the Q side to possible counter-attacks, and he discarded it in favour of the more solid 3.b3 . Nevertheless, as this game shows, if Black reacts in too simple and obvious a manner, this line can yield a fierce attack for White.

3. ... Bg7

4.Bb2 O-O

5.g3 d5

Obvious, natural-looking and a mistake. The opening up of lines, together with the exchange of Black's KB (a vital piece in the defence), means that Black must desperately strive to keep his K side safe, whilst White can utilise the time thus given him to attack along both wings. Much better is 5...b6 , keeping the game close, as in Réti-Capablanca, Game No. 44.

6.cxd5 Nxd5

7.Bxg7 Kxg7

8.Qb3 Nf6

Black wishes to get his minor pieces out as soon as possible, and at the same time avoid both a possible Queen check or an inconvenient discovery of attack on his Knight, after 9.Bg2 . Hence the passive-looking text move. Other lines also lead to a difficult game for Black. If 8...Be6 9.Ng5 , or if 8...a5 9.b5 c5 10.Bg2 , and Black will find it difficult, if not impossible, to complete the development of his Q side.

9.Bg2 Nc6

Though this was Black's intention, it is altogether too simple a solution to an intricate problem. The best method of smothering the effect of White's KB (which grows more powerful every move) would be by 9...c6 .

10.O-O e5

11.d3 ...

Black must not be allowed to play e5-e4 followed by Nd4.

11. ... Bg4

Not 11...Be6 12.Qb2 Nd7 13.b5 , when the KP falls.

12.Nb1-d2 ...

Premature is 12.b5 Bxf3 13.Bxf3 Nd4 .

12. ... Qe7

13.b5 Nd8

He loses a pawn after 13...Na5 14.Qc3 .

14.Qb2 Nd7

15.Rac1 Rc8

Black is already reduced to the most defensive moves; after 15...c6 16.bc bc his pawn structure would, however, be even weaker.

16.h3! ...

Now Black Is forced to exchange when White's Bishop becomes extremely powerful. If 16...Bf5 17.g4 wins the KP.

16. ... Bxf3

17.Bxf3 f6

18.Rc1-c3 ...

Partly in order to play the Queen to the R file, but also so as to threaten to double Rooks and thus making Black take steps to block the QB file at the expense of creating fresh weaknesses on his Q side.

18. ... b6

19.Qa3 Nc5

Exchange of Queens allows White both to win the QRP and to establish his Rook on the 7th rank.

20.Nb3 Nd8-e6

21.Nxc5 Nxc5

22.Qxa7 ...

Though at first glance the Queen may appear to be out of play in this remote corner of the board, it is in reality exerting a great influence on the course of the game, as soon becomes apparent.

22. ... Qd7

With this double attack, Black seems on the point of regaining his pawn with some freedom of position.

23.a4 ...

But now Black cannot take the RP, for if 23...Qxh3 24.a5 Qe6 25.Bc6 , when Black not only loses his QNP, but is forced to abandon his post on QB4 with his Knight, and when, too, his whole position will soon collapse.

23. ... f5

Threatening to win the Queen by 24...e4 25.de Ra8 .

24.Bc6 Qd4

25.Rc4 Qd8

Or 25...Qd6 , when White wins by 26.a5 Rb8 27.Bg2 e4 28.ab Rxb6 29.Rfc1 Ne6 30.de .

26.a5 Rf7

27.Rxc5! ...

Neat and expeditious; the passed pawn, in conjunction with the sweep of the Bishop along the diagonal, will cost Black a Rook.

27. ... bxc5

28.Qxc5 Qf6

29.a6 Rf7-f8

30.a7 Resigns

He has no adequate means of dealing with the threat of 31.Ra1 .

Réti — E. Straat

3.Bg2 g6

4.c4 Bg7

For 4...d4 , see Game No. 36. As pointed out there, 4...c6 is best.

5.cxd5 Nxd5

6.Nc3 Be6

An uneasy post for the Bishop, since he always has to reckon with a possible Ng5. Preferable is 6...Nxc3 7.bc O-O 8.O-O c5 , with a position resembling that arising out of the Grünfeld Defence.

7.O-O ...

Not at once 7.Ng5 , because of 7...Nxc3 8.bc Bd5 9.e4 Bc4 and if 10.Qa4+ b5 11.Qd1 e5 , with advantage to Black.

7. ... O-O

This innocuous-looking move must be classed as a mistake, since it allows White to make full use of his powerful fianchettoed Bishop. Whether he likes it or not, Black must waste a move to keep White's Knight out of g5 by 7...h6 .

8.Ng5 Nxc3

9.dxc3! ...

An unexpected way of recapturing, but much the best. If now 9...Qxd1 10.Rxd1 Bc8 11.Be3 , Black will experience great difficulty in completing the development of his Q side and White will obtain control of the Q file by doubling his Rooks there.

9. ... Bc8

10.Be3 Nc6

11.Qc1 ...

It is best to avoid the Queen exchange now, since after 11...Qxd1 12.Rxd1 , Black, with his Knight already developed, has time to exchange a pair of Rooks by 12...Rd8 .

11. ... Bf5

Anticipating that Black too will play Qc8, when Bh3 will be a threat. The text averts this and also prepares to harry the QB by g3-g4.

12. ... Qc8

13.Kh2 h6

14.Ne4 Kh7

So as to prevent the threatened g3-g4.

One of those subtle pawn endings in which Réti excelled, this study is based on the theory of corresponding squares, the most important of which are White's g6 and e7. White has two alternative aims: either to capture Black's two pawns at such a time and in such a way that Black's King cannot get in front of the queening RP, or to queen his NP before Black's QP. The problem therefore is how to make a judicious blend of the two ideas.
