IV
A YEAR OF GREAT TOURNAMENTS, 1922

From April onwards of this year, Réti was almost continuously occupied with great international tournaments. For in April he played in Pistyan in Czechoslovakia, in August here in London, in October at Teplitz-Schönau, again in Czechoslovakia, and in November and December he played in Vienna. These all having been tournaments of the highest quality and with a large number of participants, the strain on Réti must have been immense and would alone account for the varying quality of his performances. Nevertheless, in none did he do badly and in one, at Teplitz-Schönau, he was equal first and second with Spielmann. His score against the other prize-winners was most impressive — 5½ out of 6, beating Spielmann, Tartakower, Rubinstein, Kostic and Teichmann and drawing with Grünfeld.

His play was increasingly modern and original in outlook, with an ever-growing tendency towards the Queen's Pawn and away from the King's, though he sometimes still played the latter against opponents who he knew were averse to it.

How much present-day players owe to the pioneers of the past appears in Game No. 28, where Réti's Old Indian Defence presages almost every theoretical discovery that has been made in this defence since his time. The game itself is a pleasure to play through just as a game, without worrying about its historical significance, and the winning combination is very pretty. Similarly, the other game from Pistyan, No. 29 against Opocensky, might well have been played many years later, since the type of pawn sacrifice Réti employed against the Slav Defence was reintroduced with great effect as late as 1937.

Réti did not do as well as expected on his first visit to England, when he played in the great London tournament that was won by Capablanca. Nor indeed did he ever really succeed in all his later and numerous visits to this country in producing anything like his best form. He himself was heard to complain that, much as he liked to visit England, it seemed to be written in the stars that he should never be successful there. Still, he did manage to achieve in Game No. 30 against Znosko-Borovsky a model example of the K side attack with especial reference to breaking down the pawn formation protecting the King.

Réti's increased technical powers (an essential part of any great master) are well exemplified by the three games that follow from Teplitz-Schönau. In the first, against Kostic, he exerts relentless pressure against one weak central point and wins an exceptionally difficult Rook and pawn ending. In Game No. 32 against Spielmann the decision comes earlier by a central break-through, but perhaps this is even more noticeably a triumph of technique. Finally, from this tournament there is the win over the great master who was reckoned supreme in his generation from this very aspect — Akiba Rubinstein himself. Despite the flaw on his 18th move, Réti's conduct of a long and (for all its length) furious attack, followed by his equally consummate skill in the ending, make this one of the outstanding games of this book.

The Nimzowitschian theory of blockade, adorned with Réti's own elegance of style, is the instructive theme of Game No. 34, and in the final game we have a curiosity — one of the earliest Catalans ever played. Réti treats it as Black with a drastic severity that is rather cruel, coming as it does from one innovator to another.

A remarkable move that is not easy to find. The Queen is now better placed on its original square so as to give much-needed support to the centre.
