VII
FROM BRATISLAVA TO BUDAPEST, 1925-1926

Variety is the keynote of Réti's career in this chapter. His activities ranged from the home championship of Czechoslovakia at Bratislava, where he was far and away first, to the great international tournaments of Moscow and Semmering, and back again to another of what might be termed his native haunts at Budapest.

This variety, too, seems to be found in his play; for the openings, all modern and mostly original in concept, widen their scope to a great extent.

Game No. 54 shows him crushing inferior opposition with some pretty sacrificial play against the King.

The next three games are from the 1925 Moscow tournament, a landmark in the history of tournaments. Though Réti was not a high prizewinner, he produced some very fine games here. He wrote much for the Russian chess magazine, Schachmatny Listok, and other writings of his were translated into Russian, so that he had a great influence on the progress of Soviet chess — so great that it is still visible in present-day Soviet chess circles, both as regards style of play and types of opening chosen.

Game No. 55 figures in this collection not because of the opening, where for once Réti is worsted when playing his own system; but his fine recovery and his still finer endgame play more than redeem the faulty beginning. If anybody wants to see exactly how a vigorous counter-attack should be delivered by Black, he is recommended to play over Game No. 56, in which Iljin-Genevsky is beaten almost before he realises that the game has really started. Even the winner of the tournament, Bogoljuboff, then at the height of his powers, succumbed to Réti in Game No. 57.

The profound combination that distinguishes Game No. 58 shows what a deep insight Réti had in all types of position, for this is far from being a normal Réti kind of game. Even the opening — a Nimzowitsch Defence to the Queen's Pawn — figures only once in this book. Grünfeld always seemed to draw out the best in Réti, and Game No. 59 is indeed a great strategic triumph — a masterpiece demonstrating the exploitation of control of greater space. The charming Queen sacrifice in Game No. 60 crowns a most dramatic contest, and another fine K side attack with the aid of the all-important KB is to be found in Game No. 61.

BRATISLAVA, 1925
English Opening (or Sicilian Defence by Transposition)
Réti — A. Walter

1.c4 c5

Black is determined to copy White's moves as long as possible, but there comes a time when this proves impracticable, and he is beguiled into an inferior variation of the Sicilian Defence.

2.Nf3 Nf6

3.Nc3 Nc6

After this White dictates the course of events. Better is 3...d5 4.cd Nxd5 , and if 5.e4 Nxc3 6.bc g6 .

4.d4 cxd4

5.Nxd4 g6

6.e4 ...

We have now transposed to the Maroczy Variation of the Sicilian Defence, in which White has a strong grip on the centre white squares.

6. ... d6

7.Be2 Bg7

8.Nc2! ...

The Knight must move, since Black was threatening 8...Nxe4 9.Nxc6 Nxc3 . It is very well placed on c2, since from there it can go to e3 and eventually d5.

8. ... Nd7

So as to play BxN+, with a resulting weakening of the white Q side pawns. The disadvantage of this plan is that White has two powerful Bishops with some open diagonals along which they can work; how effectively, Réti now proceeds to demonstrate. Therefore Black would be better advised to continue his development by 0-0, Be6 and Rc8.

9.Be3 ...

Also good for White is 9.Qd2 O-O 10.O-O Nc5 11.f3 , as in the game Euwe-Landau, Amsterdam, 1934.

9. ... Bxc3+

As noted above, this exchange is faulty, and better would be 9...Nc5 .

10.bxc3 Nc5

11.f3 b6

Much too slow. If Black wishes to attack the QB pawns, he should do so at once with 11...Qa5 when, however, White can adequately reply 12.Qd2 , and if 12...Na4 13.Nb4 , but at any rate this provides some counterattack. Now White forces through his attack in a few moves.

12.O-O Na5

13.Bh6 Ba6

14.Ne3 Rc8

15.f4! ...

Vigorous play; if now 15...Nxe4 16.Qd4 Nf6 17.Bg7 Rg8 18.Bxf6 ef 19.Nd5 , with a winning attack.

15. ... Rg8

16.Qc2 f6

17.e5! ...

No matter what Black now does, the position is broken wide open.

17. ... dxe5

18.fxe5 f5

Black vainly tries to close one file at least. Taking the pawn also loses, e.g. 18...fe 19.Rad1 Qc7 20.Nd5 Qb8 21.Bg4 Rd8 22.Qf2 .

19.Nxf5! Ne6

Or 19...gf 20.Bh5+ Rg6 21.Rad1 Qc7 22.Qxf5 , with mate to follow. The text move does not put off the end for long either.

20.Ne3 Bxc4

21.Nxc4 Nxc4

22.Ra1-d1 Qc7

23.Qa4+ Qc6

24.Bxc4! ...

A move as pretty as it is effective; after 24...Qxa4 25.Bxe6 , Black, in order to avoid mate, must play 25...Qxd1 , when 26.Rxd1 leaves one of Black's Rooks to be taken for nothing. What Bishops!

24. ... Resigns.

55
MOSCOW, 1925
Réti Opening
Réti — P. A. Romanowski

1.Nf3 Nf6

2.c4 c5

3.g3 e6

Rightly abandoning symmetry in order to contest the white squares in the centre.

4.Bg2 Be7

5.O-O O-O

6.b3 Nc6

7.Bb2 d5

8.cxd5 ...

White must not allow Black to shut in his QB by d5-d4.

8. ... Nxd5

8...ed 9.d4 would give rise to a sort of Tarrasch Defence, in which White's pieces would be well placed to take advantage of Black's weak pawn on d5.

9.Nc3 Bf6

10.Rc1 ...

Later on (at Trenchin-Teplitz, 1928, against Grünfeld) Réti was to vary this with 10.Qc1 Nxc3 11.Bxc3 e5 (bad for Black is the further exchange, 11...Bxc3 12.Qxc3 b6 13.Ne5 Nd4 14.Rfe1 Rb8 15.e3 Nb5 16.Qc4 Nd6 17.Qa4 Bb7 18.d4 , as in the game, Golombek-Winter, Nottingham, 1946) 12.d3 Rb8 13.Qb2 Nd4 , with an interesting conflict based on the mastery of contrasted coloured squares in the centre.

10. ... Nxc3

11.Bxc3 e5

Exchange of Bishops would be bad because, after 11...Bxc3 12.Rxc3 , Black would have great difficulty in defending his QBP, 12...b6 , losing the exchange by 13.Ne5 .

12.Bb2? ...

Here Réti commits one of his rare inaccuracies in his own opening. He should play 12.d3 at once, since after the text move the fact that his Bishop is momentarily unprotected allows Black to obtain the upper hand both in the centre and as regards the initiative.

12. ... Qd6!

13.Ne1 ...

For now 13.d3 loses a piece after 13...e4 14.Bxf6 ef .

13. ... Bf5

Preventing the threat of Nd3, attacking Black's QBP.

14.d3 Ra8-d8

15.Nc2 b6

So as to play Nd4 without leaving the QNP en prise.

16.Ne3 Be6

17.f4 ...

This, followed by a further advance of the pawn, is a risky plan that dangerously weakens him on the diagonal c1-h6 but, owing to White's faulty 12th move, it is the only aggressive manoeuvre at his disposal.

17. ... Nd4

And not 17...ef 18.Bxf6 gf 19.Rxf4 , to White's advantage.

18.f5 Bg5!

19.Nc4 Bxc4

20.Rxc4 ...

Position after 20.Rxc4

20. ... Be3+?

Peter Romanowski, whose researches into chess theory greatly benefited his contemporaries and successors in Russian chess and who might well be termed the 'father of modern Russian chess', has played the game with great finesse and understanding so far; but here he fails to find the most vigorous continuation. As Bogoljuboff points out in the tournament book, correct was 20...b5 , when the White Rook is embarrassed for a good square, and he must play the awkward 21.Rc3 c4 22.Kh1 (if (a) 22.dc Nf3+ , wins the exchange; or (b) 22.bc b4 23.c5 bc 24.cd cb 25.Qb1 Bc1 , and the black Knight cannot be prevented from reaching c3; or, finally, (c) 22.Bc1 Bxc1 23.Rxc1 Qa3 , threatening Qxc1), 22...cb 23.ab Rc8 , with a clear advantage in position for Black.

After the text move White is out of his troubles, and with amazing art Réti proceeds to demonstrate that the presence of pawns on Black's e5 and c5 impede the action of Romanowski's Bishop.

21.Kh1 Qh6

22.Bxd4 ...

The Knight is too strong on d4, and therefore White has to surrender one of his two Bishops — this, too, an almost unprecedented occurrence in Réti's games.

22. ... Rxd4

After 22...ed , White would assail the Black pawn chain by b3-b4.

23.Rxd4 Bxd4

Here, and for the next few moves, Black is living in a fool's paradise, in which he imagines that the presence of Bishops of opposite colour ensures a simple draw. He refrains from playing 23...ed , since this would yield White some attack on the Q side by an eventual b3-b4. This was, however, the right continuation, as he would get a compensatory attack along the K file.

24.Be4 Rd8

25.Kg2 ...

Now it is White who entertains aggressive ideas on the K side. He plans an attack by Rf3, Qe1 and g3-g4. Alarmed at this possibility, Black tries to counterbalance it by a Q side advance.

25. ... b5

26.Qc1 ...

Now that Black has somewhat weakened his Q wing, White offers the exchange of Queens, since he realises that the Rook and Bishop ending will be in his favour.

26. ... Qxc1

But Black has not seen so far into the position, and falls in with White's plans. Instead, he should avoid the exchange by 26...Be3 .

27.Rxc1 b4

Threatening to shut off the QBP from attack by 28...Bc3 .

28.Rc4 Kf8

29.Kf3 Rc8

Otherwise White's next move wins the QBP.

30.e3 Bc3

This Bishop is now, as it were, landlocked by its own pawns and worth a great deal less than its White counterpart.

31.a4 Ke7

32.Bd5 Rc7

33.Rh4 h6

34.Ke4 Kf6

35.Rh5 Rd7

If 35...g6 36.fg (not 36.Rxh6 Kg5 ) 36...Kxg6 37.Rf5 a5 (otherwise White advances the QRP to the 6th and then plays his Rook to b7 via f2, a2, a5 and b5) 38.h4 f6 39.h5+ Kg7 40.Rf1 , when Black is reduced to passive Rook moves, whilst White cannot be prevented from attaining the winning formation of Rook on g6 and King on f5.

36.g4 ...

Now White threatens to advance his other pawn with decisive effect, e.g. 36...a5 37.h4 Rc7 38.g5+ hg 39.hg+ Ke7 40.Rh7 Kf8 41.Rh8+ Ke7 42.Rg8 .

So Black tries his only possible counter-attack under the belief that White cannot reply with Rxh6.

36. ... g6

37.Rxh6 ...

But he can and does! The continuation from this move till the end of the game is worthy of a fine endgame study, and must have been foreseen by Réti from at least the 36th till his last (47th) move.

37. ... Kg5

38.Rh7 Kxg4

What is White now to do?

Black threatens mate by 39...gf , and equally 39.fg f5 is mate. If 39.f6 Kg5 40.Rxf7 Rxf7 41.Bxf7 Kxf6 , and draws, as does 39.Rxf7 Rxf7 40.Bxf7 gf+ .

39.Be6! fxe6

Declining the sacrifice by 39...Re7 would be no good, because of 40.Rxf7 Rxf7 41.fg dis ch.

40.fxg6 ...

Not 40.Rxd7 gf mate.

40. ... Rd8

41.Rxa7 Kg5

He must use the King to stop the KNP, since the black Rook has to deal with the menace of a4-a5-a6, followed by Rb7 and a6-a7. Meanwhile, the Bishop, cut off by its own pawns, remains a helpless spectator.

42.g7 Kh6

43.a5 Kh7

44.a6 Rd6

White was threatening Ra7-b7, a6-a7 and Rb8; hence the black Rook must be placed behind the RP or laterally.

45.h4 ...

The last pawn to become active decides the issue; there is nothing to be done against the advance of this pawn to h6, followed by Ra8.

45. ... Be1

46.h5 Bh4

47.h6 Resigns

If 47...Bf6 , so as to sacrifice the piece for two pawns after 48.Ra8 , then 48.Rf7 . For this game Réti received the second brilliancy prize.

MOSCOW, 1925
Alekhine's Defence
A. F. Iljin-Genewski — Réti

1.e4 Nf6

2.e5 Nd5

3.Nf3 d6

4.d4 Bg4

5.Be2 Nc6

6.exd6 ...

A premature exchange which gives Black the opportunity of recapturing with the Queen and a rapid Q side castling. Correct is 6.c4 Nb6 , and then 7.ed , when 7...Qxd6 fails against 8.c5 .

6. ... Qxd6

7.Nc3 ...

White is obsessed by the necessity (as he thinks) of eliminating Black's central Knight, but the whole manoeuvre only helps Black. Much more to the point was the system subsequently employed by Yates: 7.c3 e6 8.Na3 Be7 9.Nc4 Qd8 10.O-O , with a good game for White.

7. ... O-O-O

8.Nxd5 ...

If 8.O-O Nf4 is to Black's advantage, but better than this exchange was 8.Ne4 .

8. ... Qxd5

9.Be3 e5!

Black plays the whole game with the most enlivening vigour.

10.dxe5 ...

After this White is taken by surprise at a series of fierce thrusts by Black that shatters his position. Rather better is 10.O-O , though even then, after 10...Bxf3 11.Bxf3 e4 12.Bg4+ f5 13.c4 Qf7 14.d5 Ne5 15.Be2 f4 16.Bxa7 f3 , Black's attack gets in first on the K side.

10. ... Bxf3!

11.gxf3 ...

He has to wreck his K side in this way, since 11.Bxf3 Qa5+! 12.Bd2 Qxe5+ leaves Black with a won game, whether White continues 13.Be2 Qxb2 or whether he plays 13.Kf1 Nd4 .

11. ... Qa5+

12.Bd2 Qxe5

13.c3 ...

If he tries to unpin his Queen by 13.Qc1 , then 13...Nd4 is an overwhelmingly strong move.

13. ... Bc5

14.O-O ...

Not a satisfactory position into which to castle, but he cannot sit and wait for Black to triple his major pieces on the K file (or double them on the Q file, for that matter).

14. ... Rh8-e8

With the simple but potent threat of Re6 that in turn threatens to go to either d6 (winning the Bishop) or h6 (winning the King).

15.Bc4 ...

Temporarily preventing Re6 and attacking (the first signs of a White attack in the game) the KBP. A blunder would be 15.Bb5 Bxf2+ , followed by 16...Qxb5 .

15. ... Qf5!

Protecting the pawn, preparing for the final K side attack and vacating e5 for the Knight; short of delivering mate, the Queen could hardly do more.

16.b4 Ne5!

17.Be2 ...

The Bishop has to retreat, since 17.bc Rxd2 18.Qa4 Nxf3+ 19.Kh1 Qh3 leads to mate.

17. ... Bb6

18.f4 ...

Opening up another diagonal for Black's attack, but against other reasonable moves 18...Re6 is decisive.

18. ... Qg6+

19.Kh1 Qc6+

20.Kg1 ...

Or 20.f3 Ng4 21.b5 Qd5 22.c4 Qxd2 , and Black wins.

20. ... Re6!

21.Bh5 ...

If 21.fe Rxd2 22.Qxd2 Rg6+ , followed by mate.

21. ... Rxd2!

22.b5 ...

Desperation; but if 22.Qxd2 Rg6+ 23.Bxg6 Nf3+ .

22. ... Rg6+

23.Bg4+ Nxg4!

Resigns

White is mated after 24.bc Nxf2 dis ch 25.Qg4 Rxg4# .

MOSCOW, 1925
Réti Opening
Réti — E. D. Bogoljuboff

1.Nf3 d5

2.c4 c6

3.b3 Bg4

Not a bad alternative to the more usual 3...Bf5 . It sets a small trap, for if now 4.Ne5 dc 5.Nxg4 Qd4 , and Black regains his piece whilst holding on to his extra pawn.

4.Bb2 e6

5.e3 ...

5.Ne5 does not really win either space or time, since Black replies 5...Bh5 and if 6.g3 Nd7 7.Bg2 Ngf6 8.O-O Nxe5 9.Bxe5 Bd6 , with a very satisfactory position. However, instead of the text move, which seems to acquiesce in the fact that Black has fully developed his QB, it would be better to play 5.d3 Nf6 6.Nbd2 Nbd7 7.g3 , planning an eventual Qc2 and e2-e4, when White would have the powerful threat of e4-e5.

5. ... Nf6

6.Be2 Be7

7.O-O O-O

8.h3 Bh5

9.d3 c5

A strong move that gains a better developing square for the QN.

10.cxd5 ...

White makes this exchange in the hope of attacking Black's c5, but this attack does not materialise, and 10.Nc3 was preferable.

10. ... Nxd5

11.Nc3 ...

Here rather than 11.Nbd2 , since then Black would play 11...Bf6 , and White's c3 would become weak.

11. ... Nc6

12.Rc1 Rc8

13.Qd2 Nxc3

14.Bxc3 Qb6

15.Qb2 f6

White has achieved something, since this pawn move must be reckoned as a slight weakening of the K side; but Black's position is still very compact and White's own pawn on d3 is not particularly safe.

16.Rf1-d1 ...

An all-out attack by 16.Kh1 Rfd8 17.g4 Bg6 18.g5 could be safely dealt with by 18...e5 .

16. ... Rf8-d8

17.Ne1 Bxe2

18.Qxe2 ...

Here, according to Réti in Schachmatny Listok for 1925, the game is approximately equal. If anything one would have said that Black had rather the better of it.

18. ... Qa6

19.Rc2 ...

19.Qg4 , would react in Black's favour after 19...Nb4 20.Bxb4 cb 21.Qe2 Rc3 . But after the text move 19...Nb4 20.Bxb4 cb would allow White to gain control of the QB file by 21.Rdc1 .

19. ... Rd6

20.Qg4 ...

Trying for a K side attack by the method of advancing his KR and KB pawns.

20. ... Rc8-d8

21.h4 b6

22.h5 ...

Part of his plan, but better, as Réti afterwards indicated, was 22.Rdc1 , and if 22...Qb7 23.b4 , when 23...cb 24.Bxb4 Nxb4 25.Rc7 would win for White.

22. ... Qb7

23.Rd1-c1 Qd7

So that if now 24.b4 , he can reply 24...Rd5 .

24.f4 e5

25.f5 Bf8

26.Rf2 Qf7

27.Rf3 a5

28.Rg3 Ra8

29.Qf3 ...

Seeing that Black is about to advance his QRP, White pins the Knight on the Rook. 29.h6 would not succeed in breaking through Black's defences, for then would come 29...g5 30.fg hg 31.Qxg6+ Qxg6 32.Rxg6+ Kh7 , and Black regains the pawn with a good game.

29. ... a4?

Black wrongly discounts the force of White's last move. Instead, he should play 29...Ra7 .

30.b4! ...

Threatening to win a piece by b4-b5.

30. ... e4?

Naturally, 30...cb 31.Bxb4 would win the exchange for White; but the text move is also a miscalculation based on the expected reply of 31.Qxe4 Re8 when the Knight is unpinned. He could and should still play 30...Ra7 .

31.dxe4! Ra8-d8

For if 31...cb 32.e5! , and however Black plays he must lose the exchange.

32.bxc5 bxc5

For the moment White's central pawn mass does not appear very menacing; but now he returns to his K side attack and cunningly utilises latent threats in the centre to enhance the force of his wing assault.

33.h6! Kh8

Not 33...Qxa2 34.hg Bxg7 35.e5! Nxe5 36.Bxe5 fe 37.f6 , and wins.

34.hxg7+ Bxg7

35.Qe2 Nb4

36.Bxb4 cxb4

37.Nf3 b3

38.Nd4! ...

Here the Knight is a tower of strength. White must be careful about the sequence of his moves here, for if 38.ab Qxb3 39.Nd4 Rxd4 , and Black wins!

38. ... bxa2

39.Ra1 ...

And not 39.Ne6 Rxe6 40.fe Qxe6 41.Ra1 Qe5

39. ... Bh6

Threatening 40...Bxe3+ 41.Qxe3 Rxd4 .

40.Ne6 Rxe6

He cannot leave the Knight so strongly posted. If 40...Rd2 41.Nxd8 , and wins.

41.fxe6 Qxe6

42.Rxa2 Qxe4

43.Qf3 Bxe3+

This loses at once, but there is nothing to be done now. If 43...Qb1+ 44.Kh2 Qxa2 45.Qxf6+ , followed by mate.

If 43...Qxf3 44.Rxf3 Re8 45.Kf2 Re4 46.Rxf6 Bxe3+ 47.Kf3 Re7 48.Re2 Bg5 49.Rxe7 Bxf6 50.Ra7 , with an easy win.

44.Qxe3 Resigns

After 44...Qb1+ 45.Kh2 Qxa2 46.Qe7 forces mate.

SEMMERING, 1926
Queen's Pawn, Nimzowitsch Defence
Réti — K. Gilg

1.d4 Nf6

2.c4 e6

3.Nc3 Bb4

4.e3 b6

Quite a good variant from the more usual pawn advances, 4...c5 or 4...d5 .

5.Bd2 ...

Rather slow and not calculated to put much difficulty in Black's way. Stronger is 5.Ne2 , followed by 6.a3 .

5. ... Bb7

6.Nf3 c5

This does not fit in with Black's fianchetto development. He should concentrate on controlling the e4 square. Hence 6...O-O , and if 7.Bd3 Bxc3 8.Bxc3 Ne4 9.Qc2 f5 was a reasonable and satisfactory line.

7.dxc5 Bxc5

This too is the wrong idea; it is far better to increase the number of Black's centre pawns by 7...bc .

8.Bd3 O-O

9.O-O d5

10.Qe2 dxc4

11.Bxc4 Nc6

12.a3 ...

So as to play b2-b4 and eventually develop the QB on c3; one of the drawbacks of 7...Bxc5 as opposed to 7...bc is now apparent.

12. ... Qe7

12...a5 would leave a bad hole on b5, but this Queen move deprives the Bishop of its natural retiring square. It is true that the Queen must eventually leave the Q file when White plays Q or K Rd1, but Black should wait for this move first to be played. Correct is 12...Rc8 , and if 13.b4 Be7 14.Rfd1 Qc7 15.Nb5 Qb8 .

13.b4 Bd6

14.Nb5 ...

More accurate was, first, 14.Rac1 , and if 14...Rac8 15.Ba6 .

14. ... Bb8?

For now Black could have freed his game by 14...Ne5 15.Nxd6 Bxf3 (not 15...Nxf3+ 16.gf Qxd6 17.e4 , when Black's Bishop has no future and White's two Bishops are very powerful) 16.gf Qxd6 , and since Black threatens QxB, White must allow one of his Bishops to be exchanged.

15.Bc3 a6

16.Nb5-d4 Nxd4

17.Bxd4 ...

Trying to induce Black to play b6-b5, when White will soon gain control of c5.

17. ... e5!

18.Bb2 ...

Not 18.Bxb6 e4 19.Nd4 Qd6 , and Black wins a piece.

18. ... b5

19.Bb3 e4

Black's aggressive intentions betray him. Since his Q side is not properly developed, he is unable to exploit the weakness White is now forced to create on his K side, and meanwhile White has the beautifully central square of d4 for his Knight.

He should release his Q side Rook by 19...Bd6 .

20.Nd4 Qd6

21.g3 Qd7

22.Rf1-d1 Qh3

23.Qf1 Qh5

The endgame after 23...Qxf1+ 24.Kxf1 Ba7 25.Nf5 would be greatly in White's favour.

24.Qe2 ...

Both players being rather short of time, they repeat moves so as to gain on the clock.

24. ... Qh3

25.Qf1 Qh5

Réti has used the time gained by the repetition to look deeply into the position. He sees that unless he repeats moves, Black will have the dangerous attacking manoeuvre of Nf6-g4-e5-f3+, but resolves to run the risk and embark on a profound combination, starting off with his next move (that looks quite innocuous) and extending as far as his 32nd.

26.Ra1-c1! Ng4

27.Qg2 Ne5

28.Rc5 ...

Now if 28...Nf3+ 29.Nxf3 Qxf3 30.Qxf3 ef 31.Rd7 , with a won game.

28. ... Bd6

Apparently a most embarrassing move but White has prepared a crushing rejoinder.

29.g4! Qxg4

Probably not foreseeing White's 32nd move, Black goes into a clearly losing variation. If instead 29...Qh6 or 29...Qh4 , White wins by 30.Nf5 . If 29...Qg5 30.h4 Qf6 31.g5 , and White has gained some moves in the attack.

Against 29...Qg6 30.Rxe5 Bxe5 31.Nf5 , Réti gives the following variations:

(a) 31...Qf6 32.Rd6 Qxd6 (if 32...Bxb2 33.Rxf6 Bxf6 34.g5 Bb2 35.g6 hg 36.Ne7+ Kh8 37.Qh3 mate) 33.Nxd6 Bxd6 34.g5 Kh8 35.Qh3 (threatening 36.Qd7 ), 35...Rad8 36.Qh6 f6 37.gf! , and wins.

(b) 31...Rae8 32.Bxe5 Rxe5 33.Rd6 Qg5 34.h4 , winning the Queen.

(c) 31...Bf6 32.Rd6 Rae8 33.Nxg7 Bxg7 34.Rxg6 hg 35.Bxg7 Kxg7 36.Qg3 , and White should win. He threatens both Qc7 and Qd6, and can also play at his leisure h2-h4-h5, with further attack on the King.

30.Qxg4 Nxg4

31.Rg5 Ne5

Or 31...Nxh2 , when again a preliminary 32.f4! wins for White.

32.f4! ...

So that if 32...ef e.p. 33.Nf5 , and wins, whereas 32.Nf5 at once loses after 32...Nf3+ .

32. ... Nd3

If 32...Ng6 33.Nf5 Bb8 ( 33...Bc7 34.Rd7 ) 34.Bxg7 Re8 35.Bf6 , followed by 36.Nh6 , which wins even if Black plays 35...Kf8 .

33.Rxd3 h6

33...ed 34.Nf5 wins at once for White.

34.Rg3 Resigns

If he does not take the Rook now, he is a piece down whilst White's attack persists. If he does, then after 34...ed 35.Nf5 g5 36.Rh3 d2 37.Rxh6 d1Q+ 38.Bxd1 f6 39.Bb3+ Rf7 40.Nxd6 , wins still more material. Réti was awarded a brilliancy prize for this game.

SEMMERING, 1926
Réti Opening
Réti — E. Grünfeld

1.Nf3 Nf6

2.c4 e6

3.g3 b6

A good variation against the Réti system, but one that must be conducted with the utmost accuracy. A single false step leaves Black struggling for the rest of the game.

4.Bg2 Bb7

5.O-O Be7

6.Nc3 c5

7.d3 O-O

Allowing White to gain control of the central white squares (as so often happens in Réti's games) by the following move. Correct is 7...d5 .

8.e4! d6

9.Re1 ...

Threatening d4-d5 and so inducing Black to play e5. This in turn provides Réti with a target for one of his characteristic and enduring attacks by an eventual f2-f4-f5.

9. ... e5

10.Nh4 Nc6

11.f4 Nd4

12.Ne2 ...

In order to eliminate the one strongly posted piece in the Black position. This is an interesting reversal of the normal methods of position play. He prefers to attack the strong points first and only when these are subdued will he go for the weak ones.

12. ... Nxe2+

Rather obliging; it would be preferable to wait and allow White to make this exchange by playing, for instance 12...Nd7 .

13.Qxe2 Ne8

Not now 13...Nd7 , because of 14.Nf5 , when, owing to the necessity for defending his QP, Black must let White exchange Knight for Bishop with the consequent advantage for White of two Bishops.

14.Nf3 Nc7

White could now win a pawn by 15.fe de 16.Nxe5 Qd4+ 17.Kh1 Qxe5 18.Bf4 , winning back the piece, but instead of QxN, Black could play 17...Rad8 with strong pressure on the centre in return for the pawn. Hence White prefers to ignore the possibility and continue with his plan for a K side attack.

15.Be3 f6

He must protect his KP, since White's last move means that Black no longer has Qd4+ at his disposal.

16.f5 b5

Both sides have clearly marked plans: White's is to break through Black's K side pawn chain whilst Black will try for exactly the same on the Q side.

17.Nh4 Qd7

18.Bf3 Rf8-b8

19.Kh1 ...

Before advancing the KNP he must have his King in safety on the R file (a necessity that comes out clearly later on) and also clear the way for his KR.

19. ... Bc6

20.g4 Ne8

Black is not without aggressive ideas; he threatens Qb7 and b5xc4.

21.Qg2 ...

Parrying the above-mentioned menace and himself preparing to play g4-g5. This Black prevents by his reply.

21. ... Qd8

22.Re2 a5

23.Rg1 Rb7

24.Qh3 ...

Threatening to force through g4-g5 by means of 25.Ng6 , followed by Nxe7, since the Knight cannot be captured by the pawn because of mate.

24. ... Qb8

Now if 25.Ng6 hg 26.fg Bd8 , and the King has a flight square out of the mate.

25.Bc1! ...

The assiduous care with which Réti deprives his opponent of any tangible counter-attack is both instructive and pleasant to watch. Had he played 25.b3 , then 25...a4 would have allowed Black to break through on the QR file.

25. ... Bd8

26.Ng2 Ra8-a7

27.Ne3 h6

Black, seeing that his counterattack on the Q side cannot be carried out, decides to fortify his K side pawn chain as much as is practicable, and then to bring his King away to the other wing.

28.Nd5 bxc4

29.dxc4 Bxd5

30.exd5 ...

Not 30.cd c4 , and Black's Bishop will get to d4 via b6.

30. ... Kf8

31.Qf1 ...

Now the break-through by h2-h4 and g4-g5 cannot be stopped.

31. ... Ke7

32.h4 Kd7

33.Rh2 Ra8

34.Bd1 ...

Threatening 35.Ba4+ and Bc6.

34. ... a4

35.g5 hxg5

36.hxg5 Qa7

36...fg 37.Rh8 would give rise to variations similar to that in the actual game.

37.Rh8 Be7

38.Bh5 ...

Threatening 39.Rg8 followed by Bxe8+ and Rxg7.

38. ... fxg5

39.Rxe8! ...

This temporary sacrifice of the exchange contains a subtle point. Admitting that the Knight must be suppressed, since it threatens to block the attack by Nf6, one might ask, 'Why not take with the Bishop? What is the difference?' The difference lies in the fact that the black King remains on Q2 and so yields White an extra tempo in the attack by Qh3.

39. ... Rxe8

40.Qh3 ...

The threat of f5-f6 dis ch forces Black to block the pawn.

40. ... Bf6

41.Bxe8+ Kxe8

42.Qh8+! ...

A check with a point; 42.Bxg5 at once would allow Black to fortify himself by 42...Rf7 .

42. ... Kd7

43.Bxg5 Kc7

If 43...Rb8 44.Qh7 , and wins.

44.Bxf6 gxf6

45.Rg7+ Kb6

46.Qd8+ Ka6

Or 46...Rc7 47.Qxd6+ .

Now there comes a neat finish.

47.Qe8! Resigns

If 47...Rxg7 48.Qb5 mate, and if 47...Ka5 48.Qc6! A beautiful game against stiff opposition.

SEMMERING, 1926
Queen's Pawn, Budapest Defence
H. Kmoch — Réti

1.d4 Nf6

2.c4 e5

Réti was one of the earliest pioneers of this defence, and played it with a rare dash.

3.dxe5 Ng4

4.e4 ...

Also playable is the temporary retainment of the pawn by 4.Bf4 Nc6 5.Nf3 Bb4+ 6.Nbd2 , when, though Black may recover his pawn by 6...Qe7 , White has a good game without weaknesses.

The text move plays for more and risks more.

4. ... Nxe5

At Kiel in 1921 Réti tried against Bogoljuboff 4...h5 but got the inferior game after 5.Nc3 (still better for White is 5.Be2 Bc5 6.Nh3 Nxe5 7.Bg5 , as in the game Golombek-Tartakower, Staunton Centenary Tournament, 1951), 5...Nc6 6.Nh3 Bc5 7.a3 .

5.f4 Ne5-c6

Though it may appear odd to place the KN on the normal developing square for the QN, this is much better than Ng6, when the piece remains quite out of play. In this variation, as will be seen, the QN can be usefully developed via a6.

6.a3 a5

In order to play Bc5 without fearing being driven back by White's b2-b4. He has also in mind an eventual a5-a4.

7.Be3 Na6

8.Nf3 Bc5

9.Qd2 d6

10.Nc3 O-O

11.Bd3 Bxe3

12.Qxe3 Nc5

Both black Knights are now working to great effect, and though White has an imposing centre, it is liable to become weak unless the first player can manage to make the centre pawns dynamic rather than objects of attack themselves.

13.O-O ...

It would be preferable for White to take his life in both hands and Castle Q. Though this procedure is clearly risky, the position is such that nothing can be achieved without risk, and it is imperative to bring the Rooks to the centre files as quickly as possible.

13. ... Re8

14.Bc2 a4

Fixing the White Q side pawns and reserving the possibility of attacking the QBP by Nc6-a5 when occasion serves.

15.Ra1-e1 f6

16.Qf2 Bg4

Black wishes to prevent or discourage White from playing e4-e5, when his Bishop would come alive and threaten Black's side. Hence the text move, which undermines a vital support of White's e4-e5.

17.Nd4 Qd7

18.Nxc6 ...

Quite the wrong policy; he should play 17.Nd5 . The exchange of Knights both hinders White and helps Black: for the latter it opens up a potentially dangerous QN file, and it prevents the former from establishing a Knight on the strong outpost of d5.

18. ... bxc6

19.f5 ...

Threatening to win a piece by 20.Qh4 and planning a frontal assault on the King with his major pieces.

19. ... Bh5

20.Qh4 Bf7

21.Rf3 Re5

Not at once 21...Bxc4 , because of 22.e5 .

22.Re1-e3 Bxc4

23.Rh3 h6

24.Re3-g3 Kf8!

The right way, whereas 24...Kh8 would place the King in a mating net after 25.Rg6 , threatening 26.Rxh6+ .

25.Rg6 ...

To attack by doubling Rooks on the KN file looks promising, but it fails owing to a counter-stroke in the centre. A better chance was 25.Qf4 , when, however, Black retains an advantage by 25...d5 26.Rxh6 de 27.Rh8+ Bg8 .

25. ... Ra8-e8

26.Rh3-g3 ...

White's attack has reached its culminating point, and it seems as though Black cannot parry the numerous threats on his position (amongst these being the threat to triple on the N file by Qg4 and also the double capture on g7, followed by Qg4+ and Qg6). But now Black has a problem-like winning move.

26. ... Qxf5!

27.Rxf6+ ...

For if 27.ef Re1+ 28.Kf2 Rf1 mate.

27. ... Qxf6

Not 27...gf 28.Qxh6+ Ke7 29.Rg7+ , and now White can take the Queen, since his King can escape to g3.

28.Rf3 Re8-e6

29.Nxa4? ...

A mistake that shortens the game; in any case, however, White is lost, since Black has two Rooks and a pawn for the Queen, with much the better position.

29. ... Nxa4

30.Bxa4 Rxe4

31.Rxf6+ Rxf6!

32.g4 Be2

Resigns

The only move which does not lose the Queen or lead to a quick mate is 33.h3 , when Black simply takes the Bishop.

BUDAPEST, 1926
Réti Opening
Réti — A. Havasi

1.Nf3 d5

2.c4 dxc4

Quite a good system of defence that provides Black with the time for building up the centre, since White must expend two moves in regaining his pawn. The chief danger for Black is a tendency to become weak on the white squares, which danger he does not succeed in avoiding in the present game.

3.Na3 c5

This move is also good, as it provides a potential strong outpost for the Knight on d4. 3...e5 is interesting, but reacts to White's advantage after 4.Nxe5 Bxa3 5.Qa4+ b5 6.Qxa3 Bb7 7.e3 Qd6 8.Qxd6 cd 9.Nf3 Nc6 10.b3 .

4.Nxc4 Nc6

5.g3 f6

So as to force through e7-e5 and so prevent the useful development of White's QB on b2.

6.Bg2 Nh6

Black appears to be fascinated by White's method of developing his QN, and plays an eccentric move that is far from meeting the demands of the position. The square that needs strengthening is Q4, and to this end he should play 6...e5 , and if 7.d3 Be6 8.O-O Nge7 9.b3 Nd5 , with a good game.

7.O-O e5

8.d3 Nf7

9.Be3 ...

The commencement of a well-planned attack on the Q wing which, incidentally, could not have been conducted if Black were in a position to play Nd5.

9. ... Be7

10.Rc1 O-O

11.Nc4-d2 Nd4

11...b6 would lose the QBP after 12.Ne1 Bb7 13.b4 .

12.Nb3 Qb6

The only reasonable way of defending the QBP with the Queen, but also one that renders the black Queen liable to attack by White's Knights. If instead 12...Nxb3 13.Qxb3 Qb6 14.Nd2 Be6 15.Qa3 Rfc8 16.Rc3 Rc7 17.Rfc1 Rac8 18.Ne4 , winning the QBP.

13.Nf3-d2 Be6

14.Nc4 Qb5

Black has overlooked (or underestimated) the strength of White's next move. He must give up one of his two Bishops and play 14...Bxc4 15.Rxc4 Rfc8 .

15.Nb3-a5 Bxc4

Now, on the other hand, this capture is inferior, since White can retake with the Knight with advantage. Rather better is 15...Rab8 though White would still have the attack.

16.Nxc4 Qd7

17.Bd2! ...

This and the next move constitute an original manoeuvre by means of which White suddenly creates a K side attack whilst maintaining his pressure on the Q wing.

17. ... Ra8-c8

18.Ne3 Kh8

19.Nd5 ...

White has gained complete mastery of d5 as a result of Black's faulty 6th move, and also of his own subtle play. It is interesting to observe how all White's decisive moves in attack now centre on this square.

19. ... Bd6

20.e3 Nc6

21.Qh5 ...

With the immediate threat of 22.Bh3 .

21. ... Ng5

If 21...f5 22.Bh3 g6 23.Nf6 gh 24.Nxd7 Rfd8 25.Bxf5 , and wins.

22.f4 Qf7

23.Qh4 exf4

This exchange helps White, since it clears a way for his Rook to reach the KR file. 22...Ne6 at once was better.

24.gxf4 Ne6

25.Rf3! Nxf4

An ingenious combination, which, however, merely accelerates the loss. The trouble for Black in any case is that he has not the vestige of a counter-attack but must painfully watch White's attack increase in strength, whilst any such attempt as that made by this move brings immediate ruin.

26.exf4 ...

Much stronger than either 26.Rxf4 or 26.Nxf4 , when 26...g5 does succeed in gaining a counterattack.

26. ... Qxd5

27.Rh3 Qg8

Now White makes the final decisive use of the square d5 with a charming move that wins quickly.

28.Bd5! g5

29.fxg5 Qg7

If 29...fg 30.Qh6 , wins a piece.

30.Be4 Rc7

Or 30...f5 31.Bxc6 Rxc6 32.Bc3 , and wins.

31.g6 f5

If 31...Ne5 32.Bh6 , wins.

32.Bxc6 bxc6

33.Bc3 Be5

34.Qxh7+ Qxh7

35.Bxe5+ Resigns
