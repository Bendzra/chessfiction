ENDGAME STUDY SOLUTIONS

No. 1. 1.Kg7 Kb6 2.Kf6 h4 3.Ke5 h3 4.Kd6 h2 5.c7 , and both sides queen pawns with a drawn ending.

1...h4 2.Kf6 Kb6 3.Ke5 , and draws as above.

2...h3 3.Ke6 or 3.Ke7 , and draws.

[...]

No. 2. 1.Ke4 b3 2.Nd5 b2 3.Nc3 Kb3 4.Kd3 , and wins. The point of the ending is that other first moves by White allow Black to obtain a draw by stalemate. If 1.Ne4 b3 2.Kc4 b2 3.Nc3 b1Q 4.Nxb1+ Ka4 5. Bishop moves, and Black is stalemate. Similarly, if 1.Kc4 b3 2.Ne4 b2 , entering into the same variation as above.

[...]

No. 3. 1.Kf5! Be2 2.Kf4 , and now Black is in zugzwang. A Bishop move would allow one of the pawns to advance and a King move takes the black King too far away from the Q side pawns, so that White can play Kg5 and force the KBP forward, winning the Bishop, when one of his Q side pawns will queen.

If 1...Ke3 2.a5 Kd4 3.b6 ab 4.ab Kc5 5.Kf4 Bd5 6.Ke5 Bf3 7.h5 , and neither pawn can be taken without allowing the other to queen.

If 1...Kg3 2.Kg5 Be2 3.h5 Kf2 4.h6 Bd3 5.a5 . This gives the zugzwang picture as far as the attack is concerned; but there is also a zugzwang defence.

If White plays at once 1.Kf4 , then 1...Be2 , and now White is in zugzwang himself. Any pawn move loses a pawn, whilst if 2.Ke4 Kg3 , and if 2.Kg5 Ke3 , draws.

[...]

No. 4. 1.Kg5 Kf7 2.Kf5 Ke7 3.Kg6 Ke6 4.g5 , and wins, since his pawn queens with check before Black's QP gets to the 8th. If 3...d5 4.Kf5 Kd6 5.g5 , and wins, as the black King must go after the KNP and cannot get back in time to place itself in front of White's QRP. These two variations show the two ideas mentioned on p. 104.

1...Ke7 2.Kg6 transposes into the first variation given above.

Note that a straightforward rush for the QP leads to a draw by 1.Kg6 Ke7 2.Kf5 Kf7 3.Ke4 Kg6 4.Kd5 Kg5 5.Kxd6 Kxg4 6.Kc5 Kf5 7.Kb5 Ke6 8.Kxa5 Kd7 9.Kb6 Kc8 .

Equally, the ending is drawn if White attempts to force through his pawn to queen by 1.Kg6 Ke7 2.g5 d5 3.Kh7 d4 4.g6 d3 5.g7 d2 6.g8Q d1Q .

No. 5. 1.Ke1! g3 2.Nd2 g2 3.Nf3+ Kg3 4.Ng1 h2 (or 4...Kg4 5.Bc8+ , followed by Bxh3) 5.Ne2+ Kh3 (otherwise Bxg2) 6.Bc8 mate. If in this variation 2...Kg1 3.Nf3+ Kg2 4.Bc6 Kh1 (or 4...h2 5.Ng5+ Kg1 6.Nh3 mate) 5.Kf1 g2+ 6.Kf2 h2 7.Ne1 h3 8.Nxg2 hg 9.Bxg2 mate.

1...Kg1 2.Nc3 h2 3.Ne2 mate. This line shows why Ke1 is necessary: the e2 square must be vacated for the Knight, but at the same time the squares f2 and f1 must be prohibited to the black King.

No. 6. 1.Kb5 f4 2.Kc6 (not 2.Kxb6 f3 , when the game is drawn) 2...Kf2 (if now 2...f3 3.Kd6 f2 4.Kxe7 f1Q 5.Rf8 , and wins) 3.Kxb6 f3 4.Kc6 (not 4.c4 Ke2 5.Kc6 f2 6.Kd6 Rxh7! ) 4...Rf7 (if 4...Ke2 5.Kd6 , with a similar variation to that given in the first note. Or if 4...Kf1 5.Rf8 , wins at once) 5.Kd6 (other moves allow Black to play Kf1, e.g. 5.c4 Kf1 6.c5 f2 7.Kd6 Rf6+ 8.Kd5 Ke1 9.Re8+ Kd2 10.h8Q f1Q 11.Qh2+ Kc1 ) 5...Rf6+ ( 5...Kf1 is now met by 6.Ke6 , and if 5...Ra7 6.c4 Kf1 7.Rf8 , wins) 6.Kd5 Rf5+ 7.Ke6 Rh5 (or 7...Rg5 8.Rf8 ) 8.Kd6 (Black draws after 8.c4 Ke3 9.Kd6 Kd4 ) 8...Rh6+ 9.Kc5 Rh5+ 10.Kb4 , and now White fends off the Rook checks and wins at the same time by advancing the BP.

No. 7. 1.Kd7 (threatening Ke6 and, if necessary, Kf6 and Kg7) 1...Rh8 2.Kd6 (if 2.Ke7? Rxh7 , and Black wins) 2...Kc4 3.c6 bc 4.Ke5! (threatening Kf6 and Kg7. A mistake by which the wrong side would come into zugzwang is 4.Ke6? Kc5 . Now the RPs can be moved up, but then White must move the King. If 5.Kf6 Kd6 6.Kg7 Ke7 7.Kxh8 Kxf7 , and the game is drawn. Or if 5.Ke5 Rb8 6.Kf6 Kd6 7.Kg7 Ke7 , and Black wins) 4...Kc5 (preventing White from winning by 5.Kf6 , because of 5...Kd6 ) 5.Ke6! (now Black is in zugzwang. Both sides move up their RPs and then, if Black moves his King, White wins by 5.Kf6 if he moves his Rook, say 5...Rb8 6.Ke7 Rb7+ 7.Kf8 Rb8+ 8.Kg7 , and wins).

One other neat point: 1.Kd6 will not do because of 1...Rh8 2.c6 bc 3.Ke6 Kc5 , and again White finds himself in the zugzwang position described above.

No. 9. 1.Kd8! Bd6 2.Kd7! Kd5 (the only way of getting further, but it deprives the Knight of the all-important square, d5. No Knight move will serve here, e.g. 2...Ne7 3.b8Q or 2...Nf4 3.Kc8 ) 3.Kc8 Ne7+ 4.Kd7 , and draws.

One might ask why not 1.Kd7 at once, but this would lose after 1...Bd6 2.Kc8 Ne7+ 3.Kd7 Nd5! 4.Kc8 Nb6+ 5.Kd8 Ke6 6.Ke8 Bc7 , etc.

No. 12. 1.Kh6 (he cannot move his Queen, e.g. 1.Qf2 Be5 , with the threat of mate; hence he proposes to bring his King to h5, freeing his Queen. Bad would be 1.Kg7 Be5 2.c4 Bxf6+ 3.gf b4! , when White is in zugzwang, since 4.Kh7 loses after 4...Kd7 ) 1...Be5 2.Kg7! (the retort courteous! Now Black is in zugzwang, and he must move the Bishop away, since 2...Bxf6+ 3.gf means that White wins the Rook with a won pawn ending) 2...Bh2 3.c4 bc (after 3...b4 4.c5 wins) 4.e5! Or 1...Qh2+ 2.Kc6 Qe2 (if 2...Qe5 3.Rg3+ Kb4 4.Nc2+ Kc4 5.Ne3+ Kb3 6.Nf5 dis ch, followed by Re3. If in this variation 3...Kd4 4.Nb5+ , followed by Nd6+) 3.Nb5+ , and now we have five King moves from which to choose:

(a) 3...Kd2 4.Rd1+! Kc2 5.Nd4+ .

(b) 3...Kd3 4.Rd1+ Kc4 5.Nd6+ .

(c) 3...Kb2 4.Rb1+! , and wherever the King moves the Knight administers a fatal fork next move.

(d) 3...Kb4 4.Rg4+! Kxa5 5.Ra4+ .

(e) 3...Kc4 4.Nd6+ .
