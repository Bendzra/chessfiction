Chapter Seven. Miscellaneous Sacrifices

This chapter does not really need an introduction, since a brief note before each game or set of related games can do the task. So let's pitch right in. I promised to discuss the following game in the notes to Stean-Browne, so I'll take care of it now. Indeed, it's no hardship, because this is one of the most beautiful games ever played in a World Championship match.

Game 99
B.Spassky-T.Petrosian
World Championship (Game 19), Moscow 1969

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 Nbd7 7.Bc4

It's worth pausing a moment here to point out that White's sixth and seventh moves can be inverted. I believe in that case that 7.Bg5 is undoubtedly White's most testing move.

7...Qa5

After 7...e6 8.O-O , the reply 8...h6 was seen in Stean-Browne, whereas the move 8...Qc7? allows 9.Bxe6! , as in Keres-Sajtar. Both these games have been discussed earlier in this book. Otherwise, 7...h6 8.Bxf6 Nxf6 9.Qe2 is pleasant for White, while Gligoric considers 7...Qc7 and 7...g6 as playable, but dismisses 7...b5? 8.Bd5 Nxd5 9.Nxd5 as inferior.

8.Qd2 h6?! 9.Bxf6 Nxf6 10.O-O-O e6 11.Rhe1

Spassky's idea is simple. He will wait until Black castles short, then blast him with g2-g4. In the meantime he prepares a central advance to rush Black along.

11...Be7? 12.f4 O-O 13.Bb3 Re8 14.Kb1 Bf8 15.g4!

Routine but effective. This kind of line-opening sacrifice is more often played in the Open Sicilian by Black: for example, a ...b5 sacrifice in the Dragon. By now Petrosian was probably regretting his eighth move.

15...Nxg4

Gligoric mentions 15...Nd7 16.h4 with g4-g5 to come.

16.Qg2 Nf6 17.Rg1 Bd7 18.f5

This thrust, stock in the Sozin, here has the added bonus of threatening to blow open the doors to Black's home. Maybe Black should submit to passivity with 18...e5 . The Armenian's choice was unsuccessful, to put it mildly.

18...Kh8 19.Rdf1

Threatening 20.fxe6 and 21.Rxf6 .

19...Qd8 20.fxe6

If 20...Bxe6 White can lop off the bishop then continue as he does in the game.

20...fxe6

21.e5! dxe5 22.Ne4

A lovely breakthrough: f6 is crumbling, while 22...Nxe4? 23.Rxf8+ mates.

22...Nh5 23.Qg6! exd4

Robbing the fans of an even nicer finish. Gligoric gives 23...Nf4 24.Rxf4 exf4 25.Nf3 Qb6 26.Rg5! Bc6 27.Nf6! , which is certainly strong.

24.Ng5 1-0

This final position deserves a diagram. The end now would be 24...hxg5 25.Qxh5+ Kg8 26.Qf7+ Kh8 27.Rf3 , so Petrosian resigned. Not even Fischer ever made such short shrift of 'El Tigre' as did Spassky here.

Game 100
R.Fischer-J. Rubinetti
Palma de Mallorca Interzonal 1970

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4

Fischer's pet line which now bears his name.

6...e6 7.Bb3 b5 8.O-O Bb7?!

Chiefly as a result of this game, 8...Be7! is now generally accepted as the best defence. You can find discussion of that move elsewhere in this text, including in Nikolenko-Nagy in Chapter Two.

9.Re1!

Fischer was not the first to play this move, but he was the first to demonstrate its full power.

9...Nbd7 10.Bg5

Now Black is in a quandary. He cannot play 10...Be7 because of 11.Bxe6! . Instead in an earlier game K.Honfi-S.Tatai, Monaco 1968, cited by Wade and Blackstock in the English tournament book, Black chose 10...Nc5 and was hit with 11.Bd5! . The Italian master calmly replied 11...h6 , and eventually won the game! However, after 12.Bxf6! (rather than Honfi's 12.Bxb7 , but White might transpose to Fischer-Rubinetti with 12.Bh4 ) 12...Qxf6 13.b4! exd5 14.exd5+! White should be doing pretty well.

10...h6 11.Bh4

Fischer is content to lie in wait, but objectively 11.Bxf6 is probably stronger. King gives 11...Nxf6 12.Qf3 as promising for White, while Ivkov in Informator suggests 12.a4 b4 13.Na2 Nxe4 14.Nxb4 .

11...Nc5?

Walking right into the trap. Kavalek gives 11...g5! 12.Bg3 Ne5 as playable. King continues here with 13.Bxe6!? fxe6 14.Nxe6 Qd7 15.Nd5 which he likes for White.

12.Bd5? exd5

Instead 12...b4 is Tal-Mukhin from Chapter One.

13.exd5+

13...Kd7

White wins after 13...Be7? 14.Nf5 .

14.b4! Na4 15.Nxa4 bxa4 16.c4

Fischer is following earlier analysis by Maric. White has two pawns and a massive initiative for his piece.

16...Kc8 17.Qxa4 Qd7 18.Qb3 g5 19.Bg3 Nh5 20.c5!

Here they come!

20...dxc5 21.bxc5

21...Qxd5?

I find it amazing that the final moves of this game are glossed over in every published commentary I have yet read. No one has yet asked the obvious question: 'Why not 21...Bxd5 ?' Clearly the answer is not 22.c6? Bxb3 23.cxd7+ Kxd7 24.Nxb3 Nxg3 which trades too many pieces. Surely Mikhail Tal would not have repeated this line if that was all he could achieve! I believe Fischer would have played 22.Qc2! when I don't see a good defence for Black. The main line runs 22...Nxg3 23.hxg3 Bg7 24.c6 Qg4 25.Nf5! Bxa1 26.Ne7+ Kc7 27.Nxd5+ Kd6 28.Ne3! Qb4 29.Rd1+ Bd4 30.Qe4! Kc5 31.Qd5+ Kb6 32.Nc4+ and wins.

22.Re8+ Kd7 23.Qa4+ Bc6 24.Nxc6 1-0


The analogue for White of the standard ...Rxc3 sacrifice by Black is a relatively rare bird in the Open Sicilian, and even rarer when it scores a victory; many prominent examples end in a draw. Rarest of all is an effective Rxf6 when the black king's address is still e8. The following is that most rara avis:

Game 102
L.Yudasin-Van Wely
Philadelphia 1994

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.a4 e6 7.Be2 Nc6 8.O-O Be7 9.Be3 O-O 10.Kh1 Qc7 11.Qd2

Yudasin frequently avoids standard paths. This game is no exception.

11...Bd7 12.f4 Rfe8 13.Bd3 Ng4 14.Bg1 Nxd4 15.Bxd4 Bf6 16.Bxf6 Nxf6

The simplifications have been beneficial to Black. White faces the prospect of an inferior middlegame and ending due to his bad bishop. Thus he must act quickly.

17.Rae1 e5 18.fxe5 Rxe5

19.Rxf6 gxf6 20.Nd5 Qd8 21.Qh6

Now if 21...Re6 there follows 22.e5 with a mating attack. Van Wely finds the only way out.

21...Bf5 22.Nxf6+ Kh8 23.Rf1 Bg6 24.h4

It's very natural to make luft with such a frail back rank, but we think Yudasin missed a stronger plan here. 24.Bc4! does many good things: it stops ...Re6, it threatens Bxf7, and it prepares Bd5 followed by a queenside pawn roller. Indeed, 24...Qf8 ( 24...Rc8 25.Bd5! Rxc2?? 26.Bxf7 works this time) 25.Qd2! ( 25.Qxf8+? Rxf8 26.Nd7 Rxe4 dissipates all the pressure) 25...Qe7 26.Bd5 leaves Black with many problems to solve.

24...Re6

Intending to repulse the invading queen with ...Qf8.

25.Qg5

Yudasin analyses 25.Qe3 Rxf6 26.Qd4 Kg7 27.g4 d5! (once more the old saw about meeting a flank attack with action in the centre is affirmed) 28.exd5 ( 28.g5? dxe4 29.gxf6+ Kg8 is very good for Black) 28...Qb6 29.Qc3 Bxd3 30.cxd3 Rc8 31.Rxf6 Qxf6 32.Qxc8 Qf1+ with perpetual.

25...Kg7

Allowing a repetition. McDonald in Modern Chess Miniatures considers some alternatives: 25...Rc8 26.g4 Rc5 27.Qh6! or, better, 25...Qe7 26.g4 when Black should again submit to a repetition as in the game.

26.Nh5+ Kh8 27.Nf6 Kg7 1/2-1/2


Our next example illustrates a rare tactical theme I choose to call Paulsen's Coffin, having in mind the unfortunate predicament Louis Paulsen found himself in against Paul Morphy in their famous encounter at New York, 1857. One of the joys in writing a book like this is the unearthing of a treasure buried in some forgotten tome. The game which follows is one such. It's so obscure that I don't know the full name of the winner! There were two players with the surname 'Gusev' playing in 1951. Nikolay Gusev has no games in my database which start 1.e4, so I assume the white player is Yury Gusev. (Yury and Nikolay? Coincidence or kismet?)

At any rate, this unknown masterpiece is most likely the greatest achievement in the career of a modest Soviet Master, squashing one of the three survivors (at the time of writing) of the great Candidates Tournament at Zurich in 1953. I found this game in Rolf Schwarz's 1966 compendium Die Sizilianische Verteidigung and have based my notes upon his. Any translations from the German are my own.

Game 103
Y.Gusev-Y.Averbakh
Moscow 1951

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 g6 6.Be2 Bg7 7.Nb3

A bit early for this. More frequently seen in this position are the moves 7.Be3 and 7.O-O .

7...Nc6 8.O-O Be6 9.f4 Rc8?

White's unusual move order has caught his eminent adversary napping. The superior 9...Qc8! will probably transpose to standard lines.

10.f5!

Instead 10.Be3 O-O would transpose to a main line. In that case 11.f5 Bd7 12.g4?! Ne5 13.g5? Rxc3! is a favourable exchange sacrifice for Black, but this doesn't work in the game.

10...Bd7 11.g4 Ne5

With the white pawn storm already under way, Black was reluctant to 'castle into it', but castling was probably a better try.

12.g5 Ng8

Here 12...Rxc3? is, of course, well met by 13.gxf6! .

13.Nd5!

White plans 14.f6! exf6 15.gxf6 Nxf6 16.Bg5! .

13...f6

Hideous, but 13...gxf5 14.exf5 h6 15.h4 , leaving the horse on g8 'zugtot' (literally, move-dead), was also not appetizing.

14.Be3 b6 15.Nd4

White at once sets off to exploit the weakness on e6.

15...Kf7 16.c3

Preparing Qb3.

16...Qe8

To answer 17.Qb3 with 17...Ba4 . Now it was already possible to play technically with 17.a4 , but this was Mr. Gusev's day!

17.Ne6! Bxe6 18.fxe6+ Kf8

Here 18...Kxe6 19.Qb3 Kd7 20.Qa4+ Nc6 21.Bg4+ e6 22.gxf6 and wins is given by Schwarz. After the text, again White could switch fronts with 19.a4 , as perhaps Averbakh might have preferred if he had been White, but Gusev would not be distracted.

19.Nxf6! Nxf6

Instead 19...exf6 20.Qxd6+ and 21.Qxe5 is killing.

20.gxf6 Bxf6 21.Bh6+ Kg8

22.Rxf6! exf6 23.Qxd6 Rc6

Now what?

24.Qxe5!!

This position, diagrammed by Schwarz, caught my eye.

24...fxe5 25.Rf1

By now poor Averbakh was probably sorry he showed up for this contest.

25...Rc8

The other try is to put this rook on c7, aiming to blockade the e-pawn on e7. However, after 25...Rc7 26.Bd1! Re7 27.Bb3 White has a clear winning plan: put the bishop on d5, the king on g2, leave the rook on the f-file to answer ...g5 with Rf5, tying down the black queen, and make a passed c-pawn whose advance will finish matters. (The advance h4 if permitted will do no harm.) We won't deprive you of the pleasure you will get playing out this position against your computer by analysing any further.

26.Bd1 Rc4 27.Bb3 b5 28.Bxc4 bxc4 29.b3 a5 30.bxc4 Qe7 31.Kg2 Qa3 32.Rf2

Schwarz criticizes this move, calling it 'an aesthetic flaw which prolongs the game.' He prefers the direct 32.Rf7 Qb2+ 33.Kg3 Qxc3+ 34.Kg4 Qa3 35.c5! Qxc5 36.Rg7+ , winning. I must disagree. Herr. Schwarz was a correspondence player for whom shortening the game and saving postage was a serious consideration. Any OTB player will gladly milk this position for all its worth by repeating moves once or twice.

32...Qe7 33.Rf1 g5

Of course, 33...Qa3 34.Rf7 is quite hopeless, but nothing helps.

34.Rf5

'A cold-blooded winning move', according to Schwarz. Now the black queen can only oscillate between d8 and e7 to keep guard of the mating squares f8 and g5.

34...g4

Schwarz refutes 34...Qd8 with 35.c5 Qd2+ 36.Rf2 Qd8 37.c6 .

35.c5 Qd8 36.c6 Qe7 37.c7 1-0

'And Black strikes his colours. A demonic combination!' On that matter Herr. Schwarz and I are in complete accord.


There are not many variations in any opening in which a queen sacrifice is thematic, and in most lines in which the queen is offered, it's Black who does the offering; think of the Open Ruy Lopez with Qe2 or Bronstein's sacrifice in the Saemisch King's Indian. Nonetheless, the Najdorf features a couple of lines in which the white queen can surrender herself for a few bits of enemy pieces. Game 104
R.Nezhmetdinov-L.Chernikov
Rostov on Don 1962

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 g6 5.Nc3 Bg7 6.Be3 Nf6 7.Bc4 O-O 8.Bb3 Ng4

Suetin considers this dubious.

9.Qxg4 Nxd4 10.Qh4

The idea.

10.Qd1 is more natural.

10...Qa5 11.O-O Bf6

It seems White must resign himself to a draw by repetition after 12.Qh6 Bg7 13.Qh4 Bf6 , etc, but now Nezhmetdinov reveals his plan:

12.Qxf6!!

White will get only two minor pieces for the queen, but he gains the bishop-pair and inflicts severe damage to the enemy pawn structure. Moreover, his minor pieces will have safe and aggressive squares from which to ply their trade; in particular the dark-squared bishop becomes a monster in the absence of its rival.

12...Ne2+!

A finesse which gains time. After 12...exf6? 13.Bxd4 the combined attack of this bishop and the knight about to land on d5 will lead to a rout, and 12...Nxb3 13.axb3 Qxa1 14.Qxe7 is also quite hopeless.

13.Nxe2 exf6 14.Nc3

14...Re8?

Black omits the essential counterblow 14...d5! , which would permit him to develop and perhaps trade off one of White's more dangerous men.

15.Nd5 Re6 16.Bd4 Kg7 17.Rad1

The calm manner in which White masses his forces is most instructive.

17...d6 18.Rd3 Bd7 19.Rf3 Bb5 20.Bc3 Qd8 21.Nxf6! Be2

21...Bxf1 22.Ng4+ is even stronger here than 22.Nxh7+ , which is also good.

22.Nxh7+! Kg8

Suetin gives 22...Kxh7 23.Rxf7+ Kh6 24.Bxe6 Bxf1 25.Bd2+ g5 26.Bf5 Qh8 (or 26...Qg8 27.Rf6+ Kh5 28.g4+ ) 27.h4 with a winning attack.

23.Rh3 Re5 24.f4 Bxf1 25.Kxf1! Rc8 26.Bd4!

Nezhmetdinov will not allow his valuable bishop to be traded for a mere rook.

26...b5 27.Ng5 Rc7

28.Bxf7+!

One last combination finishes things.

28...Rxf7 29.Rh8+ Kxh8 30.Nxf7+ Kh7 31.Nxd8 Rxe4 32.Nc6 Rxf4+ 33.Ke2 1-0

Enough was enough. A beautiful example of sustained attack.


Theory has grouped all those 6.Bg5 Najdorf lines in which Black plays an early ...h6 under the convenient heading 'the Browne Variation', in honour of the six-time United States Champion who assumed the mantle of America's Najdorf guru upon the retirement of Bobby Fischer. Browne tried ...h6 in many different positions, but probably his greatest success was in the position arising after 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Be7 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.0-0-0 Nbd7 10.Bd3 h6 11.Qh3 Nb6!, as he established in several encounters with the Dutch GM Jan Timman in the 1970s.

The Browne Variation has a sound positional basis, the idea being to gain control of the e5-square with a well-timed ...g5, but when things go wrong, look out! Indeed, White is often able to offer his queen for a devastating attack, as in the following game played when Browne was just a schoolboy.

S.Gligoric-M.Bobotsov
Hastings 1959/60

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 h6

It later became the norm to play first 7...Be7 8.Qf3 and only then 8...h6 .

8.Bh4 Be7 9.Qf3 Qc7

9...g5 is the Gothenburg Variation, which we discussed in Chapter Two.

10.O-O-O Nbd7 11.Bd3

So we have transposed to Browne's line should White retreat his bishop to h4 in lieu of Timman's 11.Qh3 . In this move order the text has, though, been superseded by 11.Be2! , which we'll see in our next game.

11...b5?

Without the insertion of ...h6 and Bh4 this is a perfectly good move, but here it is fatal! Correct is 11...g5! .

12.e5 Bb7 13.Nxe6!

Yet another example of the weakening ...h6 provoking a lethal strike on e6.

13...fxe6 14.Bg6+ Kf8

Game 106
J.Grefe-W.Browne
U.S. Championship, El Paso 1973

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 h6 8.Bh4 Be7 9.Qf3 Nbd7 10.O-O-O Qc7 11.Be2!

For one thing, this covers the g4-square, which could be significant after ...g5. For another, it discourages 11...b5? because of 12.e5 Bb7 13.exf6! Bxf3 14.Bxf3 Bxf6 15.Bxf6 Rc8 16.Bh4! , according to Geller. Note too that Schwarz points out an interesting idea here: 16.Bxg7 Rh7 17.Bh5! , which might be worth looking into.

Importantly too the logical 11...g5 can be met by 12.fxg5 Ne5 13.Qg3 Rg8 14.Nf3 when Black cannot maintain his outpost on e5. So Black has to turn to other approaches:

11...Rb8 12.Qg3!

Now if Black castles, both 13.Nd5 and 13.Bf3 are good for White, according to Geller, while 12...g5 13.fxg5 Rg8 14.Nxe6! fxe6 15.gxf6! Rxg3 16.Bh5+ is a disaster. So Browne played the popular...

12...Rg8 13.Rhf1!

In an earlier round Tarjan had played 13.Rhe1 against Browne but only drew. The text is stronger.

13...g5 14.fxg5 Ne5 15.Nf3! b5

Can Black play better? A wiser choice is 15...hxg5! when White has two choices: 16.Nxg5 Rg6 17.Qh3 was agreed drawn in A.Soltis-B.Zuckerman, Birmingham 1973, but my preference is 16.Bxg5!? Nh5 17.Qh3 Bxg5+ 18.Nxg5 Rxg5 19.Bxh5 , which is untested, but I would prefer to be White.

16.Nxe5 b4

On 16...dxe5 Geller shows 17.gxf6! Rxg3 18.fxe7 Rg5 19.Bxg5 hxg5 20.Bh5 to be winning.

17.Nxf7! bxc3

18.gxf6!

By now this should come as no surprise.

18...Rxg3 19.fxe7 Rg5 20.Bxg5 hxg5 21.Nxd6+ 1-0

This game was instrumental to Grefe's sharing of the Championship with Lubomir Kavalek that year.

Game 107
Pedersen-Hallmeyer
Denmark 1973

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 b5

Polugaevsky's move. White's response is probably the simplest in this hellishly complex opening.

8.e5 dxe5 9.fxe5 Qc7 10.exf6 Qe5+ 11.Be2 Qxg5 12.O-O

12...Qe5

The immediate 12...Ra7 is also possible. The last word on this move seems to be Leko-Ghaem Maghami, Yerevan 2001. See the next game.

13.Bf3 Ra7 14.Nc6 Qc5+?!

15.Kh1 Rd7

Or 15...Nxc6 16.Ne4 and the threats of fxg7 and Nd6+ leave Black in a mess, such as after 16...Qd4 17.Nd6+! Qxd6 18.fxg7 .

16.Nxb8! Rxd1 17.Raxd1 gxf6 18.Ne4

Black has but one saving move...

18...Qc7?

19.Nxf6+ Ke7 20.Bh5! Bg7

Black avoids mate by 20...Qxb8 21.Ng8+ Rxg8 22.Rxf7+ Ke8 23.Rg7 .

21.Nc6+! Qxc6 22.Ng8+! Ke8 23.Bxf7+ Kf8 24.Rd8+ 1-0

The most ruthless, and sufficient to induce resignation.


In chess, as in life, first impressions last. Paul Keres introduced a fascinating queen sacrifice against Fischer in Zurich 1959, but unfortunately he lost the game. Indeed, Fischer's improvised defence was so effective that all but a few hardy souls were deterred from exploring the line. One such intrepid individual was the Colombian GM Gildardo Garcia, who dared to test his ideas against Fischer's successor as the American Najdorf guru, the redoubtable Walter Browne.

Of course, not 11...Bxf6? 12.Bxb5! axb5 13.Nxb5 with 14.Nxd6+ to follow, leaving Black's king a sitting duck in the centre.

At present White has a rook and two light pieces for the lady, but the king's bishop is awaiting execution behind enemy lines.

16.Bc6+!

This was Garcia's new idea. Keres played 16.Bxd5 straightaway, but Garcia uses his bishop's dying breath to sabotage Black's harmony.

16...Ke7?!

Browne's punctuation in Informator. In hindsight he preferred 16...Kf8 17.Nce2 Bxd4 18.Nxd4 Qxf4+ 19.Kb1 Qd6 20.Rhf1 g6 intending ...e5 with, he claimed, a slight plus for Black. The problem with this is the killing shot 21.Nxe6+! Qxe6 22.Bxd5 . Now if Black doesn't want to give back the queen and defend a lost rook and pawn ending, he must play something like 22...Qe5 23.Rxf7+ Ke8 24.Ra7 Qf6 when he is holding on by his fingernails. Many moves look good, for instance 25.c4 or even 25.a3 . I don't see a forced win for White, but I would not mind taking over his chair.

Flash forward 17 years to Las Vegas 2007, another big Swiss, the National Open, and Gildardo Garcia repeats the variation and gets the same position after 16.Bc6+ . His young opponent, N.Yap, played 16...Kf8 17.Nce2 g6! ,

although after 18.a4 b4 19.Rhf1 Kg7 20.g4 Rc8 21.g5 Be7 22.h4 Qb6 23.a5 Qxa5 24.Kb1 Qb6 25.Ba4 White had rescued the bishop with continuing pressure. Indeed, White later won, although Black may have missed a draw.

So what should Black do? Nunn and King both mention that Black can transpose to Keres-Fischer with the move order 15...Bxd4! 16.Rxd4 d5 .

Nunn follows with 17.Bxd5 exd5 18.Re1+ Kf8 19.Re5 (instead of Keres' 19.Nxd5 which is playable, though) 19...g6 20.Nxd5 which he implies is about equal. The whole thing is food for thought, and inspiration to investigators everywhere.
