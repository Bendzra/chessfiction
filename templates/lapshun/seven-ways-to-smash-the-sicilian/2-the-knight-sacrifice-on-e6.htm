Chapter Two. The Knight Sacrifice on e6

The sacrifice N(x)e6 is one of the less common sacrificial ideas in the Sicilian, as it tends to be a true sacrifice; White generally obtains only two pawns for his piece. However, from a strategic viewpoint, White gains complete domination of the vital central squares d5 and f5, whilst ripping away the pawn cover of the black king. The light-squared bishop which settles on e6 can play a leading role in the attack, especially if the g6-square has been weakened by an advance of Black's h-pawn. The weakness of g6 is in fact a green light for Nxe6, as in the following classic contest.

Nice Olympiad 1974

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 Nbd7 7.Bc4 e6

At that time 7...Qa5 had been getting hammered, as in Spassky-Petrosian, 19th matchgame 1969, which will be discussed in more detail in Chapter Seven.

8.O-O

Threatening a timely Bxe6!.

8...h6 9.Bxf6!

White plays a la Spassky.

9...Nxf6 10.Bb3 b6 11.f4 Bb7 12.Qd3 Be7

Now White must decide whether to play fairly calmly with the standard thrust f4-f5, or to sacrifice something on e6. Since the American chess patron I.S.Turover had offered a prize of $1000 for the most brilliant game at Nice, the young English IM went for the sacrifice with gusto.

13.Nxe6! fxe6 14.Bxe6

Now Black's king is stuck in the centre, and there is the nasty if obvious threat of e4-e5 followed by Qd3-g6+. Note that White had to sacrifice this way round: 13.Bxe6? fxe6 14.Nxe6 Qd7 15.Nxg7+ Kf8! 16.Nf5 Bxe4! wins for Black.

14...b5

In the tournament book, Stean noted that he would have answered 14...Nd7 with 15.Rad1 Nc5 16.Qh3 Nxe6 17.Qxe6 . If instead 14...Bc8 , the bishop drops back to b3 and Black is no closer to escaping from the centre.

15.e5 Qb6+ 16.Kh1 dxe5 17.Qg6+ Kd8 18.Qf7!

Now the plan is Rad1+ and/or fxe5.

18...Qc5 19.fxe5 Bxg2+

Hoping for a miracle on the g-file. Gligoric gives 19...Rf8 20.Qxg7 Nd7 21.Rfd1 as winning, while 19...Ne8 20.Rad1+ Kc7 21.Rd7+ is futile.

20.Kxg2 Rf8 21.Rad1+ Kc7 22.Qxg7 Rg8 23.exf6!

Keeping the bishop around to support the resulting passed pawn proves to be the cleanest way to win.

23...Rxg7+ 24.fxg7 Bd6

The point of White's 23rd is apparent after 24...Qg5+ 25.Kh1 Qxg7 26.Rf7 Qg5 27.Rxe7+ Qxe7 28.Nd5+ .

25.Rf7+ Kc6 26.Bd5+ Kb6 27.Bxa8 Qg5+ 28.Kh1 Be5 29.b4 a5

30.Rb7+

White has mate in 8 with 30 Nd5+ but the text is good enough.

30...Kc6

It's mate after 30...Ka6 31.Rxb5 Qxg7 32.Rxa5+ Kb6 33.Nd5 , as Gligoric pointed out.

31.g8Q Qxg8 32.Rb8+ 1-0

Browne was undoubtedly short of time and could not pause to resign sooner. By the way, Stean got his $1000!

12...Qa5 is another choice which after the popular 13.b3 Nc5 14.a3 Nxa4 15.axb4 Qc7 16.bxa4 leads to a very complicated position with chances for both sides.

Kasparov suggests 14...Rg8!? with a playable game.

18.Bxg5 Qa5?

Better is 18...Qxg5! 19.Qxg5 Bh6 , although White is still better according to Kasparov.

19.f4 Rh2

The attempt to slow White down by playing 19...e5 proves fruitless after 20.Nf5 (Kasparov gives 20.Nb3! as winning) 20...f6 21.Ne3 fxg5 22.Nxd5 gxf4 23.Nab6 Bxd5 24.Nxd5 Rd8 25.Bf3 Bg7 26.Qxf4 . 20.Nxe6!

This concludes the game in style.

20...fxe6 21.Rxe6+ Kf7 22.Qd3 Bg7 23.Qf5+ Kg8 24.Rxd5 Qxa4 25.Re7 1-0

Game 27

U.S. Championship, Pasadena 1978

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 e6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 d6 6.g4 a6 7.g5 Nfd7 8.Be3 b5 9.a3 Nb6 10.Rg1 N8d7 11.f4 Bb7 12.f5

12...e5 13.Ne6?

This looks like a good sacrifice where all White's pieces are ready to pounce on an open black king, but in reality Black has enough resources to protect his Majesty. Instead 13.Nb3 Rc8 14.Bd3 Nc4 15.Bxc4 Rxc4 16.Qd3 Qa8 17.Nd2 Rc8 18.O-O-O Nc5 19.Bxc5 Rxc5 left White with a slight advantage in Y.Balashov-B.Malich, Leipzig 1973.

13...fxe6!

If Black refuses this kind offer, White can get a better position: for example, after 13...Qb8 14.Nxf8 Kxf8 15.Qd2 Nc5 16.Rg4 Black has to solve many problems.

14.Qh5+

The alternative 14.fxe6!? doesn't give White enough initiative for a piece after 14...Nc5 : for instance, 15.Bxc5 dxc5 16.Qf3 Qe7 17.Nd5 Nxd5 18.exd5 Rd8 19.O-O-O Rd6 20.Bh3 g6 21.Qf6 Bg7 22.Qxe7+ Kxe7 23.Rgf1 Rf8 and White's position is hopeless.

14...g6?

We believe the fearless 14...Ke7!! was winning in all variations: 15.fxe6 (fruitless is 15.g6 Nf6 16.Bg5 Kd7 17.fxe6+ Kc7 18.Bxf6 Qxf6 19.gxh7 Qxe6 and White is losing) 15...Kxe6 16.Bh3+ Ke7 17.Rf1 Qe8 18.Qh4 Kd8 and Black will finish castling by hand while keeping an extra piece.

15.fxg6 Ke7 16.gxh7

Black has simply handed his opponent two passed pawns for no reason.

16...Bg7 17.O-O-O Qe8 18.g6 Nf6

A rule says: 'If you are up material, trade pieces.' Thus after 18...Na4 White would play 19.Nxa4 bxa4 20.Qg5+ Nf6 21.Qxe5! Rd8 22.Bc4 Bc8 ; this slows down White's attack a bit, but most likely Black is doomed in the long run.

19.Qxe5!

This neat tactic equalizes the material count.

19...Qc6

It's mate after 19...dxe5 20.Bc5 . Meanwhile the passive 19...Nc8 allows White to mobilize all his forces in amusing fashion as follows: 20.Bc5 Qd7 21.Bh3 Ne8 22.Qg5+ Bf6 23.Qe3 Kd8 24.Bd4 Bg7 25.Nd5 and the white army will tear Black's fortress to shreds.

20.Rxd6!

A further blow. Now nothing can save Black.

20...Qxd6 21.Bc5 Rad8 22.Bh3 Bc8 23.Bxd6+ Rxd6 24.Qg5 e5

The only way to save the piece.

25.Qxe5+

In this endgame Black is down the equivalent of three pawns. In order to win White will trade as many pieces as possible and then his queen and passed pawns will decide matters.

25...Be6 26.Bxe6 Rxe6 27.Qc5+ Ke8 28.Qf5 Re7 29.Rd1 Nfd7 30.Nd5 Nxd5 31.Qxd5 Be5 32.Qc6 Kd8 33.Rd5 Rf8 34.Qa8+ 1-0
