Chapter Three. The Bishop Sacrifice on e6

The sacrificial possibility Bxe6 is one of the most common themes in the Sicilian, if not one that is commonly realized. In some circles it is referred to as the 'Capablanca sacrifice', because of a couple of games the Cuban played in the 1920s. Consider the following:

Game 39
J.Capablanca-K.Havasi
Budapest 1928

1.d4 d5 2.c4 e6 3.Nf3 dxc4 4.e4 c5 5.Bxc4 cxd4 6.Nxd4 Nf6 7.Nc3 a6?

At Moscow 1925 Bogoljubow played 7...Bc5 8.Be3 Nbd7 which allowed 9.Bxe6 , but at least White had not castled there.

8.O-O Bc5 9.Be3 Nbd7?

He really had to castle. Leaving the king in the centre is asking for it.

10.Bxe6! fxe6 11.Nxe6 Qa5 12.Nxg7+ Kf7 13.Nf5

Three pawns and an attack for the piece. So far, so caveman, but the rest is a bit more subtle...

13...Ne5 14.Qb3+ Kg6 15.Rac1 Bf8 16.Ne2 h5 17.Rfd1 Rg8 18.Nf4+ Kh7 19.Bb6 Qb5 20.Rc7+ Kh8 21.Qxb5

Trading queens in the midst of a violent attack is so typically Capablanca.

21...axb5 22.Rd8 Rxa2 23.Rdxc8 Nc4 24.h3 Nxb6 25.Rxf8! Nfd7 26.Rf7 Rxb2 27.Nd5 1-0

Yes, I know it was a QGA, but if you like you can think of it as a funky Maroczy Bind. And, yes, I know that Black played the opening like a zombie, but a lot of people played like that back then. However, this is as clear and simple an example of the mechanics of the Bxe6 sacrifice as we could find. Virtually every game in this chapter has some complication which makes life difficult. (Don't ask about Fedorov-Kobalija, which could fit into four different chapters! I'm still not wholly sure why we put it in this one.)

We will consider differing placements of the black king, as per custom.

1. The Uncastled King

The aim here is naturally to keep His Majesty in the centre and expose him to attack. If you can get three pawns and an attack, then go ahead and sacrifice! If you get only two pawns, though, you need to start with a big lead in development to support that knight on e6 to make things fly. At least, once the e6-pawn leaves the premises, you get to use d5. You may also be able to push f4-f5 and nail down exclusive use of e6. Another positive indicator is a weakening move like ...h6.

Of course, if the black king can run to the queenside as in Cervenka-Schneider, below, then look for a better opportunity to sacrifice.

2. The Castled King

It's hard to give specifics here. Look for a weakness in the castled position and/or a massive concentration of your forces near the king's address. And, while you're at it, look for other sacrifices as well!

15.h4 Rg8 16.Bc4 gxf6 17.Rhe1 Rg6

Worse is 17...fxg5? on account of 18.Bxe6! fxe6 19.Qf6 and after 19...Qxe1 20.Rxe1 Rc7 21.Qxe6+ Re7 22.Qc8+ Kf7

This obvious sacrifice is enough only for a draw. Tseshkovsky gives the stronger 18.Bxf6 which prepares a Bxe6 blow. He analyses, for example, 18...Rxg2 19.Kb1! 19...Kf7 20.Rxf6+

20...Kg8 21.Qc4+ Kh8

or 17...Ba4 18.Bb3 Bb5 19.Qd2 h5 20.Rhe1 Qf5 21.Rf1 Qxf1 22.Rxf1 Bxf1 23.Bxe6 fxe6 24.f7+ Kxf7 25.Qd7+ and wins.

18.Qb3! Rd8

White wins immediately after 18...Bxg2?? 19.Rhf1 Bxf1 20.Qxb7 .

19.Rdf1 Qg4 20.Bxe6!

This is a dynamic way to destroy the blockade of the f-pawn.

20...fxe6

Neither is Black saved by 20...Qxe6 21.Re1 Be4 22.Qa4+ b5 23.Qxb5+ Rd7 24.Qa4 and it's game over.

21.Rf4 Qe2 22.f7+ Kd7 23.Rd4+ Bd6 24.Rhd1 Bd5

24...Kc7 25.Bxd8+ Rxd8 26.Rxd6 Rxd6 27.Rxd6 Qf1+ 28.Rd1 Qxf7 29.Qe3 is just as bad, according to Svidler.

25.Rxd5!

The final nail in the coffin.

25...exd5 26.Qxb7+ 1-0

Game 42
R.Kasimdzhanov-P.Van der Sterren
German League 2001

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 e6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nc6 5.Nc3 d6 6.g4 h6 7.Be3 Nf6 8.h3

The more aggressive 8.Rg1 Be7 9.h4 d5 10.Bb5 Bd7 11.exd5 Nxd5 12.Nxd5 exd5 13.g5 hxg5 14.hxg5 was slightly better for White in S.Movsesian-A.Maksimenko, Chalkidiki 2002.

8...Be7 9.Qd2 a6 10.O-O-O Nxd4 11.Qxd4 Bd7

Black might do better with 11...e5 : for example, 12.Qd2 Be6 13.f4 Qa5 14.a3 exf4 15.Bxf4 Rd8 16.Bg2 Nd7 17.Kb1 Ne5 18.Nd5 Qxd2 19.Bxd2 Bxd5 20.exd5 and the strong central knight maintained equality in A.Rogic-Z.Kozul. Austrian League 2005.

12.f4 Bc6 13.Rg1

Kasimdzhanov doesn't like 13.g5? hxg5 14.fxg5 e5 15.Qd3 Ng4 . We continue his line as follows: 16.Rg1 Nxe3 17.Qxe3 Rh5 18.g6 Bg5 19.gxf7+ Kxf7 20.Rxg5 Qxg5 21.Qxg5 Rxg5 22.Rxd6 and Black has good chances to win this ending.

13...Nd7

It's too dangerous for Black to castle kingside, because after 13...O-O 14.g5 hxg5 15.fxg5 Nd7 16.g6 Ne5 17.gxf7+ Rxf7 18.Be2 Rc8 19.Bh5 White will get a tremendous attack.

14.Bc4 Qa5

According to Kasimdzhanov, 14...b5 is losing after 15.Bxe6 fxe6 16.Qxg7 Rf8 (it would not help to try 16...Bf6 17.Qg6+ Ke7 18.e5 Nxe5 19.fxe5 Bxe5 20.Bxh6 Qg8 21.Qg5+ Qxg5+ 22.Bxg5+ Kd7 23.h4 and one extra pawn with two passed pawns should give White good winning chances) 17.Qg6+ Rf7 18.Qxe6 and Black's various problem pieces give White more than enough compensation for the bishop.

15.Bxe6!

A piece sacrifice which opens Black's king to a deadly attack. Instead the seemingly aggressive 15.f5 weakens the dark squares and gives Black a chance to get a small initiative, such as with 15...Bf6 16.Qxd6 O-O-O 17.Qd2 Qb4 18.Bd3 Ne5 19.a3 Qa5 20.Rgf1 Nc4 21.Bxc4 Rxd2 22.Bxd2 Qe5 .

15...fxe6 16.Qxg7 Bf6!

Another possibility with 16...Rf8 17.Qg6+ Rf7 18.Qxe6 Nf8 would have saved the king, but not for long. Here 19.Qb3 (best, since 19.Qxh6? Rh7 traps the queen) 19...Nd7 20.Nd5 looks good, but this position is so complicated that even a computer can get lost. We believe, though, that White's activity and pair of passed pawn should be enough for a win: for instance, 20...Rc8 21.g5 Ba4 22.Qa3 Rxc2+ 23.Kb1 Rc4 24.Rc1 Rxc1+ 25.Kxc1 Bd8 26.g6 Rg7 27.Qxd6 Qb5 28.Qe6+ Kf8 29.Qf5+ Kg8 30.b3 Qc6+ 31.Kb1 Bb5 32.Bd4 Bd3+ 33.Ka1 and White's attack should be enough.

17.Qg6+ Ke7 18.e5

This is a decoy sacrifice to open the d-file and prepare the next coup.

18...dxe5?

We agree with Kasimdzhanov that 18...Nxe5 was the only way to save Black's position. He gives the following variations: 19.fxe5 Qxe5 20.Bd2 (alternatively, 20.Rge1 Bh4 is equal, while 20.Qd3 Qxe3+ 21.Qxe3 Bg5 22.Rge1 leads to a level endgame) 20...Rag8 21.Qd3 Bb5 22.Qf3 Bc6 and now 23.Qd3 repeats, but in our opinion 23.Qf2!? could perhaps help to light a fire around the black monarch.

19.Rxd7+!

And now an elimination sac, with decoy ideas added. Rudolf Spielmann would have loved this game, as he could have displayed his entire sacrificial nomenclature!

19...Kxd7

19...Bxd7 20.Ne4 Raf8 21.Bc5+ Kd8 22.Bxf8 Rxf8 23.Nxf6 Qxa2 24.Qg7 is winning for White.

20.Rd1+ Ke7

If 20...Kc7 , then 21.Qf7+ Kb8 22.Qxf6 Re8 23.fxe5 Kc7 24.Qg7+ Kb8 25.Rd6 Qc7 26.Qxh6 and the active white pieces and two passed pawns are enough for a win.

21.g5 Rhf8 22.Qh7+ Rf7

According to Kasimdzhanov, after 22...Bg7 23.Qxg7+ Rf7 24.Qxh6 exf4 25.g6 fxe3 26.Qh4+! Rf6 27.Qh7+ Black can't stop the passed g-pawn.

23.gxf6+ Kxf6 24.Qxh6+ Ke7 25.f5! 1-0

This diversionary sacrifice crowns a fine effort. White wins in all variations: for instance, 25...Rxf5 (or 25...exf5 26.Qd6+ Ke8 27.Qe6+ Kf8 28.Bh6+ Kg8 29.Rg1+ Kh8 30.Qxf7 ) 26.Qg7+ Ke8 27.Ne4 Qb4 28.Bc5 and it's all over.

Tony Kosten prefers 12...Bxd4 in his Easy Guide to the Najdorf and this move would avoid the following headaches for Black.

Better is 14.Qe2 Bxe3+ ( 14...Bxf6 15.Rad1 Qc7 16.Rxf6 gxf6 17.Ne4 is too dangerous for Black's king) 15.Qxe3 Qd4 16.Rae1 gxf6 17.Ne4 O-O-O 18.Rxf6 Qxe3+ 19.Rxe3 , which gave White a slight advantage in the game N.De Firmian-M.Carlsen, Copenhagen 2004.

14...Bxe3+ 15.Kh1 Rg8 16.Bxe6?

We believe that the simple 16.Qf3 Rxg7 17.Qxe3 gives chances to both sides in this complicated position, which holds perils for both kings.

16...Rxg7

16...fxe6 leads to complicated variations, but should be enough to win: for example, 17.Qh5+ Kd7 18.Rad1+ Bd4 19.Ne2 Qe8 20.Rf7+ Kc8 21.Nxd4 Rxg7 22.Nf5 Rxf7 23.Nd6+ Kd7 24.Nxe8+ Kxe8 25.Rd7 Kxd7 26.Qxf7+ Ne7 and Black's three pieces should win.

17.Bxf7+

17...Rxf7 18.Qh5 Ne5

Instead 18...Qd7! 19.Rae1 Kf8 20.Rxf7+ Qxf7 21.Qxf7+ Kxf7 22.Rxe3 was an easier way to win.

19.Qxe5+ Qe7! 20.Qh8+?

Better was 20.Qh5 , but the accurate 20...Kf8 21.Qxh7 Bd4 22.Rae1 Bg7 still leaves White with almost no chances for survival.

20...Kd7 21.Rad1+

According to Florian, 21.Rxf7 is losing after 21...Qxf7 22.Qe5 Bxg2+! 23.Kxg2 Rg8+ .

21...Ke6! 0-1

Black's king is safe enough and 22.Rfe1 Qg5 leaves White facing a bunch of deadly threats.

White opens up the black king, placing a bone in the throat on e6 while preparing to destroy Black with two rooks in the centre.

14...fxe6 15.Nxe6 Qe7 16.O-O-O Kf7 17.Rhe1 Be5

18.f4! Qxe6 19.fxe5

24.g4

24...Qf3 25.Nd5 Rc8 26.Qd6 Bxg4 27.Re7+ Kg6 28.Nf4+

29.Qxf4 Bxd1 30.Qe4+ 1-0

It's forced mate: 30...f5 31.Re6+ Kg5 32.h4+ Kh5 33.Qf4 Bxc2+ 34.Ka1 Rc6 35.Rxh6+ Rxh6 36.Qg5 and mate.

12.Bxe6!!

After this move a deluge of white pieces descends on the black king's head. 12...fxe6

13...Qc4

(c) 13...Qd7 14.Nd5 Bxd5 15.Qh5+ g6 16.Qxd5 Nc6 17.e5! Bxe5 18.f4 Bf6 19.Rhe1 Be7 20.f5 gxf5 21.Qxf5 Qc8 22.Ng7+ Kd8 23.Qc5 h5 24.Rxe7 Kxe7 25.Qxd6+ Kf7 26.Rf1+ Kg8 27.Qd5+ is mating.

(d) 13...Qc6 should be better for White after 14.Nd5 Nd7 15.Nec7+ Kf7 16.Nxa8 Bxa8 17.Qh3 Nb6 18.Nxf6 gxf6 19.Qh5+ Kg7 20.Rhe1 .

Game 46
L.Nisipeanu-R.Ruck
Croatian Team Championship 2006

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bc4 e6 7.Bb3 b5 8.Bg5 Be7 9.Qf3 Qc7 10.O-O-O Nbd7

11.Bxf6

The wild complications after 11.e5 Bb7 12.Qg3 Nxe5 13.Bxe6 fxe6 14.f4 Kf7 ( 14...Ng6 15.Nxe6 Qd7 16.Rhe1 Kf7 17.f5 Nf8 18.Bxf6 Bxf6 19.Rxd6 Qc8 20.Ng5+ Kg8 21.Rxf6 gxf6 22.Nge4+ Ng6 23.fxg6 forced Black to resign in L.Christiansen-A. Wojtkiewicz, U.S. Championship, San Diego 2006) 15.fxe5 dxe5 16.Nxe6 Kxe6 17.Qh3+ Kf7 18.Bxf6 gxf6 19.Rd7 Qc6 20.Rhd1 Rad8 21.Qh5+ Ke6 22.Qh3+ Kf7 23.Qh5+ Ke6 led to a draw in the earlier game L.Nisipeanu-S.Karjakin, Foros 2006.

11...Nxf6

According to Nisipeanu, 11...Bxf6 can get Black into big trouble after 12.Bxe6 fxe6 13.Nxe6 Qc6 14.Nd5 Kf7 15.Ng5+ Ke8 ( 15...Kf8 doesn't help because 16.Qh5 g6 17.Qh6+ Kg8 18.Nxh7! Rxh7 19.Qxg6+ Kh8 20.Nxf6 Nxf6 21.Qxf6+ Rg7 22.Rxd6 will soon finish off the black king) 16.Qh5+ g6 17.Qg4 and White's mating net will soon force resignation.

12.g4 Nd7

In the later game Y.Vovk-S.Tolontegin, Tashkent 2008, after 12...O-O 13.g5 Nd7 14.g6 Nc5 15.gxh7+ Kh8 16.Rhg1 Bb7 17.a3 Nxb3+ 18.cxb3 b4 19.Rxg7! Bxe4 20.Qxe4 Kxg7 21.h8Q+ Kxh8 22.Rd3 White got a better position, but 22.Qg4! would have been a more precise way to finish.

However, we believe Nisipeanu is right that 12...b4! 13.Nce2 Bb7 14.Ng3 d5 15.exd5 Bxd5 16.Bxd5 Nxd5 17.Ndf5 O-O gives Black a slight edge.

13.Bxe6!

At first glance, this looks like a simple case of 'three pawns and a check', but in fact it is much deeper.
