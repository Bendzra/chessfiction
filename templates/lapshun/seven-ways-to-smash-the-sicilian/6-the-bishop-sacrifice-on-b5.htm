Chapter Six. The Bishop Sacrifice on b5

The sacrifice Bxb5 is after Nc3-d5 the most common sacrificial theme in the Open Sicilian, since in many variations Black plays ...a6 and ...b5 at an early stage when White's lead in development is best exploited by this direct assault. In almost all cases Black is uncastled or has castled long, and the considerations outlined next apply in either case.

After playing Bxb5 axb5, White will normally recapture with his knight from d4, especially in the typical case of the black queen being posted on c7. White thus gains a second pawn and a tempo straightaway. In certain cases, when the pawn on d6 is insufficiently guarded, White will gain a third pawn with check, disturbing the enemy king and, if Black has castled long, achieving a decisive fork on the weakened point f7. Other times, White may follow up Ndxb5 with the thrust e4-e5 which often ensures a trade of dark-squared bishops to enable a big check on d6. (See Velimirov:c-Al Kazzaz below for an illustration of these latter ideas.)

We haven't examined many endings in this book (nor will we), but the following is a well-known classic which illuminates some considerations in the 'three-pawns-for-a-piece' structure which can arise if White is fortunate.

11...Qb8

I'm not aware that anybody has tried Pachman's suggestion of 11...Qa5 yet.

12.Nxd6+ Bxd6 13.Qxd6 Qxd6 14.Rxd6

White has acquired three connected passed pawns for his piece. The question is: can he advance them without jeopardizing his king and without permitting a blockade?

14...h6 15.Bd2

Pachman in his Modern Chess Strategy prefers 15.Bxf6 Nxf6 16.Rhd1 Bb7 17.f3 Ke7 18.Rb6 .

15...Bb7 16.f3 O-O?

I must agree with Pachman that it is much more sensible to play 16...O-O-O here. The black king would be well placed on the queenside to assist the blockade of White's pawns, and just as important, it would be out of the way for Black to seek counterplay with ...g5-g4.

17.b3 Rfc8 18.Kb2 Nc5 19.Be3

19...e5?!

In a few moves this will look like hara-kiri, but it is hard to suggest a spirited alternative. At present I think the following is a good try: 19...Kf8 20.Rhd1 Ke7 21.e5 Nd5 22.Nxd5+ exd5 23.c4 when Black should return the piece with 23...dxc4! 24.Bxc5 Rxc5 25.Rd7+ Ke6 26.Rxb7 c3+ 27.Ka1 Rxe5 and this double rook ending offers some counterplay for Black. Also possible in this line is to put the king on e8 instead of e7; at least the computer does not show a forced win in that case.

20.Rhd1 Ne6 21.Rb6 Bc6 22.Nd5 Bxd5

After this virtually forced capture, White has four connected passers and the rest, as they used to say, is a mere bagatelle.

23.exd5 Nc5 24.Rb5

This is an ideal square for the rook. It is a perfectly safe perch from which to fight for the key blockade square c5.

24...Nfd7 25.c4 e4 26.Bxc5 Nxc5 27.fxe4 Nxe4

28.d6!

Establishing a pawn pair on the sixth rank is much more to be valued than hoarding all the booty.

This assault has pretty much put Black's 12th move out of business, whereas Spassky preferred 13.Bxf6 Nxf6 14.Qxg7 which won a pawn but Fischer scrounged up enough play to draw.

13...axb5 14.Ndxb5 Qb6 15.e5

15...d5

Moving the knight at once or swapping pawns on e5 will inevitably lead to a fatal knight check on d6. Thus Black offers to return a piece, hoping to recapture with tempo with his pawn and at least secure a bit of play. However, Velimirovic will not be mollified.

16.f5!

With this intermezzo, White opens both the e-file and the diagonal leading to c7. Moreover, he threatens 17.fxe6 fxe6 18.exf6 gxf6 19.Rxe6! Qxe6 20.Qc7 mate and, of course, 16...exf5 17.exf6 forces the bishop to recapture and so allows the knight into d6.

16...Nh5 17.Qh4 Bxg5+ 18.Qxg5 Nxe5 19.Qxh5 d4 20.Rxe5 dxc3 21.Nxc3 Rxd1+ 22.Qxd1 Rd8 23.Qe1

The rest is perfunctory.

23...exf5 24.Rxf5 Qh6+ 25.Kb1 Qxh2 26.Rxf7 Qxg2 27.Qe6+ Kb8 28.Qe5+ 1-0

As a matter of fact, it's mate in ten from here, but taking Black's rook with check would be enough to finish matters among the civilized.

The following is an example of what I like to term 'one-time cons', a name appropriated from the American poker writer John Fox. Should you happen to catch an unprepared opponent in this line, it is highly probable your victim will walk right into the trap and so you will garner an easy point.

(d) Unfortunately I came crashing back to earth when I analysed this variation with chess expert Joel Yoffie.
