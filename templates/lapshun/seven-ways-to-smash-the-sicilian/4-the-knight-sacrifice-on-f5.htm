Chapter Four. The Knight Sacrifice on f5

The sacrifice Nd4-f5 is a relatively infrequent visitor to the Open Sicilian, at least compared to the knight sacrifice on d5. It commonly occurs these days in two systems: the Velimirovic Attack and the Scheveningen.

For a change, rather than start with a verbal explanation, let's begin by looking back at the game that put the Velimirovic and its creator on the map.

Game 54
D.Velimirovic-J.Sofrevski
Yugoslav Championship, Titograd 1965

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 e6 5.Nc3 d6 6.Be3 Nf6 7.Bc4 Be7 8.Qe2 a6 9.O-O-O Qc7 10.Bb3 Na5

Less accurate is 10...b5? 11.Nxc6! Qxc6 12.Bd4 when 12...O-O is met by 13.Nd5! .

11.g4 b5 12.g5 Nxb3+

Of course, 12...Nd7? 13.Bxe6! gets three pawns and an attack for the bishop.

13.axb3 Nd7

Over forty years later, this is still a common position in grandmaster play. The sedate 14.h4 has its followers too, but the more adventurous still play Velimirovic's original idea. To Sofrevski it must have felt like a left hook to the jaw.

14.Nf5! exf5

Acceptance is pretty much forced. Evans gives 14...Bf8 15.Bd4 exf5 16.exf5+ Kd8 17.Rhe1 Qa5 18.Nd5 as winning.

15.Nd5 Qd8

Velimirovic dismisses 15...Qb7 with 16.exf5 Ne5 17.f4 Bxf5 18.fxe5 Be4 19.exd6 Bxd5 20.Bc5 Bxh1 21.d7+ Qxd7 22.Rxd7 Kxd7 23.Qxe7+ Kc6 24.Qd6+ mating.

16.exf5

Here we see the basic idea of White's sacrifice. He has diverted the pawn from e6 to allow Nc3-d5 with tempo, and he has opened the e-file while the Dark Majesty still resides there. So it's now very tempting for Black to castle...

16...O-O?

Fatal. Praxis has shown the correct defence to be 16...Bb7 17.f6! gxf6 18.Rhe1 Bxd5 19.Rxd5 Rg8! (an idea of Larsen's, first played in F.Gheorghiu-S.Hamann, Vmjacka Banja 1967) when Yrjola gives 20.gxf6 Nxf6 21.Rf5 Ng4 and now White can force a draw with 22.Bg5 or play in risky fashion for a win with 22.Bd4!? . For more details see Finnish GM Jouni Yrjola's fine work Easy Guide to the Classical Sicilian.

17.f6! gxf6

The penitent 17...Nxf6 18.gxf6 Bxf6 fails to gain absolution after 19.Bb6 Qd7 20.Nxf6+ gxf6 21.Rhg1+ .

18.Bd4

Gaining a necessary tempo to open the g-file and grab the dark squares with killing effect.

18...Ne5 19.gxf6 Bxf6 20.Rhg1+ Bg7 21.Bxe5 dxe5 22.Qxe5 f6 23.Ne7+ Kf7

23...Kh8 24.Rxd8 fxe5 25.Rxf8+ Bxf8 26.Rg8 is checkmate.

24.Qh5+ 1-0


When Black has castled the Nf5 sacrifice has more limited aims. Generally the big idea is to allow White to follow up with Nc3-d5, although occasionally the point might simply be to divert the e-pawn or to gain a tempo in some fashion. Game 55
D.Minic-R. Fischer
Rovinj-Zagreb 1970

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Bg5 e6 7.f4 Be7 8.Qf3 Qc7 9.O-O-O Nbd7 10.g4

The main alternative to 10 Bd3, as we've already seen.

10...b5 11.Bxf6 Nxf6 12.g5 Nd7 13.a3

This was in vogue in the 1960s, but has been superseded by 13.f5 .

13...Rb8

The older move 13...Bb7 became unpopular after M.Tal-S.Gligoric, Moscow 1963, which saw Tal win a fine game with 14.Bh3! .

14.h4 b4 15.axb4 Rxb4 16.Bh3 O-O!?

A daring idea introduced in this game.

16...Qc5 is considerably safer.

In this position White would like to eliminate the pawn on e6 so as to play Nc3-d5. There are two ways to do this.

17.Nf5!?

The knight need not be accepted, so a better approach is the forcing 17.Nxe6! fxe6 18.Bxe6+ Kh8 19.Nd5 Qc4 and now 20.Bf5! with the menace of Bxh7 is very strong.

17...Nc5!

Fischer continues to play thematically and press against the e-pawn whilst building up against the white king.

18.Nxe7+ Qxe7 19.h5

Gligoric prefers 19.Qe3 .

19...Bb7 20.h6 Bxe4 21.Nxe4 Nxe4 22.hxg7 Rc8!

Gligoric explains that Minic's likely intention 23.Bf5 exf5 24.Qh5 fails to 24...Rxc2+! 25.Kxc2 Qc7+ 26.Kb1 Nc3+ , so he had to slow down.

23.Rh2 Ra4 24.Kb1 d5

And Fischer, whose counterattack was by now in full swing, won in 10 more moves.

We shall see many successful examples of Nf5 against a castled king in the body of this chapter, including some fiendishly complex Velimirovic Attacks. I did say in the introduction that this is not intended as a theoretical work, but we will analyse no fewer than six examples of Pereyni's passive Nf5 sacrifice in the Scheveningen/English Attack.

Wijk aan Zee 2001

1.e4 c5 2.Nf3 d6 3.d4 cxd4 4.Nxd4 Nf6 5.Nc3 a6 6.Be3 e6 7.g4 e5 8.Nf5!? g6 9.g5 gxf5 10.exf5

White offers the knight to clear the e4- and d5-squares and the long light-square diagonal, as well as to keep the initiative. Instead recapturing the knight at once allows 10...f4. As usual, the engines tell us that this sacrifice is unsound, but many grandmasters do not agree. We feel, in fact, that Black should be able to unravel and eventually win with the extra piece, but many top grandmasters have lost trying to uphold our point of view, as in this game.

18.Qd3

18...Rg8

At present the daring try 18...O-O is unrefuted. 21...Rg5

14.a3!

After 14.gxf6 dxc3 15.Bxc3 Bb4 16.Rd5 my German friend IM Stefan Bromberger (I know him as a master of novelties which he creates with his computer; I remember how in a tournament in Barcelona he destroyed a seemingly stable variation with a new move) played the novelty 16...Qb6! , and after 17.Bxe5 Nxe5 18.Rxe5+ Kd8 19.Bg2 Kc7 20.Rd1 Bd7 21.Qf4 Bd6 22.Qc4+ Kb8 23.Re7 Rc8 24.Qxf7 Qc7 25.Rd2 Bf4 26.Qb3 Bxd2+ 27.Kxd2 Qf4+ 28.Kd1 Rc7 29.Qg3 Qxf5 30.Be4 Qg4+ 31.f3 Qxg3 32.hxg3 Ka7 33.g4 Kb6 34.g5 Bc6 35.Rxc7 Kxc7 36.Bxh7 Bxf3+ 37.Kd2 Re8 38.g6 Bd5 39.f7 Rd8 Black won with his extra rook, M.Carlsen-S.Bromberger, Gausdal 2004.

14...dxc3 15.Bxc3 Qa4 16.gxf6 Qf4+

16...Bh6+ looks like a good developing move, but after 17.Kb1 Bg5 18.Rd6 Qf4 19.Qd5 Bxf6 20.Bh3 Qh4 21.Rd1 Kf8 22.Bb4 Be7 23.Rxd7 (the engines point out that 23.f6! Nxf6 24.Qxe5 , intending 24...Bxh3 25.R6d4 , is another winning line) 23...Bxd7 24.Qxe5 Rg8 25.Rd4 Rg1+ 26.Ka2 Qxh3 27.Bxe7+ Kg8 28.Rxd7 White is winning due to his piece activity and the open black king.

17.Qxf4 exf4 18.Bc4!

According to Ivanchuk, 18.Bg2 Rb8 19.Rhe1+ Kd8 20.Re7 Bxe7 21.fxe7+ Kxe7 22.Bxh8 f6 23.Bg7 Ne5 24.Be4 is another way to give White the advantage.

18...Bc5 19.Rhe1+ Kd8 20.Re7!!

The rook on e7 exacerbates all Black's problems: paralysed queenside pieces, a weak second rank, numerous pawn islands, a weak king, and a soon-to-be-realized passed f-pawn.

20...Rf8

Fritz believes that 20...Bxf2 gives Black a playable game, but our analysis shows that after 21.Bxf7 Ra7 22.Be6 Be3+ 23.Kb1 b6 24.f7 Kxe7 25.Bxh8 Ra8 26.Bg7 h5 27.Bd5 Rb8 28.f6+ Kd6 29.f8Q+ Nxf8 30.Bxf8+ Ke5 31.f7 Bg4 32.Bg7+ White's f-pawn wins the game. Note too that 20...Bxe7? 21.fxe7+ Kxe7 22.Re1+ Kd6 23.Bxh8 is winning for White.

21.Bb4

This move helps White anchor his rook on e7, but the exchange of pieces weakens his attack. According to Ivanchuk, 21.Bxf7!? b6 22.Be6 Bxe7 23.Bxd7 Bxd7 24.fxe7+ Kxe7 25.Bb4+ Kf6 26.Bxf8 Bxf5 27.Bh6 Re8 28.Bxf4 Re2 29.Rd6+ Kf7 30.Rxb6 Rxc2+ 31.Kd1 is good for White.

21...Bxb4 22.axb4 b5 23.Bxf7 Ra7 24.Be6

Black simply can't untie his pieces without losing material, while White can improve his pieces as much as he wants.

24...h6

24...Rxf6 25.Rxh7 seems unhelpful, as a healthy black pawn is traded for a rickety white pawn, and White gets a new passer to boot.

25.Rh7 Rxf6 26.Rh8+ Kc7 27.Rd3! Nf8?

Black can't save material. Ivanchuk points out that 27...Nb6! 28.Bxc8 Ra8 29.Rc3+ Kd6 30.h3 Nxc8 31.Rcxc8 Rxc8 32.Rxc8 Ke5 could give Black better chances to survive, but at any rate was good for White.

28.Rc3+ Kb6 29.Bxc8 Rc7 30.Rxc7 Kxc7 31.Bxa6 Kb6 32.Bc8

Three extra pawns should be enough material to win.

32...f3 33.b3 Kc7 34.Be6 Nxe6 35.fxe6 Rxe6 36.Rh7+ Kc6 37.Rf7 Re1+ 38.Kb2 Rh1 39.Rf6+ Kd5 40.Rxf3+- Rxh2 41.Rf5+ Kc6 42.c4 h5 43.Rc5+ Kd6 44.Rxb5 h4 45.Rh5 h3 46.Kc3 Rh1 47.b5 h2 48.Kb4 1-0

22.Bc4

Black has problems untying his pieces while White coordinates her army step by step.

22...b5
